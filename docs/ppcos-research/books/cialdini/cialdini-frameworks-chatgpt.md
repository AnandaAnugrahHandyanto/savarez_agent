1. Reciprocity – Give Before You Ask

Core Principle & Psychology: People feel obliged to return favors and kindnesses. As Cialdini explains, “people are obliged to give back to others the form of a behavior, gift, or service that they have received first”
influenceatwork.com
. This reciprocity rule is a universal social norm: if you provide value to someone, they’ll likely want to repay you in kind. For example, waiters who gave an extra mint saw tips soar by 23% – not due to the tiny gift itself, but how it was given (personally and unexpectedly)
influenceatwork.com
. The takeaway: be the first to give – and make it personalized – to trigger the powerful urge to reciprocate

.

. By giving value first, you create a feeling of indebtedness (in a positive way) – the client may feel compelled to give you their business in return. In pricing terms, you might include a small add-on at no extra charge or start with a discounted pilot project. The client’s mindset becomes “they’ve done something nice for me, so I’ll at least consider their paid offer.” As one marketing guide notes, if a customer is given a positive free experience, “they will be more compelled to do something for you in return.”

Sales Conversations: Lead with generosity. Share a useful tip, insight, or resource before pitching your services. This makes your outreach intrinsically valuable and earns continued engagement

Even in negotiations, the reciprocal concession technique can help: if you make a concession (e.g. offering a slight discount or a flexible term), the other side often feels pressure to concede something back. The key is that your help should feel sincere, personalized and unexpected (not a generic sales freebie)

2. Scarcity – People Want What They Can’t Have

Core Principle & Psychology: The less available something is, the more we desire it. Cialdini’s Scarcity principle says people want more of what they can have less of
influenceatwork.com
. This is rooted in both psychological reactance (we resist losing freedoms/options) and simple supply-demand perception (rare = valuable). Classic example: when British Airways announced it would discontinue the London–NYC Concorde flight, sales immediately spiked – nothing about the flight changed, except it became a scarce resource
influenceatwork.com
. Scarcity works because we fear missing out on opportunities; the perception of limited time or quantity makes an offer feel more valuable and urgent.

Pricing & Offer Strategies: Leverage legitimate scarcity to prompt action. For instance, use limited-time pricing (e.g. “20% off until the end of the month”) or limit the number of slots/products available (e.g. “Only 3 client openings left this quarter”). Small agencies can frame offers as exclusive or time-sensitive to encourage prospects not to delay. Another tactic is creating exclusivity – people jump at an offer if they feel not everyone can get it. For example, “We’re inviting just 5 businesses into our beta program at a special rate” signals both scarcity and exclusiveness. Importantly, scarcity should be highlighted in what they stand to lose if they don’t act. Cialdini notes it’s not enough to tout the benefits; you should also point out “what they stand to lose if they fail to consider your proposal.”
influenceatwork.com
 For example: “This discount saves you $5,000, but it expires Friday – after that, you lose that savings.” In pricing conversations, having a deadline or limited availability offer can spur indecisive prospects to commit.

Sales Conversations: Introduce scarcity in a factual, non-aggressive way. You might mention genuine constraints like, “I only take on two new clients per month to ensure quality. Next opening is in two weeks.” This signals that your time (or service slots) are limited – encouraging the prospect to secure a spot. In proposal discussions, you can say, “If you’d like to start this project by Q1, we’d need a signed agreement by December 20, because our schedule fills up”. This sets a natural deadline. Authenticity is key: only use scarcity when it’s real. If your service really is in high demand or pricing will increase later, communicate that. For example, “Our rates will go up in January, so signing a retainer now locks in the lower rate for you” – a true time-based scarcity incentive. Scarcity can also be productized: an agency might offer a limited edition package or a bonus for early committers (“First 10 sign-ups get an extra coaching session”). Always pair scarcity with the unique value of your offer – why is it worth grabbing now? (e.g., unique expertise that’s hard to find, or a result that’s tough to obtain elsewhere).

Ethical vs. Manipulative: Ethical scarcity means being honest about limits. If something is limited, it truly is limited (by time, quantity, or capacity). This creates urgency without deception. For example, saying you have only a few client spots because you really do all the work yourself is fair. Manipulative scarcity is using false or manufactured limitations – e.g., a countdown timer that resets each day, fake “only 1 left!” claims, or bluffing about other offers. Such tactics can backfire and destroy trust if discovered. As a rule, never fabricate scarcity. Prospects today are savvy; a false sense of FOMO will feel like pressure and erode credibility. Instead, position scarcity as a “planning constraint”, not a mere gimmick

. For example, “We have two onboarding windows next month” conveys limited availability in a helpful, scheduling-oriented tone
expandia.ch
. In sum, authenticity wins – use scarcity when it naturally exists (your time, a promotional price, limited inventory) and be transparent about it. That way, urgency comes across as helpful information, not a high-pressure scare tactic.

Example (B2B Services): A boutique consulting firm usually handles 3 major projects at a time. When pitching a new client, the partner says: “We’d love to work with you, and we have one slot opening up next month. If we can finalize an agreement by then, we can dedicate the team to your project; otherwise, our next availability may be a few months out.” This genuine scarcity (limited capacity) often motivates the client to prioritize a decision. Another example: a freelance UX designer offers a year-end package for website revamps at 15% off, valid only until Dec 31. She reminds the prospect that after that date, the price returns to normal. Several clients quickly sign on in December to “lock in the savings,” illustrating how a real deadline moves people to act. In both cases, scarcity was used honestly – there truly was limited capacity or a time-bound discount – aligning the client’s urgency with the provider’s schedule or pricing policy.

Core Principle & Psychology: People defer to credible experts. We’re more likely to say “yes” when we perceive the requester as knowledgeable and trustworthy in a relevant domain

. As Cialdini puts it, “people follow the lead of credible, knowledgeable experts.”

 We use authority as a mental shortcut – much like we trust a doctor’s advice or a certified mechanic’s recommendation. Signals of authority (titles, attire, credentials, endorsements) lend weight to one’s requests. Psychologically, it reduces uncertainty: if an expert says this is the right choice, it probably is. High credibility can even support premium pricing – clients are often willing to pay more for the assurance of expertise. For instance, an agency might label one package as “Expert Review Included” or have a senior consultant (with notable credentials) assigned to the project, explaining that this expertise commands a higher fee. When structuring offers, back them up with authoritative evidence: include data, research results, or best-practice frameworks that lend legitimacy. For example, “Our strategy follows a proven framework endorsed by [Industry Association]” or “This plan is based on a Harvard Business Review-recommended approach.” These references to external authority reassure the client that the pricing is grounded in real value, not fluff. Also consider social authority: if you’ve achieved something noteworthy (e.g., “Google Certified Partner” or “Forbes Coaches Council member”), integrate that into your proposal or pricing page as a value-add. Essentially, embed proof of expertise into the offer – it makes the decision about “which consultant to choose” easier if one clearly has stronger credentials or experience.

 Show expertise in a way that guides the client to feel safer and informed, rather than using it to browbeat them into agreement.

4. Social Proof (Consensus) – Use the Power of Peers

Core Principle & Psychology: Especially in uncertain situations, people look to what others are doing to decide on their own actions
influenceatwork.com
. This is the principle of Social Proof (also called consensus). We figure if many others (or similar others) trust something, it’s probably good. It’s basic herd behavior: following the crowd feels safer. Cialdini demonstrated this dramatically in a hotel experiment: simply telling guests that “75% of people who stayed in this room reused their towels” boosted towel reuse by 33% – the highest increase achieved, more than just asking them to help the environment
influenceatwork.com
influenceatwork.com
. In business, testimonials, client lists, and usage statistics all serve as social proof. Seeing that “companies like yours” have succeeded with a service reduces the perceived risk. Psychologically, it taps our desire to belong and our assumption that if others chose it, it must be the right choice.

Pricing & Offers: Incorporate social proof into how you present your services and prices. For example, highlight your most popular package (“Most of our clients choose the Professional Plan”) – this can gently nudge new buyers toward that option because it signals a trusted choice. You can also mention numbers of customers (“Join 50+ businesses who have worked with us”) or specific outcomes others achieved (“$5M in revenue growth delivered to our clients last year”). Another strategy: include testimonials or brief case studies alongside your offer. On a proposal, you might add a quote from a happy client on the page with the recommended solution, reinforcing that others have benefitted. When making an offer to a small business owner, you could say, “We’ve helped businesses similar to yours – in fact, 3 other SaaS companies your size hired us for this and saw a 20% boost in conversions.” This both reduces uncertainty and justifies the investment (it’s worked for peers, so likely worth the price). Peer benchmarking can also influence pricing discussions: e.g., “90% of our retainer clients renew for another year – we become their long-term partner,” subtly tells the prospect that not continuing might put them in the losing minority. Use social proof in pricing sparingly and truthfully – it should feel encouraging, not like bragging. The goal is to show, “People like you value this service,” easing the fear of making a bad choice.

Sales Conversations: Leverage stories and examples of other clients’ success to assure your prospect. During your pitch, share relevant anecdotes: “One of our other clients in the fintech sector had the same hesitation about migrating to the cloud, but after we implemented the solution, they saw a 30% improvement in response time. They’ve even referred us to others because it worked so well.” The key is to match the reference group to your prospect. As one B2B sales guide notes, “Replace generic logos with tightly matched case fragments – same industry, similar size…”

. In conversation, this might sound like: “In fact, another industrial supplier with ~50 employees we worked with faced a similar challenge, and we helped them reduce order processing time by 25%

.” That specificity (“peers like you”) makes the proof more convincing. Also, cite statistics or trends: “8 out of 10 CEOs we speak with are investing more in digital marketing this year. Many have already started – we don’t want you to fall behind.” Hearing that a majority or a credible number of others are doing something creates a bandwagon effect. Additionally, consider facilitating direct social proof: offer references. For instance, “If you’d like, I can put you in touch with a couple of current clients in your industry, so you can hear their experience.” The mere offer can boost confidence (and if they take it, the clients’ voices become powerful proof). Social proof is extremely effective for reducing uncertainty – it answers the prospect’s unspoken question, “Has this worked for others?” with a resounding yes.

Ethical vs. Manipulative: Ethical use of social proof means sharing truthful, relevant information about others’ experiences. Only use real testimonials from actual clients (and ideally get permission). Keep case studies factual and don’t overhype results beyond what was achieved. An ethical marketer never fabricates reviews or statistics. Manipulative social proof would involve lies or distortion – e.g., citing “industry data” that doesn’t exist, using stock testimonials from fake people, or claiming consensus where there is none (“Everyone is switching to us!” when that’s not true). Another grey area is peer pressure to the point of fear: e.g., “All your competitors are doing this, you’ll be doomed if you don’t” – that might cross into undue pressure unless it’s genuinely the trend. The honest approach is, as one source advises, never exaggerate outcomes; tie claims to verifiable metrics

. If 3 out of 5 clients saw X result, say that – don’t say “all our clients do amazingly 100% of the time.” Also, ensure the comparison group is appropriate. For example, telling a tiny startup “Google uses this, so you should” isn’t really relevant (Google’s “peer” is not a 5-person company). Instead, use examples that the client can identify with. Transparency is part of ethics: if asked, be ready to provide details or introductions to those references. In short, be truthful and relevant – let real customer voices do the convincing. Real social proof builds trust; fake social proof, if uncovered, destroys it.

Reading these, the prospect feels reassured that this agency is proven and reliable. In fact, seeing a quote like “[Agency] helped us grow our pipeline by 50% in 6 months – worth every penny” from a similar-sized business might be the final nudge that closes the deal. These examples show social proof in action: the prospects take comfort in the successes of peers, making it much easier for them to say “yes” with confidence.

5. Liking – Personal Connection Sells

Core Principle & Psychology: People prefer to say “yes” to those they like
influenceatwork.com
. We are more easily influenced by people we feel fondly about or similar to. Cialdini identifies three major factors that increase liking: similarity, compliments, and cooperation toward common goals
influenceatwork.com
. In other words, we like people who we perceive as like us, who praise us, or who work with us. This principle is intuitive – think about it: you’ll go the extra mile for a friend or someone who’s been kind to you. In a sales context, if the client genuinely likes you (or your team), they want to find a way to work together. Rapport isn’t just “nice to have” – it tangibly improves persuasion. For example, a study of MBA students in negotiation found that groups told to share personal info and find a commonality before negotiating reached agreement 90% of the time, versus only 55% for those who skipped the small talk
influenceatwork.com
. Simply discovering a shared hobby or hometown (“Oh, you’re from Melbourne too!”) can shift the dynamic dramatically in your favor. Friendly familiarity breeds trust.

Pricing & Offers: Build likeability into your brand and proposals. For instance, use a warm, human tone in your proposal copy rather than stiff corporate language – let your personality show appropriately. A client who feels they know you (and like you) will be more receptive even when discussing price. You might include a personal note or a tailored touch in your offer: “P.S. I noticed on your blog you love cycling – I’m a big cyclist too. Maybe we can do a ride sometime!” Such details (when genuine) set you apart from faceless competitors. In terms of pricing, while you shouldn’t drop your price just to be liked, you can frame discussions cooperatively: “Let’s work together to find an option that fits your budget” – implying you’re on their side. If the client likes you, they’re also more likely to communicate honestly about budget constraints, giving you a chance to flexibly tailor the offer. Some consultants include a small freebie or thoughtful gesture in the offer (which overlaps with reciprocity) – e.g., offering a complimentary training session for the client’s staff if they sign the deal. This feels like a friendly favor and increases goodwill. Ultimately, clients often choose the provider who “just felt right” even if the price is a bit higher – that “feeling” often comes down to liking and trust. So ensure your entire selling approach is personable. Use photos of your team (smiling, approachable) in your materials, write a brief “About us” that shares your values or story (so they find common ground), and show empathy for the client’s situation in your proposal text (“We understand how hectic it is to run a restaurant – our solution aims to lighten your load.”). These elements make your offer not just a cold document, but a promise from one human to another.

Sales Conversations: Rapport, rapport, rapport. In practice, this means spend a few minutes on personal connection before diving into business. Find a genuine common point: perhaps you notice their college diploma on the wall (hey, your friend went there), or they mention they have kids (you swap a quick parent story), or even commenting on something you truly like about their business (“I checked out your product – I love the design, it’s clear a lot of care went into it!”). Such small talk and compliments are not wasted time; they’re investments in likability and trust. Cialdini suggests to “look for areas of similarity…and genuine compliments…before you get down to business.”

 If you and the prospect discover you share an interest or perspective, highlight it: “Same here!” – that simple phrase can create an instant bond. Cooperation is another aspect: frame the meeting as collaborative (“We’re on the same team trying to solve this problem”) rather than adversarial. Something as simple as using inclusive language (“we” instead of “you vs. me”) when appropriate can subconsciously put you on their side. Also, mirroring the prospect’s communication style can help (if they are formal, be a bit formal; if they joke, you can joke too). Be authentic in praise – find something honestly praiseworthy: “Your website is really user-friendly – many companies struggle with that, so kudos to your team.” People rarely tire of sincere compliments! Such positive feelings can carry through the entire sales process. By the time you ask for the sale, the client might feel like they’re dealing with a friend or someone who really “gets” them. And psychologically, it’s hard to say no to someone you like – you want to find a way to say yes.

Ethical vs. Manipulative: The line here is sincerity. Ethical liking means you genuinely find common ground and admire aspects of the client, and you communicate that truthfully. It’s about real human connection – which often means listening more than talking, and caring about the client’s interests. Manipulative use of liking would be fake flattery or manufactured affinity. For example, lying about your interests (“Oh, you golf? Me too!” when you never have) just to ingratiate yourself is dishonest and can backfire if discovered. People can often sense forced charm or disingenuous compliments, which then erodes trust. Another manipulative move is over-doing it: e.g., constantly agreeing with everything the client says or flinging excessive praise – it can come off as pandering.  The goal is to “charm and disarm” without being deceitful

Ethical liking builds a foundation of trust and positive regard; it doesn’t guarantee a sale, but it creates an environment where influence can flourish. If you truly care about your client’s success and show up as a friendly, honest partner, that’s ethical persuasion. If you just pretend to care to get the deal, that’s manipulation. 6. Commitment & Consistency – Start Small to Get Big Yeses

Core Principle & Psychology: People have a deep desire to be consistent with their past actions and statements. If they commit to something in a small way, they are more likely to continue in that direction to stay congruent with their self-image
influenceatwork.com
. In short, getting a small “yes” paves the way for a bigger “yes.” Cialdini notes that consistency is activated by looking for small initial commitments to elicit
influenceatwork.com
. A classic illustration: researchers got homeowners to agree to put a tiny postcard in their window for a “Drive Safely” campaign; two weeks later, those same homeowners were 4x more likely to put an ugly big “Drive Safely” billboard on their lawn, compared to a control group who hadn’t made the small commitment first
influenceatwork.com
. The small act of saying “I support this cause” primed them to act consistently by agreeing to a much larger request later. Consistency is powerful because inconsistency often feels like a threat to one’s integrity or rationality – we like to appear stable and stick to our word.

Pricing & Offers: Use a “foot-in-the-door” approach with your services and pricing. Rather than pushing a large, expensive engagement upfront, consider offering a small starter engagement to gain initial commitment. For example, a consultant might propose a short discovery project or assessment (maybe a paid one-day analysis for a small fee) as phase 1, before the full 6-month retainer. Once the client agrees to that first step (and sees value), they are more inclined to proceed to the bigger project – after all, they’ve started the journey with you. In pricing strategy, you can also break down payments (e.g. monthly instead of annual lump sum) to lower the barrier for initial commitment. Another angle: ask for a small favor or agreement early – for instance, have the prospect sign a non-binding Letter of Intent or agree in writing to a project outline. The act of signing or writing reinforces their commitment
influenceatwork.com
. Studies show even something as simple as having patients write down their next appointment led to 18% fewer no-shows (because writing it themselves made them feel committed)
influenceatwork.com
. Similarly, getting a potential client to physically or verbally acknowledge parts of your proposal can increase follow-through. In practice: you might include a checklist in the proposal for the client to tick their needs – they are actively confirming their challenges, which makes them psychologically ready to accept your solution (to be consistent with their expressed needs). Align your offer with the client’s stated values or goals: early in discussions get them to verbalize what they want (e.g., “I want to double my sales this year”). Then frame your proposal exactly as the path to achieve that goal they committed to. The pricing and package should be presented as the logical next step to honor what they already said they want. If the offer clearly helps them remain consistent with their goals or promises (say, to their board or themselves), they’re far more likely to accept it.

Sales Conversations: Secure small “yeses” throughout the process. In a meeting, this can be as simple as asking questions that elicit agreement: “Do you agree that X is a challenge worth solving?” or “Would it be fair to say you’re looking for a sustainable solution, not just a quick fix?” When the prospect says “yes” to these reasonable, low-stakes questions, they are actively aligning with your viewpoint. Each yes is a micro-commitment. By the time you ask the big closing question (“Shall we move forward with the contract?”), saying “yes” is consistent with the pattern and with all the little yeses they already gave. Another tactic is to set action commitments early: for example, at the end of a discovery call, ask the prospect to commit to a next step like “Can we agree to a follow-up call next week where I’ll present a tailored plan?” Once they say yes and mark it on their calendar, they’ve made a public (to you) commitment. They’ll be likely to honor it, and during that next call, you can reference what was previously agreed (“Last week you mentioned if the plan met X criteria, you’d be ready to move ahead – does that hold true?”). Always try to get important agreements in writing or visible form, even if informal – for instance, send a recap email: “Just to confirm, you agreed that if we can solve A, B, C within 3 months, this project would be a success worth investing in. We’ll aim to demonstrate that in a pilot.” This summary not only clarifies understanding but also, as a “commitment record,” increases the prospect’s commitment to those stated conditions
expandia.ch
. They see their own words and goals echoed back, which nudges them to stay consistent with them. Over the course of the sales cycle, think of it as building a chain of commitments: from small (attending a meeting, acknowledging a problem) to medium (agreeing to a trial/pilot, giving a tentative yes pending details) to large (signing the deal). Each link in the chain should feel like a natural progression from the last.

Ethical vs. Manipulative: Ethical consistency means helping the client make commitments that are truly in line with their needs and values, then reinforcing those commitments to facilitate a beneficial decision. It’s a gentle guiding process – the client should never feel trapped. Be transparent about next steps and give them the freedom to say no at each stage (which ironically makes their “yes” even more meaningful). Manipulative use of consistency could be employing the “bait-and-switch” – get them to agree to something small and then push something they didn’t sign up for, exploiting their reluctance to back out. For example, a manipulative approach might hide the full scope or cost at first, then guilt the client by saying “Well, you already agreed to do this…” That’s not true consistency; that’s trickery. Another unethical tactic is using public pressure excessively – e.g., making someone commit to something in front of their boss or team so they feel they can’t back out, even if they want to. Also, ensure the initial commitment is meaningful, not just a gimmick. If you ask purely trivial or unrelated questions just to get a “yes” (classic cheesy sales “Yes-ladders”), savvy clients might see through it. It can feel patronizing (“They’re just trying to get me to say yes repeatedly”). Instead, ask substantive questions that genuinely clarify needs and get alignment. That way the commitments are real. Ethical consistency honors any change of heart too – if circumstances shift and the client logically shouldn’t proceed, don’t try to hold them hostage to a past minor commitment. Always keep the client’s true interest at heart: the idea is to remove friction in decision-making, not to railroad them. When done right, consistency techniques help clients stick to their own expressed goals and values, effectively helping them persuade themselves that moving forward with you is the right thing.

Example (B2B Services): A marketing consultant is selling a package of services to a startup. Rather than pitching the full annual contract immediately, she first secures a small commitment: a paid $500 marketing audit. The startup agrees and pays for this audit (first yes). After delivering the audit, the consultant asks, “Would you like me to present a 3-month action plan based on these findings? It’ll show how we can boost your lead flow by 50%.” The client agrees to see the plan (second commitment). In that plan presentation, the consultant reminds them, “Earlier you mentioned your goal is to double your leads this year. This 3-month pilot is designed to achieve half of that – a 50% increase – which puts you on track for the year. Does that sound like a plan you’d commit to implementing?” The client, who already invested in the audit and said doubling leads was a priority, feels consistent saying yes (third commitment) to the 3-month pilot project. Once the pilot is successful (and by now the client has a pattern of saying yes and seeing results), the consultant proposes the full annual engagement to sustain and build on the momentum. The client signs on, largely because at this point it feels like the natural extension of what they’ve already been doing together. At no point did the client feel forced; each step was a logical continuation, and backing out would have felt inconsistent with their earlier commitments and success. This example shows how starting with a small step and progressively earning larger commitments leads to a big win – a classic foot-in-the-door. Contrast this with a manipulative approach: if the consultant had hidden the fact that the audit was a prelude to a bigger upsell, or pressured the client by saying “But you promised!” inappropriately, the client might have gotten defensive. Instead, by aligning with the client’s own stated goal (more leads) and taking it step by step, the consultant made the client an active collaborator in the decision. The client’s final yes was simply consistent with all the positive yeses before.

7. Unity – Influence Through Shared Identity

Core Principle & Psychology: Unity is Cialdini’s newest principle of influence – it goes a level deeper than liking. Unity is about shared identity. It means the more someone perceives you as “one of us,” the more persuasive you become
medium.com
. While liking can be surface-level (we enjoy each other’s company), unity speaks to a feeling of kinship or group membership. This could be based on family ties, ethnicity, nationality, professional groups, or even a cause or creed. Cialdini describes it as the influencer and influencee being part of the same tribe
medium.com
. When people feel united, they adopt each other’s suggestions almost as if advising themselves. An example: Cialdini found that if he framed advice as something he’d tell his family, it became more convincing
medium.com
. In one case, simply by saying “I’ll tell you what I’d tell my own family member,” investor Warren Buffett increased trust tremendously in a shareholder letter
medium.com
. Another example: a researcher persuaded Japanese leaders in 1941 to protect Jews by saying “We are Asian, like you”, invoking a shared identity that powerfully influenced their decision
medium.com
. The psychology here is tribal: if you are of my tribe, I will act in our collective interest. Unity can be based on any identity marker that people deeply value (family, team, community, values, etc.).

Pricing & Offers: Frame your services in a way that fosters a sense of partnership or belonging. Instead of a pure vendor-customer relationship, present your offer as joining forces. For example, brand your offering like a membership or community (“When you become a client, you join our family of companies who get ongoing support and networking”). For a consultant or agency under $50M clients, you might say, “We consider our clients as partners – your success is our success.” Including language like “partnership” or “team up” in proposals can signal unity. Also, if there are groups you and your client both belong to (industry associations, alumni networks, local business groups), incorporate that: “As fellow members of the {XYZ Association}, we understand the standards you uphold.” This shows you share an identity or at least an affiliation. If applicable, you could offer special pricing for an in-group: e.g., “We offer a 10% discount for fellow veteran-owned businesses,” explicitly acknowledging a shared group (both you and the client are veteran-owned, in this hypothetical). This isn’t just a monetary gesture; it’s saying “we’re in the same boat.” Scarcity was about exclusivity; unity is about inclusivity – making the client feel part of your group or vice versa. When clients feel “this person is one of us,” they’re more comfortable investing money with you. Another idea: create a client community (like a roundtable or Slack group of your clients). Then in your offer, mention “access to our client network of peers.” Now the purchase is not just buying a service, it’s joining a peer group – which appeals to our social identity needs. In essence, align your pricing and packaging with messages of “togetherness.” Even a phrase like “Let’s grow together – we’ll act as an extension of your team” in a proposal can increase the client’s sense that by hiring you, they gain an ally within their circle, not an outsider.

Sales Conversations: Emphasize common identities and goals. Right from the start, find any genuine similarity that implies “we’re on the same side.” Are you from the same hometown? Did you both attend small liberal arts colleges? Do you both have a background in engineering? Any mutual connections? Even if on the surface you don’t find obvious commonalities, you can create unity by adopting the client’s team as your own: use language like “we” when talking about solving their problem (“How can we tackle this?”). Show that you identify with their mission: “Your mission to bring affordable healthcare to communities – that really resonates with me, because my family struggled with medical bills growing up.” That kind of statement, if true, forges a deep connection – you’re saying my story intersects with yours. Cialdini notes even using family language can invoke unity

medium.com
. For instance, you could say to a client, “If I were in your shoes, here’s what I’d advise my own brother to do.” This implies you’re treating them like family – a tight unity cue. Another tactic is co-creation: involve the client in brainstorming or creating the solution. When people collaborate on something, they feel ownership and unity in that creation
medium.com
. During sales meetings, make it a joint problem-solving session rather than a one-sided pitch. E.g., “Let’s sketch this out together” – literally drawing on the same whiteboard can symbolically put you on the same team. Additionally, highlight any shared stakeholders or enemies: “We both want to impress your board of directors with clear results” (shared stakeholders), or “We’re both fighting against industry complacency” (shared foe/goal). By aligning yourself with the client’s identity and cause, you move from being just another vendor to being an insider or ally. Unity essentially “reframes your role from vendor to partner.”
expandia.ch
 That reframing can dramatically increase your persuasive power, because you’re no longer an outsider asking for money – you’re a teammate working towards a mutual victory.

Ethical vs. Manipulative: Ethical unity comes from real commonalities and a sincere commitment to the client’s interests as if they were your own. It should never be faked. Manipulative unity would be pretending to share an identity or values you don’t, which is a serious breach of trust if discovered. For example, falsely claiming “I consider you like family” or exaggerating connections (“We’re basically the same company culture!” when you’re not) is dishonest. Also, be careful not to exploit sensitive identities: don’t misuse someone’s personal affiliations (religion, ethnicity, etc.) for gain – that can be offensive and unethical. Unity is powerful because it taps into tribal loyalty; abusing that (like cult leaders or unethical marketers might) is clearly wrong. Keep it authentic and respectful: find unity in places it truly exists (even if that’s just a shared goal of the project’s success). Also, ensure that by fostering unity you can deliver on that promise. If you say “We’re like part of your team,” you must be prepared to act that way – e.g., be transparent, collaborate closely, maybe even share some risk. Don’t use unity language lightly if you plan to behave like a distant contractor. In ethical practice, unity should enhance mutual understanding and cooperation; in manipulation, it’s used to lower the client’s guard for exploitation. Always test your motives: are you invoking “us” because you genuinely believe in a partnership, or just to influence? If the latter, rethink your approach. When done right, unity can create beautiful long-term client relationships (they may literally start referring to you as “our extended team”). That’s a sign it’s genuine – and it benefits both sides.

Example (B2B Services): A small digital agency is pitching to a nonprofit organization. Early in the conversation, the agency lead mentions, “Our team is actually mostly made up of former nonprofit folks – we started this agency because we all had stints in nonprofits and understood the marketing challenges. So we feel like we’re on the same mission to amplify important causes.” Immediately, the client sees the agency as part of the nonprofit community (shared identity), not just a for-profit vendor. This unity is reinforced in the proposal where the agency writes, “Think of us as an extension of your marketing department – your goals are our goals.” Throughout the project, the agency uses inclusive language, saying things like “We’ll work together to achieve the outreach targets.” The client, feeling the agency “gets it” and shares their values, enthusiastically signs on and even loops the agency into internal strategy calls as if they were internal staff. Another example: a freelance IT consultant in a sales meeting finds out the prospect is a fellow alumnus of the same university. They bond over that (“Go Wildcats! I remember those campus days…”). That common pride creates immediate goodwill and unity. The consultant later says, “We learned in school about doing well by doing good – I still carry that forward in my work, which is why I’m excited to help a fellow Wildcat’s company excel.” The prospect feels a near familial bond (alumni networks can be tight-knit) and strongly leans towards hiring this consultant over others who pitched, because “he’s one of us.” In both scenarios, unity was rooted in truth (the agency really had nonprofit experience; the consultant really shared the alma mater). And it led to a partnership vibe: the clients didn’t see these providers as outsiders, but as trusted insiders, which is enormously persuasive.

Pre-Suasion: Creating Privileged Moments Before the Ask

Beyond the principles of what to say or do, Cialdini’s concept of Pre-Suasion is about when and how you set up your message. A “privileged moment” is the ideal window of time immediately before you present an idea or proposal, in which the audience is especially receptive to your message
wizardofads.org
. In other words, what someone is focused on right before you make your request can hugely sway their response. As Cialdini defines it, “Privileged moments are the moments we create immediately before we present an idea… so that people are attuned to that idea… by creating a mindset in them that is consistent with the goal of our message.”
wizardofads.org
 In practice, this means priming the person with a related thought or feeling, so that saying “yes” feels natural and in line with what they’re already thinking about. Done right, pre-suasion can dramatically increase your chances of success – research shows it can more than double compliance just by altering the pre-message state of mind
wizardofads.org
.

Core Concept & Why It Works: Pre-Suasion works on the principle that what we pay attention to primes our subsequent decisions. Our attitudes can be shifted by seemingly minor cues that bring certain values or self-perceptions to the forefront of our minds. The famous example: researchers asked people “Do you consider yourself a helpful person?” right before requesting help with a survey. With no pre-question, only 29% agreed to help. But after affirming “Yes, I’m a helpful person,” 77% agreed to the survey – the same request became far more successful
wizardofads.org
wcponline.com
. Why? Because people wanted to act consistently with the positive identity that had just been highlighted (helpful person)
wcponline.com
. The key insight: people respond not just to your offer, but to how your offer aligns with who they believe they are – and you can influence that belief by what you draw attention to beforehand
wcponline.com
. In Cialdini’s words, the “secret doesn’t lie in the message itself, but in the key moment before that message”
amazon.com
. For consultants and agencies, this means the questions you ask or points you highlight before pitching are as important as the pitch itself.

Practical Application (Consultants & Agencies): To use Pre-Suasion, think about the mental state or identity you want your prospect to be in when considering your offer. Then, craft a brief interaction right before your pitch that brings that state to the surface:

Ask a Prime Question: Pose a question that gets the prospect to endorse a value that your service fulfills. For example, before presenting a marketing service, ask: “Do you see yourself as a business that values long-term growth over short-term fixes?” If they say yes (who wouldn’t want to be seen as growth-minded?), your subsequent pitch for an SEO campaign (a long-term growth strategy) will resonate with the identity they just claimed. Similarly, a consultant might ask: “Would you agree that staying ahead of industry trends is important for a company like yours?” – a “yes” here pre-suades the client to embrace your upcoming proposal for an innovative strategy.

Invoke a Desirable Identity: In marketing copy or sales calls, explicitly reference an identity and invite the prospect to step into it before the offer. A home services company did this in an ad: “Are you the kind of homeowner who takes pride in protecting your family’s health?” If the reader thinks “Yes, I am,” they’re primed to respond positively to the offer of a water filtration test that follows
wcponline.com
. For B2B, appeal to identities like “smart decision-maker,” “proactive leader,” “innovator,” “community-minded entrepreneur.” For instance, “Most of the business owners I work with are proactive about increasing their online visibility – I get the sense you are too. In that spirit, can I show you a strategy that’s worked for them?” Now the prospect, having just seen themselves as a proactive owner, is inclined to listen and agree, because doing so aligns with that self-image.

Evoke an Emotional Frame: Sometimes telling a quick story or showing an image before your pitch can create an emotional lens that favors your message. For example, if you’re about to propose a security solution, briefly recount a relatable story of a company that suffered a breach (focusing attention on the pain of insecurity). The client, now emotionally attuned to security concerns, will be more receptive to your security offering than if you launched in cold. Conversely, you might want a positive frame: before negotiating, share a short success story of a win-win partnership, putting the client in a collaborative, optimistic mood.

Environmental/Contextual Primes: If meeting in person or via video, even elements of the environment can pre-suade. If you want to emphasize trust and transparency, maybe start the meeting by showing a quick behind-the-scenes of your team at work (via a photo or anecdote) to prime openness. If you want to highlight urgency, you might show a recent industry headline about rapid changes – priming concern that time is of the essence. Online, the layout of a landing page can pre-suade: for instance, a site selling consulting that boosts profits might first display an image of money or growth charts – subtly putting “growth” in the visitor’s mind before they read about the service.

Consensus Priming: Pre-Suasion can overlap with social proof. E.g., at the start of a sales presentation, show a slide: “Over 80% of tech firms plan to outsource XYZ this year”. This primes the idea that outsourcing is common (and smart), so when you propose outsourcing to them, it’s already framed as the norm.

Ethical vs. Manipulative: Ethical Pre-Suasion involves primes that are truthful and relevant to the decision at hand. You are essentially highlighting an aspect of the situation or the client’s own values that genuinely connects to your offer. For example, asking someone if they care about quality when your service actually improves quality is fair and helps them clarify their priorities. Manipulative Pre-Suasion would be using a prime that confuses or unduly biases the client in a way unrelated to their welfare – for instance, stirring up an emotion or identity that has nothing to do with your product just to cloud their judgment. If a prime deceives (like leading a question to get a yes for something the service won’t actually deliver) or exploits vulnerabilities (like using fear with no solution, or appealing to vanity just to upsell), that’s unethical. A guiding rule: the pre-suasive prompt should help the client focus on a true, relevant benefit or need. It should make them more aware of why your solution could be valuable to them (aligning with their values), rather than tricking them into a yes. Also, always respect autonomy: even after priming, the client should feel it’s their conscious choice, not that they were psychologically maneuvered. When you use pre-suasion ethically, clients often later say “That just made sense to do” – because you helped them see that sense themselves. When misused, clients might later feel “I was pressured or manipulated” once the prime wears off.

Example (B2B Services): A sales consultant is about to pitch a training program to a small business CEO. Before jumping into the slide deck, he asks the CEO, “Out of curiosity, do you pride yourself on being an open-minded leader?” The CEO answers yes (few would say “no, I’m closed-minded”). The consultant smiles and says, “That’s great – being open-minded to new approaches is exactly what made other CEOs I’ve worked with successful in boosting their sales.” Now the CEO is subtly thinking of herself as an open-minded leader who considers new ideas. The consultant then introduces an unconventional sales methodology. Instead of meeting resistance, the CEO leans in — to be consistent with her self-image, she gives the new idea a fair shot. In another case, a web agency’s homepage headline pre-suades visitors with the question: “Ready to double your leads?” Visitors who nod “yes” to themselves are already mentally agreeing that doubling leads is desirable and something they are up for. Scrolling down, the agency then presents a call-to-action for a free consultation on lead generation. Because the visitor just effectively said “I want more leads” in their head, they’re far likelier to click and convert (their mindset now aligns with the offer). As a final illustration, consider a scenario from the earlier water-testing example

: when setting appointments, the office staff would say to prospects, “Most people I speak to are proactive about their family’s well-being. I get the sense you are too. Is that true?” The homeowner agrees, “Yes, I am.” The staffer follows, “Great – that’s exactly why we offer a free water test, so proactive folks like you can ensure everything’s safe. Can I schedule that for you?” The result: appointments shoot up, because homeowners, wanting to act in line with being “proactive about family’s well-being,” say yes to the test. Each of these examples shows Pre-Suasion in action: a simple question or statement before the pitch that “attunes” the client’s mind to be receptive to the pitch

Conclusion: By integrating Cialdini’s persuasion principles into your selling approach, you create a powerful yet ethical playbook for winning clients. For a small agency or consultant, these aren’t abstract theories – they’re practical tools: give value first to trigger reciprocity, highlight what’s unique and fleeting to invoke scarcity, earn trust through authority signals, reassure with proof of others’ success, build real rapport and likability, gain momentum with small commitments leading to big ones, and foster a united front with your client. On top of that, set the stage with pre-suasion – prime the mindset that will naturally gravitate toward your solution. Used sincerely, these methods clarify the value of your offering and make it easy for clients to say “Yes!” – not only because they are persuaded, but because they feel it’s the obvious, right decision for them. And that is the ultimate win-win in business influence.
