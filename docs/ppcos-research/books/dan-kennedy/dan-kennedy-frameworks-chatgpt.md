2. Resist the Discounting Trap (Sell Value, Not Price)

Kennedy is adamant: discounting is dangerous if done haphazardly. Slashing prices not only eats into profit; it trains customers to view you as a commodity and wait for the next sale. “When you offer a discount, you are taking the focus from the value you provide and placing it squarely on your price,” he warns
dannybooboo.com
. For service businesses especially, constant discounts undermine your positioning as an expert.

Avoid Price-Cutting as a Strategy: Many agencies panic and cut fees to win deals. Dan Kennedy’s advice: don’t! Deep down, discounting is a confidence issue – being afraid your service isn’t “worth” full price. Kennedy’s philosophy is to stand firm on your value. He notes that any time you do run a sale, you must have a compelling reason and strict rules for it
dannybooboo.com
. A discount should be an exception, not the norm (e.g. a very limited Black Friday deal, or a last-minute fill of an empty project slot – with clear time limits). Never simply cut price out of desperation. As a rule of thumb, if you can’t articulate “Why am I reducing the price?” in a way that still preserves your value story, don’t do it.

Add Value Instead of Subtracting Price: Kennedy often encourages offering bonuses or packages rather than lower prices. For example, if a client hesitates at your $10k proposal, resist dropping to $7k. Instead, stack on more value: an extra consulting session, 3 months of support, or an additional service module. This way you maintain rate integrity while increasing the perceived value. It turns the conversation from “price is too high” to “look at everything we’re getting.” He also suggests framing any concession in value terms: “This is a $2,000 strategy workshop I normally charge for – I’ll include it free if you decide by Friday.” The price stays full, but the client feels they gained a deal. You’ve enhanced value without capitulating on price.

Have a “No Discount” Policy (with Planned Exceptions): Many top consultants publicize that they don’t discount. Dan himself is famous for holding the line on his fees. This policy can be part of your positioning: premium services don’t go on sale. However, you can still reward clients in other ways – e.g. fast-action bonuses, referral credits, or added services – that have high perceived value but low cost to you. Kennedy notes that any sale or special must have a rationale (e.g. “end of quarter clearance” or “new product launch celebration”) and a hard end-date
dannybooboo.com
. Without a reason, customers just conclude your regular prices are arbitrary.

Defend Your Margins Relentlessly: Once you start habitually discounting, it’s a slippery slope that can cripple your business. Kennedy’s No B.S. Price Strategy reminds us: “Once you start to compete on price… even if you win, you lose,” because you’ve eroded profit and someone can still undercut you further
dannybooboo.com
. He cites tough economic times where businesses instinctively reach for the red pen to slash prices – calling this a huge mistake. “The best recession or tough-times price strategy is creative and complex – not a red pen to mark down prices,” Kennedy writes
dannybooboo.com
. In downturns, innovate your offers, don’t just cheapen them. For instance, a marketing agency could create a results-based payment plan or a smaller starter package – different pricing structures rather than across-the-board discounts. This way you address client budget concerns without devaluing your core service.

✋ Where This Clashes with Trends: In the age of Groupon and constant promo codes, Kennedy’s stance is boldly contrarian. Modern e-commerce trains customers to expect 20% off or endless sales; Dan would say this is “pricing suicide” for a high-value service provider. While many SaaS firms and coaches lure new clients with $1 trials or heavy discounts, Kennedy would argue you’re attracting the wrong clients – bargain-hunters who will churn or nickel-and-dime you.  if they perceive them as higher quality. The renegade approach is to hold your price, demonstrate superior value, and let competitors wreck their margins in the discount wars. As an agency, your mantra should be: Premium outcomes justify premium prices.

3. “Takeaway Selling” – Make Clients Qualify Themselves

One of Dan Kennedy’s most famed sales tactics is “takeaway selling.” This is the art of pulling back an offer to increase the prospect’s desire. It flips the script: instead of you chasing the client, the client chases you. Kennedy uses takeaway selling to position himself as a prized expert whose time is scarce. As he famously practices, he won’t do project work for a client until they’ve first completed a (paid) day of consulting – and even that requires jumping through hoops to book

. By not being too eager to “close” every prospect, you paradoxically close more (and better) deals.

Invoke the Fear of Loss: Takeaway selling works because of basic human psychology – we want most what we might lose or can’t have. As one commentator of Dan’s notes, “When something we want is put in front of us then taken away, we’ll work harder to get it…than if it were freely available.”

 Scarcity and exclusivity create desire and perceived value. You can apply this by, for example, telling a prospect, “I’m not sure we’re accepting new clients this quarter, but I might have one slot – why don’t you fill out an application and we’ll see if it’s a fit.” This simple “maybe you can’t have it” moment often makes them want it more. Key point: You must genuinely be prepared to say no. Kennedy’s rule is to never bluff about scarcity – actually have limited slots or criteria, so you can confidently pull away if needed.

Position Yourself as the Prize: Kennedy urges professionals to stop acting like a desperate vendor and start acting like a sought-after specialist. For instance, rather than saying “Please hire us, we can start anytime,” you might say “We’re booked solid for six weeks, but I could start your project on X date if we mutually determine it’s a good fit.” The difference? In the latter, you are qualifying them, not the other way around. You’re an expert whose time is valuable.

Rather than a prospect immediately booking you, have them fill out a questionnaire or do a brief discovery call to see if they qualify for your programs. This “hoop” serves two purposes: (1) It filters out the tire-kickers and price-shoppers – only serious prospects will follow through. (2) It telegraphs that you have standards and that not everyone is accepted

4. Direct Response Principles for Service Businesses

Dan Kennedy is a die-hard advocate of direct response marketing, especially for service businesses that traditionally rely on word-of-mouth or “brand advertising.” His core message: Every marketing activity should provoke a response or measurable action – otherwise, why do it? In his No B.S. Direct Marketing books and Magnetic Marketing system, he translates old-school direct mail tactics into modern strategies for consultants, agencies, and other experts. Here are key direct-response principles you can apply to consistently attract and convert B2B clients:

Always Make an Offer (CTA in Everything): “There should always be an offer or offers,” Kennedy preaches

ytscribe.com
. Every piece of marketing – whether it’s a Facebook post, a webinar, or a conference speech – should invite the prospect to take a specific next step. This could be downloading a guide, booking a call, or even a limited-time promotional bundle. Too many businesses put out bland content that educates or entertains but doesn’t ask for the sale. Kennedy’s rule: never let a reader or listener walk away without a clear call-to-action. For example, if you publish a blog on “5 Mistakes in SEO,” end it with a compelling offer: “Get a FREE 30-minute SEO Audit (usually $500) if you contact us by this Friday.” The offer makes your content work as lead generation, not just “branding.” As Russell Brunson (one of Dan’s successful students) summarized: when you add extra components to your service and wrap it as a unique offer, you cease being a commodity and pricing power shifts to you
ytscribe.com
. Tip: Review your agency’s website and sales materials – do they explicitly tell the prospect what to do next and why they should do it now? If not, add an irresistible offer with a deadline.

Use Urgency and Scarcity: A foundational direct response tactic (that Kennedy loves) is giving people a reason to act now

. This can be a time deadline (“Offer expires December 15th”) or limited availability (“Only 3 spots left for our January cohort”). Without urgency, even interested prospects procrastinate – “I’ll think about it” syndrome. Kennedy often integrates scarcity into offers: for instance, a consulting package might only be available to the first 5 who sign up, or a special bonus is only for those who respond by the webinar’s end. Modern twist: Digital marketers can automate urgency with expiring coupon codes or webinar special offers. Just ensure it’s genuine – fake scarcity will damage trust. In line with takeaway selling, if a prospect misses the deadline, Kennedy would actually enforce it (no exceptions), which trains your market that you mean what you say. The result? When you announce an offer next time, they take it seriously.

Leverage “Two-Step” Lead Generation: Kennedy’s Magnetic Marketing emphasizes two-step marketing for service businesses. Step 1 is not selling your service outright, but offering valuable information to attract leads. Step 2 is following up to convert those leads into clients. For example, Step 1: run an ad (or LinkedIn outreach) offering a free report like “The 7 Costly Mistakes CFOs Make When Hiring a Marketing Agency” or host a free webinar on AI automation. Interested prospects raise their hand (by opting in), identifying themselves as qualified leads. Step 2: you then follow up by sending a proposal, sales letter, or scheduling a consultation – the direct sales conversation happens after you’ve provided value and built some trust. This approach is classic Kennedy: lead with information and value (sometimes called education-based marketing), then make your offer to a warmed-up prospect. Action step: Develop a lead magnet relevant to your niche. If you’re a web development agency, it might be a short PDF, “Website ROI Checklist: 5 Ways to Turn Your Site into a Lead Machine.” Promote that in your marketing. Ensure that the follow-up sequence after someone downloads it includes a strong offer or invitation to talk. This systematic funnel ensures you’re always marketing, as Dan commands, rather than passively waiting for referrals

.

Measure Everything (No Blind “Branding”): Kennedy is infamously critical of branding-only advertising (“spray and pray” ads without accountability). He insists on tracking ROI on every campaign. “If I put a dollar in, what happens to that dollar?” is the question he poses

. For an agency, this means use tracking links, unique promo codes, or specific landing pages to know exactly how many leads or sales each marketing effort produces. Modern analytics make this easier than ever. But the principle is timeless: if you can’t measure it, don’t do it
ytscribe.com
. This clashes with the current trend of obsessing over vanity metrics (likes, shares, impressions). Kennedy couldn’t care less about “awareness” unless it leads to dollars in the bank. He’d rather have a plain-looking direct mail piece that brings 50 inquiries, than a clever viral tweet that brings none. Tip: Set up a simple spreadsheet for your marketing initiatives. Track money spent and leads/sales generated. Cut what doesn’t work; scale what works. This data-driven mindset is how direct response folks like Dan win where others are guessing.

For example, perhaps your agency specializes in guaranteed results (“We increase your B2B SaaS sales leads by 30% in 90 days, or you don’t pay.”). That kind of USP, if backed by a strong guarantee, sets you apart instantly. Kennedy himself often uses bold risk-reversal guarantees in his sales letters – because it forces you to deliver and it reassures clients you stand behind your claims. Work on crystallizing what truly makes you different. Is it a niche focus? A proprietary process? A remarkable guarantee? Once you have it, shout it everywhere. This prevents you from ever sounding like just another generic service provider.

Multi-Step Follow-Up (The Fortune’s in the Follow-Up): A single ad or one sales call is rarely enough to close higher-end clients. Kennedy emphasizes persistent, multi-touch follow-up. He often cites that most businesses give up too soon. In Magnetic Marketing, he outlines sequences of letters, emails, even phone calls to nurture prospects. For a modern agency, this could mean: after a webinar, you send a follow-up email series addressing common objections, a retargeting ad with a client testimonial video, and a personal LinkedIn message offering to answer questions. You might also mail a physical “shock and awe” package to really stand out (Dan is known for sending boxes of sample ads, DVDs, a book, etc. to impress big prospects). The key is polite persistence. You’re not annoying people who asked for your info; you’re serving them by helping them make a decision. In Kennedy’s experience, the majority of sales happen after several contacts – so plan for it. Action step: Map out a 5-7 step follow-up for new leads. Vary the media (email, phone, mail) if possible. Continue to provide value in these touches (tips, case studies, etc.) while reiterating your offer. This alone will put you ahead of most competitors who stop at one email.

Kennedy strikes a balance: be direct and proactive in pursuit of sales, but do it via valuable content and systematic follow-up, not one-call closes or brand gibberish. For instance, some agencies today pour effort into social media content hoping people magically inquire; Dan would say that’s wasted effort unless every post or video intentionally leads prospects down a path to purchase

. He also bucks the trend of chasing shiny new platforms. While others might abandon email for TikTok, Kennedy still finds ways to make “old” channels like email and direct mail highly profitable – because he focuses on fundamentals: the right message, to the right person, with a clear call-to-action. The No B.S. approach calls out the fact that most social media “likes” won’t pay your mortgage – but a well-crafted direct-response campaign will. Kennedy’s students often integrate his old-school principles with new-school tech (e.g. a Facebook ad that feels like a classic Dan Kennedy direct mail piece). The medium can change, but human psychology doesn’t. So when the marketing world zigs into the latest fad, consider zagging back to direct-response basics. As Dan might put it: be a marketer, not a social media butterfly. Your agency’s bottom line will thank you.

6. Create “Apples-to-Oranges” Comparisons (Stand Out & Safeguard Price)

One of Kennedy’s most quotable marketing strategies is to avoid apples-to-apples comparisons in your offers. In plain terms: make it impossible for prospects to directly compare your service to a competitor’s on price because what you offer is uniquely different. “Rather than offer an apples-to-apples value proposition, make your product or service unique so that it becomes ‘apples to oranges’ and subsequently your offering can never be compared,” Kennedy advises
karlbryan.com
. This tactic increases perceived value and lets you charge premium prices without being undercut.

Bundle and Differentiate Your Offer: If everyone else sells “Digital Marketing Package – 20 hours of work on XYZ for $3,000,” you’re easy to compare (just hours for price). The Kennedy approach would be to bundle your service into a unique program with proprietary elements. For example, you might offer the “Growth Catalyst System” for businesses: it includes not just marketing execution, but a quarterly strategy workshop, an analytics dashboard, staff training videos, and VIP support. Now, no one else is offering that exact bundle. It’s no longer just marketing services; it’s a comprehensive system. By stacking value and branding it uniquely, you’ve created an apples-to-oranges scenario. Clients can’t say “Agency B will do the same for less,” because no one else has your Growth Catalyst System with those exact components. This is exactly what Kennedy means by crafting a unique offer. As one of his mentees put it, “take a product that’s a commodity… add other things to make it special, where you’re the only person on the planet who can sell that thing”
ytscribe.com
. When your offer is one-of-a-kind, price shopping largely disappears.

Use Comparison to Higher Anchors: Another apples-to-oranges tactic Dan teaches is to frame your price against a much higher-cost alternative (instead of against your direct competitors). For instance, if you sell an information product or consulting package, don’t compare it to a book or hourly fee – compare it to the cost of a full-time hire, or the revenue loss of not solving the problem. A classic example: marketers selling a $300 home-study course would say, “You could attend a seminar for $5,000 to learn these secrets, but this course gives you the same knowledge for a fraction of that”. They’ve made the reference point a $5,000 seminar (much higher), rather than a $20 book or another $300 course. Develop a Signature Process or Brand: Kennedy often encourages entrepreneurs to name and trademark their unique process. If you’re a consultant, create a framework or methodology with a catchy name. For example, instead of saying “I do marketing audits,” you could sell your “Revenue Accelerator Audit™”. It also adds a perception of sophistication – you’re not just winging it, you have a defined method. This can justify higher fees. So, brainstorm how you deliver results and package it into a branded system. Even if the underlying work is similar to others, the presentation matters. It’s the difference between a plain burger and a Big Mac with “special sauce” – the latter supports a premium because it’s a branded experience.

Highlight Intangible Benefits: Another way to create an oranges-to-apples contrast is to emphasize benefits that others aren’t talking about. Kennedy used to give the example of a lawn care service: if you just sell “we cut your grass,” you’re a commodity. But if you sell “a pristine lawn without lifting a finger, giving you back your weekends”, you’re actually selling free time and peace of mind. That’s an intangible benefit that goes beyond what a typical mow-and-go competitor offers. Suddenly, the value of your service isn’t just measured in square feet of grass cut, but in hours of relaxation for the homeowner – which is priceless to some. In a B2B context, think what bigger outcome your service enables. If you build websites, maybe the true benefit you offer a client is confidence and pride in their brand image, or the freedom that comes from a fully automated sales process, etc. Those higher-level benefits are harder to put a sticker price on, which means you can charge more than someone who is only selling the technical task. Make the client feel, “This is not a cost, it’s an investment in [desired outcome].” By changing the conversation, you move away from line-item price comparisons.

Educate Why Your Approach Is Different: It’s not enough to be different; you must convey it compellingly. Kennedy and his students often use educational marketing to explain why their approach or product is unique. This might be a detailed sales letter or video that essentially says, “Here’s why the ordinary approach fails… and here’s how our approach is totally different.” By educating prospects, you shape the criteria they use to judge options. Ideally, you introduce new criteria that only you meet. For instance, an agency could educate the prospect: “Most marketing firms focus on vanity metrics; we focus strictly on revenue-generating activities. That’s why we include a KPI guarantee in every project.” This plants the idea that any firm without a KPI guarantee is a risk. Thus, competitors look inferior without you ever naming them. Your service becomes the standard by which others are measured, rather than vice versa.

✋ Where This Clashes with Trends: Many businesses today fall into the trap of commoditization because they mirror each other – same offerings, same buzzwords. Kennedy’s apples-to-oranges strategy is almost an anti-trend tactic: deliberately zig when the market zags. It requires a bit of creative thinking, which some might find “extra work” compared to just selling like everyone else. But that extra work is exactly what insulates you from price competition. Also, in an era where price comparison websites and transparent pricing are common, being apples-to-oranges is more important than ever. If you sell the exact same thing as others, the internet will out you and the lowest price often wins. Kennedy would argue that much of what’s sold online today has become homogenized. His method challenges you to innovate your offer structure so that it can’t be directly compared. While some modern marketers might focus on incremental improvements or fancy tech, Dan focuses on fundamental offer differentiation. The clash is really between short-term thinking (just lower the price or copy a competitor) and strategic thinking (build an offer so good and unique that competitors become irrelevant). Embracing this Kennedy principle might mean reconfiguring your services, but the payoff is huge: you escape the commodity trap and enjoy pricing power. As Kennedy taught, “One way to build perceived value is through an apples-to-oranges comparison… bundling to defy comparison.”

Conclusion: Implementing the No B.S. Playbook

Dan Kennedy’s marketing and pricing philosophies might sound boldly contrarian, but they are rooted in deep understanding of human psychology and decades of real-world results. As an agency or consultant, you operate in a noisy marketplace where many follow the latest fads and underprice themselves. By applying this No B.S. playbook, you deliberately set yourself apart.

To recap, charge what you’re worth and then some – and deliver an experience to match. Never let your value be reduced to a line-item price; build unique, comparison-proof offers. Hold the line on discounts (your wallet and reputation will thank you), and use smart tactics like bonuses or payment plans instead of cutting price. Position yourself as the sought-after expert, with clients chasing you and not vice versa – which means having the courage to walk away and the discipline to qualify prospects. Make direct-response marketing your best friend: give every campaign a compelling offer, a reason to act now, and track the results like a hawk. And underpinning it all, cultivate that renegade mindset: question convention, seek the gap in the marketplace, and don’t be afraid to do the opposite of what “everyone else” is doing.

Kennedy’s core belief is that these principles are timeless. Technology changes (from fax machines to AI chatbots), but people’s desires, fears, and motivations remain. Clients still want to feel they’re getting the best, still fear making a wrong choice, and still gravitate to those who exude confidence and authority. The No B.S. approach taps into these enduring truths.

Now it’s your turn. Take this playbook and implement it step by step: maybe start by rethinking your pricing strategy this quarter (raise those rates and craft a juicy high-end package), then revamp one of your marketing funnels to add a strong offer and follow-up sequence. Introduce a little scarcity in your next sales conversation. Draw some firmer boundaries to command more respect. Each change may feel a bit uncomfortable – as Kennedy jokes, it’s about having the “courage to not conform” – but that’s exactly where the growth (and profit) lies.
