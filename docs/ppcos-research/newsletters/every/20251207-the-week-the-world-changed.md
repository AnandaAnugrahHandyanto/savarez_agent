Alignment
Sitting with ambiguity. Last week I bought Dwarkesh Patel’s book The Scaling Era: An Oral History. When it arrived, it felt like I was holding something of reverence, like a passage from the Secret History of the Mongols or a scroll from an ancient Greek philosopher. I couldn’t figure out why at first, but after reading a couple of chapters and letting it marinate, I think I know.
The feeling comes from reading history before it becomes history. The book captures the architects of artificial intelligence—Dario Amodei, Ilya Sutskever, and others—in their own words, from Patel’s hundreds of podcast interviews.
That’s not how history usually works. When you learn about it later, it always feels inevitable—of course the Allies were going to win World War II and Rome was going to fall. But when you live through a defining moment, nothing feels certain, and everything’s still up for grabs. That’s what makes this book strange to hold—because we’re still in the process of shaping what it documents.—Ashwin Sharma
