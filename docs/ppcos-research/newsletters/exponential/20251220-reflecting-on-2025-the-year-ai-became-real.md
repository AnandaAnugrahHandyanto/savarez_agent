As we approach the end of the year, I want to reflect on what made 2025 special – what we learned about AI, what surprised me, and what it all means for the road ahead. This was the year artificial intelligence stopped being a curiosity and became infrastructure. The year models got good enough to do real work. And the year we began to understand just how profoundly this technology will reshape everything.

1. The models matured
My favourite development of 2025 has to be Nano Banana, Google’s image generation service. Beyond its fantastic name (which, as a Brit, I’ll admit Americans say much better than I do 🤭), it represents something remarkable. Pair it with Gemini 3 Pro, present a complex idea, and ask it to turn that into an explanatory diagram. The visual results can be phenomenal with some clever prompting. It’s also really fun for video generation:
But the deeper story is that in 2025, many models got good enough to do long stretches of work. Claude 4.5 from Anthropic is fantastic for working with long documents and for coding. GPT-5 excels at deep research and certain classes of problem-solving  – I’ve been using version 5.2 for financial modeling. Google’s Gemini 3 Pro is markedly better than previous generations. And I’ve been turning to Manus more often.
All of these models are now capable of doing what I would describe as a few hours’ worth of high-quality work. This has meant that I, as someone who hasn’t been allowed near software code for more than a decade, have been able to build my own useful applications. What was derisively called “vibe coding” [ https://substack.com/redirect/f0fb8ed7-9a5d-45ce-8208-d61b00203e77?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] a year ago has transformed into something genuinely productive for me. I will write more on this in my EOY lessons for genAI use in the next few days.
2. The work shifted
One of the biggest changes I’ve noticed is cognitive. There’s been a shift from the effort of actually doing the work to the effort of judging the work, specifying problems well, turning those problems over to a model, examining the output, and deciding what and how to move forward. You need to maintain a high degree of mental acuity as you evaluate: are the assumptions reasonable? Are there obvious errors?
Those of us who have managed people and led teams will recognise that it’s a bit like managing and leading, except your team member is a machine that can work in parallel across many domains simultaneously. And therein lies an additional challenge: the cognitive load of selecting which model to use for which task has now landed squarely on me, the user. It’s clear for coding – that’s Claude. But for the nebulous set of other problems, the choice between GPT 5.2 and Gemini 3 is never obvious beforehand, though it usually becomes clear in retrospect.
In a way, we all start to behave like developers. Developers think constantly about their tooling and workflows. They consider what in their weekly cadence can be automated. If you’re using AI effectively, you’ll spend less time putting bricks in the wall, more time figuring out how best to organise the bricks and specify what you need. If you have a static way of working with your AI system, you’re missing out. The models are becoming more capable. More importantly, the application layer around the models – the tools they can access, the use of memory, the use of projects – is making them dramatically more useful. So you simply have to keep experimenting and trying out new things.
My experience is that about three-quarters of what I’m doing today, I wasn’t doing three or four months ago. Models have become that much more capable. If you treat AI like a simple operating system upgrade, rather than something whose capacity grows and requires regularly changing how you work, you will miss out on the benefits.
3. Orgs are slow
One of the toughest lessons of 2025 for many will be how slow and painful organizational rewiring is compared to the progress we’re making with the AI systems themselves. Let me share Exponential View’s story.
We’ve been thinking and writing about large language models since the first transformer paper. I’d been experimenting with GPT-3 for years. In the summer of 2022, when image generation models like Midjourney and Stable Diffusion were gaining popularity, I told the team we needed to take generative AI seriously. On November 15th 2022, I doubled down with an email reinforcing how important this would be. Just weeks later, ChatGPT was released.
But it took us until January 2024 before we fundamentally changed how our team of six people actually worked. That’s more than a year of embedding AI into daily practice – running daily stand-ups where people shared their experiences with tools that, frankly, were a bit shonky before GPT-4.
Today, the team at Exponential View has a discretionary budget – typically a few hundred dollars per person per month – to spend on whatever AI tools they need [ https://substack.com/redirect/e26b9ab8-8a9e-45c7-a5bc-1781bed2e7f1?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. Some get ChatGPT Pro, some get Claude Pro. They have room to experiment, to figure out what works best for them, to build agentic workflows with Zapier or Lindy, to write their own code with Cursor or Replit.
Through 2025, we’ve introduced AI build sprints, experimentation sessions, and upskilling moments. In spring 2025, we established “the rule of five”: if you’re doing something five times in a week or month, automate it. We haven’t adhered to it perfectly – not even me – but it’s been helpful. Now we’ve gone further, encouraging everyone to build the tools they need for their specific projects.
This took more than a year for a team of six people. What does that mean for larger, more hidebound organizations?
4. Atoms still matter
One development I anticipated last year was the clash between AI’s requirements and physical infrastructure – specifically, data centres and the electricity to power them. I wrote an op-ed in the  [ https://substack.com/redirect/7db2b57b-949e-48ad-9819-814141a08e29?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]New York Times [ https://substack.com/redirect/7db2b57b-949e-48ad-9819-814141a08e29?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] in December 2024 [ https://substack.com/redirect/7db2b57b-949e-48ad-9819-814141a08e29?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] making exactly this point.
AI is intangible to us, like so much software. But it’s backed by enormous physical infrastructure: substations, gas turbines, solar panels, batteries, transformers, grid connections. Building that out requires a human workforce – lots of electricians, among other skills, to build a Stargate-scale data centre. In the pre-AI world, this was measured in decades, not years, let alone months.
You can build a data centre in 18 to 24 months, but you can’t necessarily connect it to the grid that fast. With demand surging, it’s often taking longer due to backlogs in power components and the staff needed to wire everything up. This tension will continue through at least 2026 as demand outstrips supply.
5. Money’s real
One of the most surprising developments of 2025 is how fast AI revenue has grown. We’re still far from the levels needed to pay for all the investment in data centres. But revenue is growing incredibly quickly.
We take a conservative approach, de-duplicating revenues across the AI stack. Our mid-case estimate is around $60 billion [ https://substack.com/redirect/afac6dce-6a5a-4471-9544-5b58f4aabf07?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] in generative AI revenues for 2025, 230% annualized growth over the year. And we’re not counting integration fees from systems integrators or the advertising revenue uplift that Google has seen. Those would push the number even higher.
That’s remarkably rapid commercialisation: two years to reach $60 billion. The PC took nine years. The internet took thirteen. And the growth rate isn’t slowing significantly. You’d expect deceleration starting from such a high level, but our exit growth rate for the last quarter of 2025 is around 214% annually – close to the overall rate for the year.
6. K is the letter of the year
If I had to assign a letter to 2025, it would be K. The K-shaped economy. Everything is splitting into two tracks: one ascending, one descending.
There’s the world of AI, which increasingly looks like magic turned into engineering. Then there’s the rest of the economy, the stalwarts of the Dow that aren’t quite as exciting.
You see the K-shape everywhere. The US is pegging growth on data centres – they accounted for more than half of US GDP growth in 2025. Yet there’s growing resistance, with politicians from Bernie Sanders on the left to figures on the right celebrating when local action halts data centre buildouts. ChatGPT and its peers are the fastest-growing, most popular products in history. The more people use them, the more they use them – a revealed preference for how much people value these tools. Yet 75-80% of Americans are not optimistic about what AI will mean for their lives, families, jobs, communities, or society at large.
The most common question I’ve been asked in 2025 was are we in an AI bubble? [ https://substack.com/redirect/afac6dce-6a5a-4471-9544-5b58f4aabf07?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] We’ve written extensively about this and track it live at boomorbubble.ai [ https://substack.com/redirect/99efb23f-7bb0-41eb-a8a6-40d9fdfed090?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. But the question that really matters to people is: what’s going to happen to jobs?
If they’re my age, they’re asking about their children. If younger, they’re asking about their own prospects. There’s deep uncertainty in the market and it’s the uncertainty that gives employers another reason to delay hiring, particularly of young people, who tend to be the riskiest investments. I wouldn’t say this is primarily about AI. It’s mainly about an environment of profound uncertainty to which AI is contributing. And the expectations set about what AI can do aren’t helping.
We’ve also learned that AI is an equalising technology only in some cases. In 2024, research showed that AI lifted bottom-quartile performers to second-quartile levels. But it’s becoming clear that AI also supercharges the very best, through returns to expertise.
When the internet arrived, I was young, and it helped me enormously – senior leaders knew they needed to act but didn’t know how, so they turned to people like me. Now the default is that AI enables executive skills that come with experience. This creates a signalling problem for young people: How do you signal value in a labour market that prizes judgment over credentials?
