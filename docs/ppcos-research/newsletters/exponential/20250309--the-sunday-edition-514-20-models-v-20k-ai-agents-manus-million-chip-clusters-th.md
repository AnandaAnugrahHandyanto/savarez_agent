The economics of AI agency
OpenAI plans to charge between $2,000 and $20,000 monthly for AI agents [ https://substack.com/redirect/1c6adaaf-3312-45bf-867a-50ed1270355e?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] capable of high-value tasks, such as advanced coding or PhD-level research. OpenAI’s reasoning is that these agents function as digital employees with direct economic returns – not merely as tools.
This is Level 3 of OpenAI’s Stages of Artificial Intelligence, where agents autonomously handle complex tasks – an approach previewed by projects including Operator [ https://substack.com/redirect/026aceca-daea-443d-adfe-78fc8563e9b6?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] and Deep research [ https://substack.com/redirect/6a048752-028f-4dd7-afb9-9c6f28407e0a?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].
But even if OpenAI’s top-tier agents outperform competitors initially, that advantage may not last – particularly given China’s aggressive investment in AI as a strategic priority. Chinese companies have already demonstrated they can train competitive AI models at a fraction of the costs of Western companies – a trend likely to extend to premium-priced AI agents. DeepSeek-R1 proved [ https://substack.com/redirect/14336ec2-ef41-4f9f-9de1-6c693ed0132f?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] that Chinese firms can match or approach Western capabilities with fewer parameters and less compute. Just this week, Alibaba released QwQ-32B [ https://substack.com/redirect/de1af643-e315-4b3b-93af-85c0936ed9e1?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], an even smaller model delivering comparable performance at a dramatically lower price.
The stakes for OpenAI are high. To sustainably fund frontier development, they must find reliable ways to prevent competitors from undercutting their pricing. Once a new AI capability is proven possible, efficient open-source alternatives inevitably emerge, applying constant pressure on pioneers to maintain lasting competitive advantages. So, how can companies like OpenAI form a durable moat? Three potential avenues include:
Continuously scaling up to larger, more powerful models to stay ahead – similar to how premium smartphones maintain a temporary edge over budget alternatives.
Establishing “walled gardens”, so the most powerful AI capabilities remain exclusive to their own ecosystems. OpenAI currently does this with its agent products which can’t be accessed via the API.
Developing specialized AI solutions that are custom-trained for specific high-value business tasks (such as drafting legal documents or analyzing medical data) . This would be challenging for general-purpose competitors to replicate.

This video is a fair reflection [ https://substack.com/redirect/d52d855b-bb1c-4a22-b690-f11715b74058?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] of what it can do.
AI agents consume hundreds, if not thousands more tokens than chatbots. We’re only just beginning to see the scale of AI demand. Broadcom’s CEO announced that the company’s three largest customers – likely Alphabet, Meta and ByteDance – each plan to build 1-million-chip AI clusters by 2027 [ https://substack.com/redirect/a58f41be-22df-4adc-96f2-2c848f9422de?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. This hardware expansion could trigger hundreds of billions in capital spending across the tech giants. As I highlighted last week [ https://www.exponentialview.co/p/ev-513 ], Nvidia’s Jensen Huang believes reasoning models require 100 times more compute than traditional ones – with future needs potentially millions of times higher. Clearly, the broader market agrees and for good reason.
How big is China’s AI market?
Tokens are the building blocks of AI communication. As such, we can use them as proxies to understand the scale of the generative AI economy and its growth. They also help us to estimate the infrastructure necessary to sustain this growth, particularly in terms of computing resources such as GPUs and other specialized chips. I’ve looked at the data coming out of China, including the DeepSeek numbers we referenced [ https://www.exponentialview.co/p/data-to-start-your-week-14 ] in the Monday email, to estimate how big China’s market may be and which companies have the lead. You can read my full analysis here [ https://www.exponentialview.co/p/just-how-big-is-chinas-ai-market ].
Jordan Schneider  from ChinaTalk makes the case that the shift to reasoning models, which do their thinking during the inference phase, advantages the GPUs and other infrastructure available in China [ https://www.chinatalk.media/p/ban-the-h20-competing-in-the-inference?r=f84r&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web ]: “U.S. export controls aim to restrict China’s ability to train frontier AI models but overlook the growing importance of inference and China’s capacity to scale it.”
AI generating value
We’ve seen new pieces of evidence this week.
Bridgewater Associates launched a $2 billion fund [ https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-03-04/bridgewater-ceo-says-firm-s-ai-fund-comparable-to-human-ones?sref=U0wOqcqE ] last year that uses machine learning and AI to make decisions. Its performance is comparable to the firm’s human-led strategies and has developed, in CEO Nir Bar Dea’s words, a “unique alpha that is uncorrelated to what our humans do”. No details yet on the returns but we’ll keep an eye on it.
A quarter of the latest Y Combinator cohort of startups used genAI to generate 95% of their code [ https://x.com/garrytan/status/1897303270311489931 ]. The “vibe coding” phenomenon is real. In fact, I see it extending into all work – using AI has allowed me to “vibe work [ https://www.exponentialview.co/p/introducing-the-vibe-worker ]”, to capture those fuzzy, unstructured, vague ideas and feelings and turn them into reality. Most of the time they’d be lost, forgotten and procrastinated on.
I came across an interesting paper on legal AI [ https://substack.com/@exponentialview/note/c-98211240?utm_source=notes-share-action&r=f84r ] this week, as well. Law students using OpenAI’s o1-preview produced legal writing that was clearer, more professional and more rigorous, with quality gains of up to 28%. They also worked 12-28% faster.
And, all the magic we’ve seen Anthropic’s Model Context Protocol [ https://www.anthropic.com/news/model-context-protocol ] deliver. MCP acts as an “HTTP for AI”, like a universal standard for connecting AI systems to diverse data sources. Users have documented [ https://x.com/AtomSilverman/status/1897409286466363626 ] MCP upping their coding workflow by ten times and have built an enterprise-grade Slack-bot that can do things such as prepare a shopping list on Amazon from a simple Slack message.
Europe’s Reagan moment
Europe has finally heeded the warning that diplomacy alone won’t deter Russia. In a move that echoes Reagan-era resolve, the EU has pledged a massive €800 billion in defense [ https://www.theguardian.com/world/2025/mar/04/eu-plan-to-bolster-europes-defences-could-raise-800bn-for-ukraine ] – €650 billion from member states plus €150 billion in joint loans – after decades of underinvestment.
As I argued in my recent essay [ https://substack.com/home/post/p-157797402 ], the Kremlin’s war economy is straining under 35% defense spending and runaway inflation sees potato prices soaring 81%. Europe’s GDP is ten times Russia’s, its manufacturing base twelve times larger – the real question has always been will, not capacity. The message to Putin is unambiguous: Europe is done playing small, is paying its share at last and is prepared to stand firm against any aggression.
