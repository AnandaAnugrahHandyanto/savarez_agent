Part 4: The macro view
9. Sovereign AI is splintering the stack
In early 2025, sovereign AI was mostly posture. The AI landscape has become starkly bipolar: the US controls 75% of global AI compute, China 15%, leaving everyone else marginalised. You can adopt US/Chinese AI systems and become vulnerable to data exploitation, service restrictions, embedded values, and unfavourable terms. Or accept weakness, limit adoption and fall behind as frontier states achieve economic, scientific, and military breakthroughs.
You can see the logic hardening. Britain is building national infrastructure with Nvidia; the Gulf is spinning up its own polity via the $100 billion MGX vehicle. OpenAI’s “Stargate” initiative fits this fractured landscape perfectly. Stargate UAE offers a gigawatt-scale compute cluster under US-aligned governance. Meanwhile, South Korea joins the orbit as a chip arsenal, and Saudi Arabia finances “AI factories” for local workloads.
Even Brussels is softening regulations [ https://substack.com/redirect/2952203c-3add-4c58-88d1-db5fff6c3cd2?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] to keep champions like Mistral onshore. Officials now discuss compute and models as strategic assets to be rationed through alliances, not left to the market.
The challenges are most acute for mid-sized and smaller nations, where mid-sized means anything smaller than the US or China. One possible route may be multinational or minilateral approaches that involve pooling capabilities or resources, as argued in this working paper from an Oxford University research group [ https://substack.com/redirect/a04890a8-d862-4b17-8719-477fc8cf1c48?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. This question is also the focus of a working group I co-chair [ https://substack.com/redirect/db447126-218b-4c1e-ba01-faa6bd6a737d?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] on how governments need to approach next-generation computing infrastructure.
This logic virtually guarantees a splintered world. We are moving toward distinct US-aligned, China-aligned, and “non-aligned” stacks. The era of the borderless internet has come to an end; the era of the sovereign stack has begun.
10. A growing utility–trust gap risks a societal schism
We are living through a massive psychological split. On the one hand, utility is winning: 900 million people now use ChatGPT and other LLMs to code, write and think, bringing the “sovereign stack” into their personal lives. The utility is too high to ignore. Yet, familiarity has bred fear, not contempt.
Three-quarters of Americans now believe AI will negatively impact their lives, a figure that has hardened even as adoption skyrockets. We have become pragmatic addicts, dependent on a tool we fundamentally do not trust. This isn’t a “tech backlash” of the previous eras. I see it as a new kind of social bargain – we will use the machine to do our jobs today, even while we suspect it will take our jobs tomorrow.
In the UK, employment in sales and customer service occupations is already down more than 10% since 2021. If current trends continue, between one and three million jobs in “high-risk” low- and mid-skill occupations could disappear by 2035. In the US, college graduates now comprise a record share of the unemployed [ https://substack.com/redirect/d6f816b5-4a43-4705-908f-b4f2b0de70cd?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].
While the macro data isn’t yet showing AI [ https://substack.com/redirect/47a5c4b9-f839-4bbe-9689-483bf6151142?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] as the core proximate cause of graduate hiring slowdowns, some bosses in services firms have told me that they are slowing intake.
The political economy of this schism deserves our attention. Previous technological disruptions – the loom, the assembly line, the spreadsheet – displaced workers who largely lacked political voice. This time is different. The knowledge workers in AI’s crosshairs are precisely those who shape public discourse: journalists, analysts, lawyers, and the professional-managerial class that staffs campaigns, writes op-eds, and dominates social media. When the articulate classes feel threatened, narratives shift fast.
We may be approaching what I’d call a “legitimacy crunch.” The companies building these systems derive their social licence from an implicit promise: we are making tools that augment human capability. But if users experience AI primarily as a survival necessity rather than a genuine enhancement – something they must use to remain competitive, even as it hollows out their sense of professional identity – that licence frays. Trust, once lost at scale, could be extraordinarily difficult to rebuild.
The contours of the future
Reflecting on the year behind us, I can’t help but notice that key bottlenecks have moved. It is no longer about what the models can do. It is about how quickly our grids and institutions can absorb them. Revenue is racing ahead of reality; energy competes with inference; and markets must fund a system-level shock using tools built for single-firm cash flows. The technology is exponential. The organizations adopting it are linear.
The next 18 months are the crucible. If the productivity figures stay muted, today’s capital expenditure boom will look like a historic misallocation, and the taxpayer will pay the price. But if productivity moves, we will have bolted a Ferrari engine onto a chassis of fractured social contracts.
We cannot yet label this cycle a revolution or a bubble. But we can debate the contours. These are not technical problems; they are design choices for firms, regulators, and voters. That is the map as I see it. Now, tell me: where am I wrong?
