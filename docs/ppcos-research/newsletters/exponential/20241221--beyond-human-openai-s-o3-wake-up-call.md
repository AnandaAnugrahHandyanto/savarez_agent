Last night, OpenAI previewed a new reasoning model, o3. I feel it’s important enough to share some quick thoughts about it.
Basic facts: The new model is good at reasoning in mathematical and programming domains. It scores really well on a particular benchmark called ARC-AGI, which is above the average human. Spend more money on it, up to $3,500 per task, and it approaches the performance of a STEM graduate.
The ARC-AGI benchmark has been very tough for machines [ https://substack.com/redirect/7945eba6-b808-4f59-a75b-0651cd192031?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. At the start of the year, the best they could do was 5%, but in September, they got to 32% and now, just three months later, to this 88% score. (The crazier version of this chart is at the foot of this letter.)
On maths, o3’s score is “incredibly impressive” according to one researcher [ https://substack.com/redirect/8655a2cc-a0e9-4266-87da-d31b52205078?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. It scores 25% on a “brutally difficult benchmark with problems that would stump many mathematicians.” Early this year, maths guru Terence Tao  [ https://substack.com/redirect/b0c7414d-8806-4045-85c3-4bac29bc93a9?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]said that benchmark was “extremely challenging [and] resist AIs for several years at least.”
In coding, O3’s benchmark score would place it 175th on the leaderboard of Codeforces [ https://substack.com/redirect/92f551da-b568-4927-9d07-549f71a84482?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], one of the toughest competitive coding tournaments in the world. In other words, it is better than virtually every human coder.
#1 Things went fast
o3 has arrived just a couple of months after o1. This marks a dramatic shift from large language models' 18-24-month development cycles. Unlike large language models, which need the huge months-long industrial process of training bigger and bigger systems on more and more data, systems like o3 improve their performance at the point where the query is asked (aka inference time).
My conclusion is that we could continue to see performance improvements quite quickly.
#2 The economics will improve
The best results came from spending $3,500 for each query, which is the cost of computing, searching, and evaluating possible answers at inference time. Early versions are often expensive, but we can assume that the performance we get at $3,500 will cost us substantially less, perhaps a dollar or two, within no more than a couple of years.
The cost of GPT4 quality results has declined by more than 99% in the last two years. GPT-4 launched in March 2023 at $36 per million tokens. Today, China’s DeepSeek offers similar performance for $0.14, or 250 times cheaper.
Lower cost means more use.
#3 Push the frontiers of science and engineering
What constraint does expert-level human maths or coding alleviate? The most obvious place would be in science and engineering. These tools would allow experts to expand their capabilities, pursue more avenues more rapidly, and test and iterate faster. After all, we don’t have an unending surplus of top-class coders and mathematicians.
At some point, the ongoing improvement of these tools could give us access to conceptual problem spaces we can’t access without them. This is the case with many complex fields, especially mathematics, which are far removed from everyday experience and direct observation. Consider, for example, category theory, homology, and higher-dimensional algebra.
Our basic sciences, like studying proteins, subatomic particles, and large-scale cosmology, are outside direct observation. We rely on tools to make observations and then tools and structures to interpret those observations.
Just as x-ray crystallography, the telescope and electron microscopy, allowed us to understand nature—and harness it—with greater acuity, so too will AI tools.
It might be the case that the next major breakthroughs in physics, biology, or materials science will come not just from better observations but from AI systems helping us recognize patterns and relationships that lie beyond human intuitive grasp. Just as Einstein's insights about relativity required mathematical frameworks that challenged everyday perception, future discoveries may require AI-assisted reasoning to bridge the gap between what we can measure and comprehend.
#4 It’s still humans and machines
Noam Brown, the researcher who leads reasoning efforts at OpenAI, argues that humans still have a role [ https://substack.com/redirect/98c3a06b-775a-4822-984f-39eee4841522?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] in this.
Practice working with AI. Human+AI will be superior to human or AI alone for the foreseeable future. Those who can work most effectively with AI will be the most highly valued.
Although the word “foreseeable” is doing a lot of work there. What does foreseeable mean today? A month? A year? A decade?
Humans will increasingly be valuable in judgement, governance, and direction. However, machines will do the work better, faster, and cheaper than we do, so we’ll want to push that work toward them. That’s why we use dishwashers and calculators where they are available.
We’ll want machines to run the automated infrastructures of our lives. And that is an extension of processes we already live with. (If you live in a city or a richer country, you probably don’t give much thought to the layers of complex processes integrating engineering, fluid dynamics, chemistry, electricity and finance that deliver potable water from every tap in your home.)
#5 We should plan on what happens if this continues
Former OpenAI research Miles Brundage  argues that “AI that exceeds human performance in nearly every cognitive domain is almost certain to be built and deployed in the next few years.” And that given that,
AI policy decisions made in the next few months will shape how that AI is governed. The security and safety measures in place for governing that AI will be among the most important in history.
I don’t have the same certainty that Miles has. But we should expect continued progress, catalysed by innovation and competition, over the coming years and start to make more robust plans.
This includes working out what an economic transition might look like. I believe that vast numbers of new classes of jobs will emerge, that a majority of white-collar jobs will change significantly and that a minority of them will disappear. Policies need to match, and social expectations need to be managed. Quickly!

When I opened this letter noting o3’s arrival, I highlighted its impressive performance on mathematical and programming tasks. But what we’re witnessing isn’t just about benchmark scores or cost curves - it's about a fundamental shift in how quickly AI capabilities advance and how they may reshape our understanding of complex domains. Just as the jump from o1 to o3 compressed traditional development cycles, we may find ourselves compressing our assumptions about the timeline of AI progress.
The most profound implication isn’t that machines can now score well on tests - they're beginning to operate in realms of reasoning previously exclusive to human experts. As these tools become more accessible and affordable, they won’t just augment human capabilities - they may fundamentally expand the boundaries of what we can discover and understand.
This will mark a substantial shift, disrupting established patterns and behaviours. And that calls for much broader engagement by you, the public, and policymakers with the firms on this trajectory. Make it a priority for 2025.
