This week, I’m thinking about structural advantages, the hidden forces that determine who wins when technology shifts.
In climate, a paradox: could the anti-green agenda actually accelerate innovation? In AI, we explore why speed, not just smarts, could shape the next phase and how xAI may be quietly building an unmatchable platform advantage. Plus, a parable from the age of horses that every CEO should revisit.
When going backward drives progress
Over the past decade, the big lie of the energy transition was that environmental virtue would drive change. In reality, power – and thus the ability to create change – flows to whoever can produce energy and critical materials at the lowest global price. The Trump administration may have sidelined environmental ideals, but it can’t undo that underlying economic fact.
VC investor and EV member Vinod Khosla suggests something counterintuitive [ https://substack.com/redirect/e11e197b-118b-4cfa-bee3-8247dd03df5f?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], that America’s rollback of green subsidies might unlock the next climate breakthrough. If mature sectors like wind and solar no longer need state support, that money could be freed up for riskier bets: fusion, super-hot geothermal, or low-carbon steel. Once a technology becomes economically viable, continued subsidies risk distorting the market and propping up winners that no longer need support.
The bet only works, of course, if funds don’t end up propping up coal. But the fundamentals have shifted. In most places, clean energy is already cheaper than fossil fuels. If the US wants to outcompete China, it has no choice but to lean in.
Does AI actually slow you down?
AI was supposed to save time. In software development, it might be doing the opposite.
A new METR study found that experienced open-source developers using early-2025 AI tools (Cursor Pro with Claude 3.5 and 3.7) actually took 19% longer to complete tasks [ https://substack.com/redirect/4f92171e-cf4a-4b9d-ab7e-7fbbd6aa600e?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. Developers had expected a 24% speedup. Experts had predicted even more. In reality, AI added overhead when used in complex projects.
The main drag on performance came from the time spent prompting, waiting, and verifying outputs, often compounded by over-reliance on flawed suggestions.
Anecdotally, I’ve seen the same issue elsewhere.
Today’s top models like o3 can take minutes to respond, and if the output is off, you’re stuck waiting again. My colleague Nathan Warren  switched to o4-mini for most tasks because of this. It makes the prompt-verify loop 100x faster, though at the cost of using a less capable model. We often underrate speed when assessing AI. It would matter less if models were more reliable. But for most tasks, a fast 70% answer beats a slow 80% one. You can fix fast, but you can’t wait fast.
xAI’s ecosystem advantage
Every AI company has an edge. For OpenAI, it’s a massive consumer base. For Google, the search engine. For Anthropic, a strong developer focus. But what about xAI?
This week, xAI released Grok 4 [ https://substack.com/redirect/c128df00-651f-43ca-ab4b-af01e9ee9904?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], the best model we’ve seen from anyone so far. But for all its impressiveness, Grok 4 doesn’t yet reflect a unique structural advantage. Most of its improvement appears to come from scaling the training run 100-fold, powered by 200,000 Grace Hopper GPUs. Impressive, expensive – and likely replicable.
As AI analyst Nathan Labenz  notes, Elon Musk’s companies – Tesla, SpaceX, Neuralink – sit on a goldmine of hard, structured problems. If future Grok models are trained inside real R&D environments, with engineering-grade tools and robotic feedback loops, xAI could close the loop between software and the physical world.
No other lab has this kind of vertically integrated platform.

Missing the S-curve
Stanford University professor Steve Blank recently shared one of my favorite kinds of parables:
What can the carriage and wagon industry teach us about navigating a technology transition? [ https://substack.com/redirect/4b0bf062-d4bb-47ba-845d-e08cbfdec4ad?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]
At the turn of the 20th century, 4,000 companies built wagons and carriages in the US. Then came the automobile. Early cars were clunky, expensive, and easy to dismiss. Most firms doubled down on horse-drawn vehicles. Then Ford introduced the Model T, slashing prices via assembly lines from $825 to $260. Cars became mainstream and the carriage industry collapsed. Less than 1% of firms survived. Those that did had pivoted early: into parts, distribution, coachbuilding.
Carriage makers missed the S-curve. Many CEOs today are making the same mistake with AI. Short-term earnings pressure and risk-averse boards create structural incentives to avoid bold reinvention.
