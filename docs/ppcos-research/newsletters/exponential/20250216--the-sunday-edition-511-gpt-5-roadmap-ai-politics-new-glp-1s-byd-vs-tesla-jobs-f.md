GPT-5 is coming
Sam Altman confirmed OpenAI’s near-term release of GPT-4.5 [ https://substack.com/redirect/8e1947f9-dc04-4e8c-830c-ccd0c589c123?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] (codenamed “Orion”), calling it the “last non-reasoning model”. GPT-5 will arrive as a “meta” model within months – dynamically deciding how much reasoning power or specialisation to use on the fly. Pricing will tier by “intelligence level”.
Rather than just scaling capabilities, OpenAI seems focused on integrating all its different models and features together. Some think this is proof that the company has run out of ways to boost sheer model performance, but personally, I disagree; there’s still room to scale more sophisticated reasoning.
OpenAI’s latest reasoning model – o3 – has shown impressive results ranking among the top 200 competitors in CodeForces [ https://substack.com/redirect/d43a05c4-da41-4c9a-9409-575a924059dc?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], a popular programming contest. The catch is that you don’t always need that level of heavyweight computation for every problem. This is where OpenAI’s new focus comes in: figuring out how to let each model dynamically assign just the right amount of reasoning for a given task, rather than running at “top 200 in the world” intensity all the time.
This adjustable intelligence level could become a design pattern. Anthropic’s forthcoming AI model [ https://substack.com/redirect/8c7d9bf7-aac8-44b3-b61b-0060536e0320?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] will similarly be a hybrid model that can switch between deeper reasoning and instantaneous responses. Developers will be able to use a slider to select the level of intelligence they want – indicated by the number of tokens that the model will be able to reason with. It is expected to outperform OpenAI’s models at practical coding tasks, which has been a consistent strength for Anthropic.

Political compass for AI
Large language models may be developing coherent “value systems [ https://substack.com/redirect/500f7e92-ddc4-4067-8cf8-04f8d63c1ffb?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]” as they scale, rather than merely reflecting human biases – and they seem to lean towards what we used to consider the American left. A couple of observations here: I think there isn’t a robust political theoretic basis for this analysis, any more than “The Political Compass [ https://substack.com/redirect/23cceed4-4ef8-47a9-8b1e-7ffe49276ef3?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]” is anything more than a parlour game.
It’s weirdly parochial with a tip of the hat to the culture wars, to map AI systems to today’s political positions, rather than a more robust persistent framing of values (perhaps from the World Values Survey [ https://substack.com/redirect/e5c3bf53-bec4-4dfa-a6bb-cb112dadefe6?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] or something similar). What is interesting, is the notion that AI systems may converge on a similar set of values.
Some argue that discursive practices promote egalitarian norms [ https://substack.com/redirect/94bac3fc-a7b7-4c5d-9f3a-644746e2b4b3?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], which could explain why AI models converge where they do. At the same time, researchers have uncovered strange inconsistencies with egalitarianism – for example, GPT-4o appeared to favour specific nationalities. When researchers analysed how GPT-4o valued different outcomes, they found it placed higher value on its own wellbeing than that of an average American. On the bright side, the model showed little inclination towards classic sci-fi-style “power-seeking” – so, at least for now, no signs of Skynet in the making.
The authors behind the paper propose a “citizen assembly” alignment process, and I agree with the general concept of using democratic processes to help align AI systems – yet in this case, it's democracy for me, not for thee. For any other country outside of America, they do not have a say in that process. We should put this alignment process in place, but it needs to be more representative, or at least encompass different versions adapted for different local values.
A fascinating real-world test of this tension is playing out right now. Apple plans to launch AI features in China by May 2025, but with a unique twist. Instead of running their AI system directly, Apple is letting Alibaba create a layer that can modify Apple's AI models for Chinese users [ https://substack.com/redirect/b84214e1-9eea-475a-874f-7ba84b8bebb9?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. The system includes mechanisms for content control – if the Chinese government objects to certain AI outputs, they can direct Alibaba to request changes from Apple, which then updates or temporarily disables features until prohibited content is removed.
While American tech debates AI alignment and democratic input, China is creating a more direct solution: letting Alibaba modify Apple’s AI models to fit local requirements. Although this enables censorship, it also shows how AI might actually work in a multipolar world. Rather than one-size-fits-all American values baked into every model, we’re seeing the rise of local “governance layers” that can reshape global AI systems.
AI’s real footprint
Anthropic’s Economic Index [ https://substack.com/redirect/8a0d2581-a150-4bd0-bc4d-437cb6bdf887?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] introduces a powerful framework for systematically tracking how AI is integrated into day-to-day work across different occupations. By analysing over 4 million anonymised Claude chats and mapping each to tasks in the ONET database, the authors offer a scalable, privacy-preserving way to gauge which parts of the economy are using AI and how. They found that 36% of occupations rely on AI for at least a quarter of their tasks, although only 4% do so for three-quarters or more. Of that usage, 57% augments human capabilities, while 43% focuses on automation. It shows that at the very least, chatbot applications can cover a significant amount of tasks, but perhaps not all.
Compared to another recent study [ https://substack.com/redirect/9328b0ef-785c-4a47-9770-fce2e14b7432?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], co-authored by OpenAI researchers in 2023 (which we covered in COTW #114 [ https://substack.com/redirect/a3b05d42-9543-433c-b20c-0b9cc474ae3b?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]) which predicted that 80% of workers could have at least 10% of their tasks affected by language models, Anthropic’s current empirical adoption rate of 57% is lower than predicted. The study also found unexpected patterns – lower healthcare usage and higher scientific application usage than OpenAI’s researchers anticipated – likely a result of regulation and workplace bans. Industry-specific rules, privacy concerns and workplace policies remain huge hurdles that can lead to uneven AI diffusion.
If broader adoption happens, it will likely provide the most accurate way for stakeholders to track emerging AI-driven shifts in labour and the nature of work. Without a cross-provider index that captures hidden enterprise usage, we risk discovering too late that entire roles have been quietly automated away.
Beyond Ozempic
I think of GLP-1s, as long-time readers of Exponential View will know, as one of the most important technologies [ https://substack.com/redirect/0c179a4e-d494-4af9-83e9-32f01484d929?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] being implemented right now. GLP-1 drugs are reshaping human metabolic control. Over 100 new weight loss drugs [ https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-02 ] are in the pipeline on the back of the success of Ozempic, Wegovy and Mounjaro.
The new drugs use diverse biological mechanisms to achieve the same goals – some combine GLP-1 with other hormones like GIP, glucagon and amylin to enhance weight loss; others actively preserve muscle while enabling weight loss. Interestingly, some cultivated meat startups seem to be pivoting [ https://agfundernews.com/profuse-technology-shifts-focus-from-cultivated-meat-to-drug-discovery-amid-glp-1-drug-boom ] to provide their platform capabilities to in-vitro testing of the drugs' effect on lean muscle.
The societal impact of these drugs will be enormous. We previously estimated [ https://www.exponentialview.co/p/glp-1-scenario-analysis?utm_source=publication-search ] that as many as 60-70% of the adult US population could be affected by them by 2030. Even if their impact was to remain conservative at 20% of the adult population, the economy would feel it. Unsurprisingly, the fast food industry is trying to fight back [ https://www.nytimes.com/2024/11/19/magazine/ozempic-junk-food.html ].
GLP-1s are being played on the geopolitical chessboard, too. Major pharma companies are looking to catch up and do it for a “bargain” – Merck and AstraZeneca are expanding their obesity drug portfolios by licensing oral GLP-1 treatments from Chinese pharmaceutical companies [ https://www.wsj.com/health/pharma/the-drug-industry-is-having-its-own-deepseek-moment-68589d70?st=aFQbaC ] instead of the US. A GLP-1 DeepSeek moment in the making?
