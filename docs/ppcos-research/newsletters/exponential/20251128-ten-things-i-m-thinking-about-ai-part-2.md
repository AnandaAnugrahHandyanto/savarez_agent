Part 2: Physical limitations
That simple “exchange” with Gemini, if done by all 4.5 billion people who use Google, would use 1-1.5 gigawatt‑hours of electricity, roughly 20 minutes of London’s power demand.
And that is just the baseline. Some in our team occasionally consume 50 million tokens a day. If 4.5 billion people reached that level of use, it would exceed global annual electricity consumption.
This highlights the massive compute and energy load we are potentially dealing with in AI. Is there enough raw capacity to meet that demand?
Microsoft [ https://substack.com/redirect/a1c61206-8399-4867-8b5c-1ae009e4bb8d?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], Amazon [ https://substack.com/redirect/e5669201-6026-4c6e-a6f0-2fa6f93ae092?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] and Google [ https://substack.com/redirect/0a5a6fb7-0e72-45ec-86d2-32b8ac21f4ad?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] have all highlighted that extreme compute demand is causing bottlenecks. But the compute is physical: it needs to be housed in datacenters, filled with racks, cooled and powered by electricity.
At some point, you run up against physical bottlenecks. Some of this is the demand for the chips, processing and memory, evidenced by Nvidia’s reported $500 billion order backlog [ https://substack.com/redirect/de838bb0-ba38-47f3-a651-debf28075419?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] and the fact that SK Hynix has officially booked out its entire high-bandwidth memory (HBM) capacity through 2026 [ https://substack.com/redirect/e4f9a4b1-2626-4233-a8ec-afd70939ccb3?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. While silicon availability is one bottleneck, it is not the ultimate one.
The real scaling wall is energy
Energy is the most significant physical constraint on the AI build-out in the US, as I argued in the  [ https://substack.com/redirect/19d28070-9cf1-483c-90ec-9d2f4818c77c?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]New York Times [ https://substack.com/redirect/19d28070-9cf1-483c-90ec-9d2f4818c77c?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] back in December [ https://substack.com/redirect/19d28070-9cf1-483c-90ec-9d2f4818c77c?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. The lead time for new power generation and grid upgrades, often measured in decades, far exceeds the 18-24 months needed to build a data center. The US interconnection queue has a median wait of four to five years [ https://substack.com/redirect/6815d10b-d817-4b5f-846e-df2f4f08e50a?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] for renewable and storage projects to connect to the grid. Some markets report average waiting times as long as 9.2 years [ https://substack.com/redirect/bda532ac-b917-47bf-a2fb-e58390aa1faf?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].
This is also a problem in Europe. Grid connections face a backlog of seven to ten years  [ https://substack.com/redirect/4cb71485-df84-429a-983d-4080d5c20cac?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]in data center hotspots.
For the Chinese, the calculus is different. Rui Ma  points out that “current forecasts through 2030 suggest that China will only need AI-related power equal to 1-5% of the power it added over the past five years, while for the US that figure is 50-70%.”
Because the American grid can’t keep up, data center builders are increasingly opting out and looking for behind-the-meter solutions, such as gas turbines or off-grid solar. Solar is particularly attractive – some Virginia projects can move from land-use approval to commercial operation in only 18 to 24 months [ https://substack.com/redirect/b3c45dfd-8133-4a31-9eef-fb5dc9b26f3b?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. Compute will increasingly be dictated by the availability of stranded energy and the resilience of local grids [ https://substack.com/redirect/08ff49ae-d042-43ac-bba4-d6c6331d192d?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] rather than by proximity to the end user.
These grid limitations cast doubt on the industry’s most ambitious timelines. Last year, some forecasts anticipated 10 GW clusters by 2027 [ https://substack.com/redirect/ba9d9d37-adb8-4285-99d6-efb17bdfb47d?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. This now appears improbable.
In fact, Epoch AI  suggests that we would be on track for a one-trillion-dollar cluster [ https://substack.com/redirect/8b45cce5-ea70-4d3b-9853-5df16c75fbe3?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] based on historic trends by 2030. Assuming such a cluster had a decent business case and there was cash available to fund it, energy and other physical constraints would mean 2035 is a more realistic timeline. As long as it remains dependent exclusively on the scaling paradigm and this timeline slips from 2030 to 2035, capability progress will slow. It’s worth noting, the “slow” is relative here. Even if such a delay materialized, progress might feel fast.
And we shouldn’t assume the researchers lack agency in all this. If a strategy like scaling isn’t possible due to physical constraints, labs may switch tacks and find ways to improve performance that are less reliant on ever-larger models.
The inference-training squeeze
Even if firms secure power, they face a brutal explore-vs-exploit trade-off. Projections of 10 GW training clusters [ https://substack.com/redirect/ee394ca5-dc7f-43a2-a950-19f50c602082?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] assume companies can dedicate resources solely to building the next frontier model. But inference and training compete for the same scarce inputs: power, GPUs and interconnects. Inference is the only one generating revenue.
In 2024, OpenAI spent roughly $2 billion on inference [ https://substack.com/redirect/cffbbbf4-dfd1-4e03-a0f0-709c63966be1?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] vs. $5 billion on R&D and training, with about a quarter of compute going to serving customers rather than advancing capabilities. Since then, ChatGPT prompt volume has grown from 1 billion [ https://substack.com/redirect/6f57611e-ec7c-4e9e-ae6a-6781b977d46e?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] to 2.5 billion queries daily [ https://substack.com/redirect/b22a3a9f-d017-4e61-acd1-df6a765d427e?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], a 150% increase in eight months. This eats into training headroom.
If token demand continues compounding faster than hardware capacity, with a gap of perhaps two to five percentage points a month, inference’s share of compute will keep rising. On those numbers, a lab with ~30% of its compute on inference today would see that share rise toward ~50% within about two years and ~70% within four, squeezing the budget for new frontier training runs. At some point, there simply isn’t enough left over for frontier training runs.
Indeed, Lukasz Kaiser, leading research scientist at OpenAI, admits that around the time of GPT-4 [ https://substack.com/redirect/4a0eb807-09d3-4cd5-8ed8-67d07383600c?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] all of OpenAI’s compute was dedicated to training. But when ChatGPT really took off, the GPUs switched to supporting inference – that is, serving consumers.
Even with Google’s enormous resources, and even on the Gemini Ultra plan, we regularly run into limits.
Three factors could accelerate or delay this crunch:
Efficiency gains have historically delivered 3-4% monthly improvements, potentially closing the gap. This is one reason OpenAI introduced model routing in GPT-5: to assign an appropriate level of compute to a given query [ https://substack.com/redirect/bc4a13c9-b03d-488b-90f4-357f2dcfb401?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], rather than having a model spend 30 minutes figuring out how to respond to “Hi”. According to some testing, Gemini 3 consumes 35% fewer token [ https://substack.com/redirect/8d8a2d29-739b-4970-8a5e-050d000e57fc?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]s [ https://substack.com/redirect/8d8a2d29-739b-4970-8a5e-050d000e57fc?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] than the existing stack while delivering better quality.
Reasoning models break the arithmetic entirely. o1-class systems use 10-100 times more inference compute per query. If these account for even 20% of traffic, efficiency gains get swamped and the crunch accelerates dramatically.
Price remains a lever. Companies can throttle demand by raising prices, but that undermines the growth story that justifies their capital expenditure. OpenAI needs revenue growing toward $100 billion to justify its valuation; you do not get there by rationing access.
The implications are uneven. Tech giants like Google and Amazon can afford to burn cash on training, subsidized by their cloud and retail empires. Competitors without those safety nets face a harder road. If you see labs aggressively gating access to reasoning models next year, take it as a signal: demand is scaling, but energy and compute are hitting a ceiling.
