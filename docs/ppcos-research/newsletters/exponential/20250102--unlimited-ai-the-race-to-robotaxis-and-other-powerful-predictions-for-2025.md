# 🔮 Unlimited AI, the race to robotaxis and other powerful predictions for 2025

**From:** "Azeem Azhar, Exponential View" <exponentialview@substack.com>
**Date:** 2025-01-02T19:42:01.000Z
**Folder:** exponential

---

View this post on the web at https://www.exponentialview.co/p/unlimited-ai-the-race-to-robotaxis

A year is a short, foolhardy horizon to capture the force of exponential change. Yet, each January, I distil conversations with technologists, investors, policy wonks, and defence insiders to produce a set of predictions—helpful guideposts, I hope, rather than crystal balls. Many themes repeat (exponential drivers don’t take coffee breaks!), so my book, published three years ago, is still relevant.
This coming year feels like it’ll be a wild one, a crazy one; I feel it deep in my craw. My subconscious tickles me, and I hear the “du-du-du-du-da-du-duuh” of Billy Joel’s “We Didn’t Start the Fire [ https://substack.com/redirect/4c2860b7-3241-4f3f-99df-223635d359c1?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]” in my head. We’re going to need some new verses.
In 2024, I logged hundreds of discussions across Europe, the Middle East, and the US, co-led research on complex risks, and witnessed tech breakthroughs up close. The result: seven bold calls for 2025. Last year, I slightly beat my own expectations, so I’m raising the bar further. Let’s see how we fare a year from now.
1 There will be no AI wall
Contrary to fears of plateauing, AI research is speeding up. DeepSeek's ultra-efficient model shows labs competing to cut costs and boost performance.
Expect new frontiers crossed: 10-million-token context windows will let models digest entire books, medical archives, or enterprise knowledge bases in one go—almost “human-like”, perhaps more so, in continuity.
An AI model will topple challenging benchmarks like FrontierMath and Arc-AGI thanks to leaps in reasoning capabilities. Other models will have massive context windows, effectively being able to hold all the information that matters to us in their “heads”.
Test: A 10-million-token model is released publicly; new record highs on logic-heavy benchmarks.
2 Deployment will move at warp speed
Because the cost of intelligence has collapsed, GPT4-ish quality has come down several hundred times in price for end users, we’ll use more of it. This will mean usage in simple agentic systems. Six months ago I didnt’t use any agentic workflows. Starting in 2025, I am using a few. I expect I’ll be using dozens by this year's end, each consuming oodles of cheaper and cheaper tokens. Our AI systems will more robustly use our computers for us if we want. I use my computer pretty well, and I’m more interested in getting AI to do important things for me that I find hard to do.
Three years into the genAI awakening of ChatGPT, we’ll see a series of startups move into scaling out to customers, going from tens of millions to hundreds of millions of dollars of revenues.
Large companies will be a year into their first pilots and will start to scale out and expand these initiatives.
Test: Three GenAI companies, outside of foundation model makers, surpass the $100m annualised revenue milestone for the first time.
3 Machines will chat more than humans
Corporate security pros already see that over half of web traffic is bot-based. But as agentic AI explodes, bots exchanging data among themselves will dwarf human traffic.
In practice, large swathes of text, computations, negotiations, and even coding will be automated behind the scenes by bots calling each other’s APIs, web APIs and websites directly.
Test: New metrics show Automated Software-to-Software Token (AST) usage eclipsing human-generated tokens.
4 Waymo overtakes Uber in San Francisco
Waymo's tenfold growth last year let it catch up to Lyft's ride volume. If vehicle supply isn't a bottleneck, half of all city rides might become robotaxi trips in 2025.
Expect near-zero wait times and rapid growth in Waymo’s other cities. Apollo will also grow at an incredible clip.
Test: Waymo has a month with more rides in SF than Uber.
5 Extreme climate events will increase
Brace for an uptick in storms, floods, droughts, and wildfires. Insurance markets will wobble as supply chains scramble.
Expect more challenging questions about paying for decarbonization—and more focus on accelerating the transition in a world where global consensus is fraying.
Test: Climate-related insurance costs will be higher in 2025 than in the annual average of the previous twenty years.
6 Renewables surprise again
Solar’s record-breaking run continues. Battery prices keep dropping on better capacity and efficiency.
Home solar plus storage—so-called “prosumer”—will spread. We’re on the cusp of a more decentralized energy model.
Test: Global solar deployments grow at least 30% year-on-year.
7 The EV market will hit a higher gear
Demand for battery-electric and plug-in hybrids is near-boiling. In 2025, EV sales will gain market share faster than the year before, even if Europe wobbled a bit this year.
With new models, faster charging, and robust incentives, EVs are on track to be the default choice for new buyers.
Test: EV global share from 2024 to 2025 outstrips the 2023-to-2024 jump.
Taking a step back
There are several other important themes, but I’ve struggled to find an adequate test for these. They are significant enough that I’ve included them here.
We can’t exclude the volatility of politics: the uncertain change in American domestic politics, the nature of domestic politics in many Western countries, the instability in certain regions, and the competition emerging between the US and China.
Europe as an entity, and individual European nations, have their work cut out to adjust to this new environment—of economies built on radically different technologies; of how to change a culture of superiority into one of enterprise & innovation; and, finally, how to confront the reality of full-scale war on their continent. Two years on from the Russian invasion of Ukraine, I don’t think every European capital has recognised what is happening. Europe has yet to learn that at times like these, with aggressive misbehaving nations at your frontier, high walls make good neighbours.
For the US, a major wildcard is the Trump Administration. On the one hand, I expect deregulation will open up an investment spigot that will push GDP above consensus estimates. On the other, the Administration’s unconventional approach to policy and politics may see it tripping over itself or dealing with chaotic second-order effects.
As for China, has it found itself in a march to war of the likes of Germany and France a hundred and ten years ago? Is there a similar cycle of security dilemmas, military buildups, and nationalist rhetoric that makes conflict increasingly challenging to avoid? Is there a moment where the hand has to be played and must be played? I sincerely hope not. Collaboration, dialogue and compromise are a better path to peace and prosperity. And China has much to offer the world with its dynamic solar, battery and electric vehicle industries.
Across the world, with falling birthrates, our leaders (and us) need to understand whether this matters and, if it does, what the real drivers behind it are. As nation after nation (even Turkey [ https://substack.com/redirect/31ffd53b-7e49-469e-a122-e31323d47a34?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], I learnt recently) drops below replacement rate, it’s telling us something, and we should talk about it.
As for climate change, we’ll see more extreme weather events this year. This year will be another scorcher. As voluntary agreements between countries and capital to tackle climate change fall apart, this picture will look gloomy. The numbers, CO2 levels, and declining climate commitments will make grim readings.
I expect wider stakeholders, from employees to investors and the insurance industry, to apply various types of pressure to move companies back towards robust climate commitments. It's not perfect, but it's better than nothing.
The force of technology will continue to march, making energy cheaper and more widely available and making clean technologies (from desalination to carbon capture to recycling to electrified industrial processes) cheaper and cheaper. And we’ll be able to apply artificial intelligence tools to build new industrial green industrial methods and accelerate a transition to a fossil-free grid. An electrified, low-carbon economy will be better, more productive, wealthier and more sustainable than our 20th-century carbon-intense one.
Looking ahead to 2025, we stand at the intersection of multiple exponential curves—AI, renewable energy, autonomous systems, and climate impact. The interplay between these forces and simmering geopolitics will likely produce outcomes more complex and nuanced than any single prediction can capture.
Happy New Year!
