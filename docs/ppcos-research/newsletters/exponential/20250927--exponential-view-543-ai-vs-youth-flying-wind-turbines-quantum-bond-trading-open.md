Workforce realities
AI is likely already outperforming 50–70% of your workforce on defined tasks. OpenAI’s new test, GDPval [ https://substack.com/redirect/d90a68a4-0d45-4e62-8e19-2df98577db64?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], shows models nearly on par with industry experts who average 14 years of experience. If models are as good as or better than experts in ~47% of tasks, they are probably closer to 70–80% against junior staff with only two or three years on the job. In other words, your junior analysts, entry-level lawyers and new marketing associates are already lagging behind AI.
We’ve been tracking this shift for a while. Most recently, back in January, we noted in “Jevons and the automated developer [ https://substack.com/redirect/4205960f-b657-4ef4-83d4-1db5d1cced20?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]” that AI was reshaping entry-level work in software: machines could handle more of the rote tasks, but human oversight and architectural judgment still mattered. By summer, new evidence was coming in. Last month we looked at Erik Brynjolfsson ’s research showing that early-career hiring in AI-exposed sectors was falling [ https://substack.com/redirect/fd113048-a52d-4103-b105-d4ca9c054356?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], precisely as demand for experienced workers rose.
What we have is a wicked problem… Benchmarked AI is a credible competitor to human task work. Let’s assume that it is a major driver of firm decisions right now. What does this mean?
First, firms pause junior hiring as managers figure out what can be automated to deliver cost cuts without having to get rid of anyone. Second, companies start pruning non-adapters. Accenture is explicit on this: retrain for AI or be “exited,” [ https://substack.com/redirect/b4b63091-4df4-4aa6-9671-f24a5613e3a8?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] even as it hires selectively into AI roles. Other firms will do the same. Third, experience still confers an edge. People with strong domain knowledge and experience can better prompt and judge models’ work.
Studies show that expertise strengthens delegation and verification [ https://substack.com/redirect/30c4adf6-42e8-4274-a48a-c600386ac54d?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. But the advantage for the seasoned worker might be temporary. On new, harder benchmarks, frontier models improve by double-digit percent every year, compressing the gap between experts and machines. Those complementary skills for our AI supervisors need continuous upgrading.
The result is a fundamental change in the division of labor as tasks are pulled toward the center [ https://substack.com/redirect/945c4983-82a8-44bf-b25d-66ec36e0b87d?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. This is likely to lower the broad incentives employers have to invest in their people.
Therein lies the wickedness: we have to build new bundles of skills to manage systems increasingly capable of outstripping their human bosses. But “new skills” are easy to demand and hard to deliver. Pedagogy, roles and incentives all lag.

The challenge of AI independence
Last week, Beijing barred its tech giants from buying Nvidia chips [ https://substack.com/redirect/ddc02dba-6a78-40de-96a2-1f3dcd7c8342?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].
As Andrew Ng noted [ https://substack.com/redirect/d6d80572-e56e-4783-a7e4-edc9c537ca49?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], China now believes it has advanced far enough in semiconductors to chart its own path. Meanwhile, America’s chip supply remains dangerously concentrated. The bulk of cutting-edge nodes still come from Taiwan’s TSMC. Even with the Arizona fab coming online, workforce shortages and fragile supply chains mean the US will stay exposed to geopolitical shocks in a single, precarious geography. In semiconductors, China is moving toward independence, while the US has to contend with a single point of failure: Taiwan.
If it is hard for the US and China to achieve sovereignty, imagine what it is like for middle powers. Europe’s ASML just bet more than $1.5 billion [ https://substack.com/redirect/278a4057-8d4a-4f0a-b9a5-9a09809c2234?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] on France’s Mistral AI to create a sovereign stack, but they are still years behind in compute infrastructure, with data center equipment lead times now exceeding 100 weeks. Malaysia launched its first AI chip with $6 billion in government backing [ https://substack.com/redirect/297dbb77-86ab-4ce5-824f-ffce3bf67026?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], yet remains constrained by US export controls. Saudi Arabia is building 1.9GW of data center capacity [ https://substack.com/redirect/da6e8fa5-f544-46fd-85de-ff8aee307e76?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] and Arabic-language AI, but must source every advanced chip from abroad. These nations face an impossible trinity: they lack the capital scale for competitive infrastructure, the technical ecosystem for chip fabrication and the geopolitical weight to secure reliable supply chains. The window for meaningful AI sovereignty is rapidly closing as compute requirements spiral beyond their reach.
The many futures of compute
The semiconductor industry is still grinding out gains. What might be beyond? Rather than hunt for a single success, perhaps we need a yardstick: tokens per second per joule. For that, a plurality of paradigms might compete: statistical, natural and physical. I came across a map of evolutionary possibilities [ https://substack.com/redirect/fc546d9c-7188-42da-9230-16ae2d7b37fd?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] for computing by computer scientist Andrew Sloss.
At the bottom left of that map sit the “continuous” advances that reshape bottlenecks without breaking today’s toolchains: chiplets, advanced packaging, high-bandwidth memory, domain-specific accelerators and reversible computing. These shift energy from movement to compute by localizing memory and short-circuiting data motion. This is the real tax on modern AI workloads. The upper right is bolder with neuromorphic, analog in-memory, photonic/ballistic, DNA/biological and, of course, quantum. Different physics and different software.
The boundaries are already blurring. Researchers have demonstrated analog in-memory computing [ https://substack.com/redirect/44c9e25a-7226-4d7c-94a5-c869ee8df7c8?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] for transformer attention that achieves a 70,000-fold reduction in energy use compared with H100 GPUs. This is not quantum or DNA computing, but it is not traditional silicon either. It’s the kind of hybrid approach that might define the transition era: familiar materials, new architectures. HSBC and IBM just ran the world’s first quantum-enabled bond-trading trial [ https://substack.com/redirect/e4dbd86f-fabb-4540-bab9-566435ddab98?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], reporting a 34% improvement in predictive accuracy over classical techniques. And Intel’s Hala Point neuromorphic computer is demonstrating performance 50x as fast [ https://substack.com/redirect/68c6bc98-3274-4cc5-a19b-9efd4c79b39c?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] at one hundredth the energy cost for certain AI tasks.
Whether any of these becomes platforms depends less on peak FLOPs than on whether the complements that make them useful emerge on time. Those ecosystems thrive on applications, developers and revenue. That bit is hard; consider it the law of QWERTYnomics, which we discussed a few weeks ago: [ https://substack.com/redirect/de7a3fbd-10b6-4a24-9c27-57b0625aca37?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] the fundamental lock-in to a given standard due to “technical interrelatedness, economies of scale and quasi-irreversibility of investment.”
Elsewhere
In AI, tech and science:
Scientists have created an AI-powered robotic platform that autonomously designs and tests new materials [ https://substack.com/redirect/4f5c7c4f-00e9-4d93-89db-9ea53143bfa7?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] by combining machine learning with physical laboratory automation. In just three months, this system explored more than 900 chemical combinations and discovered a new catalyst for clean-energy applications that delivers 9.3 times better performance per dollar than existing alternatives.
China is testing a flying wind turbine [ https://substack.com/redirect/4da7b4ed-c822-416f-bd12-07af7d76742c?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].
A one-time gene therapy slowed Huntington’s progression for three years [ https://substack.com/redirect/20ceb542-c9da-4b29-a6d8-ba108c21d4b0?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] — the first time a disease-modifying effect has been reported.
Toward adaptive tissue repair. A “smart bandage [ https://substack.com/redirect/c4dd1088-f6d4-4e72-a51c-693118d0fd09?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]” has a microscopic camera and uses machine learning to assess the wound-healing stage, then autonomously delivers drugs or gentle electrical stimulation. In large-animal trials it accelerated tissue repair.
