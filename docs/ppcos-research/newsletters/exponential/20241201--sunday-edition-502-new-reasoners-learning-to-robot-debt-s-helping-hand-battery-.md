Ideas of the week
Smol is, indeed, beautiful
Chinese AI firm Qwen has launched a rival to OpenAI’s reasoning models, called QwQ [ https://substack.com/redirect/857217a2-4f0b-4e69-8a0b-0c5de48541e0?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] – and it’s surprisingly compact. At 32 billion parameters, it is one-fifth the size of GPT-3, a model released in 2020. QwQ is competitive with o1 on coding and maths benchmarks, while outperforming non-reasoning models such as GPT-4 and Claude 3.5 Sonnet. This goes to show that much smaller models can be highly effective when scaled during the inference phase. See our recent essay on AI scaling [ https://substack.com/redirect/28d54b1e-2786-4712-857e-0a8b99537fb6?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] for more context and my 2024 forecast [ https://substack.com/redirect/f3607b89-fa68-4877-9ea8-c958e9c3af4b?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] that small models will be in demand.

It may also suggest that the gap between Chinese and American AI capabilities is not as wide as previously thought.

Should we be worried about debt?
Governments manage debt best when economic growth outpaces interest rates (r-g). For centuries, this relationship trended in governments’ favour. However, a new paper from the NBER [ https://substack.com/redirect/edaf040f-9072-4d17-b8ec-68546c9c8f30?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] shows that this reversed in the 1930s as welfare states emerged and growth slowed. Welfare spending increased from below 5% of GDP before 1914 to over 20% by the 1960s, marking a shift in public finances. This reversal, after centuries of improving debt sustainability, points to a structural problem where government debt may soon become unsustainable.
What could fix this? AI might provide the answer by boosting economic growth. Its impact on borrowing costs is less clear: better risk forecasting through AI prediction could lower rates, though there are many other factors to consider. Economic growth is vital. Governments face a triple challenge that will require even more spending in the future: ageing populations, climate change costs and labour market disruption from AI itself.
Learning robotics
Robotics is the next technology to benefit from learning rates. Elon Musk said that Optimus needs production volumes above 1 million per year to get the price down to $20,000 – this would represent a $20 billion market, roughly the size of the iPhone market in 2010 [ https://substack.com/redirect/2ab8746a-9827-4527-8a0a-99346337ad71?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].
Notice how he frames this [ https://substack.com/redirect/2ab4d0b8-b44f-466d-b51a-97c0c7e9ae45?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]… Not in terms of technical hurdles, but in the language of technological learning, with the expectation that manufacturing costs will fall as production scales up and processes improve. Skeptics of Musk’s claims (remember he was certain of fully autonomous driving by 2018), should bear in mind that competitors are already leading on price. Unitree Robotics’ G1 is $16,000 [ https://substack.com/redirect/88ac5548-138c-497d-bdf0-c0f9cf282a1e?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] today, 90% cheaper than its previous model. The G1 is increasingly impressive in real-world environments thanks to Unitree’s in-house actuators and use of Nvidia’s GR00T robotic stack.
Technical challenges are real, however. As the IEEE notes [ https://substack.com/redirect/33663497-e8ff-4aa7-bc65-86f422d95631?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], even simple home robots, such as Roomba vacuum cleaners, operate in environments that they still find tricky to maneuver, encountering obstacles from power cords to pets. For humanoid robots, these challenges multiply on the hardware and software front. That said, hardware gets cheaper through vertical integration and software gets better the more data you collect. There is a viable path to progress: both are solved through scale.
Failure is life’s greatest teacher
What lessons are there to be learned as Europe’s battery darling goes bust? At its core, Northvolt’s failure was one of execution. The company produced just 1/200th of its promised battery production, largely due to manufacturing issues and poor equipment specification. It raises questions about whether the West can successfully reclaim manufacturing in sectors where Asian countries already have more mature manufacturing capabilities. Chinese manufacturers sell iron-based battery storage systems at just one-third the price of Western equivalents [ https://substack.com/redirect/4deb91d3-a652-42c6-bc22-0c1ce20b745d?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. Therefore, the West’s best hope could be to focus on what’s next, looking at next-generation components, as Steve LeVine argues in  [ https://substack.com/redirect/48b7cf95-ff62-4e2d-a2b3-7f6bd0f25b64?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]The Information [ https://substack.com/redirect/48b7cf95-ff62-4e2d-a2b3-7f6bd0f25b64?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. Several European startups are already showing how this might work: Molyon [ https://substack.com/redirect/8c025622-bdc8-4c0d-8a71-6d37c952ec1e?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] is developing novel lithium-sulfur batteries that could offer higher energy density while using more readily available materials, while Nyobolt [ https://substack.com/redirect/9d888e44-8f74-492c-9179-9d14bed2357a?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] is pioneering fast-charging technology using niobium and tungsten.

This week, I sat down with Michael Liebreich [ https://open.substack.com/users/12322724-michael-liebreich?utm_source=mentions ] to discuss the intersection of exponential technologies, energy systems and the climate crisis [ https://substack.com/redirect/b0fdb6cc-847f-4120-ba37-43530771b50e?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. A few years later, Bloomberg acquired the business and BloombergNEF became an essential part of my syllabus in understanding what was happening with the energy transition.
