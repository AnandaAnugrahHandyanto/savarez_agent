Over the past week, I published a four-part series examining the forces shaping AI’s trajectory over the next two years. I’ve now brought those pieces together into a single, unified document – partly for convenience, but mainly because these threads belong together.
This is my map of the AI landscape as we head into 2026.
I’ve spent the past decade tracking exponential technologies, and I’ve had the privilege of spending time with the people building these systems, deploying them at scale, and grappling with their consequences — as a peer, investor and advisor.
That vantage point shapes this synthesis of where I believe the critical pressure points are, from physical constraints on compute to the widening gap between AI’s utility and public trust.
A map, of course, is not the territory. The landscape will shift – perhaps dramatically – as new data emerges, as companies report earnings, as grids strain or expand, as the productivity numbers finally come in. I offer this not as prediction but as a framework for paying attention.
Use it to orient yourself as the news unfolds. And when you spot something I’ve missed or gotten wrong, I want to hear about it.

1. Enterprise adoption is hard and may be accelerating
There is a clear disconnect between the accelerating spend on AI infrastructure and the relatively few enterprises reporting transformative results.
By historical standards, the impact is arriving faster than in previous technology waves, like cloud computing, SaaS or electricity. Close to 90% of surveyed organizations now say they use AI in at least one business function [ https://substack.com/redirect/92bf67e8-08e0-4527-b946-d39b371711eb?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].
But organizational integration is hard because it requires more than just API access. AI is a general-purpose technology which ultimately transforms every knowledge-intensive activity, but only after companies pair the technology with the institutional rewiring that’s needed to metabolise them. This requires significant organizational change, process re‑engineering and data governance.
McKinsey shared that some 20% of organizations already report a tangible impact on value creation from genAI [ https://substack.com/redirect/e767347d-3eb3-4fca-8090-9548873ae1ab?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. Those companies have done the hard yards fixing processes, tightening data, building skills and should find it easier to scale next year. One such company is BNY Mellon [ https://substack.com/redirect/3e2e4144-f18f-4225-ae3a-95a9145987e6?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. The bank’s current efficiency gains follow a multi-year restructuring around a “platforms operating model”. Before a single model could be deployed at scale, they had to create an “AI Hub” to standardize data access and digitize core custody workflows. The ROI appeared only after this architectural heavy-lifting was completed. The bank now operates over 100 “digital employees” and has 117 AI solutions in production. They’ve cut unit costs per custody trade by about 5% and per net asset value by 15%. The next 1,000 “digital employees” should be less of a headache.
The best example, though, is JP Morgan, whose boss Jamie Dimon said [ https://substack.com/redirect/dc7e4391-45f6-45fd-8ddc-522b89d0c08b?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]: “We have shown that for $2 billion of expense, we have about $2 billion of benefit.” This is exactly what we would expect from a productivity J‑curve. With any general‑purpose technology, a small set of early adopters captures gains first, while everyone else is reorienting their processes around the technology. Electricity and information technology followed that pattern; AI is no exception. The difference now is the speed at which the leading edge is moving.
I don’t think this will be a multi‑decadal affair for AI. The rate of successful implementation is higher, and organizations are moving up the learning curve. As we go from “hard” to “less hard” over the next 12-18 months, we should expect an inflection point where value creation rapidly broadens. The plus is that the technology itself will only get better.
Crucially, companies are already spending as if that future value is real. A 2025 survey [ https://substack.com/redirect/18fa0ecf-aa19-473d-b973-8ea7890db826?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] by Deloitte shows that 85% of organizations increased their AI investment in the past 12 months, and 91% plan to increase it again in the coming year.
One complicating factor in assessing the impact of genAI on firms is the humble mobile phone. Even if their bosses are slow to implement new workflows, employees have already turned to AI – often informally, on personal devices and outside official workflows [ https://substack.com/redirect/2a537749-3e9f-4c6e-826b-6512fa52e70c?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] – which introduces a latent layer of traction inside organisations. This is a confounding factor, and it’s not clear whether this speeds up or slows down enterprise adoption.
On balance, I’d expect this to be the case. In diffusion models inspired by Everett Rogers [ https://substack.com/redirect/22a223eb-e37b-42dc-8d07-0d9d335158cd?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] and popularised by Geoffrey Moore, analysts often treat roughly 15-20% adoption as the point at which a technology begins to cross from early adopters into the early majority. Once a technology reaches that threshold, adoption typically accelerates as the mainstream follows. We could reasonably expect this share to rise towards 50% over the coming years.
However, 2026 will be a critical check-in. If the industry is still relying on the case studies of JP Morgan [ https://substack.com/redirect/39695b85-d5f9-4456-8ddd-08ff5a563f58?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], ServiceNow [ https://substack.com/redirect/ef9a3707-11ce-46c1-8b4d-2ed574304048?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] and BNY Mellon rather than a slew of positive productivity celebrations from large American companies, diffusion is taking longer than expected. AI would be well off the pace.
2. Revenue is already growing like software
We estimate that the generative AI sector experienced roughly 230% annual revenue growth in 2025, reaching around $60 billion [ https://substack.com/redirect/a8a6a595-532e-4b58-b46a-d31c8d106014?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].
That puts this wave on par with commercialization of cloud, which took only two years to reach $60 billion in revenue [ https://substack.com/redirect/0ed20d2b-0025-459b-8fa6-fce7ef057782?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. The PC took nine years [ https://substack.com/redirect/09d2c424-be9d-44cb-9fa5-b68095d924a3?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]; the internet 13 years [ https://substack.com/redirect/0022ebec-f3e7-453e-be48-2319dc4fefeb?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].
More strikingly, the growth rate is not yet slowing. In our estimates, the last quarter’s annualized revenue growth was about 214%, close to the overall rate for the year. The sources are familiar – cloud, enterprise/API usage and consumer apps – but the fastest‑growing segment by far is API, which we expect to have grown nearly 300% in 2025 (compared to ~140% for apps and ~50% for cloud). Coding tools are already a $3 billion business against $157 billion in developer salaries, a massive efficiency gap. Cursor reportedly hit $1 billion ARR by late 2025 [ https://substack.com/redirect/c4bcb49d-372c-4145-9bb5-8673f2b1880f?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], the fastest SaaS scale-up ever, while GitHub Copilot generates hundreds of millions in recurring revenue (see my conversation with GitHub CEO Thomas Dohmke [ https://substack.com/redirect/6e388f12-8c6d-4d68-b119-00c91090d6e2?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]). These tools are converting labor costs into high-margin software revenue as they evolve from autocomplete to autonomous agents. The current market size is just the beginning.
Consumer revenues, meanwhile, are expanding as the user base compounds. Monthly active users of frontier chatbots are driving a classic “ARPU ratchet”: modest price increases, higher attach rates for add-ons, and a growing share of users paying for premium tiers. There are structural reasons to expect this to continue, even before AI feels ubiquitous inside firms.
First, the base of adoption is widening. If 2026 brings a wave of verified productivity wins, this trajectory will steepen. More firms should enjoy meaningful results and the surveys should show unambiguously that 25-30% of firms that started pilots are scaling them. As the remaining majority shift from pilots to production, they will push a far greater workload onto a small number of model providers. Revenue can rise even while most firms are still doing the unglamorous integration work; pilots “chew” tokens, but scaling up chews more.
Second, the workloads themselves are getting heavier. A basic chatbot turn might involve a few hundred tokens, but agentic workflows that plan, load tools and spawn sub‑agents can consume tens of thousands. To the user it still feels like “one question,” but under the surface, the token bill – and therefore the revenues – is often 10-40x higher.
Of course, this growth in usage will see token bills rise. And companies may increasingly use model-routers to flip workloads to cheaper models (or cheaper hosts) to manage their bills.
But ultimately, what matters here is the amount consumers and firms are willing to spend on genAI products.
3. The real scaling wall is energy
Energy is the most significant physical constraint on the AI build-out in the US, as I argued in the  [ https://substack.com/redirect/03843b34-e7da-4da3-bee0-e9dde6c0c041?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]New York Times [ https://substack.com/redirect/03843b34-e7da-4da3-bee0-e9dde6c0c041?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] back in December [ https://substack.com/redirect/03843b34-e7da-4da3-bee0-e9dde6c0c041?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. The lead time for new power generation and grid upgrades, often measured in decades, far exceeds the 18-24 months needed to build a data center. The US interconnection queue has a median wait of four to five years [ https://substack.com/redirect/1fd9171e-12ff-4247-b005-32a1a0bc090e?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] for renewable and storage projects to connect to the grid. Some markets report average waiting times as long as 9.2 years [ https://substack.com/redirect/b7ffcd9a-0b8c-4d3c-aa79-46b4ceb897ef?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].
This is also a problem in Europe. Grid connections face a backlog of seven to ten years  [ https://substack.com/redirect/b8dbc932-711e-4e9a-a289-b7381ee58bcd?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]in data center hotspots.
For the Chinese, the calculus is different.
Rui Ma  points out that “current forecasts through 2030 suggest that China will only need AI-related power equal to 1-5% of the power it added over the past five years, while for the US that figure is 50-70%.”
Because the American grid can’t keep up, data center builders are increasingly opting out and looking for behind-the-meter solutions, such as gas turbines or off-grid solar. Solar is particularly attractive – some Virginia projects can move from land-use approval to commercial operation in only 18 to 24 months [ https://substack.com/redirect/0ca16016-5c86-4ae4-aa9f-741e0afaa6fd?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. Compute will increasingly be dictated by the availability of stranded energy and the resilience of local grids [ https://substack.com/redirect/c571dff8-680b-4087-bf21-c143ada12f0a?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] rather than by proximity to the end user.
These grid limitations cast doubt on the industry’s most ambitious timelines. Last year, some forecasts anticipated 10 GW clusters by 2027 [ https://substack.com/redirect/2406823a-085c-4448-95f1-c44a7450a07f?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. This now appears improbable. In fact, Epoch AI  suggests that we would be on track for a one-trillion-dollar cluster [ https://substack.com/redirect/0ca9105b-1e35-44be-9c32-895e3dd6191c?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] based on historic trends by 2030. Assuming such a cluster had a decent business case and there was cash available to fund it, energy and other physical constraints would mean 2035 is a more realistic timeline. As long as it remains dependent exclusively on the scaling paradigm and this timeline slips from 2030 to 2035, capability progress will slow. It’s worth noting, the “slow” is relative here. Even if such a delay materialized, progress might feel fast.
And we shouldn’t assume the researchers lack agency in all this. If a strategy like scaling isn’t possible due to physical constraints, labs may switch tacks and find ways to improve performance that are less reliant on ever-larger models.
4. The inference-training squeeze
Even if firms secure power, they face a brutal explore-vs-exploit trade-off. Projections of 10 GW training clusters [ https://substack.com/redirect/58c8c00e-0906-4f4b-bd7b-b60b8ec5d90c?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] assume companies can dedicate resources solely to building the next frontier model. But inference and training compete for the same scarce inputs: power, GPUs and interconnects. Inference is the only one generating revenue.
In 2024, OpenAI spent roughly $2 billion on inference [ https://substack.com/redirect/1facda19-5469-44ab-a1a7-9e27c3acce34?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] vs. $5 billion on R&D and training, with about a quarter of compute going to serving customers rather than advancing capabilities. Since then, ChatGPT prompt volume has grown from 1 billion [ https://substack.com/redirect/4d62cb4e-73b6-4bd6-beeb-deac057d4efa?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] to 2.5 billion queries daily [ https://substack.com/redirect/b288e880-10ce-4a4d-9542-b3183ef34279?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], a 150% increase in eight months. This eats into training headroom.
If token demand continues compounding faster than hardware capacity, with a gap of perhaps two to five percentage points a month, inference’s share of compute will keep rising. On those numbers, a lab with ~30% of its compute on inference today would see that share rise toward ~50% within about two years and ~70% within four, squeezing the budget for new frontier training runs. At some point, there simply isn’t enough left over for frontier training runs.
Indeed, Lukasz Kaiser, leading research scientist at OpenAI, admits that around the time of GPT-4 [ https://substack.com/redirect/98326962-caad-4465-b389-0de2d3d92754?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] all of OpenAI’s compute was dedicated to training. But when ChatGPT really took off, the GPUs switched to supporting inference – that is, serving consumers.
Even with Google’s enormous resources, and even on the Gemini Ultra plan, we regularly run into limits.
Three factors could accelerate or delay this crunch:
Efficiency gains have historically delivered 3-4% monthly improvements, potentially closing the gap. This is one reason OpenAI introduced model routing in GPT-5: to assign an appropriate level of compute to a given query [ https://substack.com/redirect/5a0b846e-aef8-437d-9435-551836d72d9c?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], rather than having a model spend 30 minutes figuring out how to respond to “Hi”. According to some testing, Gemini 3 consumes 35% fewer token [ https://substack.com/redirect/999b1170-3195-4f70-bcd4-387652119bb0?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]s [ https://substack.com/redirect/999b1170-3195-4f70-bcd4-387652119bb0?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] than the existing stack while delivering better quality.
Reasoning models break the arithmetic entirely. o1-class systems use 10-100 times more inference compute per query. If these account for even 20% of traffic, efficiency gains get swamped and the crunch accelerates dramatically.
Price remains a lever. Companies can throttle demand by raising prices, but that undermines the growth story that justifies their capital expenditure. OpenAI needs revenue growing toward $100 billion to justify its valuation; you do not get there by rationing access.
The implications are uneven. Tech giants like Google and Amazon can afford to burn cash on training, subsidized by their cloud and retail empires. Competitors without those safety nets face a harder road. If you see labs aggressively gating access to reasoning models next year, take it as a signal: demand is scaling, but energy and compute are hitting a ceiling.
5. Capital markets struggle with exponentials
The fundamental dynamic at play is about the nature of things modern capital markets are designed to fund. General-purpose technologies are not one of them. AI is just such a technology.
Its benefits accrue diffusely across the economy, with long lags and large spillovers. [ https://substack.com/redirect/d7c2bbef-e5f9-4d45-a2a7-2d590cbd6669?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] It is even more complex than a typical general-purpose technology because it also changes how we invent [ https://substack.com/redirect/b81a0a8e-f890-44b6-a764-2b6c683eb2df?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], it raises productivity in the production of ideas, not just goods and services.
Those two characteristics skew the calculus. The economics of general-purpose technologies are hard enough. As a new method of invention [ https://substack.com/redirect/34826934-3487-4978-8058-798a79558bf8?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], AI will lower the cost of exploring the design space, change who can invent and shift the distribution of returns toward second- and third-order effects. But the inventor may not hold the surplus [ https://substack.com/redirect/4c2fd8fd-8333-456f-9e16-35eb7e82e4ee?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].
Think of how much of the internet’s value accrued to users and downstream firms, not to those who laid the first fiber. Early 2000s ISP Qwest may have encouraged us to “ride the light” but it didn’t find profits.
Profits of the internal combustion engine took a different shape. While car companies captured most of the profits, oil companies and finance businesses were not far behind and both of those enjoyed better margins than auto marques.
For capital markets, this uncertainty isn’t just about who might win in a well-defined game; it’s about the type of game that’s being played. Markets use tools that assume relatively stable competitive structures and roughly linear growth in order to price company-level cashflows over three- to ten-year horizons.
Where will profits accumulate in the AI stack? With the chip companies, the cloud companies, with distinct model providers, and application vendors?
Complicating this, most of the value will show up as firm-level transformation, better products in other sectors, faster diffusion of ideas and lower research and development costs across the economy. Very little of that maps cleanly to “this specific GPU cluster produces this specific revenue stream.”
Capital markets need identifiable projects with appropriate cash flows, not a bundle of spillovers and system-level option value. AI can raise long-run GDP; investments in individual projects may miss because the claim on those gains is too indirect.
So capital markets are “struggling” not because investors are foolish or complacent but because they are being asked to price a system-level productivity shock funded by balance sheets already near their capex tolerance, with payoffs that are skewed, path-dependent and heavily mediated by policy and infrastructure.
It’s another expression of the “exponential gap” I described in my first book [ https://substack.com/redirect/9bc40e71-40ba-47f6-972a-bee0f6116c96?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], when exponential AI collides with 20th-century tools for pricing risk and allocating capital. The rational response, in that context, is exactly what you see: extreme dispersion, Nvidia and a handful of hyperscalers absorbing almost all the AI market cap [ https://substack.com/redirect/fe33c777-8fc1-4853-841a-08366490e25e?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], persistent boom-bust anxiety [ https://substack.com/redirect/c77b65a0-0e85-45de-9dbe-de577c068c59?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] and a chronic undersupply of truly long-duration, system-level capital.
6. Perhaps GPUs do last six years
A major concern that the market has is over the lifespan of the GPUs that dominate AI processing. And for good reason, over the past few years, the tech firms have told us that these GPUs should be depreciated over six years not two years, as they said four years ago.
It feels fishy, doesn’t it. We’re told demand is through the roof, the GPUs are “burning” with utilization rates about 80%, and that Nvidia is making them obsolete faster than ever. And yet, as if by magic, they are lasting twice as long as before.
So, who is right? Hedge fund investor Michael Burry (Cassandra Unchained [ https://open.substack.com/pub/michaeljburry ]), who famously shorted sub-prime in 2006, today argues for a two- to three-year useful life. That is what I believed in March of this year. Back then I wrote [ https://substack.com/redirect/2f564386-84a8-43b2-9556-69fb22c2a1c0?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]
those expensive GPUs have a shelf-life. The accountants may depreciate them over five years, but the infrastructure managers know that a couple of years of hard running will leave exhausted silicon needing replacement.
But are the cloud providers, who insist a four-to-six-year lifespan is more realistic, correct?
Michael Intrator [ https://substack.com/redirect/8441bc57-4107-4924-b731-18c97478f178?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], who runs CoreWeave, a neocloud, reports that inventories of NVIDIA A100s – technology dating back to 2020 – remain fully booked. Meanwhile, 2022-era H100 chips are being re-contracted at 95% of their original price. Analysis by Bernstein [ https://substack.com/redirect/b8ac08fe-ea3a-43df-8936-0141be35fbb8?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] suggests that even aging processors command a contribution margin of roughly 70%. An older A100 generates $0.93 in hourly revenue against a cash operating cost of just $0.28. As long as marginal revenue exceeds electricity costs, the asset retains value.
This is not accounting optimism; it is grounded in the order book. Nitin Agrawal [ https://substack.com/redirect/d1d3d76e-766a-4862-b024-9974cce52453?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], CoreWeave’s CFO, notes that customer commitments have structurally lengthened, with five- to six-year contracts replacing shorter durations. Scarcity has forced the market to accept “trailing edge” silicon, pricing the utility of chips far into the future.
I’ve dug fairly deeply into this question. And I’ve changed my mind since March. Yes, long-depreciation schedules indeed help companies burnish their earnings. They spread depreciation expenses over more years with a multi-billion dollar impact to their earnings this year.
But there’s also increasing evidence that fleets of high-performance chips do – today – last longer than 2 years. And yes, up to six. Google’s TPUs, for instance, remain in use for seven to eight years [ https://substack.com/redirect/03407259-2000-4027-abf6-599c3038a398?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], with older generations still achieving 100% utilization.
AI cloud provider, Lambda, makes the case  [ https://substack.com/redirect/c875b987-f1b4-4e9d-b594-0e4fd4608207?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]that the “effective lifespan of GPUs can stretch to seven or eight years” and warranties on GPUs are five years long. After two years, GPUs may not be cutting-edge enough to support training workloads, but there are places for them to go. “Because there’s a large diversity of models to solve many different problems, there’s a lot of room to use GPUs for a long time, just transitioning them from one type of job to the next”, according to a senior exec at Crusoe, another cloud provider.
The key here is this “value cascade.” Two years doing the main job (training), two years moving to high-value inference and then two years working on batch processing and utility workloads. And, of course, this approach makes sense – yielding as it does potentially billions of dollars of bottom line impact annually. It’s worth investing in the fleet and workload management systems to enable it.
But here is a critical point – and one overlooked by many commentators – the big tech companies started changing depreciation schedules well before ChatGPT was around. And their changes to those schedules are quite likely consistent with the growing importance of managing the lifespan and viability of larger and larger fleets of chips.
Amazon extended the useful life of servers from 4 to 5 years, effective in 2022. A second extension in 2024 took it to six years. And in February 2025, they shortened the life of their AI gear to five years.
Microsoft extended their lifespan for servers from four years to six years, five months before ChatGPT was launched.
Alphabet started to extend the useful life of servers from three years to four back in January 2021 and then in January 2023, extending if further to six years.
In other words, this pattern of extension is consistent with a more technical focus on managing expensive computing assets better. It is scarcely conceivable that any of these firms knew how workloads would grow in the months and years leading up to it before ChatGPT was launched.
7. Compute capacity has become existential for platforms
Computing infrastructure is existential for the major tech companies. Their cash cow businesses – search, enterprise app, shopping – depend on compute. And for Microsoft, Google and Amazon, it’s Azure, AWS and Google Cloud that are the fastest growing parts of their stack. And if there is one thing we can be confident of, their business customers will be putting heavier workloads into the cloud over the coming years. (For more details, read our joint research [ https://substack.com/redirect/5e3e4a6b-e21e-471d-a186-b8828ac36976?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] with BCG Henderson Institute.)
Google cannot sensibly rely on Azure or AWS infrastructure by 2029 without ceding its destiny to direct competitors. And the same is true for Microsoft and Amazon. The hundreds of billions in “defensive capex” flowing into proprietary data centers are the price of sovereignty. They are an insurance policy against finding your core product roadmap gated by a rival’s pricing committee.
So we have two different rationales here. One is that the demand growth is so strong that this capacity is needed. That seems plausible, although it’s also clear that investments are happening well ahead of revenues showing up. It’s why we track both revenue momentum and industry strain on our “boom or bubble” [ https://substack.com/redirect/74228ce2-13f3-457a-a0f4-a41d7ff4968a?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] dashboard. The second is that competitive dynamics give the firms little choice but to invest, even if that turns into overinvestment.
8. The “productivity clock” rings around 2026
Because AI is a general-purpose technology, the value may or may not accrue to its inventors. The best way to see value is in its effect on productivity. The question for 2026 is whether AI shows up in those numbers.
So far, estimates from the St Louis Fed suggest a potential 1.3% increase in US productivity [ https://substack.com/redirect/1155da2a-51b8-43ad-a8c2-6fdc5ffdfd3b?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] since the launch of ChatGPT, but that evidence is not yet fully convincing. Much of it rests on self-reported time-saving models, which are flimsy as evidence. Productivity growth after ChatGPT [ https://substack.com/redirect/7a8af9b6-f093-4a61-b8bd-254519d75602?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] looks promising, but is also affected by confounding factors such as post-COVID recovery. Typically, productivity growth has averaged about 1.4 to 1.5%. To be persuaded of AI’s impact, we would need to see a sustained 2.5 to 4.0% [ https://substack.com/redirect/97a1bd11-bc68-45c2-b5bc-8b129573efe3?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] with no obvious confounding factors.
Right now, we’re in an odd superposition. At the micro level, some teams and firms say their output per head has doubled [ https://substack.com/redirect/e8d71b5b-2745-42b3-a3ee-ad4a669b4f49?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. At the macro level, the national accounts mostly shrug. The “AI boom” has not yet turned into a visible step change in GDP per hour worked. I explore this paradox in a recent episode of my show:
For many large companies, at the enterprise level, the clock started ticking by the latter part of 2023, when Microsoft shipped Copilot. The first serious pilots began at the beginning of 2024.
So we’re approaching the second anniversary of the first reasonable cohort size of those projects. This is long enough for results to be known and for firms to give the go-ahead for wider-scale internal change.
The earnings cycles of American public companies in the first half of next year should start giving concrete evidence. If we’re on a boom trajectory, more firms will provide detailed ROI disclosures in the second and third quarters of 2026. We should look for specific information about payback periods, cost savings or new revenues or headcount details. Firms might be cagey about sharing success stories, so decent proxies would be signs they are increasing their AI budget. Absent those, the evidence for weaker gains and an overinvestment path grows.
Even if tension in the capital markets does ease, it might tighten elsewhere, perhaps even more acutely. We will suddenly have more output per worker flowing through systems already strained by inequality, regional divides and institutional mistrust. What are we going to do about it?
9. Sovereign AI is splintering the stack
In early 2025, sovereign AI was mostly posture. The AI landscape has become starkly bipolar: the US controls 75% of global AI compute, China 15%, leaving everyone else marginalised. You can adopt US/Chinese AI systems and become vulnerable to data exploitation, service restrictions, embedded values, and unfavourable terms. Or accept weakness, limit adoption and fall behind as frontier states achieve economic, scientific, and military breakthroughs.
You can see the logic hardening. Britain is building national infrastructure with Nvidia; the Gulf is spinning up its own polity via the $100 billion MGX vehicle. OpenAI’s “Stargate” initiative fits this fractured landscape perfectly. Stargate UAE offers a gigawatt-scale compute cluster under US-aligned governance. Meanwhile, South Korea joins the orbit as a chip arsenal, and Saudi Arabia finances “AI factories” for local workloads.
Even Brussels is softening regulations [ https://substack.com/redirect/aff53811-6acd-4a3c-949e-5dca00766e3d?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] to keep champions like Mistral onshore. Officials now discuss compute and models as strategic assets to be rationed through alliances, not left to the market.
The challenges are most acute for mid-sized and smaller nations, where mid-sized means anything smaller than the US or China. One possible route may be multinational or minilateral approaches that involve pooling capabilities or resources, as argued in this working paper from an Oxford University research group [ https://substack.com/redirect/2c52c458-70ee-4203-8cba-cb2755dc62fc?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. This question is also the focus of a working group I co-chair [ https://substack.com/redirect/a1ba7fbc-7d86-48b4-b4b6-31208f9a2bb0?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] on how governments need to approach next-generation computing infrastructure.
This logic virtually guarantees a splintered world. We are moving toward distinct US-aligned, China-aligned, and “non-aligned” stacks. The era of the borderless internet has come to an end; the era of the sovereign stack has begun.
10. A growing utility–trust gap risks a societal schism
We are living through a massive psychological split. On the one hand, utility is winning: 900 million people now use ChatGPT and other LLMs to code, write and think, bringing the “sovereign stack” into their personal lives. The utility is too high to ignore. Yet, familiarity has bred fear, not contempt.
Three-quarters of Americans now believe AI will negatively impact their lives, a figure that has hardened even as adoption skyrockets. We have become pragmatic addicts, dependent on a tool we fundamentally do not trust. This isn’t a “tech backlash” of the previous eras. I see it as a new kind of social bargain – we will use the machine to do our jobs today, even while we suspect it will take our jobs tomorrow.
In the UK, employment in sales and customer service occupations is already down more than 10% since 2021. If current trends continue, between one and three million jobs in “high-risk” low- and mid-skill occupations could disappear by 2035. In the US, college graduates now comprise a record share of the unemployed [ https://substack.com/redirect/c0edd4a9-a5aa-4e25-9181-f11bbe6d8112?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].
While the macro data isn’t yet showing AI [ https://substack.com/redirect/7e8a7fa3-7c7d-4a5d-b7d3-b2c0dd179a61?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] as the core proximate cause of graduate hiring slowdowns, some bosses in services firms have told me that they are slowing intake.
The political economy of this schism deserves our attention. Previous technological disruptions – the loom, the assembly line, the spreadsheet – displaced workers who largely lacked political voice. This time is different. The knowledge workers in AI’s crosshairs are precisely those who shape public discourse: journalists, analysts, lawyers, and the professional-managerial class that staffs campaigns, writes op-eds, and dominates social media. When the articulate classes feel threatened, narratives shift fast.
We may be approaching what I’d call a “legitimacy crunch.” The companies building these systems derive their social licence from an implicit promise: we are making tools that augment human capability. But if users experience AI primarily as a survival necessity rather than a genuine enhancement – something they must use to remain competitive, even as it hollows out their sense of professional identity – that licence frays. Trust, once lost at scale, could be extraordinarily difficult to rebuild.
The contours of the future
Reflecting on the year behind us, I can’t help but notice that key bottlenecks have moved. It is no longer about what the models can do. It is about how quickly our grids and institutions can absorb them. Revenue is racing ahead of reality; energy competes with inference; and markets must fund a system-level shock using tools built for single-firm cash flows. The technology is exponential. The organizations adopting it are linear.
The next 18 months are the crucible. If the productivity figures stay muted, today’s capital expenditure boom will look like a historic misallocation, and the taxpayer will pay the price. But if productivity moves, we will have bolted a Ferrari engine onto a chassis of fractured social contracts.
We cannot yet label this cycle a revolution or a bubble. But we can debate the contours. These are not technical problems; they are design choices for firms, regulators, and voters. That is the map as I see it.
Now, tell me: where am I wrong?
