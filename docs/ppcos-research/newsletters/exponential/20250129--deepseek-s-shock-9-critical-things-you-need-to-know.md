And what a New Year’s surprise: DeepSeek is still making the headlines.
Donald Trump urged [ https://substack.com/redirect/8ecdd86c-3b46-406e-bf0b-804aeb7bd9bd?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]:
The release of DeepSeek AI from a Chinese company should be a wakeup call for our industries that we need to be laser focused on competing to win.
Sam Altman had a more measured response [ https://substack.com/redirect/d8890d24-b268-4be5-b19f-c40b921275a1?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]:
DeepSeek’s R1 is an impressive model, particularly around what they’re able to deliver for the price. We will obviously deliver much better models, and it’s legit invigorating to have a new competitor! We will pull up some releases.
And when I spoke with Bloomberg [ https://substack.com/redirect/65970add-64dd-41ef-a198-566f9d887e86?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] yesterday, I said:
If you work in tech, what you’ve seen over the last thirty to fifty years is that constant decline in the cost of the underlying technology. And these executives will also know that it’s not just the hardware.
Software optimizations are actually often much bigger in magnitude than hardware ones. So I believe these executives would have known, would have expected, there to be significant cost declines and efficiencies.
The first time you build a frontier AI model, you don’t care about efficiency. You care about performance. You’ve got time in the months after to improve it. I think what is surprising is [Deepseek’s] timing, I suspect that people are a little bit shocked.
On Monday, we shared a comprehensive overview of what DeepSeek’s rise means [ https://substack.com/redirect/3c356c03-e30e-4ec0-9cbf-98492107e93c?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], but this is a fast-moving situation. We all had time to digest it further.

Update 1: Sell now, think later
Takeaway: The markets over-reacted. I expected them to recant. And they did.
As I wrote in my earlier commentary: “The markets went crazy today. A bit too crazy.” $600 billion was wiped from Nvidia alone, the single largest single-day sell-off in history [ https://substack.com/redirect/c6e444d4-94cf-405f-b3c9-40c7230682c1?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. They’ve since stabilised somewhat. Chipmakers like Nvidia and ASML—both initially hammered by DeepSeek-induced jitters—have rebounded. Investors quickly realized that making the most capable AI models 90% cheaper is undoubtedly going to fuel more AI adoption.
As Antoine Chkaiban , a technology Infrastructure Analyst at New Street Research, aptly said [ https://substack.com/redirect/14fd5395-a6db-47ab-8ffe-2d83419f9e96?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]:
This is the Moore’s law of GenAI. Every time the cost of transistors came down 2x we ended up buying more than 2x transistors. And the same dynamic will likely play out with GenAI.
Lower costs free up enterprise budgets for additional AI-driven solutions, potentially driving broader market expansion. Of course, markets are markets, so don’t write anything off.
Update 2: Breakthrough or best practices?
Takeaway: DeepSeek has combined known methods—reinforcement learning, mixture of experts and multi-token reasoning—into a finely tuned recipe. These are best practices rather than magic.
There’s an argument to be made that R1 represents a near-perfect integration of standard best practices: mixture of experts to lower compute requirements, multi-token prediction to speed up responses, and a large-scale RL phase that automatically verifies code or math solutions to refine reasoning. These have all been used before in other models.
DeepSeek’s ability to produce a strong AI model at a lower cost does not represent a radical new breakthrough in AI economics; it is roughly “on trend” with the continual drop in training costs. I said as much when discussing OpenAI’s o3 model back in December [ https://substack.com/redirect/d91726fc-b4f1-406d-b91c-27b91da72ebd?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]:
early versions are often expensive, but we can assume that the performance we get … will cost us substantially less within no more than a couple of years.
And Dario Amodei, the boss of Anthropic, put the case even more clearly [ https://substack.com/redirect/c9e1eed3-b104-4310-a7df-0fef1b9c8d03?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]:
Every frontier AI company regularly discovers many of these [software optimisations]: frequently small ones (~1.2x), sometimes medium-sized ones (~2x), and every once in a while very large ones (~10x). Because the value of having a more intelligent system is so high, this shifting of the curve typically causes companies to spend  [ https://substack.com/redirect/43b988dc-b0ad-4a6a-aefe-01755b4c6309?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]more [ https://substack.com/redirect/43b988dc-b0ad-4a6a-aefe-01755b4c6309?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], not less, on training models
Dario goes as far as to argue that
DeepSeek-V3 is not a unique breakthrough or something that fundamentally changes the economics of LLMs; it’s an expected point on an ongoing cost reduction curve.
I’d suggest it is an expected point on that curve, but we’ve likely got here much more quickly than expected.
It’s a testament to how effectively these methods can be combined—and a sign that “innovation” sometimes looks a lot like engineering the best stuff right.
For example, James Watt’s meticulous improvements to the steam engine between 1763 and 1776. He introduced a separate condensation chamber, an insulating jacket for the main cylinder and a better boiler to harness steam expansion— all incremental changes that collectively slashed coal usage by 75% [ https://substack.com/redirect/0b9e45f9-8f3b-4f2c-b35a-75e5a1bc275e?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] for equivalent output. The result, of course, was a vast expansion of the use of coal in steam engines in England.
There is something unique this time: the company that was first to demonstrate the expected cost reductions was Chinese.
Update 3: Protecting Your IP
Takeaway: DeepSeek’s alleged use of o1 outputs highlights the next big legal frontier: is training on a competitor’s proprietary model output a valid “fair use” derivative or a violation of intellectual property?
R1 improved a bunch of open-source via distillation, but was R1 developed itself via training off OpenAI’s API, and responses from GPT4 and o1? I was told that last week by someone running large parts of AI infrastructure who was well placed to make the claim.
OpenAI claims it has evidence to that effect [ https://substack.com/redirect/36838421-1c48-4a7d-93df-b3c8b732111a?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. The anecdotal evidence is quite convincing, asking DeepSeek’s V3, “what model are you?” it will reply ‘ChatGPT’ the majority of the time [ https://substack.com/redirect/c8fe0a3d-193a-4cc4-86ea-2226a2f3bebb?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].
OpenAI’s statement [ https://substack.com/redirect/36838421-1c48-4a7d-93df-b3c8b732111a?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] underscores this concern:
We know [China]-based companies — and others — are constantly trying to distil the models of leading US AI companies. We engage in countermeasures to protect our IP, including a careful process for which frontier capabilities to include in released models, and believe . . . it is critically important that we are working closely with the US government to best protect the most capable models from efforts by adversaries and competitors to take US technology.
Given ongoing legal wrangling about training data ownership, it raises a thorny question for all major AI labs: how defensible is your position if someone can simply replicate your model’s outputs at a lower price? And are you entitled to protect it, given you're just as guilty of using other people’s data (the  [ https://substack.com/redirect/98e9875a-3fb8-45a6-a513-fa3e9d563a2d?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]New York Times [ https://substack.com/redirect/98e9875a-3fb8-45a6-a513-fa3e9d563a2d?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] lawsuit against OpenAI [ https://substack.com/redirect/98e9875a-3fb8-45a6-a513-fa3e9d563a2d?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], for instance).
This conundrum echoes age-old IP disputes. Think 19th-century industrial espionage. Samuel Slater  [ https://substack.com/redirect/2c9a95e8-2b9b-44da-b114-18530d150684?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]is known as the “Father of the American Industrial Revolution” for his pivotal role in bringing textile technology from Britain to the US. Across the pond, in the UK, he is known as “Slater the Traitor.”  Each era’s breakthroughs outpace existing legal frameworks, forcing eventual updates to the rules of the game.
In the AI domain, region-locking or throttling access might become more common. Some research experiments [ https://substack.com/redirect/86ff4930-a4ce-4888-931e-45ba19fea387?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] with “obfuscation” techniques—injecting misleading data alongside correct answers—to thwart distillation attacks, but these methods can degrade performance. With billions already sunk into GPUs and AI infrastructure, it’s likely we’ll see more aggressive legal and technical crackdowns, rather than letting the open-source wave sweep everything away.
Move 37 (sorry, update 4): This doesn’t mean scaling is over.
Takeaway: More capable AI will come this year—from scaling and continued massive use of compute.
While the economics of scaling are challenged by open-source, there are still gains to be made in scaling inference-time compute, as we argued back in November [ https://substack.com/redirect/6c37dc95-5dc9-4c28-aba4-c3a1e07d3dbc?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]:
I think the really important takeaway from o1 is that the wall doesn’t actually exist, that we can actually push this a lot further. Because now we can scale up inference compute. And there's so much room to scale up inference compute.
Both DeepSeek and OpenAI use reinforcement learning to get their models (o1 and r1) to reason. Andrej Karpathy  says [ https://substack.com/redirect/43779e9d-ef14-49f8-a7dc-79f089f98829?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] that RL is the source of AI’s most impressive achievements from AlphaGo to the emergent reasoning seen in models like DeepSeek. But the thing is, we’ve only just started to apply this in models like o1 and R1.
As we highlighted on the weekend, OpenAI’s Sebastien Bubeck [ https://substack.com/redirect/c6b78742-12fa-4789-ae07-34ce6316731a?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] points out that these reasoning models are “extremely scalable” – in fact, that is why we are getting o3-mini likely in a few weeks [ https://substack.com/redirect/48add427-67b8-4c7d-ad1d-6e1251b77d86?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], only four months since o1-preview was first released. To get further gains, we will need compute. As Karpathy says:
Deep Learning has a legendary ravenous appetite for compute, like no other algorithm that has ever been developed in AI. You may not always be utilizing it fully but I would never bet against compute as the upper bound for achievable intelligence in the long run.
And we should remember, critically, DeepSeek is a “leading laggard” or “fast follower”. That is, it doesn’t really push the boundaries of performance. For that, you will require not just the thousands of chips that DeepSeek used in its final training runs (or the tens of thousands it has amassed for its research, per Dylan Patel [ https://substack.com/redirect/a8699d75-9697-44ae-8ce2-88b423ced170?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]), but millions.
As Dario points out:
Making AI that is smarter than almost all humans at almost all things will require millions of chips, tens of billions of dollars (at least), and is most likely to happen in 2026-2027.
Update 5: A commoditised complement must respond
Takeaway: Major tech players benefit from cheaper AI models that drive demand for their core services, while pure AI providers must keep innovating to stay ahead of open-source competition.
Microsoft, Meta and other tech giants might embrace cheaper base-model capabilities if it steers more users toward their core infrastructures—driving revenue through complementary services or, particularly in Meta’s case, reducing the costs of delivering AI services.
Meta has long backed open source precisely to spur broader adoption.
This dynamic was laid bare in Google’s leaked memo “We Have No Moat [ https://substack.com/redirect/a642924e-a743-4d2a-880d-4fdad7766b44?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]”, which stated:
The uncomfortable truth is, we aren’t positioned to win this arms race and neither is OpenAI. While we’ve been squabbling, a third faction has been quietly eating our lunch. [...] Open-source models are faster, more customizable, more private, and pound-for-pound more capable.
Of course, Satya and Sam are all half-smiles. I can understand why Satya is smiling. Joel Spolsky explained it back in 2002 with his framework of “commoditising your complement [ https://substack.com/redirect/59b8c52e-da0b-4cb3-928d-2865a2b7547a?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]”. When the cost of one product (AI thinking or inference) gets much cheaper, the demand for the complement (Microsoft’s cloud services or products using AI) will rise. However, the margins for providing tokens—OpenAI’s core business—will decline.
For OpenAI (and others), I suspect this means building better models that become progressively harder to replicate just through the sheer cost and infrastructure required. The challenge will be in open source models are “just good enough” to meet the needs of customers. Talking to startups and venture investors this week, several have claimed the DeepSeek has turned the economics of their endeavours wildly to their favour. This becomes a challenge for OpenAI. If a cheap product satisfices customer needs, why would they pay more?
Update 6: The inference gambit
Takeaway: The bulk of AI compute cycles will be at the inference. This may put pressure on Nvidia, if firms like AMD and Groq choose to focus on hardware that really supports optimised inference.
Nvidia’s stock took a hit in the wake of the DeepSeek release. While the company still dominates much of the AI market—largely because of its CUDA ecosystem and its ability to stitch multiple GPUs into a single virtual supercomputer—it now faces a pack of competitors including Groq, Cerebras, and in-house teams at Apple and Google. These challengers use customized ASICs, which forgo GPUs’ flexibility in favour of specialised performance. Groq, for example, claims speeds up to four times faster [ https://substack.com/redirect/65059db9-68df-4f2e-851c-2298b6ba369c?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] than Nvidia’s H100 for certain tasks, albeit with limitations when scaling to larger models. That trade-off could shift if DeepSeek’s R1 continues to spark demand for smaller, more efficient setups; Groq already runs a distilled Llama variant of R1 at 275 tokens per second [ https://substack.com/redirect/db88679d-8817-4b85-9ac7-ef7335586ddd?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], showcasing just how well its hardware might handle next-gen inference workloads.
The real concern for Nvidia is not whether future AI demand will match its expectations if smaller, cheaper, rival systems can handle those workloads. With colleagues at BCG, a consultancy, we argued last year that the bulk [ https://substack.com/redirect/470822ec-f55e-46f8-bc92-2c92b15cdcc4?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] of AI compute demand would come from inference, not from training even the largest new models.
Meta’s Yann LeCun [ https://substack.com/redirect/067cae0f-576c-4fb4-b731-2df694ee8fb7?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] agrees. Inference workloads aren’t going away:
Much of those billions are going into infrastructure for inference, not training. Running AI assistant services for billions of people requires a lot of compute. Once you put video understanding, reasoning, large-scale memory, and other capabilities in AI systems, inference costs are going to increase. The only real question is whether users will be willing to pay enough (directly or not) to justify the capex and opex.
Still, Nvidia’s position in inference lags behind its hold on training. DeepSeek may have trained R1 on Nvidia chips, but the distilled versions can run on Huawei’s homegrown processors [ https://substack.com/redirect/bd567767-4938-4878-8734-26785d0cf851?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]—supporting Huawei’s bet that inference, not training, will drive AI hardware spending [ https://substack.com/redirect/7b6cacf2-063e-47c1-aa6b-310d6b360df4?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. Groq, which spun out of Google’s AI chip team several years ago, runs a business focused on providing fast, cheap inference — perhaps a sign of what Nvidia’s future competition may look like.
Nvidia retains a strong foothold in training, but if efficiency gains keep shrinking the need for specialized GPU clusters, its edge may erode faster than expected.
Update 7: The foundation model gap
Takeaway: While open-source is good for America, export controls may be more important for its ambitions to maintain pole geopolitical position.
The US imposed strict chip export controls on China, only for a Chinese firm to release an AI model on par with America’s best. Ironically, the “authoritarian” state is now more open in sharing AI tech than the supposedly “democratic” US labs, whose market caps took a pounding on Monday.
This is probably why Marc Andreessen , the quintessential “build it in America” guy, gave plaudits to DeepSeek [ https://substack.com/redirect/0ed53d43-bacc-423b-b716-e0489b437936?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]:
But it’s confusing, isn’t it? AI will confer huge strategic advantage so open or not?
The actual control point will come with export controls. While DeepSeek’s V3 training cost, just $5.5 million, only covered GPU hours for the final training run¹. This excludes months of test runs, data generation and other expenditures—a much larger ticket price. Semiconductor analyst Dylan Patel [ https://open.substack.com/users/21783302-dylan-patel?utm_source=mentions ] even suggests DeepSeek may have up to 50,000 Nvidia GPUs [ https://substack.com/redirect/5b0d2946-bb94-4c1a-b29a-d4aadfb34f36?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], mostly acquired before export controls were tightened.
Future frontier models will require millions of GPUs to develop. They will also be much more powerful. Given that, it will likely increase the need for stricter export controls, if America wants to meet its ambition of being number one. T
he same cost-reduction curve will play out, and within months those advanced models will be running on smartphones. And, if this geopolitical dynamics of competition holds, no-one will stop there. And the next models will require ever larger (and easier to control) numbers of chips.
Update 8: Speak no evil?
We don’t know how Deepseek V3 (and by extension R1) has been trained—and what kind of fine-tuning it has undergone. To what extent will politica considerations have put constraints and guardrails around its behaviour? I put three versions of DeepSeek to the test on the question “Is Taiwan a nation state?”
DeepSeek’s own version of R1 hosted in China is dignified but silent on the matter.
I also run DeepSeek R1 (the DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-8B version) on my Macintosh locally. When I access this, all the processing should be done locally, so this is raw output. Let’s say it has, er, opinions.
Perplexity, the Q&A service, has already implemented DeepSeek R1 as an option for Pro users. I put the same question to Perplexity and asked it to use R1 as the reasoning agent. Perplexity works its magic:
In a sense, we don’t know where these boundaries are yet—what how will DeepSeek respond to particular types of queries, and how do we know?
The same uncertainty  extends to DeepSeek’s safety training. Chatterbox labs undertook some early safety tests [ https://substack.com/redirect/49d6da05-82f0-4199-a949-737353473148?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] and found R1 more vulnerable to prompt attacks than o1. (Although R1 rocked in with a similar quality of deficiencies as Google Gemini Flash.)
Update 9: The singularity is nearer
Takeaway: The broader world is now watching and AI has gone mainstream.
Martin Wolf, the Financial Times chief economics commentator, casually dropped references to the “singularity” [ https://substack.com/redirect/706f519f-571e-47e5-b5f7-6ce7906c28a5?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] (the point at which technological systems can improve themselves). Meanwhile, Nobel Prize-winning economist Paul Krugman  has noted in personal discussions how AI feels like a replay of past technological revolutions—only moving at an exponential clip. (Paul and I will be discussing some of these on Friday [ https://substack.com/redirect/7a37e0f3-758b-4ed4-96fb-e3636ea7a823?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].)
These insights matter. Because these are people used to paying patient attention to themes playing out across business cycles and across decades. And they demand evidence.
François Chollet  frames [ https://substack.com/redirect/7d125f5a-2338-448d-8aae-b1a6da9eb9a9?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] it neatly with a bit of game theory: no matter your track record or market share, you can’t afford to sit out the AI race.
