Stargate: A $500 billion bet

That’s an incredible advantage for the already leading AI company if it comes to fruition. The first $100 billion alone will fund a Texas data centre rumoured [ https://substack.com/redirect/b1e69f05-23b3-46b5-8b86-dfa5ab6562f1?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] to house 700,000+ GPUs across multiple generations, powered by 1.8GW of energy. You can already see it being constructed below.

Is $500bn a lot? During Railway Mania [ https://substack.com/redirect/62bfa9de-f548-4d39-90fd-74863eac07bf?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] in the 19th century, 6-7% of Britain’s GDP was poured into railway infrastructure each year, reaching a cumulative 40% of annual GDP. By that measure, Stargate is small—but today’s economies are larger and more diversified than yesteryears. Rebuilding the American electrical grid would be a multitrillion-dollar outla [ https://substack.com/redirect/876fa78f-1b31-4ee4-ae43-400c5d7746de?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]y.
But in 2022, Amazon, Microsoft, Google and Meta dropped roughly $150bn on capex. This year, the four could clock in at $220-240bn and can afford to keep that investment rate up for years. We shouldn’t understate Stargate, but it isn’t the only game in town.
Seeking deep advantage
DeepSeek R-1, a new reasoning model from China, has matched the performance of OpenAI’s o1 [ https://substack.com/redirect/b110bdce-5b33-41a8-9ef8-dae9e5393950?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] while being 13 times cheaper to run [ https://substack.com/redirect/b3bf01b8-f9bf-48af-8133-5ed5685e9229?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] – and it’s fully open-source. As Marc Andreessen  said [ https://substack.com/redirect/c6b9c050-8317-41fb-be1a-6517c87fe53b?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]:
DeepSeek R1 is one of the most amazing and impressive breakthroughs I’ve ever seen – and as open source, a profound gift to the world.
It began as a side hustle [ https://substack.com/redirect/b3d28278-36cf-4161-b0e9-f93fdb0d9879?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] for DeepSeek founder Liang Wenfeng’s quant-trading firm High-Flyer, tapping leftover GPU capacity. Their earlier V3 model was built in just two months on a $5,576,000 budget [ https://substack.com/redirect/c4a5812f-4c9f-41e5-a53a-4e3f838d2cb2?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. Meta is no longer holding the top open-source spot and has gone into panic mode [ https://substack.com/@natolambert/note/c-88125919?r=2inn ]. OpenAI might also be uneasy… There’s currently little incentive to stick with o1.
While OpenAI’s Sebastien Bubeck [ https://substack.com/redirect/31b1b0c4-b765-4687-887e-218c6418bdfb?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] points out that these reasoning models are “extremely scalable” – meaning more compute, more advantage – open-source efforts have a unique power in innovation proliferation. When the entire world can access and tinker with these models – seeing every secret in the research paper [ https://substack.com/redirect/f806dc8f-f337-43dc-a1a6-df56362e6d69?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] – surprising breakthroughs will likely emerge at lightning speed.
There is a lot to parse here. DeepSeek’s approach (model distillation, sparsity and other techniques) can be copied by other firms. And it’s probably fair to say DeepSeek is a fast follower rather than a leader. It also shows the proliferation of perfomant models will continue… with all the implications on business models and safety.
See also: Another Chinese company, ByteDance, which owns TikTok, released Doubao-1.5-pro [ https://substack.com/redirect/7f3664d8-f69a-4f07-82ea-0e667a172687?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], a model that matches GPT-4o’s performance while being eight times cheaper.
Faster enzyme engineering
Enzymes are tiny biological “machines” that make many chemical reactions possible. We use them to make medicine and process food. But designing new enzymes or improving existing ones often takes months of painstaking trial-and-error work in the lab. A paper in the journal  [ https://substack.com/redirect/2394fa31-1554-4ff4-ad19-dec45918f28b?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]Nature [ https://substack.com/redirect/2394fa31-1554-4ff4-ad19-dec45918f28b?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], out this week, shows that researchers used advanced AI predictions with an ultra-fast, cell-free DNA method to generate thousands of enzyme variants in under 24 hours. The entire engineering process, including screening and optimisation, can be completed in about a week per compound – significantly faster than traditional methods. Using AI they pinpointed nine improved amide synthetases (enzymes that help stitch together molecules for pharmaceuticals and other products). Some were up to 42 times better than the original. This could open the door to an era of “divergent evolution”, where the same enzyme is continually fine tuned for different industrial applications. (via AT  [ https://substack.com/@asimovpress/note/c-87650972?r=f84r ]Asimov Press [ https://substack.com/@asimovpress/note/c-87650972?r=f84r ])
Yet, turning these breakthroughs into mass production comes with its own set of issues. At a recent discussion I led [ https://substack.com/redirect/102d2269-e1df-406e-970a-7cbe156afb76?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] with a couple of members of Exponential View, we discussed how 50,000-litre fermentation tanks magnify small shifts in pH and temperature, threatening even the best-engineered microbes. We discussed the potential solution of a feedback loop connecting automated labs, real-time sensor data, and advanced AI models – the same data flywheel approach we see in AI, now applied to the building blocks of life.

China’s compounding tech ecosystems: AT Kyle Chan shows how the overlapping ecosystems [ https://substack.com/redirect/24635233-8360-4ca1-a5ef-c671f9d82f0c?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] in EVs, batteries, lidar, drones and smartphones create a compounding feedback loop that fuels China’s industrial capacity. Each sector’s supply chains and R&D breakthroughs reinforce other industries.
While in Davos, I saw Jian Pan, co-founder of Chinese battery and technology firm CATL, talk about what China gets right with its EV supply chains – it’s not the manufacturing that’s a problem for US and European car makers, it’s the intelligence; China has cracked what Pan called “Electric Intelligent Vehicles”. The e-commerce and software talent is cross-polinating into EIV to create smart EVs. There’s a good summary of Jian’s talking points here [ https://substack.com/redirect/43e9b21c-e1e9-45a6-9519-b770b9031069?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].
DIY fusion: Over a 36-hour livestream, AT “HudZah” [ https://substack.com/redirect/a7d22061-57aa-4a22-8031-688ce9f4f8cf?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] built a neutron-producing fusor in his kitchen – guided largely by an AI assistant. He says he spent £3,000 all in, but could have done it for £1,000. He cobbled together a sub-$60 power supply from AliExpress, rigged a kid’s fuel cell kit for deuterium, and reached 2.5 mTorr of pressure with a basic pump, successfully achieving nuclear fusion.
Artificial photosynthesis in orbit: China’s Shenzhou-19 crew just demonstrated turning water and CO₂ into oxygen and rocket-fuel precursors [ https://substack.com/redirect/ab7e33b6-fe12-4916-a4f9-f97c08dd9b17?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] aboard the Tiangong space station. They used room-temperature semiconductor catalysts that mimic plant photosynthesis, drastically reducing power needs. Though still at an early stage, this could enable future missions to generate life support and fuel on the fly, making them more self-sufficient.
Deepfake meltdown: The UK just saw its first deepfake settlement [ https://substack.com/redirect/5386e790-2fd6-47f6-8231-ae121320c905?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] after a teacher was falsely shown in a viral AI-generated racist tirade. Police debunked the clip using original CCTV, but not before 800 complaints hit her school and strangers arrived at her home. We urgently need ways to verify authenticity at the point of consumption – efforts like C2PA metadata and digital watermarks [ https://substack.com/redirect/68086e60-5d36-4ec5-91e7-eb34d7fa3c25?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] are promising, but screen recordings can strip metadata. (via EV reader Henry Coutinho-Mason )
YesNoError: A new decentralised initiative has used reasoning models to scan and audit 90 million scientific papers for mistakes [ https://substack.com/redirect/0e9b404c-96d0-4192-b42d-ab892adef1f8?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], from simple arithmetic errors to potential data falsification. Inspired by the black plastic spatula fiasco – where inflated toxic flame-retardant levels in plastic utensils caused public uproar – YesNoError aims to catch errors before they drive bad policy or misdirect research.
