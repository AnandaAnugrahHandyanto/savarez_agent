Reading Breakneck: China’s quest to engineer the future
Main argument: “China is an engineering state, which can’t stop itself from building, facing off against America’s lawyerly society, which blocks everything it can.”
The meaning of China as an engineering state
Political and social engineering
Political system geared towards delivering monumental projects and social engineering.
Social engineering:
Using tools of control and “scientific” methods to engineer society to the Party’s goals (e.g.: 0 covid, 1 child policy)
“Sometimes, it feels like China’s leadership is made up entirely of hydraulic engineers who view the economy and society as liquid flows, as if all human activity - from mass production to reproduction - can be directed, restricted, increased, or blocked with the same easy as turning a series of valves”
Political control:
“The engineering state is more than autocracy or technocratic high modernism. China has succeeded better than any other authoritarian country in history at combining economic growth with political control. The Communist Party has relentlessly broken up entrenched interests, partly to prevent rich people from gaining political power and partly to spread material benefits throughout the country.” (p. 16)
Engineering state and building things:
Achievements: highways, bridges (ex in poor Guizhou province), public transit, cars, energy, housing.
Features of the engineering state:
Large public works regardless of cost.
Manufacturing hubs in unexpected sectors.
Under Xi’s third term, elites and entrepreneurs increasingly leave.
Manufacturing strength continues in EVs, clean tech, and advanced industries.
Infrastructure improvements generate a sense that “progress still courses throughout the country,” even under slower growth.
Monumental approach: build first, profitability later. Successes include high-speed rail; failures include ghost cities.
Technology and industrial success
Clearest successes: renewables. Dominance in the solar value chain, batteries, wind turbines, and hydrogen electrolysers.
Weaknesses: aircraft engines, semiconductors, pharmaceuticals (no blockbuster drugs), and universities (no breakthrough papers).
Xi emphasises the “real economy” of manufacturing over the “fictitious economy” of finance.
China excels at scaling and manufacturing depth but struggles with breakthrough invention.
Process knowledge
Where innovation comes from: In the US, from labs, NASA, universities - with celebration in moments of invention. In China, innovation arises on the factory floor through scale and iteration. “At the heart of China’s ascendance in advanced technology is its spectacular capacity for learning by doing and consistently improving things.”
What is process knowledge?
Tacit, embodied know-how residing in workers, supply chains, and relationships. Visible in hubs like Shenzhen.
Wang views technology as people and knowledge because it’s more accurate and it empowers our sense of agency to control the technologies we produce.
Why export controls don’t work: “The Trump administration certainly throttled Chinese companies. But it did so by making American companies (especially those selling semiconductors) unreliable vendors. Previously, Chinese companies bought the best components on the market, which were often American, because they wanted to sell a globally competitive smartphone or drone. When they couldn’t buy American, it lit a fire under Chinese companies to try domestic vendors that they would never have previously given the time of day.” (p. 83)
Economic model
China does not redistribute wealth downward. Instead it enacts a Leninist model, where the state commands resources to maintain control and build toward post-scarcity.
Property tax proposals repeatedly abandoned. Light taxes reduce demands for participation but result in stingy welfare. Social spending is ~10% of GDP, compared with 20–30% in the US and Europe.
Tolerates inefficiency and low profits to sustain a massive workforce.
Supply-side focus: during covid, the government prioritised firms restarting production over household support.
Environmental damage consistently subordinated to growth.
Self-sabotage and fragility
The Party distrusts its own citizens, curbing creativity and entrepreneurship.
The Party would sacrifice a lot, even its own goals, as long as it retains full political power. Dislikes economic actors getting more power.
“But they issued regulations with a speed and ferocity that no other state can match. It did so for reasons that the West would not: to shift investment and talent into state-prioritized industries and to crush the power that these companies were gaining at the expense of the state.” (p. 182)
Cracking down on sectors like digital, housing, etc.
2021 crackdown on digital firms erased ~$1 trillion in market value. Alibaba fell from $800bn to one-quarter of that within two years (p. 181).
“Over the course of 2021, hardly any major Chinese company emerged unscathed. Xi’s regulatory storm wiped out a trillion dollars of market value from Chinese companies.”
Problem: reforms hit so hard that sectors cannot recover (big tech, real estate, bureaucracy). “Sometimes, the only thing scarier than China’s problems are Beijing’s solutions.”
Weak at producing compelling cultural products.
United States: the lawyerly society
Historic shift: Once an engineering nation. But in the 1960s, elite lawyers pivoted towards litigation and regulation in reaction to pollution, excessive highways, and corporate overreach.
Today, the US excels at invention but fails at scaling and infrastructure.
Two enduring flaws of this lawyerly society:
Elevation of process over outcomes (proceduralism). Blocking is easier than enabling.
Heavy saturation: ~400 lawyers per 100,000 people, triple Europe. Proceduralism seeps into everything
“American cities have broadly failed to build adequate housing or infrastructure. What they do complete arrives embarrassingly late or over budget. Americans live today in the ruins of an industrial civilization, whose infrastructure is just barely maintained and rarely expanded.”
In competition with China, America deploys legal weapons such as blacklists and restrictions. These controls often backfire, spurring Chinese alternatives.
Systematic bias towards the wealthy (lawyers serve the rich).
Checks and balances are necessary, but in practice uneven.
Strengths: robust youthful population, digital innovation, and strong alliances.
Why old political labels are obsolete: “Capitalist America intrudes upon the free market with a dense program of regulation and taxation while providing substantial (albeit imperfect) redistributive policies. Socialist China detains union organisers, levies light taxes, and provides a threadbare social safety net. The greatest trick that the Communist Party ever pulled off is masquerading as leftist.”
The dynamic between the US and China: both eroding governance - Xi centralising decision-making; US gridlocked.
The future:
China will not displace the US as the preeminent nation in economy, diplomacy, culture, or prestige.
“China would be better off if it built less and built better.” But China will dominate high-tech supply chains and possess military power to challenge US influence in Asia.
“The obsession with invention has clouded Silicon Valley’s ability to appreciate China’s actual strength. Rather than seeing tools and blueprints as the ultimate ends of technological progress, I believe we should view them as milestones in the training of better scientists and manufacturers. Viewing technology as people and process knowledge isn’t only more accurate; it also empowers our sense of agency to control the technologies we are producing.”
