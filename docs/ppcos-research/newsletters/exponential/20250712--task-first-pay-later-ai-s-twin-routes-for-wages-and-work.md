# 🔮 Task first, pay later: AI’s twin routes for wages and work

**From:** "Azeem Azhar, Exponential View" <exponentialview@substack.com>
**Date:** 2025-07-12T05:22:55.000Z
**Folder:** exponential

---

View this post on the web at https://www.exponentialview.co/p/will-ai-flatten-or-widen-the-worlds

A Mad Max economy, where your hard-earned expertise trades at commodity prices, is a plausible future of work. One type of AI automation deployment could move millions into low-paid service roles even as employment survives on paper.
However, that same technology could also have the opposite effect. It could turn scarce, high-paying jobs into mass-market opportunities. Furthermore, create jobs that don’t yet exist.
MIT economists David Autor and Neil Thompson explore what is at stake in their landmark study [ https://substack.com/redirect/9f1bee0b-69a9-470e-b3c7-c910a0bc8779?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. After analyzing four decades of data across 303 US occupations, they found that AI’s wage effects hinge on one crucial factor. That factor is what gets automated. Not whether firms adopt AI, but which tasks they hand over to machines.
Let’s look into this further.
Two pathways
When firms automate the complex bits of a role—say, the knowledge or judgment-heavy tasks—wages tend to fall, while employment increases. Autor and Thompson's data shows that a one-standard-deviation drop in task expertise over a decade is linked to an approximate 18% wage decline while employment roughly doubles.
Telephone operators from 1980-2018 are the canonical case, where technical simplification made it easier for more people to enter, but at a lower pay.
And Uber is a modern parallel. By breaking the stranglehold that medallion owners once held over New York’s taxi market, the mix of GPS routing and app-based matching opened ride-hailing to a far broader pool of drivers and passengers. Between 2014 and 2024 the city’s total ride market, measured by fares, expanded 228%, while the number of active drivers nearly doubled from 44,000 to 95,000. This greater supply cut average earnings – after allowing for roughly 32% inflation, a typical yellow-cab driver earned 10-15% less in real terms. Notably, about two-thirds of former taxi drivers left the sector altogether, with some transitioning to drive for Uber and others pursuing alternative employment.
The other path looks very different.
Several major firms are now embracing routine-task automation, targeting predictable and codified tasks like call handling, screening, scheduling. The outcome flips: employment shrinks, but wages rise for those who remain. BT plans [ https://substack.com/redirect/c2fe7963-2d18-4bcb-9d77-8fcce4704489?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] to cut up to 55,000 jobs – about 40% of its workforce – by 2030, as fibre roll-out and generative AI take over routine customer service work. Recruit Holdings, which owns Indeed and Glassdoor, just cut 1,300 recruiting and HR roles [ https://substack.com/redirect/124482ea-71f5-43ca-a453-ba57df11ab88?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] after embedding large-language models across its platforms. At Amazon, where robots already outnumber human staff, CEO Andy Jassy admits [ https://substack.com/redirect/7799e1b1-c153-4e4c-82ac-ac83c03f1507?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] that generative “agents” will eliminate some job families even as they create new technical ones. In each case, automation strips out low-complexity work. Headcount contracts, the skills bar rises and the wage ladder steepens. This may already be the case for AI coding roles, with sky-high salaries for super-coders, widening [ https://substack.com/redirect/74c2a2b4-a215-4bc7-85b5-6471f1146357?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] the gap between them and the average coder.
Yet this outcome isn’t inevitable.
Consider India’s IT services sector. Once known for data entry and back-office support, it has steadily moved up the value chain to analytics, dev-ops, and software engineering. Now, 90% of the top technology service providers are investing heavily in generative AI training. Nasscom expects [ https://substack.com/redirect/cca4bab6-790f-4714-9f55-62942741992b?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] the domestic AI market to triple to $17 billion by 2027. Upskilling routine staff into prompt engineers before automation strikes is the route taken.
Micro decisions, macro consequences
Task-level automation decisions inside firms ripple outward into the labour market. They shape employment, wages, welfare spending and the urgency of training needs.
Firms will make their own decisions. Automating high-skill tasks reduces costs, broadens access to those roles, and expands markets for the services. But it can compress expert rents, that is the high wages certain groups can secure. Those experts often have market power within companies or through professional associations (consider doctors in the US). Automating routine tasks squeezes low-skill wages and decreases employment while increasing the returns to expertise.
And all of these decisions will aggregate from the micro to the macro. Firm strategy dictates labor market outcomes. Getting these micro decisions right is becoming a macro imperative.
Yet labour markets are uneven by nature. They’re shaped not just by technology, but by growth cycles, trade patterns and cultural norms. Technology diffusion will vary across the economy—large tech-savvy firms like Microsoft or Tesla may move quickly, but half of US workers are employed by small- or medium-sized firms, which tend to adopt technology more slowly. And only 40% of American workers [ https://substack.com/redirect/a25d4ac4-d0a7-44cc-bff4-464891d7567e?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] work in offices.
Job cuts linked to AI draw outsized attention. But the tech sector is just one part of a fragmented picture – still hiring in many areas, and far from its worst period of retrenchment. My view is that it is that the evidence that the reduction in young graduate hiring is predominantly associated with AI deployment is weak.
Even so, history suggests that general-purpose technologies first compress costs and boost productivity, then broaden the fiscal room for governments to grow. AI should follow the same arc, eventually spawning fresh roles in data stewardship, model oversight and downstream services.
The timing, though, is uncertain. If the gains arrive slowly, the short-term shake-out can harden: layoffs that begin as temporary could calcify into long-term deskilling.
A choice, not a fate
Whether AI flattens the wage ladder or steepens it depends on which tasks get handed to machines—and how quickly governments can turn foresight into action.
It is not the technology that determines the shape of the labour market. It is us.
If wage and employment outcomes depend more on the tasks firms automate than on AI itself, policy must focus squarely on those task choices.
Governments should treat task-level data as critical infrastructure. Firms already disclose ESG risks and carbon exposure—why not forthcoming automation? This information could guide training budgets, curriculum design, and skills investment before the pink slips arrive.
Instead of subsidising layoffs, governments could subsidise upskilling targeted at the sectors and tasks most at risk. But to do that, we need to stop treating AI disruption as an aftershock and start treating it as a strategic input.
