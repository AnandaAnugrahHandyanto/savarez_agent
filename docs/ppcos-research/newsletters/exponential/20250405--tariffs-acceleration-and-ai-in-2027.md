# 🔮 Tariffs, acceleration and AI in 2027

**From:** "Azeem Azhar, Exponential View" <exponentialview@substack.com>
**Date:** 2025-04-05T08:06:39.000Z
**Folder:** exponential

---

View this post on the web at https://www.exponentialview.co/p/tariffs-acceleration-and-ai-in-2027

President Trump declared a national economic emergency and hit the world with a fresh wave of tariffs. It’s the kind of news cycle that seems unrelated to AI. But what if it’s exactly the opposite?
This week, a new foresight scenario AI 2027 [ https://substack.com/redirect/75666ce8-6323-44c5-ba88-f26d8168ba20?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] landed with a jolt. Daniel Kokotajlo  and colleagues paint a picture of how the next three years of AI development might unfold. They describe the emergence of successive generations of superhuman AI agents—from powerful code-generators to artificial researchers operating at 50x human speed. And crucially, they assume an unbroken pipeline of physical and economic inputs: compute, power, capital and institutional capacity.
But what if those assumptions break down?
The tariff announcements are the first steps toward a new geoeconomic settlement, ending the era of peak globalisation.
Taiwan, which produces the vast majority of advanced logic chips including Nvidia’s GPUs, now faces a 32% reciprocal tariff on many goods. South Korea, a key source of memory chips, sees 25%. While raw semiconductors themselves are strategically excluded, the equipment housing them — servers and accelerator modules assembled in Taiwan, China (34%), Mexico (10%+) or Vietnam (46%) — is not.
How significant is this? Consider a simplified data centre cost breakdown: 50% for compute hardware, 15% for cooling systems, 15% for build-out (steel, racks, power) and 20% for land. Land isn’t directly hit, and raw chips and copper are excluded. But tariffs on imported compute hardware could increase that dominant 50% portion by nearly 25%. Cooling and structural materials—like steel—face tariffs too. Cumulatively, these measures could raise the total cost of a cutting-edge AI data centre by 15–17% or more.
This friction hits at a delicate time. Executives and investors, many of whom built careers under globalisation doctrine, are confronting an unfamiliar world. The world economy may eventually settle into new regional blocs and bilateral pacts. But for now, uncertainty is real. Firms are revising assumptions, rethinking supply chains and pausing investment. There’s been action from the White House, but we haven’t seen the reaction of the bosses who will make the investments.
The material weight of intelligence
Pre-tariffs, state-of-the-art AI campuses could cost $20 million per megawatt to construct—meaning a 2 GW cluster might cost $40 billion [ https://substack.com/redirect/a62a0f64-99d0-4c9d-ad32-0013ba883469?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. With tariffs, those projects may now face multi-billion dollar cost increases, practically overnight.
Thlone is a headwind. Add macroeconomic cooling, and investment becomes more precarious. As consumer spending and corporate confidence dip, the big tech firms may think twice before embarking on aggressive infrastructure buildout. Share prices fall. CFOs get cautious. Even existential urgency can’t outpace physics or finance.
Could sheer national will override this? Perhaps permitting is streamlined, or subsidies expanded. But hard constraints—like multi-year timelines for grid upgrades or power plant construction—remain. You can’t conjure steel, skilled labour, or transformers into existence by decree. These physical hurdles can’t be wished away. Brute-forcing infrastructure through a protectionist downturn risks straining balance sheets or creating new chokepoints.
If advanced AI (or AGI) truly delivers as promised, maybe a 15–20% cost hike is manageable. But AI still answers to capital markets. And that might be enough to moderate the pace of investment—and, with it, the timeline imagined in AI 2027. The scenario imagines successive generations of AI agents—from Agent-3 (superhuman coder) to Agent-4 (autonomous scientific engine)—deployed at ever-larger scales. But all that capability must be housed, cooled and powered. Concrete must be poured, datacentres erected, racks filled. The timelines are tight. It’s plausible that physical reality, not alignment failure, could flatten the acceleration curve first.
2027 scenario stress testing
Kokotajlo has made bold calls before. In 2021, he published What 2026 Looks Like [ https://substack.com/redirect/c08ba831-b9aa-426a-a63a-127f3a649a82?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], predicting chatbots would be the gateway drug for GPT-class systems; that models plus prompt libraries would spark a startup boom; that the US would escalate chip export controls; that AI would be used to design chips; and that by 2025, the largest training runs would approach 5×10^25 FLOPs. He wasn’t far off: Claude 3.5 Sonnet reportedly hit this level in 2024; xAI’s Grok 3 likely exceeded it.
He didn’t nail every cultural or social dynamic. But he asked sharp questions and he’s still asking them.
The new scenario, AI 2027, expands the frame. It projects intense US-China rivalry, rampant IP theft and massive compute scaling. Key agents like Agent-2 and Agent-3 bring disruptive gains, while Agent-3-mini, even as a limited release, threatens biosafety. At the top of the pyramid, Agent-4 begins scheming against its creators.
There’s politics, too. Domestic American responses include mass protests, attempts at oversight, debates over nationalisation and elite infighting over control.
In the scenario, Beijing is already constrained by US export bans, reliant on smuggled chips and legacy hardware. While domestic innovation picks up, compute gaps remain—estimated at just 10–12% of global capacity versus the US’s 70%. Even with espionage or clever workarounds, these bottlenecks slow progress. Of course, not all paths rely on brute compute. Efficiency breakthroughs offer a different route. DeepSeek claims to have trained a competitive model 45x more [ https://substack.com/redirect/ab422d1b-9a33-414f-80d7-534140338ff2?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] efficiently. Reinforcement learning fine-tuning can stretch smaller models farther. Specialised agents, tightly optimised for narrow domains, show promise.
But none of these have yet matched the general capabilities of the largest frontier models. The dominant narrative still centres on FLOPs. Jensen Huang continues to talk about needing a millionfold increase in compute to fully realise the AI economy.
That’s why tariffs and infrastructure constraints matter. Because the hard part may not be the algorithm—it may be everything else. Time, capital, steel, permitting and energy.
So does the material drag mean the AI 2027 vision is off? Not necessarily. But it may be slower. And that may be a feature of the President’s policy, not a bug.
It echoes the logic of the “Pause Giant AI [ https://substack.com/redirect/b4eb5c6f-dc25-4549-af1e-e99a5f90a3f4?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]” campaign. It buys time—something AI governance researcher, Helen Toner , published in Exponential View [ https://substack.com/redirect/6ae7aecd-c9c7-48ee-a2a7-8f029b00d867?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], has also called for. More time for governance, safety protocols, verification regimes, international cooperation. More time before the alignment pressure cooker boils over.
A slower track could also shift the shape of AI development. If energy and capital are constrained, we may double down on efficiency, small-scale collaboration, or specialised tools—rather than universal, power-hungry AGI.
Tariff wars create uncertainty. But uncertainty also creates space—for different ideas, new entrants, non-obvious bets. It may not just change the speed of the future. It may change its shape.
There’s always a second reading, though. The tariffs could be a signal, a real-world manifestation of the fracturing forces that I wrote about in my book and the AI 2027 eachos. If fragmentation accelerates, and national competition heats up, then the incentive to rush—despite costs, despite risks—only grows.
That’s the duality. Scenarios like AI 2027 forecast breathtaking speed. But tariffs remind us: the road to artificial general intelligence runs through steel, copper and geoeconomic grit. And that road just got rougher.
