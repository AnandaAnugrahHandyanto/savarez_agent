# 🚀 What OpenAI’s DeepResearch means for search

OpenAI released yet another add-on on to its growing suite of AI tools: DeepResearch [ https://substack.com/redirect/33c9a8da-bafa-4cd4-8ab9-96124fbcee52?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. The product, which shares its name with Google Gemini’s Deep Research tool [ https://substack.com/redirect/8457c1d1-c7f6-49fc-a1b6-19f97ad5ff4f?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], also does near the exact same thing. For a given research question it will formulate a research plan and consult a variety of sources to provide a compelling research brief.
DeepResearch is a milestone in how we access and manipulate knowledge. When GPT-3 was first made available a few years ago, it quickly became clear that these LLM-based tools would create a new paradigm for accessing information. In A Short History of Knowledge Technologies [ https://substack.com/redirect/9f2237c1-2db2-476e-a2dc-4efd4a7d3696?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], I argued:
[GPT-3] does something the search engine doesn’t do, but which we want it to do. It is capable of synthesising the information and presenting it in a near usable form.
Four years is an eternity in AI, of course. GPT-4 is much more powerful than GPT-3 and the new family of reasoning models, o1 and o3, are in a class of their own. DeepResearch from OpenAI goes much further than my short quote suggested.
How it works
I have run several queries through DeepResearch. Each time I pass a request to DeepResearch it evaluates it and, like a good researcher, asks for clarifications. In one of my these, I asked it to research the comparative environmental costs — from energy, water, waste, and emissions — of a range of mainstream activities.
Once I have responded to the question, DeepResearch disappeared off to do the work. In this case, the bot worked for 73 minutes and consulted more than 29 sources. The output was a table covering 11 different activities with six different dimensions of environmental impact. The full text is 1,900 words, excluding the dozens of footnote hyperlinks.
For 73 minutes’ work, this is excellent. I certainly could not have done this in an hour. For a researcher receiving this output, it would form a strong basis to push further, probably assisted by other AI tools to produce a more detailed and robust outcome.
This was a pretty basic data gathering query. And readers of  Exponential View will benefit from it in a future essay in which I compare the environmental load of ChatGPT and other tools to mainstream leisure activities. (Let’s just say Disney World makes data centres look like paragons of sustainability.)
But I have dug deeper with DeepResearch. LLMs are not mere stochastic parrots, as was once argued. Their abilities to find more-abstract relationships between words, potentially teasing out conceptual connections, goes beyond simple steroidal autocorrect.
And this conceptual capability coupled with the primitive reasoning that models such as o1 and o3 offer, means we can do far more sophisticated analysis. One example I tried was an evaluation of the impact of DeepSeek on the AI sector, including geopolitical ramifications. In this case, I asked DeepResearch to also consider ‘What if’ scenarios. The final 7,946 word report took 12 minutes.
I was impressed. The report was well-structured. Each section followed a sensible, logical approach. Initially, it presented a baseline of agreed facts before moving into further analysis. Each section concluded with two or three scenarios, as the tool provided a “what if” analysis.
The quality? I would equate the output with what a strategy consulting team could produce in  two or three days working flat out. Certainly, if I were going to create something with that level of depth, it would have taken more than a couple of days. There are limitations: it may be prone to a bit of simplism, but nothing a little editing couldn’t fix. You can read it in full below and judge for yourself.
Here is the full result of my query. [ https://substack.com/redirect/1caa30a7-b468-4f79-885f-6ee453eaf0dd?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] When you take a look you’ll get a sense of the quality.
Another query I asked was about long-term energy dynamics in a world of growing computation demand. This is an area I know something about as I have explored these questions for several years and have invested in start-ups in the field.
Once again, it processed for eight minutes. DeepResearch could have answered this with the level of quality you might get from reading the mainstream media or a typical Substack. In fact, I expected it to do no more.  The response was well-structured, although some of its analysis trod fairly well-worn paths.
In fact, it stood out and pushed its thinking in one or two areas. I did not expect, for example, it to explore the ideas behind reversible computing. That is a surprising level of insight that I have not seen in any mainstream discussion.
The jagged frontier
We know these models are getting better and better. We see this from their performance against tough benchmarks, such as GPQA, which measures graduate-level knowledge of biology, physics and chemistry. It is notable that the models can already surpass human PhDs in their fields. Most of us are not PhDs, and none of us are at a PhD level in every field we approach. It’s also the case that you don’t always need PhD-level expertise for every problem you encounter in life.
But it is inescapable that the red line above will continue to climb up and up and up. And perhaps the question about whether you always need PhD quality may start to shift. Perhaps the reality is that PhD quality is often desirable but almost always too expensive. We don’t have binders full of PhD lying around so we settle for using a heuristic or other shortcut.
But what if, organisations start to recognise that PhD-level thinking (consider that shorthand for more thoughtful, structured and researched reflection) is readily accessible. Imagine if things that have “always been done that way” can be challenged, reviewed from first principles, at PhD-quality with a seasoning of practicality? Organisations could push for higher performance, whether that was efficiency or innovation.
Hallucinating at the jagged frontier
Despite this level of raw performance on a narrow benchmark, DeepResearch’s results are good but mixed. It’s that jagged frontier [ https://substack.com/redirect/e9eb4766-95a1-4e25-b9ea-4f466c1dee19?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], again: that AI systems are not deterministic systems so performance on seemingly similar tasks can wildly variable.  In a sense that makes this product no different to any human or human team: still open to challenge, improvement and editing.
And, of course, while the hallucination rate is down, these models still have a tenuous grounding of facts. Although, I would reckon the reasoning process will go someway to reduce the incidence of hallucinations.
The thing is that humans ‘hallucinate’ too. In a recent team discussion, I produced a shameful howler, confidently asserting that Venus took 243 days to orbit the sun. Of course, that is total crap. Venus’s day is 243 earth days long, whereas its solar orbit is 225 days. Hannah Petrovic , our team cosmologist, was too gracious to point out my appalling and unforgivable error. Even the best of us can make simple mistakes… hallucinations.
We actually have ways of dealing with errors, through process and guardrails. We haven’t solved ‘hallucinations’ from humans, yet we’ve build a fairly sophisticated society despite that.
Competitors to DeepResearch
DeepResearch is a brilliant tool. It is better than Google Gemini’s DeepResearch [ https://substack.com/redirect/8457c1d1-c7f6-49fc-a1b6-19f97ad5ff4f?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. For now, Google’s DeepResearch uses the Gemini 1.5 Pro LLM without reasoning capability. This makes it rather shallow, which you can sense in the results. If Gemini produces work resembling that of a junior analyst, DeepResearch feels more like the output of a team of more senior researchers. And that makes a difference.
It will not be the last word in the field. Richard Socher, at You.com, promises to deliver something much better.
As does Aravind at Perplexity.
And, of course, the reasoning models are going to improve enormously. As Sam Altman says [ https://substack.com/redirect/78dd0ee9-af6b-4306-8694-a8274ecdc659?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]:
Going from GPT-3 to GPT-4 really surprised the world. GPT-4 to GPT-5 will be a similar leap.
For $200 a month, I would say it’s ChatGPT Pro, which includes DeepResearch, is a  mandatory purchase if your work involves any kind of external research.
A new page for search
Back in February 2023, I  talked about The GPT Tidal Wave [ https://substack.com/redirect/f8291b31-a8cd-4e38-9cb7-bfd3b8aeb21e?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], Google might face. It seemed, perhaps, a bit far-fetched. But writing in February 2025, even before DeepResearch was available, I was already spending more time in LLM-based information applications than on Google.
Today, I can see hundreds, if not thousands, of my web searches migrating from Google to tools like DeepResearch. There’s simply no reason to start with Google for general research anymore. With Google I still had to search the pages for the answers — now, I go straight to them. The information is presented to me, and if I like what I see, I can dig deeper. It’s the inverse of how you would typically use Google for research.
In fact, my typical workflow is now this:
Have an inkling of an idea I want to research
Dictate it to Claude Pro, giving a full download of things I think matter. Ask Claude Pro to review that, add key research questions that I have forgotten, and then turn into into a structured prompt for DeepResearch
Edit that output and post it into DeepResearch
Google Search doesn’t even feature.
I’d also argue that this doesn’t lessen the need for human expertise. At this point, it rather increases it. Like a good quick study, DeepResearch’s output helps me understand the terrain and think about where to go next.  Of course, those subsequent steps may involve more support from AI tools. But, for now, we remain a key part of the process. Those who think that such tools like this will bring an end to management consultancies and market researchers have probably jumped the gun.
DeepResearch won’t replace us, but it will ensure we never research the same way again. And, of course, DeepResearch (and its competitors) will only get better, and better, and better, and better.
