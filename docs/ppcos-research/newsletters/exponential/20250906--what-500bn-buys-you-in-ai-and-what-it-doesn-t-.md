# 💸 What $500bn buys you in AI (and what it doesn’t)

**From:** "Azeem Azhar, Exponential View" <exponentialview@substack.com>
**Date:** 2025-09-06T08:42:11.000Z

“The level of deep insight into technological trends you share is unmatched. No one is doing it like you.” – Susan, a paying member
I was discussing the OpenAI secondary share sale with a friend. Reputable sources say the firm is allowing insiders (early investors and employees) to sell up to $10.3 billion [ https://substack.com/redirect/caf9247b-4379-4aed-98f1-e7cf181be5b7?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] of stock at a $500 billion valuation. Apart from being great news for San Francisco realtors, it gives outsiders a chance to get a slice of this epochal company.
Discussing this with my buddy, he asked me a question over WhatsApp:
This isn’t a question about whether OpenAI is good or even a great company. It isn’t a question about whether it will become profitable. It isn’t even a question about whether it has peaked or not. Or whether the firm has great prospects over the coming years.
The subtext of the question is this:
Given all the other places you could put your money, would you choose OpenAI? Will its return be better than buying the Nasdaq?
Let’s work through this systematically. The Nasdaq has delivered roughly 13% annual returns over the past decade, albeit with considerable volatility. For OpenAI to justify its valuation premium, it needs to outperform this benchmark substantially.
My gut reaction was “no way”. I did the mental math of the Nasdaq return and just felt that OpenAI could never compound faster than that.
But nothing beats pencil, paper and Excel and when I sat down with my simplistic OpenAI model and ran some scenarios, a different picture started to emerge.
Today, I’m going to lay out some of that thinking here.
The bull case math
At $500 billion, OpenAI is, today, worth roughly the same as Netflix. The streamer delivered $39 billion in revenues last year and profits before tax of nearly $10 billion. That is approximately ten times the sales of OpenAI in 2024. The AI company was, of course, deep in the red that year, losing about $5 billion.
For buyers of OpenAI’s stock today to match Nasdaq’s 13% annual return over five years, OpenAI would need to reach a $900 billion valuation.
But that wouldn’t be a good deal.
OpenAI is a privately held company with a complex shareholding structure and governance relationship. It also has a commercial deal with Microsoft which provides cloud infrastructure, access to Azure’s distribution channels and billions in capital support. This all in exchange for exclusive licensing of specific technologies and 20% of OpenAI’s revenue [ https://substack.com/redirect/de21375d-5505-42f1-b8d7-ed55926bb6d0?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] through 2030, up to a cap of $120 billion total return.
As a private company, it doesn’t face the same financial and governance scrutiny as a public firm. In practice, private shares with complex information rights and restricted transfer often warrant a material liquidity and governance discount. Your hurdle rate should reflect that, perhaps demanding another 10-12% for those idiosyncratic risks.
All of that means you’d need to demand a risk premium – and seek a return better than that of the Nasdaq.
In any case, while Nasdaq’s returns over the last decade have averaged 13% per annum, its three-year return has been closer to an exuberant 23%. Now, I don’t think that can be sustained. But over fifteen years, the Nasdaq has returned about 17% which seems like a decent place to start.
Applying different risk premia to that 17%, an investor taking a position in OpenAI might ask for a 20% or 25% annual return, with real implications on the firm’s five-year valuation – perhaps as high as $1.5 trillion.
At $1.5 trillion, OpenAI would sit somewhere between Tesla ($1.08 trillion), Broadcom ($1.42 trillion) and Meta ($1.85 trillion). Is this plausible?
Consider that OpenAI’s revenue run rate is approaching $12 billion annually, likely to exit 2025 on a $15-$20 billion run rate, from less than $1.6 billion in 2023 [ https://substack.com/redirect/58f2b612-43cb-4df9-8322-c6508540a6d7?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] and $3.6 billion last year.
Growth has been stunning – especially if we use, not unreasonably, ChatGPT’s launch in November 2022 as the start of OpenAI’s real monetisation. It achieved $10 billion in revenue even faster than Google did. My conversations with enterprise users tell me that their demand for tokens is continuing to grow. So is the demand for ChatGPT.
OpenAI’s own investor documents leaked in April forecast $125 billion [ https://substack.com/redirect/dda3372a-8995-4518-a44c-3198a1532a44?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] in revenues in 2029 and $12 billion in free cash flow. A later leak suggested a $174 billion revenue number for 2030.
Various estimates have it that genAI will realise about $40 billion in revenue across infrastructure and application providers. OpenAI is roughly 30% of that. If they maintain market share, that 2030’s AI spend would need to approach $580 billion. If they lose a bit of market share, as they might expect, perhaps the overall AI market would be close to $875 billion. In a later essay, I’ll outline how I think about it, but investment bank JP Morgan reckons AI spending will reach $1 trillion in five to ten  [ https://substack.com/redirect/4337cadd-a6a9-467f-8e0a-a69c897a81b6?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]years.
Back to OpenAI. We can bridge free cash flow to EBITDA with a simple identity: EBITDA ≈ FCF + capex – depreciation & amortisation. The leaked forecast shows $12 billion in free cash flow by 2029. If capital intensity falls as serving costs drop (for example, through routing improvements and custom silicon), that level of free cash flow could correspond to ‘tens of billions’ in EBITDA.
Looking at historical analogues, the scale of OpenAI’s ambitions doesn’t seem entirely out of step with how other tech giants have grown. Meta tripled [ https://substack.com/redirect/14578f9c-c62e-44fa-b85a-1fbd694337eb?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] its revenues from $56 billion in 2018 to more than $160 billion in 2024. ByteDance grew [ https://substack.com/redirect/1d79a5e9-0f5f-4aab-b56e-4a201da955fd?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] from $112 billion in 2023 to $155 billion just a year later, recently overtaking Meta’s quarterly [ https://substack.com/redirect/5a6de8d2-3423-4456-86f7-0c5ab7c2ad29?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] revenues. Google, steadier but still formidable, grew [ https://substack.com/redirect/3703ee48-4472-4985-8540-c64f98cd1ccb?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] cloud revenues around 35% year-on-year in Q3 2024, and 30% [ https://substack.com/redirect/20e12a7d-cd80-476f-913b-9a80c5135e01?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] in Q4, while maintaining double-digit growth in its core services. Against this backdrop, it’s plausible that by 2029 the firm is generating tens of billions in EBITDA, given its forecast $12 billion free cash flow.
That would compare quite kindly to today’s valuations of its near peers. Certainly, the valuation would be less spicy than Tesla’s and roughly in line with Broadcom. OpenAI, however, would likely be experiencing much faster revenue growth than Broadcom or Tesla today, potentially providing a tailwind to its multiples.
And so, as the table above shows, using a simple valuation metric or price/free cash flow, a punchy $1.5 trillion valuation for OpenAI in 2030 would make it as expensive as Tesla today, although somewhat more costly than Broadcom.
There’s also the brand factor. ChatGPT is becoming a cultural shorthand, like Google for search or Kleenex for tissues. That kind of linguistic adoption is rare and it creates a moat that persists. Altman recently hired the well-regarded executive Fidji Simo to lead the applications [ https://substack.com/redirect/00bd9542-73ca-4ba5-9e64-057ff8577b79?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] division that sits under that ubiquitous ChatGPT.
That strong brand is the one part of OpenAI’s growing moat. While the AI models themselves are increasingly converging in capability, the brand awareness, deep integrations with partners, sovereign relationships with national governments for electricity and privileged access to infrastructure may give the company some breathing room against competitors.
If markets warm to Sam Altman’s vision the way they’ve indulged Elon Musk’s, then OpenAI could be valued with similar generosity. On Tesla-like multiples, the company might reach $2.5 trillion. For clarity, at a $500 billion entry, $1.5 trillion by 2030 is roughly a 25% CAGR; $2.5 trillion by 2030 is about a 38% CAGR. I use 2030 consistently for all return math below.
The bear case reality check
These scenarios are based on leaked information we had access to up to yesterday. If the reality changes – as future disclosures or performance may reveal – the outcomes could shift significantly. As with any forecast, these projections are uncertain, and OpenAI’s team may not deliver on them.
Here’s where it gets tricky. As I said in the beginning, buying into OpenAI at a $500 billion valuation isn’t a straightforward bet on a great company. It’s a wager on whether its returns can beat the Nasdaq or some other benchmark.
The AI market is becoming fiercely competitive. Google has just about hopped into third gear. Mark Zuckerberg has put the pedal to the metal. Apple is still fiddling with its key but might find the ignition soon. In other words, three giants who know how to monetise consumers in different ways are about to enter the ring. One can never count out Elon Musk’s ability to swing a game.
And then there’s Microsoft. Redmond holds the keys to distribution through Azure, integrates GPT models across its productivity empire and has first call on the economics. Under their existing deal, OpenAI hands over 20% of its top-line [ https://substack.com/redirect/032fbc50-8a2b-4c08-b368-5b9662950a41?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] revenues to Microsoft through 2030, although it hopes to reduce that to about 10% by the end [ https://substack.com/redirect/032fbc50-8a2b-4c08-b368-5b9662950a41?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] of the decade. That relationship amplifies OpenAI’s reach, but it also limits how much of the upside actually accrues to OpenAI itself.
Competition could eat into revenues on both the enterprise and consumer side and drive down margins. More subtly, the pricing curve may fall faster than the cost curve: if per-token prices decline ahead of unit costs, operating leverage works in reverse.
OpenAI’s AGI drive could be blunted by cheaper, “good enough” alternatives, including small models running on edge devices. Open-source competitors could become a real threat. Specialist models tuned for narrow tasks could outperform general-purpose ones and carve out lucrative niches.
My view is that AI will become an infrastructure of thousands, if not millions, of different systems, upon which agents and services will run [ https://substack.com/redirect/cf95c644-8dc3-451f-9be7-369ed9aa0994?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. That kind of market setup might allow OpenAI to be a major player, but that market structure likely doesn’t promise the outsized market share we’ve seen with firms like Alphabet and Meta.
The cost to serve GPT-4 and o3, OpenAI’s previous models, was enormous. GPT-5 marks a departure, a set of models and a router, that aims to lower the cost to serve up the tokens customers are so greedily demanding. But it’s still expensive, as Sam Altman has alluded [ https://substack.com/redirect/ca1bfd47-75fd-4972-b91d-0bfeff620964?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] to, facing “horrible trade-offs right now. We have better models, and we just can’t offer them because we don’t have the capacity”.
What if they can’t get sufficient capacity at low enough prices? Will that force them to constrain growth? Or keep prices high, allowing competitors (or open-source alternatives) to undercut them?
And OpenAI still needs a great deal of capital spending which could turn into a structural overhang, weighing on profitability and independence.
Entirely new architectures of AI, from liquid [ https://substack.com/redirect/6d3baa5e-e999-4ae6-b9fc-1919f45e29f5?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] neural networks to novel inference chips to physics-inspired models [ https://substack.com/redirect/b2fec3bc-c96c-4c9b-8224-221477560d8b?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], could become the enterprise default, leapfrogging today’s transformer paradigm. Perhaps, as we enter the latter half of the 2020s, scaling LLMs with unfathomable amounts of data, compute, and power will become merely an expensive ladder to nowhere.
Yann LeCun has offered an alternative in JEPA [ https://substack.com/redirect/023adf84-0207-4ddc-b72a-9dc26211edc5?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] (Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture) – what he calls a path to “autonomous AI,” agents that can learn and act in the world without being spoon-fed labels or exhaustively trained on internet-scale text dumps. Instead of guessing the next word, JEPA would learn abstract representations of the world and use them to predict outcomes, reason about cause and effect, and operate in environments with far less data.
Demis Hassabis, too, has said that he’s not sure that current approaches can get to AGI, and perhaps another scientific breakthrough is needed. What if that is the case? What if a novel breakthrough undercuts the proposition of lumbering LLM models? And what if it takes time for OpenAI to absorb that breakthrough science, while competitors leap ahead with fundamentally better technologies?
Technology investor Ashu Garg  has a solid rundown of why one might be bearish on OpenAI [ https://substack.com/redirect/8dac8881-fa2c-490b-8b72-fb4f7cccc079?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], written back in December 2024, when its valuation was only $157 billion. It’s worth reading his take.
The disruption premium
What makes this interesting is the optionality. When you buy OpenAI, you’re buying exposure to the new economy built on AI-as-infrastructure and with that an entirely new economic paradigm. At the same time, the thesis holds even under a more prosaic view: that AI is simply the next phase in the long history of software development, not a leap into magic.
If OpenAI genuinely achieves artificial general intelligence first – whatever that may be [ https://substack.com/redirect/071dd1e4-8588-41ef-a903-d745dc90a5ed?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] – $500 billion could look quaint. Then again, it could also collapse to nothing if rivals like DeepMind, Anthropic, or DeepSeek crack the code instead, or if AGI’s spoils are quickly competed away.
In financial terms, this type of premium is known as convexity: the potential for a substantial, potentially staggering upside. How do you price in a 5% chance of a revolutionary breakthrough? A 1% chance of obsoleting entire industries? The venture capitalists who have supported OpenAI for a decade didn’t use discounted cash flow models; they made Pascal’s wager on technological transformation [ https://substack.com/redirect/1a53cf71-4469-44dc-ae8c-82aec1c1c924?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], focusing on the rewards. And they have done very well from it.
How disruptive could the technology be, even if it ends up just another part of the software stack and not a force that reframes our economies? As I wrote in EV [ https://substack.com/redirect/4a28993a-8397-4a2c-bc78-09e5af47b433?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]#518 [ https://substack.com/redirect/4a28993a-8397-4a2c-bc78-09e5af47b433?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]:
[T]he potential upside is massive. Global wages exceed $70 trillion [ https://substack.com/redirect/8a5c0a3b-9975-4f21-aa24-2009693d1730?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] a year. If AI can automate even a sliver of that, it could justify trillions in annual investment. The potential payoff, AI that reliably performs cognitive labor at scale, is why investors keep pouring money in.
Which brings us back to Pascal’s wager, framed for the techno-economic age: if truly transformative AI is possible, would you rather bet billions for a shot at ‘Heaven’, or save your stake but risk the ‘Hell’ of being left behind?
Of course, the question is whether at $550 billion you’re still taking that Pascal’s wager? Or are you just buying a Nasdaq-like return (or worse) with all the risks a startup faces?
Rechecking my gut
That leaked OpenAI investor deck [ https://substack.com/redirect/d5788aa3-8c03-440f-9ba4-9e5863fd56da?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] which upped their revenue estimates for 2029 from $100 billion to $125 billion is now five months old. Have they proved they can actually execute at ever larger scales? Probably yes. In that time, they have deepened the management team; finally launched GPT-5, with its smart routing architecture; reduced API costs for businesses; exceeded 700 million weekly users for ChatGPT; launched Codex, their coding assistant; announced sovereign initiatives in Norway, the UAE and India and more.
Could the market become large enough to support such a valuation? And is the math plausible? As I said, my initial reaction was no, but revisiting the numbers we have has led me to realise that my gut reaction was wrong.
It’s funny to write this sentence, but it’s far from implausible that OpenAI could achieve a $1.5 trillion valuation by 2030. It’s far from a certainty, too, but that milestone is not impossible.
If it did that, it would deliver a handsome premium over just buying broad tech exposure in the Nasdaq.
Of course, none of this is financial advice. And I don’t hold OpenAI stock.
