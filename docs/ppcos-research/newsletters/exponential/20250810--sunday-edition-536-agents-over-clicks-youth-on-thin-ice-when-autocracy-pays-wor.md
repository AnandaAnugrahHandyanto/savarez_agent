A new era of the Web
This week’s big news was the release of GPT-5 (my initial take here [ https://substack.com/redirect/d921bc75-3ba2-4716-976b-bfb810aab462?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], quoted in the FT [ https://substack.com/redirect/716dcec4-19b2-4558-9ab2-6c74697ce7a5?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] here [ https://substack.com/redirect/716dcec4-19b2-4558-9ab2-6c74697ce7a5?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]), but something bigger is brewing beneath the surface: a fundamental transformation of how the Web itself operates.
A paper [ https://substack.com/redirect/54383399-c88a-4e1d-9dc4-75944d8c1bc2?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] I read this week takes stock of the transition from the recommendation paradigm to the action paradigm – a shift toward an agentic Web with fundamentally different incentive structures.
Yang et al. [ https://substack.com/redirect/54383399-c88a-4e1d-9dc4-75944d8c1bc2?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] outline three enablers of this change: intelligence, interaction and a nascent economy. The first two form a technical layer: agents that can reason and plan, and protocols that let them communicate. The intelligence pillar is progressing – models can handle longer tasks over time, although reliability remains a concern.
The interaction pillar has some firm foundations. Agents need a shared grammar to talk to websites, APIs and one another. Nascent protocols such as MCP (agents ⇄ tools) and A2A (agent ⇄ agent discovery) promise a common interaction layer. Security remains a worry (see the lethal trifecta [ https://substack.com/redirect/4b032a17-f788-4bcb-bef4-9a11965e6023?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]), but protection is improving.
The economy pillar is by far the least developed. Attention (and money) flowed through search links and social feeds in the previous era. Now, in some cases, AI answers are swallowing up as much as half that traffic [ https://substack.com/redirect/20acd995-798c-4fd8-b813-ffa7ea0baa0e?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. Publishers have responded by blocking crawlers or charging for access. Cloudflare introduced ‘pay-to-crawl’ gates. While it’s a good experiment, it has some flaws [ https://substack.com/redirect/84616f72-04f0-4cbb-a512-5e29534162fb?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], as we discussed a few weeks ago:
A $0.01 crawl fee might sound small, but it’s ~20x more than the average revenue a human visit generates. It can get much worse: an AI might need 10 pages to answer a question, making it 200x more expensive. So realistically, will AI companies pay that much per page? Probably not. More likely, they’ll keep striking licensing deals or stick to scraping public-domain content.
But the market will not settle until a standard primitive emerges. The paper envisions an Agent Attention market where the scarce resource is an agent’s choice of which API, tool, or external service to invoke when completing a task for a human user. And crucially…
Instead of competing for human clicks, third-party services in the Agent Attention economy will vie for activation by autonomous agents. Success will depend on how often agents choose a service, how relevant its capabilities are to the task and how well it delivers. This is the Web where information flows are synthesized, exchanged, and executed largely without human eyes ever seeing them.
When stability becomes scarce
Younger workers in the US face record levels of despair [ https://substack.com/redirect/26d7cb69-d708-414b-817d-f883a9d25f81?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. Historically, despair peaked in midlife and declined in people’s early 60s. But since around 2015, mental distress has soared among the young. In the 1990s, 3.4% of workers under 25 reported despair. Since 2020, that number has risen to 8.2%. The change is particularly noticeable among women and workers with lower educational attainment.
For many young workers, the job market feels like thin ice. Gig work and insecure contracts are common, autonomy at work is shrinking, and housing, childcare and student debt weigh heavily. Even with a steady job, the margin for error is narrow.
The deepest anxiety though, comes from broader uncertainty – the very definitions of adulthood and a career are shifting. In 1975, nearly half of 25-34-year-olds in the US had ticked off the four traditional milestones [ https://substack.com/redirect/9a186158-0a0a-42bd-aae9-1cb2c757fe9d?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] of adulthood: moving out, starting work, getting married and having children. Fifty years later, less than a quarter have done all four. At work, many now wonder whether their roles will even exist in five years. 52% of US adults [ https://substack.com/redirect/730d0eef-0ba4-4e49-8021-f25cf3bfe160?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] say they’re worried about the future impact of AI in the workplace. Without clear markers, the very idea of “settling down” is giving way to a constant recalibration of what stability means.
The personalist penalty
It turns out that autocracy is not necessarily bad for the economy. Its effect, however, depends on the type of autocracy. The so-called “autocratic penalty” on economic growth is almost entirely limited to personalist regimes [ https://substack.com/redirect/3c880273-c8cd-4ba2-8030-12833ece27f2?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], where a single individual holds power. Institutionalized autocracies, such as China or Vietnam show no such growth penalty when compared with democracies. Personalist dictatorships discourage private investment, underfund public goods and experience more internal conflict, which ultimately stalls their growth.
This study could dispel a dangerous caricature. Some observers assume China will fail because it is autocratic. Yet almost everything we have seen since 2000 suggests the opposite. As Adam Tooze  noted after a summer in China [ https://substack.com/redirect/d62ca1e7-4dc6-414d-ba11-dd2a73174c5d?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]:
China’s growth is the central dynamic of world development. It is not an instance. It is not an exception. It is the ball game.
While China is still officially an institutionalized autocracy, there are signs of drifting toward personalism under Xi. The 2018 removal of presidential term limits, the absence of a designated successor and the stacking of top Party organs with loyalists have weakened the post-Deng norms of collective leadership. This all raises the risk that China could slide into the growth traps common to personalist regimes.
