How do you price intelligence?
ChatGPT Pro’s $200/month access fee and Devin’s $500/ month [ https://substack.com/redirect/334b4e2e-67b6-4970-9fc4-56d770f2e23b?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] fee for the AI coding agent are steep for a solo tinkerer, but it’s a routine business expense if the payoff is higher productivity and profit. I spoke about this with  [ https://substack.com/redirect/705673f0-666d-4133-96c0-50439b78fcc4?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]Ethan Mollick  just a few days ago and his throughline [ https://substack.com/redirect/705673f0-666d-4133-96c0-50439b78fcc4?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] is similar:
In a world of ubiquitous AI, where it turns out ChatGPT rather than the API is a large part of where the use is coming from, there is not enough value captured for $20 compared to how much value these systems create. This is a signal that this is a different kind of system. [… ] Competition will come very quickly for that $20 price point. It is a signal that higher value AI systems that might be more expensive to run are coming. But I don’t know how long you maintain that advantage. So it’s another way of pricing this. If we say AGI is coming, paying $500,000 for AGI access, it turns out to be a really good deal until the price drops.
Competition is on for the $20 price point. Google’s new Deep Research feature feels revolutionary for that money – I had it scour through more than 70 sources to put together a 3,441-word [ https://substack.com/redirect/297d9ef3-b72e-4068-9ac2-a0ddfcf39826?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] report. I wrote yesterday:
The service offers what might be called the industrialisation of basic research: not quite artisanal quality, perhaps, but reliably adequate and available at a fraction of the traditional cost in time and money. For many, including me, that trade-off will prove irresistible. And in these exponential days, we can be sure the quality will only improve.
Box CEO and co-founder Aaron Levie shared an interesting rumination [ https://substack.com/redirect/e3282d07-a0ee-483c-b68f-bacedeaed893?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] on AI agent pricing. One option is to treat AI “workers” like human labour, billing by the hour or task. Another is to charge by outcome: a legal brief produced, a research insight delivered, etc. (OpenAI CFO, Sarah Friar, revealed that they may charge customers based on the derived value [ https://substack.com/redirect/6b12e4a9-e4d5-44b3-87d2-869a80345319?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].) A third approach ties cost to underlying expenses, like computing time. Or a fourth model might offer a flat monthly subscription, allowing organisations to squeeze unlimited gains from a single fee. Each approach has downstream effects. Pricing based on underlying costs, for instance, might make the agents cheaper – and thus, more broadly deployed. Pricing based on value, meanwhile, could spur the development of smarter agents, but with fewer of them running at scale.

Tickling the quantum dragon’s tail
One stubborn problem has stood in the way of quantum computing: error correction. Without it, qubits – fragile units of quantum information – fail too quickly to solve real-world puzzles. Google’s new 105-qubit superconducting chip, “Willow [ https://substack.com/redirect/bf1e7568-15eb-4885-8f32-61507b4d2183?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]”, has achieved an accuracy milestone 30 years in the making. My view is that error correction in the system that is particularly interesting. Theoretical computer scientist Scott Aaronson has a good write-up [ https://substack.com/redirect/85f3df79-9581-45cd-991f-4b7ce4b48fb5?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] on what this means:
Scientifically, the headline result is that, as they increase the size of their surface code, from 3×3 to 5×5 to 7×7, Google finds that their encoded logical qubit stays alive for longer rather than shorter. So, this is a very important threshold that’s now been crossed. [...] After 30 years we’re now finally tickling the tail of the dragon of quantum fault-tolerance, the dragon that (once fully awoken) will let logical qubits be preserved and acted on for basically arbitrary amounts of time, allowing scalable quantum computation.
Keep an eye on this: where there’s smoke in the nostrils, there’s fire in the throat.
The domino effect
The swift collapse of the Assad regime didn’t happen overnight. With Russia bogged down in Ukraine and Iran weakened by Israel’s relentless blows to Hezbollah, the once-stalwart pillars of Syria’s dictatorship gave way. As this “Axis of Resistance” fractures [ https://substack.com/redirect/58d88368-42d9-457f-8454-890a3182728f?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], we see how local shifts can unspool globally. For instance, Syrian rebels were aided by Ukraine [ https://substack.com/redirect/feb6ee55-a3f3-4bfa-882c-a25694538fd8?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], which sent drones and 20 experienced drone-operators. Without a single stabilizing hegemon, and as regimes that seemed eternal topple suddenly, all those involved – from regional militias to outside superpowers – scramble to exploit new opportunities or guard against threats. The resulting chain reaction may open room for democratic advances, but may also  encourage more  brinkmanship and renewed meddling. Anne Applebaum  wrote hopefully [ https://substack.com/redirect/a5113010-358d-4469-bb31-a912a0b773dd?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] in The Atlantic:
[T]he end of the Assad regime creates something new, and not only in Syria. There is nothing worse than hopelessness, nothing more soul destroying than pessimism, grief and despair. The fall of a Russian- and Iranian-backed regime offers, suddenly, the possibility of change. The future might be different. And that possibility will inspire hope all around the world.
Investor democracy
An experiment is underway in corporate governance inspired by citizen assemblies – randomly chosen “investor assemblies [ https://substack.com/redirect/f94d3563-793d-457c-a83e-7a0bb56c1183?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]”:
As in court juries, the 150 people assembled would listen to experts from both sides of an issue and then vote on a guideline to follow. For example, what restrictions are appropriate for corporate lobbying? How much money should food companies spend to ensure that the animals they slaughter do not suffer? Assemblies would deliberate and vote on these questions, which would then inform how mutual funds voted on resolutions going forward.
Norway is convening a “Future Panel [ https://substack.com/redirect/56168101-91f9-4711-b2ae-b1df30d6ea7b?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]” of 66 citizens to help steer its $1.8 trillion sovereign wealth fund, the world’s largest. Such assemblies could set priorities that reflect a broader range of voices, turning everyday investors into genuine stakeholders who can influence how their money – not just their vote – is used to shape the economic future. h/t Claudia Chwalisz
See also, in social experiments: Tokyo is introducing a four-day working week [ https://substack.com/redirect/e5e53a95-037e-4a5d-ab7d-f7b4ed37f43c?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] to support working parents and increase birth rates. In New Zealand, policymakers have stripped away decades-old zoning rules [ https://substack.com/redirect/0f66904c-8346-48b7-9aa1-34f3b534bf5e?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], allowing taller, denser buildings that help rein in runaway rents. Such moves might seem radical compared to the timid steps seen elsewhere, but they demonstrate what is possible when leaders tackle shifting realities with decisive action.
