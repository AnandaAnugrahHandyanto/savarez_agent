# 🔮 Did China just break America’s chip stranglehold?

Hi, it’s Azeem. This week, we’re seeing AI systems quietly achieve new levels of self-improvement, a subtle yet profound advance. Simultaneously, the global semiconductor map continues to be redrawn, with significant developments in China’s push for homegrown silicon. The practical consequences of these shifts are already tangible – from coding to workplace autonomy to war.
You’re in exactly the right place to understand what comes next. Let’s go!
Homegrown advantage
China is breaking through chip controls. The country’s tech leaders have narrowed the gap in areas of AI hardware, notably around inference and by some accounts may only be a single development quarter [ https://substack.com/redirect/0f610ea7-d055-4dfc-aaf2-2c3d0daec8b3?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] behind the United States with a remaining holdup being software, not silicon. Alibaba, Tencent and Baidu say rewriting their large-model pipelines from Nvidia’s CUDA platform to Huawei’s CANN toolkit will delay new AI development by about three months, not years. After this, day-to-day AI workloads can run on homegrown chips instead of imported Nvidia parts. Huawei is laying that foundation with the Ascend AI processor [ https://substack.com/redirect/5b96bdbf-ef36-49f8-b798-289153fc5dcc?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. Domestic chipmaker, AMEC, is ramping up its local production [ https://substack.com/redirect/71fcfe89-d0e1-45a9-ad5b-918f1708501a?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. In practice, the companies will keep training on their dwindling Nvidia inventory, where US technology maintains a reliable lead, while shifting the fast-growing inference workload to Ascend processors and other local silicon.
Jensen Huang says China’s $50 billion AI-chip market is now “effectively closed”  [ https://substack.com/redirect/03340724-25cf-4f5c-ab9f-13686d914e54?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]to American vendors.
China is developing a new Made in China plan to focus on home-made high-end technological goods [ https://substack.com/redirect/3ace4873-efa4-4419-8690-9ad8946cd5fe?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].
Intrinsic motivation
AI models can now learn to reason better by simply trusting their own confidence –no human feedback or gold-standard answers required. A new study [ https://substack.com/redirect/dedebfdd-6924-43f8-8d4d-5641e3583ace?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] out this week introduced a method called Intuitor that rewards outputs the model feels most confident about, creating a self-reinforcing loop that encourages elaborate, structured thinking. The new method made it better at learning how to learn; the AI was only trained on math problems, yet the one that learned to trust its own judgment became significantly better at coding too.
Alongside this, a companion study shows an LLM inventing and iterating on its own  algorithms [ https://substack.com/redirect/e201e739-5ea5-49e6-9e24-98a5cd502a6d?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] and outperforming the best human-designed techniques on benchmarks. It transferred those gains to entirely new model combinations which is evidence of AI refining the very tools of its self-improvement.
These are two of several recent papers [ https://substack.com/redirect/cd6eb72d-7dc3-4e5f-9aa2-0fef01c911db?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] that show a path to self-improvement – there’s a clear pathway from here. This could offer powerful new capabilities but also require vigilance against unintended behaviors like reward hacking.
A VC reboot for Europe
The VC playbook – sprinkle dollars on lean software start-ups and let network effects do the compounding – no longer matches the geopolitical chessboard.
As John Thornhill notes [ https://substack.com/redirect/1c31af02-9d30-4f23-b88b-9355db67bfba?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], the cost curve has flipped: genAI breakthroughs depend on expensive data-centre build-outs and chip supply chains, so Big Tech and state-backed pools of capital (think Saudi Arabia’s $40 billion AI fund) are out-muscling traditional VCs. The median founding year of the companies that anchor Europe’s main equity index is 1892, versus 1946 in the US – a 54-year innovation deficit that exposes how rarely the continent refreshes its industrial leaders.
That gap shows a structural under-investment in the very scale-up pathways that turn research into geopolitical heft. Unless Europe (and, to a lesser extent, the US) rewires its VC ecosystem to blend mission-driven public funding with de-risked private money, today’s capital-intensive platforms will be captured by the few players – state-backed Gulf and north-east Asian funds, plus Big Tech cash piles – already treating venture as critical infrastructure.
This isn’t news for Europe. An EU study dating back to 2011  [ https://substack.com/redirect/a5724b64-acda-4de3-a113-041d86313d82?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]concluded that the continent’s VC-backed firms achieve higher productivity growth than their peers but remain hamstrung by fragmented markets and underdeveloped scale-up pathways. Plus ça change.
Professional autonomy is ebbing
There’s an uneven transition emerging at work – genAI disruption is less about wholesale job destruction as was previously imagined than about who designs the workflow and how quickly firms can monetise freed-up capacity. Quartz chronicles a new wave [ https://substack.com/redirect/7da6cbc7-1100-4947-975d-76eaaef832a8?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] of staff reductions that the companies themselves explicitly link to AI productivity gains: Microsoft (≈7,000 jobs, 3% of staff), Duolingo, Walmart and Accenture are among the biggest names. Unlike the 2023 tech-sector purge, these cuts reach deep into mid-career, non-tech roles and are often framed as reallocations of budget “toward AI” rather than cost-cutting.
Yet there’s another side to all this; one that plays in the moment-to-moment experience of work. The Skills & Employment Survey shows UK “task discretion” has plunged [ https://substack.com/redirect/73d755b6-d01b-4b5d-a33b-2e3b4c03a28a?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] from 62% in 1992 to just 34% in 2024, employees feel they have far less autonomy today than they did thirty years ago. Paradoxically, genAI could deepen that grip AND crack the door to bottom-up agency at the same time.
In many firms the earliest copilots were built by rank-and-file coders or CX reps long before IT issued a “single source of truth”; workers could re-design their own flow. Salesforce’s freeze on new hires [ https://substack.com/redirect/191a9523-2fbc-4993-a263-57705111e9e1?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] shows the knife-edge: internal copilots freed enough capacity to redeploy 500 service agents and avoid fresh hiring, yet executives now talk openly of standardising the way those tools are used, a move that could trade creative freedom for consistent metrics.
The AI coding race is on
Last week, I spoke with GitHub CEO Thomas Dohmke about the future of code, developer jobs and the shape of software in the next decade. He confirmed what many suspected but few wanted to say out loud: we’ve already crossed the threshold where AI generates code faster than humans can meaningfully review it. Human oversight to software now needs new tools.
