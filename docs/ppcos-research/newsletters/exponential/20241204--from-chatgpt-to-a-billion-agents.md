Towards billions of agents
By Nathan Warren  and daniel bashir
In the very near future, knowledge workers could be supported by thousands of AI agents, all operating in parallel. This is not speculative futurism. Nvidia’s Jensen Huang recently spoke about the role agents [ https://substack.com/redirect/cbc29e29-7759-4964-8ad4-1124c5c7bb19?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] already play at Nvidia and how he sees their future:
Brad: Are you already using chain-of-reasoning and tools like o1 in our own business to improve it?
Jensen: Absolutely. Our cybersecurity system today can’t run without our own agents. We have AI agents helping design chips—Hopper wouldn’t be possible, Blackwell wouldn’t be possible and don’t even think about Rubin. We have AI chip designers, AI software engineers and AI verification engineers, and we build them all internally. We have the ability, and we’d rather use the opportunity to explore the technology ourselves. I’m hoping that Nvidia someday will be a 50,000-employee company with 100 million AI assistants […] AIs will recruit other AIs to solve problems […] and so we’ll just be one large employee base, some of them digital and some biological.
Similarly, Sam Altman anticipates the emergence of a one-person unicorn [ https://substack.com/redirect/f0db3534-5318-435f-8339-f2c3ae77ef00?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]—a billion-dollar enterprise managed by a single individual leveraging an army of AI agents.
Today’s AI assistants, like ChatGPT, require constant human input—they’re co-pilots, not autonomous actors. The next evolution, already well underway, is of agents that execute tasks independently once given an objective, much like delegating to a seasoned team and stepping back while they handle the rest. And there is no limit to the size of this team.
To help you make sense of this, we teamed up with daniel bashir , ML engineer, writer and managing editor of The Gradient .
Together, we’ll explore three areas in today’s post:
The current state of AI agents and their real-world applications,
Why their widespread adoption is becoming inevitable,
How this transition could lead to a future with billions of agents augmenting human work.
Agents now
Agents have been top of mind for computer scientists for decades, but little progress was made until more recently. How far we’ve come is best captured in a quote from a 1997 paper [ https://substack.com/redirect/20c9cfcf-7ade-437f-a1e4-75f8a8203cd6?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] by computer scientists Christopher Welty and Louis Hoebel who then wrote
anyone knowledgeable about “agents” can not help but recognize that, as an artificial intelligence technology, there is typically very little actual intelligence involved. In some sense, Agents can be defined as small scale AI that works.
The ChatGPT era introduced systems based on LLMs that we currently think of as “AI agents”. Early prototypes like BabyAGI [ https://substack.com/redirect/5b3d44ff-d457-49f1-9b01-51ec45194934?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], for instance, showed that task planning could allow LLMs to act autonomously.
Example by Yohei Nakajima [ https://substack.com/redirect/1cc81dbf-a156-4c27-b6a2-893641be4a02?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]
The latest improvements have been captured in benchmarks (see the 2024 State of AI Report [ https://substack.com/redirect/d5d5ba82-799f-4210-9fd0-1b3d4be3bd25?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] to go deeper) and research evidencing that existing LLMs can be used to create agents that continuously learn in open environments (like Minecraft).
DeepMind released Gato [ https://substack.com/redirect/71831616-4eb4-4277-8cb4-f6a56ed2d8d9?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], a “generalist agent” that uses the same idea underlying LLMs to perform tasks from stacking blocks with a real robot arm to captioning images. Joon Park and colleagues proposed Generative Agents [ https://substack.com/redirect/82b55cd8-854f-4f4a-94c1-42c29691e251?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] as an interactive sandbox to be used primarily for social science. In the hard sciences, researchers at Stanford created a virtual lab which used agents to create 92 new nanobody designs [ https://substack.com/redirect/c3c63d79-b999-4f7e-8460-88c2b0e16523?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], including multiple nanobodies with successful binding activity against the virus that causes Covid-19 (as highlighted in EV#501 [ https://substack.com/redirect/ed76f2bf-6bc7-472a-a43d-3df7cedbdaa2?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]).
In the enterprise, Adept [ https://substack.com/redirect/e46f1b47-5ac7-49c5-9228-4571ce809efc?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] imagined a digital assistant that could turn a text command into a series of actions — like making a blueprint for a new car part — and raised $350 million [ https://substack.com/redirect/69be3e44-b498-4e2a-a813-8a8f48d2fcd8?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] to do so before being absorbed into Amazon [ https://substack.com/redirect/e355dcea-2160-4ce8-b7d3-4649258460b5?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].
In the consumer space, startups like Shortwave have developed [ https://substack.com/redirect/1779f2fa-53b5-47fa-a962-b180ef697bb1?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] agents that can play the role of executive assistants to manage and structure information in domains like email. Azeem has invested in two agent startups, WordWare [ https://substack.com/redirect/7d0d5ffd-f104-4bac-a05c-7f00a7ec2e94?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] and Twin [ https://substack.com/redirect/fc5ab581-bb51-41c0-bc36-08d6d65afcb1?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].
Meanwhile, Microsoft has been leveraging its strength in enterprise software to quietly establish a foothold in this area. Over 100,000 of their enterprise customers [ https://substack.com/redirect/d82e9839-8196-4060-b4d3-b31e8aebcd67?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] are already experimenting in the Copilot Studio, building custom autonomous agents or deploying out-of-the-box solutions.
Hiscox, an insurance underwriter, used agents to reduce the time to quote for complex risks from three days to a few minutes [ https://substack.com/redirect/fa7328e1-bc90-4375-b032-d4a1e1d94146?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. Similarly, McKinsey, using Microsoft’s agent ecosystem, has reduced its project intake workflow from 20 days to two days [ https://substack.com/redirect/d82e9839-8196-4060-b4d3-b31e8aebcd67?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].
The inevitability of agents
Over the past century, we’ve witnessed staggering productivity gains in industries like manufacturing and agriculture—factories churn out goods exponentially faster and farms feed billions with a fraction of the workforce they once needed. Yet, in classrooms, it still takes one teacher to educate 30 students. This is Baumol’s curse [ https://substack.com/redirect/9201ac80-1e50-45a9-adb9-804b6fb6fe7f?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. When wages rise in high-productivity sectors like manufacturing, labour-intensive services must increase wages to compete—even if their productivity remains stagnant. So, while manufactured goods become cheaper, many services grow more expensive.
At the heart of this issue is our inability to scale our own human time. As per Amdahl’s Law, a system’s speed is limited by its slowest component. In many parts of the service sector, this comes down to constraints around human-dependent processes. The Industrial Revolution overcame physical constraints through mechanization; AI could be a similar opportunity to transcend cognitive bottlenecks across…
Speed: AI systems operate far beyond human reaction times, processing data in milliseconds. The fastest LLM produces output at 6000x the speed a human can [ https://substack.com/redirect/1f133b89-a01a-47af-9e99-0fc6606f9431?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].
Scale: We can deploy as many AI agents as our computational resources allow—potentially exceeding the human population.
Parallelization: Tasks can be divided among thousands or even millions of AI agents. Rather than analysing 100 documents sequentially, 100 AI agents can process them simultaneously and merge their findings into one report.
Cost efficiency: Over time, agents become cheaper than human labour, especially when scaled. Right now we can get a proto-agentic system to perform a meta-analysis of 200 ArXiv papers for ~1% of the human cost. AlphaFold predicted 200 million protein structures, each of which would traditionally cost $100,000 [ https://substack.com/redirect/ca07b15e-10a3-40cd-964a-16686c0412f5?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] and an entire PhD to determine.
Personalization: Instead of splitting one human service among many, AI enables individualised experiences for everyone – a private tutor for you or your kid, for example.
Learning and adaptation: As independent researcher Gwern Branwen argues, “every sufficiently hard problem becomes a reinforcement-learning problem. [ https://substack.com/redirect/aebdd03a-a8f3-4c12-92ed-48b1de4e5a5e?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]” Every complex challenge requires making sequences of decisions under uncertainty where each choice affects future options and outcomes—which is exactly what reinforcement learning solves. With this, AI systems can run millions of parallel experiments, aggregate their learnings through weight-sharing and act on those insights in ways biological systems cannot.
Over the next year, deployment of agents will take a “Crawl, Walk, Run [ https://substack.com/redirect/7150ffa4-4bf9-432f-8aa1-9dec47a4040b?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]” approach. Businesses are experimenting with simple use cases, before expanding in complexity. Hence all the talk of customer-service agents, a relatively low-risk and easy implementation. But the complexity and the range of tasks an agent can handle will grow.
Towards billions of agents
To further think about an evolution we may start to see as soon as next year, let’s look at a profession everyone loves… lawyers.
According to a database maintained by the US Department of Labor [ https://substack.com/redirect/8da7f923-1c93-4300-b794-a57361f33f37?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], lawyers perform 22 distinct professional tasks. One of these core tasks is preparing legal briefs and opinions for court filings. Imagine a partner at a law firm assigning a complex appellate brief to what appears to be a single AI assistant—but is actually an orchestra of specialised agents, each with distinct “expertise”.
The process begins the moment case files are uploaded. A coordinator agent—an AI project manager, if you will—immediately parses the court’s requirements and filing deadlines. Within seconds, a research agent combs through legal databases with superhuman speed. It identifies all the relevant precedents and subtle patterns in judicial reasoning across similar cases. Simultaneously, a case-analysis agent examines the trial record, mapping case facts to legal elements and identifying promising arguments that human lawyers might overlook in thousands of pages of testimony.
As a writing agent crafts preliminary arguments in precise legal language, a citation-management agent ensures every reference adheres to Bluebook standards and validates that each cited case remains good law. A technical-compliance agent continuously monitors formatting, word counts and court rules in real-time, while a quality-control agent validates quotations and ensures logical consistency. The coordinator agent hires other agents, manages workflows and resolves conflicts.
And this is only for a single task…
The magic of the agentic system is that it can scale. You could have a dozen or more briefs being prepared in parallel. What begins with a few specialized agents handling a legal brief quickly cascades. Start with 1.3 million US lawyers [ https://substack.com/redirect/642a954f-7002-4ffd-a625-0df6881d3916?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], each deploying 5 specialized agents for each of the 22 tasks they do—that’s already 1 billion agents. That’s only for 1 million lawyers… There are an estimated 1 billion knowledge workers worldwide [ https://substack.com/redirect/8c0d0acc-13c2-4eac-af2b-cd2e1cb3842b?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. There will be billions of agents. And this is assuming 5 agents per task. But why not 5, 10, 100 agents? There is theoretically no limit other than what is effective.
But making organizations of lawyers more efficient isn’t the only thing agents will enable. As Flo Crivello, founder and CEO of Lindy, points out, when tools become exponentially cheaper, they go from being corporate assets to enabling individual creativity:
Just like AI agents, the internet was an extremely powerful democratizing force in media. If you consider YouTubers like MrBeast, who, when he got started, was a random ~17-year-old in his bedroom, with no connections, no professional equipment, no capital. Today his average episode has roughly the same viewership as the Super Bowl. But the real interesting part here is that if you look at YouTube-native content like his, it is much weirder than what existed before on TV, because it is the brainchild of just one person, and their ideas are allowed to express themselves in all their weirdness without going through all the filters that ideas have to go through when they are built inside the CNN, for example.
AI agents could fundamentally alter what’s possible for individual creators. Today, organizations often serve as risk-minimizing filters, with multiple layers of approvals needed for any significant initiative. But when individuals can harness the power of thousands of agents at minimal cost, we’ll encounter creation that was not possible before.
Who will manage the managers?
If we abstract from agents to “functions” or “tasks” or “roles,” many of the things we think of as singular — photography, computation and so on — are performed by many different components acting in concert. The A17 Pro chip in an iPhone 15 has 19 billion transistors; your iPhone camera is five different cameras. As we begin to think about agents, we need to re-examine the discrete unit that we consider an “action.” An “action” might go from sending an already-written email to writing an email to managing most email interactions with an organization.
But, in the agentic future, you won’t be dealing with every agent yourself. Technologists who build systems work hard to hide those systems’ complexity from us. When you post on Instagram, a single image replicates across millions of servers to potentially billions of devices. The user doesn't need to know anything about the underlying complexity of managing a distributed system. The whole system behaves as if it were one. If you’re in a conversation with an LLM too large to fit on a single GPU, engineers have to develop an inference system that behaves as though that LLM were on a single machine. All you see is the conversation you’re having.
We can imagine a similar pattern with agents: if you have a hundred people working for you and they perform entirely different functions, it’s unreasonable for you to manage and oversee everything. Imagine, then, an “AI chief-of-staff” who delegates and manages your personal agents as they go about their work. While many details won’t reach you in your daily interactions, the “important” information does. But details are available when you want them and you can collaborate with your chief-of-staff to understand how things are being done.
To work effectively with an AI infrastructure we all need to become managers. Just as executives must learn to delegate and verify work, interfacing with an AI chief-of-staff will demand clear judgment about tasks, goals and overall intelligent oversight. This isn’t about outsourcing our thinking; we’ll need to elevate how we manage our AI and our human coworkers.
Articulation of the agentic network won’t take away the need for expertise and understanding. Agents and their underlying systems will become better at understanding what we want and ask for over time, but financial portfolio managers will still need to have expertise in their domains to direct their AI chief-of-staff to properly handle things. When things go wrong, a manager working with a chief-of -staff should still know enough to interpret and understand what the problems are and why they’re happening.
In this essay, we wanted to lay out the theoretical case for agents. But there’s one giant hole in getting to this world: implementation. The practical challenges—from reliability to quality control systems to governance—remain to be solved. We’re going to cover this in a future essay.
