An invisible flood of productivity
Anthropic’s new study [ https://substack.com/redirect/adbc7d50-5b29-40ca-b29c-5816438d99f7?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] says that even if AI progress stopped today, existing tools could lift US labor productivity by about 1.8% a year for the next decade – a historically unprecedented pace compared with the post‑war boom or the dot‑com era. I agree with this sentiment – in fact, I think it’s probably been true since GPT-4. The tools are powerful but implementation is tougher [ https://substack.com/redirect/d4c6e58a-8fc9-4783-8085-f6578188df68?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]: companies need to go through wide-scale people-centric transformation processes to make best use of them. This takes time.
Anthropic’s projection matches what many economists expect [ https://substack.com/redirect/98ebec4a-fbe9-435b-ae0c-432b86be2c66?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. But we’re not seeing it yet: recent US productivity growth is around 1.5%, roughly the historical average.
Although visible AI use is clustered in tech hubs and roles like software development, much larger “submerged” exposure exists across admin, finance, and professional services and makes up 11.7% of wage value, five times higher than the visible tech sector (see MIT’s Project Iceberg [ https://substack.com/redirect/20de63ac-3d50-4891-97ab-c850116299f6?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] for details on this research). For this reason, the biggest impacts will emerge outside classic tech centers.
So far, the impact isn’t showing up clearly in the broad stats – and it’ll matter that it does in 2026 [ https://substack.com/redirect/4b7f4438-0a37-43a8-9786-315d03cc5db6?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].

Funding quality is continuing to show signs of strain. A new survey reveals only 11% of data center investors target 10-year-plus holds [ https://substack.com/redirect/011856aa-fb4f-40cf-99bb-32393ccc7ef5?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], yet they are financing data centers that require decades to return capital.
The new Scientific Revolution
In last week’s newsletter [ https://substack.com/redirect/9571f705-54b5-4fc6-8dfc-b97cb7a2ff6e?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], we talked about how autonomous research agents can cut through sociological barriers that have held back disruptive science. We wrote:
The incentive architecture of modern science has stifled it. A landmark 2023 analysis in Nature of 45 million papers and 3.9 million patents found a marked, universal decline in disruptive breakthroughs [ https://substack.com/redirect/ea41a723-cbfd-45e1-bccd-5d6374db9975?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] across all fields over the past six decades. […] Funding mechanisms inherently bias against novelty [ https://substack.com/redirect/82ce9996-85f3-4fdc-91b5-b7a9121ba96e?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], prioritizing feasibility and consensus over potential breakthroughs […] Scientific progress is literally dependent on failed experiments and we’ve conspired to create a system that punishes those failures.
Perhaps the US government was listening, as it has just announced the Genesis Mission [ https://substack.com/redirect/00328e18-18cb-4069-a4b1-d1ffe8e440ba?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], a project to double America’s scientific productivity within the next decade using AI. The executive order calls for using Federal scientific datasets “to train scientific foundation models and create AI agents to test new hypotheses, automate research workflows, and accelerate scientific breakthroughs”.
Science has already benefited from AI. Perhaps the lodestar so far is DeepMind’s AlphaFold 2, now five years old [ https://substack.com/redirect/867b5450-e8d6-4042-8359-eeb39bccf071?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. It has been cited 40,000 times and, unlike other papers in biomedical fields, its citation rate continues to rise.
As a partner, GPT‑5 helped immunologists [ https://substack.com/redirect/e2862170-d5a7-4be5-b598-7e7272506b79?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] pinpoint a hidden mechanism in human immune cells from an unpublished chart and suggested the wet‑lab experiment that proved it; it also offered the key idea that let mathematicians finish a decades‑old Erdős‑style proof. Future House’s Robin platform [ https://substack.com/redirect/cf4e1036-d3d6-4f00-b992-44156bbeb575?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] hypothesized a new effective treatment for age-related macular degeneration by synthesizing data from more than 400 papers. And MIT created an autonomous pipeline for polymer creation [ https://substack.com/redirect/fc094b59-9b00-433f-9237-f6db67fa0e25?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] that generates recipes and uses robots to test them. These systems massively increase the throughput of testing possibilities. And we’re only at the beginning.

Bargaining chips
For years, Nvidia’s GPUs have been the default engine for both training and deploying large models. Reports now suggest that Meta is in talks to use Google’s [ https://substack.com/redirect/f7d6c181-0eb6-4b64-a6ce-75ebd35fc7d9?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] custom chips, TPUs, instead. That alone signals a change in the infrastructure layer and raises a question: should Nvidia be nervous?
Yes and no.
Google has been using TPUs since 2015 to power the neural networks needed for search and, more recently, to train and run its Gemini models [ https://substack.com/redirect/698f00ea-52a4-4483-ba5e-90891a0e9a46?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. But something must have changed in the calculus for (a) Google to want to sell its TPUs to a competitor and (b) Meta to want to buy TPUs rather than GPUs.
Let’s start with (b) first, it’s simpler. The main question model makers are asking has moved from “Can I buy the best chips to build the best models?” to “Can I buy chips that can affordably deliver these models?” As I said in my analysis this week [ https://substack.com/redirect/e0ee57ac-c644-412c-ac8c-d51192e9c8dc?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]:
If token demand continues compounding faster than hardware capacity, with a gap of perhaps two to five percentage points a month, inference’s share of compute will keep rising. On those numbers, a lab with ~30% of its compute on inference today would see that share rise toward ~50% within about two years and ~70% within about four, squeezing the budget for new frontier training runs. At some point, there simply isn’t enough left over for frontier training runs.
TPUs are excellent chips for inference and Google claims to offer comparable performance per $ to GPUs (UncoverAlpha  has a good breakdown [ https://substack.com/redirect/105faca1-6e71-4fe2-be3d-5dabb3c4953a?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]). These chips will help supply inference capacity for Meta, while Nvidia GPUs can be saved for training.
But Google’s motivation is less obvious. One likely reason is that it still buys Nvidia chips, which carry a 75% profit margin [ https://substack.com/redirect/a84acac0-4143-4f5a-b8f6-65b9a2b3315a?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. By offering TPUs as an alternative, Google reduces Nvidia’s pricing power, something that also benefits Meta. This isn’t existential for Nvidia: it holds roughly 90% share in a supply‑constrained market and reports an order backlog around $500 billion [ https://substack.com/redirect/fe68c7c0-c289-4ef9-85b1-039b302edecd?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. Still, the rise of credible alternatives is worth watching.
