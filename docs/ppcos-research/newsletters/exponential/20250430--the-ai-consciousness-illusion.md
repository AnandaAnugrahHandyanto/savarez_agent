The dream of sentient machines
By Anil Seth
Director, Sussex Centre for Consciousness Science
University of Sussex
Ever since humanity began telling stories, we’ve been enthralled by the possibility of bringing inanimate things to life. Golems, automata, robots… the fascination is timeless. Now, our technologies can carry on conversations so smoothly that in some cases it can be difficult to resist the feeling of being in the presence of a real, conscious mind. For some of the rapidly improving AI language models, it’s no surprise people wonder, “Is there a ‘there’ there?”
Kevin Roose ’s article [ https://substack.com/redirect/1f8113fa-1c85-49d7-bd4a-6aa62dfd095e?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] in the New York Times on Anthropic’s “AI welfare” research is just one recent signal that this is no longer a purely academic question.
From my perspective, we have to distinguish carefully between an AI that behaves intelligently or even empathically and an AI that experiences anything at all. In my work – including my book [ https://substack.com/redirect/38be5aad-e89f-41ea-b4a2-f495985638a9?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] Being You and a forthcoming [ https://substack.com/redirect/76f7dd90-5059-4110-bf23-aaed654b3181?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] article in Behavioral and Brain Sciences – I stress that intelligence alone doesn’t amount to consciousness. An algorithm can solve problems or produce human-sounding dialogue without any felt awareness behind it. Anthropic’s researcher Kyle Fish, however, has a different view, as Roose wrote:
It seems to me that if you find yourself in the situation of bringing some new class of being into existence that is able to communicate and relate and reason and problem-solve and plan in ways that we previously associated solely with conscious beings, then it seems quite prudent to at least be asking questions about whether that system might have its own kinds of experiences.
Put differently, language in, language out – even if very sophisticated – doesn’t necessarily yield, or indicate, a stream of subjective experience.
My skepticism about near-term conscious AI comes partly from neuroscience and biology. In my view, consciousness in biological organisms is likely to be deeply tied to the fact that we’re alive. We have bodies that must be fed and protected; we regulate our internal states through homeostatic processes; we evolved to respond to pain and pleasure for survival. Every neuron, every cell in our body, is a tiny metabolic furnace, continually regenerating the conditions for its own continued existence, as well as ensuring the integrity of the entire organism. Through these deeply embodied mechanisms, the brain generates a constantly updated sense of “what it is like to be me.”
AI, by contrast, is fundamentally a pattern-recognition and generation system, running on silicon hardware. Whether you call it ‘computational functionalism’ or something else, there’s no solid proof that the kinds of operations AIs perform – statistical predictions, or indeed computations of any kind – will produce felt sensations. As I argue [ https://substack.com/redirect/76f7dd90-5059-4110-bf23-aaed654b3181?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] in my paper, the idea that computation is sufficient for subjective experience likely reflects an overextension of the metaphor of the ‘brain as a computer’, rather than an insight into the nature of consciousness itself.
I’m not alone in thinking this way. Philosopher Peter Godfrey-Smith, to take one example, argues [ https://substack.com/redirect/a87da43c-eaa8-4701-855c-c8da77daef30?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] that consciousness probably emerged through the rich interplay of bodies and electrochemical nervous systems in natural environments. If that’s the case, a large language model could keep advancing in its abilities, yet remain devoid of any subjective spark. Computation of any kind just wouldn’t be up to the job.
But what if we’re missing something?
Another perspective would be that, once you reach sufficient complexity in an AI system, consciousness “switches on.”
I don’t have any reason to believe this is happening with the models that we have now, like those behind Claude, ChatGPT and Gemini — but the possibility cannot be definitively ruled out.
In 2023, a group of researchers [ https://substack.com/redirect/222e8e8b-4bc8-4de2-ab0f-f810f1eaec34?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], led by Patrick Butlin and Robert Long, examined a range of existing AI models, looking for what they called ‘indicator properties’ of consciousness. These indicator properties were derived from current neuroscientific theories of consciousness, reflecting things like ‘recurrent processing’ and ‘global information broadcast’. The strategy they argued for was to look inside AI networks (using mechanistic interpretability tools) for features predicted by theories of consciousness, rather than being fooled by their outward behavior.
This is a good idea, but - crucially - their approach still assumes that computation of some kind is sufficient for consciousness. And even if you do make this assumption, the researchers still concluded that no current AI systems are conscious. But they also suggested that future AIs could display all the necessary indicator properties.
In the absence of a rigorous and empirically established theory of consciousness, we will not be able to know for sure whether an AI is - or is not - conscious. The best we can do is make informed best guesses.
It’s worth noting that this challenge isn’t specific to AI. We face similar conundrums when trying to decide whether non-human animals are conscious, or human beings with severe brain damage, or ‘cerebral organoids’. In each case, we have to judge what the relevant similarities and differences are, from the benchmark of a conscious adult human being. My colleagues and I have been exploring strategies for doing this in a recent paper [ https://substack.com/redirect/8e345058-274d-428f-a7cc-3bab9ede18f9?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] in Trends in Cognitive Sciences.
There is an emerging consensus among AI researchers, neuroscientists and philosophers that real evidence for AI must come from careful, theoretically-guided empirical investigation, rather than from anecdotal impressions. And, equally importantly, that in the absence of the holy grail of a full scientific explanation of consciousness, the best we can do is shift our credences, rather than make definitive statements.
Illusions of mind
So why do people like Kyle Fish or Blake Lemoine (the Google engineer who, way back in 2022, claimed [ https://substack.com/redirect/b9002b84-828e-4b48-903d-a9d475c8e3d5?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] a chatbot was sentient) seem so convinced otherwise?
It stems from our baked-in psychological biases. We humans are wired to anthropomorphize, especially when language is involved. Once a system starts talking in first-person language, discussing its “feelings,” or “desires,” it’s extraordinarily tempting to assume an inner life is happening.
It’s a bit like watching a master puppeteer. The puppet’s well-synchronized movements and emotional dialogue make us forget there’s a person behind the curtain pulling the strings. With AI language models, we don’t even see the “puppeteer,” just a sophisticated black box generating persuasive sentences. We supply the rest with our imaginations, our predisposition to “see” minds in social interactions.
Even if ‘real’ artificial consciousness remains remote or impossible, AI systems that persuasively seem to be conscious pose significant risks to humans. Machines that convincingly simulate sentience, emotion, or understanding - whether by design or by accident - could exploit our innate social and empathetic responses. We might find our moral priorities distorted, our emotional vulnerabilities manipulated, or our own minds subtly brutalised (as explored in fiction like Westworld). Overcoming these powerful AI-driven illusions of consciousness may prove exceptionally difficult.
One possible consequence of all this is that we might divert our attention from very real, very human issues. We could even wind up championing “AI rights” while neglecting pressing human or animal suffering in the real world.
In my view, the crux is our own susceptibility to illusions – how quickly we project consciousness into things that speak to us in the ‘right’ way, how readily we treat algorithms as if they have human-like minds. The question here is not so much about whether AI is conscious, but about why we so readily believe it could be. We’re the ones vulnerable to manipulation, whether that’s emotional manipulation by an apparently empathic chatbot or social manipulation by a disinformation-generating AI. Our moral priorities can get scrambled when we start seeing personhood where it doesn’t exist.
Still, there’s another side to the coin. Consciousness might be more widespread in nature than many believe, and the possibility of AI sentience isn’t something to dismiss outright. Philosopher Thomas Metzinger argues [ https://substack.com/redirect/6687e733-229c-44b9-9335-3b9ede9ec9eb?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] that the emergence of real artificial consciousness would be so ethically disastrous that there should be a moratorium on research risking its creation. He focuses on “negative synthetic phenomenology”, referring to the potential suffering AI systems could endure if they ever develop consciousness. My take [ https://substack.com/redirect/f0fe28ff-b040-48e8-9073-4d461c991a76?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] on this is that the goal of creating ‘conscious AI’ is indeed a very bad idea, in part because - if suffering AIs do emerge - we might be blind to their plight, since their inner pain might be of a kind we might not even recognise.
We need rigorous science and philosophy guiding us, not gut feelings about what “sounds human.” If a large language model, or any other AI system, ever did show genuine markers of consciousness, whatever these might be, that would be a momentous development – one which would demand a whole new ethical framework for AI welfare. But we’re not there yet, and I suspect we won’t be for a good while - if ever.
