Organisational innovation
Despite a year of pilot projects and enthusiastic rhetoric, many large firms remain stuck in incremental territory. Instead of using AI to imagine radically new workflows, they’re automating legacy tasks. This is akin to using electric light merely to extend working hours rather than rethinking the entire assembly line.
With AI agents and co-pilots, any employee can draw on a level of intellectual leverage previously reserved for domain experts or high-level consultants. If taken seriously, this can compress cycle times, broaden the internal innovation pipeline, and redistribute authority within organisations. Yet, firms cling to traditional ROI metrics and cost-cutting mindsets. This is a huge missed opportunity.
In 2025, the leaders will not be the companies that squeezed out 5% cost savings. They will be the ones that reconfigured themselves to exploit a near-infinite supply of cognitive capacity, testing new offerings and processes at scale and speed. Instead of “Where can we shave off costs?” the question becomes “What does it mean to have a multiplier effect?”
The agentic shift
The goal of AI agents is a qualitative shift in capacity [ https://substack.com/redirect/a27ec2ee-3f3a-4137-b26d-7aa6672cfcc4?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. A teacher might summon a custom-tailored educational game, tested by simulated student avatars, all orchestrated by an AI “manager.” A startup founder could spin up an entire product validation pipeline overnight.
This will force us to revisit notions of organisational structure, as we recently noted in an essay [ https://substack.com/redirect/a27ec2ee-3f3a-4137-b26d-7aa6672cfcc4?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. Historically, complexity in large companies has been manageable only by layering on more managers and overhead. AI agents hint that we might invert this model—scaling complexity while remaining lean. If this approach scales, then the very meaning of competitive advantage changes. Put simply, if everyone can cheaply marshal sophisticated agent teams, where is the defensibility? There’s a dark counterpart, too: if these agents proliferate unchecked, organisational coherence or cultural values could be hollowed out. Once you blur the line between employee and AI agent, how do you ensure the ethos of your firm endures?
Looking to 2025
Ethan and I agree that we must confront a persistent tension between what’s technologically possible and what humans and organisations can realistically absorb. There are abundant, unsettled questions.
If 2023 and 2024 were years of astonishment—where generative AI stopped feeling like a party trick and became a workhorse—then 2025 may be when the real grappling begins. Firms that treat AI as an IT project or a headcount-reduction strategy will find themselves outpaced by those that consider it an intelligence supply chain.
This is no comfortable equilibrium. It will feel chaotic, uncertain, and at times existentially challenging. But the winners will be those who do more than adopt AI. They will rethink assumptions about what’s scarce, what’s possible and what it means to run a business or institution when infinite cognition is at your fingertips.
