It’s the end of AI as we know it, and I feel fine
AI business adoption is down [ https://substack.com/redirect/0f40a6a4-59d5-45ca-9547-0b61f47cc68d?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. Or is it?
A few weeks ago, the mainstream swooned over a study claiming that 95% of enterprise AI pilots [ https://substack.com/redirect/06bc50c3-0ea9-48ad-a728-156d4355d6c8?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] fail to impact P&L. As we pointed out [ https://substack.com/redirect/86c01de1-4ed1-4fcf-8e18-06816a0c5e5a?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], the methodology was deeply flawed. A similar misreading lurks in the latest US Census Bureau data. At first glance, the survey seems to show that only 12% of large US firms report using AI and that the use is on the decline. But once you break the data down by sector, as Paul Kedrosky  has done, most industries show rising adoption [ https://substack.com/redirect/52110576-77d6-4f94-a918-582344f949c5?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] (with jagged swings more likely due to sampling error than real-world shifts).
The deeper problem is definitional. The survey footnote lists “machine learning, natural language processing, predictive analytics, image processing, data analytics…” as forms of AI. If you believe only 12% of large companies do any data analytics, you’ll see why I’m sceptical. Misleading numbers like these skew the narrative and fuel headlines about an “AI bubble.” It is the question I hear most often after labour-market fears (funny how countervailing those concerns can be). Next week, we’ll have a major analysis on the AI bubble question. Stay tuned.

Smol continues to be beautiful
In my annual outlook [ https://substack.com/redirect/85bfc0a9-a8ce-4811-a187-52c20d098000?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] in January 2024, I forecasted:
Smaller models will be in demand: they are cheaper to run and can operate on a wider array of environments than mega-models. For many organisations, smaller models will be easier to use.
We are on trend. Since my 2024 prediction, small models have kept pushing the boundaries. K2-Think [ https://substack.com/redirect/21e3e250-2522-4d5c-a88f-055e66299420?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], a 32B parameter model, now matches models that are 4-10x larger on complex reasoning tasks. On the right hardware, it runs 10x faster than GPT-5. For agentic applications, where models interact with each other, speed matters more than size.
The Economist ran a piece this week with a dramatic title – Faith in god-like large language models is waning [ https://substack.com/redirect/f0b6ed46-ba9d-4427-b356-2f892052baf1?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] – noting that businesses are shifting to smaller, specialised systems for efficiency and return on investment.
This, however, doesn’t mean the giants are finished. Large models are still pushing the ceiling. When the user’s priority is insight and imagination rather than quick chat responses, bigger models are often better. Andrej Karpathy [ https://substack.com/redirect/05306b43-c4a5-4941-9a62-ddafe05b5661?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] pointed this out as well:
But most people’s questions, and most business processes, do not require those ceiling capabilities. Most of the work will be handled by smaller models. Which is why smol will stay beautiful.
A puff of authority
In the week when Albania’s PM launched an AI system ‘responsible’ [ https://substack.com/redirect/273f5f50-f089-4df2-a506-4e7a002664d7?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] for public procurement and ridding the legal tenders of corruption, I came across this tweet [ https://substack.com/redirect/0cd53ad9-5459-40d6-a494-494af62e3106?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] by Turkish-American economist and political scientist Timur Kuran who claims that
Iranian Muslims are now using AI to get answers to religious questions, bypassing Shia scholars. So ayatollahs are now debating whether AI can substitute for religious scholars. Surprisingly, they agree that seeking guidance from LLMs is permitted under certain conditions. The most thorny religious questions should never be turned over to AI, they say. But on settled issues AI can give excellent guidance, doing as well as the marjaʿ (trained Shia scholars).
Many clerics fully adopted the telephone and the internet to help laypeople access religious knowledge.
But LLMs are different, University of Denver legal scholar, Zahra Takhshid, argues in this paper [ https://substack.com/redirect/52606486-b10e-4e81-afc1-c20c5c82611e?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] on the state of debate. So much so that the religious communities are asking whether “LLMs [can] replace the marjaʿ? Or is there an essential attribute of the marjaʿ [...] that distinguishes it from a machine-generated response?”
In the late 19th century, Iran’s ruler handed a British company exclusive control over the country’s tobacco trade. For farmers, merchants and smokers, this symbolized foreign domination. As a response, Ayatollah Mirza Shirazi issued a ruling that tobacco was forbidden. Within weeks, a nation of smokers was abstinent. Takhshid believes that an LLM couldn’t have made such a decision:
[It] required an understanding of social dynamics, historical context, and communal sensitivities – factors that exceed the capacities of current AI models. [...] More importantly it is unlikely that a muqallid would have ever cared to follow such ruling generated by a computational system, absent the personal attributes and the cultivated trust of the muqallid in their marājiʿ.
Albania – and countries that follow – will face a similar reckoning. What will be the glue between society and the AI systems making seemingly “perfect” decisions on the government’s behalf? Will the people (who inevitably remain corruptible) be willing to follow?
