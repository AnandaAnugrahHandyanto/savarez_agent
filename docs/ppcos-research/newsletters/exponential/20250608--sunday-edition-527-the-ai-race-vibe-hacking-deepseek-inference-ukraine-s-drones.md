Playing to win
Future competition in AI won’t be decided by innovation alone, but by the ability to shift and absorb constraints, especially across compute, energy, talent, and what Nicklas Berild Lundblad  calls “absorption capacity.” Nvidia’s Blackwell chips, which halve AI training time [ https://substack.com/redirect/53c10ca5-34b1-4e85-9996-2dd5a6ef19c8?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], are being snapped up by U.S. hyperscalers – over 3.5 million orders to date – offering early movers a critical edge in velocity. Velocity is the core advantage in AI but it depends on controlling both compute and power. That’s why companies like Meta, Microsoft and Google are locking up long-term power deals with nuclear facilities, taking entire gigawatt-scale plants off-grid to secure reliable, emissions-free energy for AI workloads. Meta’s 20-year contract for the full output of Illinois’ Clinton reactor [ https://substack.com/redirect/91906290-1930-4279-a01a-8c4bb6c8b924?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] is taking an operating reactor fully off the public grid, the first such deal of its kind. Europe, on the other hand, is currently playing a losing game. I wrote about the consequences of this [ https://substack.com/redirect/8f6191ec-e157-4a43-bf26-2c0f1a582e5a?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] yesterday:
If Europe fails to build an energy system fit for an exponential economy, it will quietly deindustrialise by default, even while celebrating its climate goals on paper.

Asymmetry, asymmetry everywhere
This week, the exponential transformation of warfare came into stark relief. Ukraine’s “Spider’s Web” operation, deploying 117 cheap, commercially available drones to disable one-third of Russia’s strategic bomber fleet, delivered a $7 billion shock to the nuclear status quo. Simultaneously, we’re seeing cyberweapons evolve at similar velocity – call it vibe hacking.
Tools like XBOW – AI-powered penetration testers – can now breach most web apps autonomously [ https://substack.com/redirect/766e01cb-bc59-4ff1-bd6d-e92907e2e3e3?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], shrinking exploit cycles from months to hours. In this environment, power no longer rests with the most resourced actor, but with the one best adapted to high-speed, software-first conflict. Cloudflare’s Matthew Prince told me that defenders still hold the advantage, for now. They operate across broad attack surfaces with rich telemetry. But the edge is shrinking.
China’s AI breakthrough in nuclear verification [ https://substack.com/redirect/3549727d-18f3-4774-a259-e95a1829fee3?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] and Anduril’s $30.5 billion valuation [ https://substack.com/redirect/d1994c71-a357-4054-bbe9-0a2ae8e6b0eb?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] show that defense is shifting from steel to silicon. The smart actors will respond not by building more bunkers, but by accelerating zero-trust infrastructure, red-teaming with AI, and owning the stack from code to counter-drone protocols. The battlefield is converging: drones in the sky, agents in the wire, and the most decisive weapon may be how fast you can learn.

The history of freedom
What does it mean to be free? In her new book The Age of Choice, historian Sophia Rosenfeld shows how freedom used to mean something quite different (read a review here [ https://substack.com/redirect/54e182ed-d0db-46d7-b089-e44ab921cb78?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]). For centuries, it was bound up with duty, virtue, and acting for the common good. But over time, thanks to Enlightenment individualism, Protestant ideas of personal conscience, and the rise of consumer culture, freedom was redefined as the ability to choose. From dance cards to department stores to voting booths, the act of picking became the symbol of liberation.
Rosenfeld argues this shift wasn’t inevitable – and it’s not without consequences. She believes that when freedom gets flattened into personal choice, we stop asking what’s actually worth choosing. Her call is to re-moralize freedom – not to return to rigid moral orders, but to reconnect choice with ethical ends. A worthwhile call as we must rethink human identity and our raison d’être.
