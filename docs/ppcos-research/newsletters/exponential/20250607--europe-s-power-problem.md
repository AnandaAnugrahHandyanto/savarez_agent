# 🔮 Europe‘s power problem

**From:** "Azeem Azhar, Exponential View" <exponentialview@substack.com>
**Date:** 2025-06-07T08:12:14.000Z
**Folder:** exponential

---

View this post on the web at https://www.exponentialview.co/p/europe-is-losing-unless

In 1878, the Avenue de l’Opéra in Paris became one of the first major urban streets to be illuminated by electric arc lighting. The transformation was visually striking and socially disorienting – and would forever change how Parisians move through their city.
Other European capitals took note. Germany, especially under Bismarck’s leadership, was in a period of rapid industrialisation and keen to avoid falling behind. Within a year, Berlin had its first experimental electric street lighting. Within four years, Berlin had operational power stations supplying electricity for streetlights and tramways. And within few more years, Berlin launched one of the first long-distance transmission lines, which became a global benchmark.
Today’s equivalent “streetlamp” is the GPU rack. Just as lighting was once the signature use-case that pulled electricity into factories and cities, AI is now the load that reshapes grids, economies and infrastructure priorities.
And Europe is falling behind. I spent a few hours with executives involved in the European electricity sector earlier this week. I was shocked by the lack of urgency. It’s railway planners in the age of jet engines – still optimising track gauges while the skies fill with air traffic.
After the conversations I had this week, I felt Europe might just cede the next stage of industrialisation – the exponential economy – to those building faster and more abundant power infrastructure elsewhere. Decarbonisation, not abundant energy, was the mantra. Both, of course, are possible over time.
Generative models are multiplying, heat pumps are displacing boilers, electric cars outnumber diesels on many dealership forecourts. Yet Europe’s wires still carry roughly the same amount of electricity they did a decade ago. That tension is about to snap.
Negawatts won’t get us far
For twenty years, Brussels preached “negawatts”: use less, waste less, decarbonize through efficiency. It worked; until everything that is valuable for economies over the next half-century started plugging in.
In transport, one in four new cars sold across Europe this spring carried a battery rather than a fuel tank; plugins now command 26% of the market [ https://substack.com/redirect/7e136c9a-e129-4691-b89b-4e811938cd98?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] and are still climbing. Heating is following: more than 2 million heat pumps were installed last year, despite a market slowdown [ https://substack.com/redirect/73a637d9-dee9-420e-993c-44f652c157d7?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], taking the cumulative stock past 26 million units.
Then comes artificial intelligence. Global data-centre demand will more than double to 945 TWh by 2030, driven largely by AI workloads. Goldman Sachs reckons [ https://substack.com/redirect/a54b7ac1-5abc-4a4b-8332-faadf89e9e62?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] that these loads could increase European power demands by 10-15% over the next decade.
Add those curves together and continental system operators are modelling annual power-demand growth of up to 7% through 2030, the steepest increase since post-war reconstruction [ https://substack.com/redirect/e0916ea3-8517-4683-a92c-c1e4526d78a3?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].
Compute will cross borders
Europe’s energy story has to switch from efficiency to growth. Fast. If Europe wants European values to have a place on the world stage, it’s vital to stay in the game.
The recent US-UAE deal green-lit a 5 GW AI campus in Abu Dhabi and access to 500,000 Nvidia chips per year, all within 100 milliseconds round-trip latency of most European capitals. Compute can cross borders far more easily than electrons can.
Likewise, Saudi Arabia’s $100 billion Project Transcendence and HUMAIN’s 500-megawatt AI factories [ https://substack.com/redirect/214bd1e0-80e9-4823-9ff5-05d86ae486b6?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] aren’t just about chips – they’re about a dedicated 4-gigawatt renewable energy system and the world’s largest green hydrogen plant producing 600 tonnes per day. These projects guarantee the cheap, abundant, clean and controllable power that AI requires.
In this context, these deals are best understood not as tech partnerships but as energy pacts disguised as AI investments. They are preemptive moves in the geopolitical energy-compute race.
Europe’s “build here, run here” digital sovereignty dream collides with infrastructure that cannot keep pace. The risk is a hollowing out of the value chain: inference clusters and the startups they spawn, will follow cheap, abundant power elsewhere.
Triple shock therapy
Think of Europe’s predicament as a triangle whose sides are:
Electrification of end-uses (EVs, heat pumps, industrial heat).
Decarbonisation mandates (the phase-out of coal and gas).
AI compute growth (training and inference clusters).
If any vertex lags, the forces simply push harder on the others. If grids fail to expand, AI and industrial investment flee to where they can plug in. If clean power lags, decarbonisation targets become unaffordable or politically toxic. If compute demand is ignored, Europe’s digital economy becomes dependent on external infrastructure and rented intelligence running on someone else’s electricity.
And the stakes are broader than AI. Chip fabs, electrolyzer plants, gigafactories – all high-value, strategic industries – follow power. If Europe fails to build an energy system fit for an exponential economy, it will quietly deindustrialise by default, even while celebrating its climate goals on paper.
European nations must put electricity growth at the centre of their industrial strategy. Without abundant, reliable power, everything else stalls. Ambitions around AI, decarbonisation and digital sovereignty will collapse under grid constraints. Unless governments clear the path for investment and expansion now, Europe risks opting out of the exponential economy, by default, not by design.
Industrial atrophy will follow. High-energy processes (steel, chemicals, semiconductors) migrate to jurisdictions that guarantee 24/7 clean power at predictable prices, eroding the continent’s industrial tax base.
The Middle East will become a primary compute zone. Cheap desert solar plus sovereign capital create a third pole between Silicon Valley and Shenzhen – a safe harbour for AI clusters that European firms can rent by the petaflop.
And Europe will come in fourth.
Voilà!
