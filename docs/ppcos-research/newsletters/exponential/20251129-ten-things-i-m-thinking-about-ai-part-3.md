Part 3: The economic engine
We’re all familiar with the increasing investment demands being made by AI companies, which are, for now, being met by the capital markets. The numbers are starting to look big, with some leaks suggesting that OpenAI needs $115 billion to get profitable.
Back in 2016, Oxford Economics estimated that the world would need $94 trillion in investment in infrastructure [ https://substack.com/redirect/ec1b44c8-0738-4fc6-9cb7-2e8be522fd05?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] by 2040, a rate of $4 trillion per annum. This was before ChatGPT, but it puts into context how large infrastructure requirements are globally.
It’s true that the capex investments made so far and promised tomorrow are way ahead of demonstrated returns. But bringing computing capacity online is not like ordering a pizza from DoorDash. They are big construction projects filled with technologies from dozens of vendors that need to connect to increasingly contended sources of electricity. If you order a 500MW data center today, it’ll be 18-24 months before you’re serving a model.
With these lead times, tech companies have to invest against where they think the market will be, not where it is today. Their plans for 2022 and 2023 fell woefully short, so are they overdoing it now?
It’s impossible to tell. If the demand is real and their forecasts are accurate, their spending would look much like it does today. If the demand isn’t real and they’ve lost their marbles, their spending would look… much like it does today.
5. Capital markets struggle with exponentials
The fundamental dynamic at play is about the nature of things modern capital markets are designed to fund. General-purpose technologies are not one of them. AI is just such a technology.
Its benefits accrue diffusely across the economy, with long lags and large spillovers. [ https://substack.com/redirect/7009e435-1202-4b21-acf5-1990bdb78319?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] It is even more complex than a typical general-purpose technology because it also changes how we invent [ https://substack.com/redirect/eed36a32-999d-4e54-9d0a-06d869839c2c?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], it raises productivity in the production of ideas, not just goods and services.
Those two characteristics skew the calculus. The economics of general-purpose technologies are hard enough. As a new method of invention [ https://substack.com/redirect/f3f7e2f2-955e-4125-a7e6-0b463b8b5e25?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], AI will lower the cost of exploring the design space, change who can invent and shift the distribution of returns toward second- and third-order effects. But the inventor may not hold the surplus [ https://substack.com/redirect/034e1df9-b953-4fb2-8fc5-527983093cac?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].
Think of how much of the internet’s value accrued to users and downstream firms, not to those who laid the first fiber. Early 2000s ISP Qwest may have encouraged us to “ride the light” but it didn’t find profits.
Profits of the internal combustion engine took a different shape. While car companies captured most of the profits, oil companies and finance businesses were not far behind and both of those enjoyed better margins than auto marques.
For capital markets, this uncertainty isn’t just about who might win in a well-defined game; it’s about the type of game that’s being played. Markets use tools that assume relatively stable competitive structures and roughly linear growth in order to price company-level cashflows over three- to ten-year horizons.
Where will profits accumulate in the AI stack? With the chip companies, the cloud companies, with distinct model providers, and application vendors?
Complicating this, most of the value will show up as firm-level transformation, better products in other sectors, faster diffusion of ideas and lower research and development costs across the economy. Very little of that maps cleanly to “this specific GPU cluster produces this specific revenue stream.”
Capital markets need identifiable projects with appropriate cash flows, not a bundle of spillovers and system-level option value. AI can raise long-run GDP; investments in individual projects may miss because the claim on those gains is too indirect.
So capital markets are “struggling” not because investors are foolish or complacent but because they are being asked to price a system-level productivity shock funded by balance sheets already near their capex tolerance, with payoffs that are skewed, path-dependent and heavily mediated by policy and infrastructure.
It’s another expression of the “exponential gap” I described in my first book [ https://substack.com/redirect/843f398a-06bc-4923-b1a4-c7cbe8c803f3?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], when exponential AI collides with 20th-century tools for pricing risk and allocating capital. The rational response, in that context, is exactly what you see: extreme dispersion, Nvidia and a handful of hyperscalers absorbing almost all the AI market cap [ https://substack.com/redirect/ef652034-8ccc-4be3-8a40-495b789bcfbd?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], persistent boom-bust anxiety [ https://substack.com/redirect/b1731c81-38f8-4ec5-8b92-c2b227cd1812?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] and a chronic undersupply of truly long-duration, system-level capital.
6. Perhaps GPUs do last six years
A major concern that the market has is over the lifespan of the GPUs that dominate AI processing. And for good reason, over the past few years, the tech firms have told us that these GPUs should be depreciated over six years not two years, as they said four years ago.
It feels fishy, doesn’t it. We’re told demand is through the roof, the GPUs are “burning” with utilization rates about 80%, and that Nvidia is making them obsolete faster than ever. And yet, as if by magic, they are lasting twice as long as before.
So, who is right? Hedge fund investor Michael Burry (Cassandra Unchained ), who famously shorted sub-prime in 2006, today argues for a two- to three-year useful life. That is what I believed in March of this year. Back then I wrote [ https://substack.com/redirect/33f12fd2-77b1-42d4-959d-1be31794e508?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]
those expensive GPUs have a shelf-life. The accountants may depreciate them over five years, but the infrastructure managers know that a couple of years of hard running will leave exhausted silicon needing replacement.
But are the cloud providers, who insist a four-to-six-year lifespan is more realistic, correct?
Michael Intrator [ https://substack.com/redirect/fc923c99-df6f-4161-9013-46c83293c5c9?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], who runs CoreWeave, a neocloud, reports that inventories of NVIDIA A100s – technology dating back to 2020 – remain fully booked. Meanwhile, 2022-era H100 chips are being re-contracted at 95% of their original price. Analysis by Bernstein [ https://substack.com/redirect/1ae1a3e7-5ce7-444e-a2e4-69e08e1d3c5d?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] suggests that even aging processors command a contribution margin of roughly 70%. An older A100 generates $0.93 in hourly revenue against a cash operating cost of just $0.28. As long as marginal revenue exceeds electricity costs, the asset retains value.
This is not accounting optimism; it is grounded in the order book. Nitin Agrawal [ https://substack.com/redirect/22d8ba2c-fb4c-4d65-b19e-804dd60de2d9?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], CoreWeave’s CFO, notes that customer commitments have structurally lengthened, with five- to six-year contracts replacing shorter durations. Scarcity has forced the market to accept “trailing edge” silicon, pricing the utility of chips far into the future.
I’ve dug fairly deeply into this question. And I’ve changed my mind since March. Yes, long-depreciation schedules indeed help companies burnish their earnings. They spread depreciation expenses over more years with a multi-billion dollar impact to their earnings this year.
But there’s also increasing evidence that fleets of high-performance chips do – today – last longer than 2 years. And yes, up to six. Google’s TPUs, for instance, remain in use for seven to eight years [ https://substack.com/redirect/24cb97c2-1f24-48bc-90f0-c4df02e70349?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], with older generations still achieving 100% utilization.
AI cloud provider, Lambda, makes the case  [ https://substack.com/redirect/882a80cf-ad8a-4df6-a685-2a9c0597b28d?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]that the “effective lifespan of GPUs can stretch to seven or eight years” and warranties on GPUs are five years long. After two years, GPUs may not be cutting-edge enough to support training workloads, but there are places for them to go. “Because there’s a large diversity of models to solve many different problems, there’s a lot of room to use GPUs for a long time, just transitioning them from one type of job to the next”, according to a senior exec at Crusoe, another cloud provider.
The key here is this “value cascade.” Two years doing the main job (training), two years moving to high-value inference and then two years working on batch processing and utility workloads. And, of course, this approach makes sense – yielding as it does potentially billions of dollars of bottom line impact annually. It’s worth investing in the fleet and workload management systems to enable it.
But here is a critical point – and one overlooked by many commentators – the big tech companies started changing depreciation schedules well before ChatGPT was around. And their changes to those schedules are quite likely consistent with the growing importance of managing the lifespan and viability of larger and larger fleets of chips.
Amazon extended the useful life of servers from 4 to 5 years, effective in 2022. A second extension in 2024 took it to six years. And in February 2025, they shortened the life of their AI gear to five years.
Microsoft extended their lifespan for servers from four years to six years, five months before ChatGPT was launched.
Alphabet started to extend the useful life of servers from three years to four back in January 2021 and then in January 2023c, extending if furtherr to six years.
In other words, this pattern of extension is consistent with a more technical focus on managing expensive computing assets better. It is scarcely conceivable that any of these firms knew how workloads would grow in the months and years leading up to it before ChatGPT was launched.
7. Compute capacity has become existential for platforms
Computing infrastructure is existential for the major tech companies. Their cash cow businesses – search, enterprise app, shopping – depend on compute. And for Microsoft, Google and Amazon, it’s Azure, AWS and Google Cloud that are the fastest growing parts of their stack. And if there is one thing we can be confident of, their business customers will be putting heavier workloads into the cloud over the coming years. (For more details, read our joint research [ https://substack.com/redirect/1692c307-5232-4de6-ae89-f98610b4ffd5?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] with BCG Henderson Institute.)
Google cannot sensibly rely on Azure or AWS infrastructure by 2029 without ceding its destiny to direct competitors. And the same is true for Microsoft and Amazon. The hundreds of billions in “defensive capex” flowing into proprietary data centers are the price of sovereignty. They are an insurance policy against finding your core product roadmap gated by a rival’s pricing committee.
So we have two different rationales here. One is that the demand growth is so strong that this capacity is needed. That seems plausible, although it’s also clear that investments are happening well ahead of revenues showing up. It’s why we track both revenue momentum and industry strain on our “boom or bubble” [ https://substack.com/redirect/dcfc3642-7ea1-427f-a0cf-5189f81790bd?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] dashboard. The second is that competitive dynamics give the firms little choice but to invest, even if that turns into overinvestment.
8. The “productivity clock” rings around 2026
Because AI is a general-purpose technology, the value may or may not accrue to its inventors. The best way to see value is in its effect on productivity. The question for 2026 is whether AI shows up in those numbers.
So far, estimates from the St Louis Fed suggest a potential 1.3% increase in US productivity [ https://substack.com/redirect/d841da1f-7855-4682-bb27-1672329eac4a?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] since the launch of ChatGPT, but that evidence is not yet fully convincing. Much of it rests on self-reported time-saving models, which are flimsy as evidence. Productivity growth after ChatGPT [ https://substack.com/redirect/ad4888c0-e227-4995-ab43-598e325a2858?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] looks promising, but is also affected by confounding factors such as post-COVID recovery. Typically, productivity growth has averaged about 1.4 to 1.5%. To be persuaded of AI’s impact, we would need to see a sustained 2.5 to 4.0% [ https://substack.com/redirect/53040439-83aa-4dea-8c43-fc58f6dff3a9?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] with no obvious confounding factors.
Right now, we’re in an odd superposition. At the micro level, some teams and firms say their output per head has doubled [ https://substack.com/redirect/21a347b6-891e-4217-917a-edf43d343393?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. At the macro level, the national accounts mostly shrug. The “AI boom” has not yet turned into a visible step change in GDP per hour worked. I explore this paradox in a recent episode of my show:
For many large companies, at the enterprise level, the clock started ticking by the latter part of 2023, when Microsoft shipped Copilot. The first serious pilots began at the beginning of 2024.
So we’re approaching the second anniversary of the first reasonable cohort size of those projects. This is long enough for results to be known and for firms to give the go-ahead for wider-scale internal change.
The earnings cycles of American public companies in the first half of next year should start giving concrete evidence. If we’re on a boom trajectory, more firms will provide detailed ROI disclosures in the second and third quarters of 2026. We should look for specific information about payback periods, cost savings or new revenues or headcount details. Firms might be cagey about sharing success stories, so decent proxies would be signs they are increasing their AI budget. Absent those, the evidence for weaker gains and an overinvestment path grows.
Even if tension in the capital markets does ease, it might tighten elsewhere, perhaps even more acutely. We will suddenly have more output per worker flowing through systems already strained by inequality, regional divides and institutional mistrust. What are we going to do about it?
