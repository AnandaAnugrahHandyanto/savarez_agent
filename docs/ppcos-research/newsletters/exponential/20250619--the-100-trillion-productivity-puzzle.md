# 💡 The $100 trillion productivity puzzle

**From:** "Azeem Azhar, Exponential View" <exponentialview@substack.com>
**Date:** 2025-06-19T18:17:19.000Z
**Folder:** exponential

---

We are only six months into the year, yet AI has already outpaced two decades of ordinary tech cycles. In January, DeepSeek shook the world. Google, OpenAI and Anthropic quickly followed with next-generation models, ones which could command software tools on the internet.
The first wave of agents then appeared: ManusAI—until recently an unknown start-up—unveiled an agent that can autonomously tackle complex tasks, while Anthropic launched Claude Code, a multi-agent system many developers call a dream [ https://substack.com/redirect/94c6bed4-bf60-4029-90ad-ea21d3b687cd?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. The lab race is heating up, but two years after ChatGPT’s debut, the macro-productivity numbers remain stubbornly flat.
This is the capability-absorption gap: frontier labs are racing ahead faster than the traditional economy can keep pace. The current generation of AI is already powerful enough to remake how we work, yet firms are absorbing these capabilities far more slowly than labs are enhancing them. I don’t blame them. Prices halve every six months; models remain stochastic, which complicates reliability; and managerial know-how is scarce.
In today’s post, we will explore this gap and what businesses can do about it.
How far capabilities have already outpaced us
The scoreboard is unambiguous: nearly every public benchmark has moved upward since last year, making previous standards of excellence look decidedly ordinary.
Personal experience underscores this vividly. Occasionally, I run models locally on my laptop, particularly when trapped on flights with poor Wi-Fi. These local models approximate the capabilities of GPT-4 roughly a year ago—lacking the reasoning and tool-use features we have witnessed since. Using these models offline now feels painfully limited compared with the current state of the art.
Practical gains are clear, especially in real-world workflows. In software engineering, AI-powered coding tools have swiftly evolved from basic code hints to managing entire processes—planning, writing, testing and submitting finished work for human review. Systems such as Claude Code and OpenAI’s Codex now automate these tasks end-to-end, reducing humans to supervisory roles. Though not flawless, their rapid improvement streamlines workflows by converting tedious coding into manageable reviews.
These advancements are possible because models can now handle increasingly complex tasks. Consider METR’s latest agent-endurance benchmark, which measures how long AI models can sustain intricate multistep workflows. On this test, top-performing models last three to five times longer than they did only six months ago. This dramatic improvement signals deeper planning capabilities and more reliable tool use.
In short, the capability curve remains steep—and it continues to climb rapidly across multiple dimensions.
Perhaps even more crucially, the unit costs of AI are plunging exponentially. This is central to my definition of exponential technology—not solely about improving performance but about rapidly collapsing costs for a given capability.
Consider ChatGPT’s inference prices, which have roughly halved every six months—outpacing even the historical cost declines in DRAM and solar power. This steep drop stems from relentless algorithmic improvements and fierce competition among providers. Lower prices in turn drive wider adoption: the cheaper an AI agent becomes, the more extensively it can be deployed. (Though clearly there is still room for improvement—I recently burned through $80 of Replit credits in a single evening.)
Importantly, even if AI labs suddenly stopped frontier research and model scaling overnight, these cost improvements would continue accumulating. In my back-room conversations, experts are roughly split 50/50 on whether scaling laws alone can carry us all the way to AGI—whatever that ultimately entails. If scaling alone is not sufficient, most believe we might still be only one or two significant conceptual breakthroughs away.
Yet debates about scaling do not fundamentally change the core argument. Future capability enhancements, while valuable, are additive—not prerequisites—for substantial economic transformation. Existing models already surpass what most enterprises can effectively absorb or leverage. At Exponential View, we are still figuring out how to redefine our workflows around o3; I expect most organizations are still navigating how to integrate GPT-4o fully.
McKinsey reports that nearly every company is investing in AI, yet only 1% claim they have fully integrated it into workflows and achieved meaningful business outcomes [ https://substack.com/redirect/50cb5abb-109d-46e9-9d7c-620d2072e031?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. And honestly, even that 1% is probably just PR.
Why absorption lags – the three frictions
This sluggish absorption, rather than frontier innovation, is the main reason we can see the AI boom everywhere except in the economic statistics. That remains true even as AI startups rack up millions to tens of billions in revenue at record speed. But the global economy is huge—about $100 trillion a year—so that is a lot of OpenAIs. It will take several years, not a few quarters, before even the fastest-growing AI startups contribute one percent to global income. (Incidentally, I have little doubt they will, and that AI-native newcomers will replace many incumbents across industries over the next two decades, just not in the next two years.)
The rest of the economy is dominated by incumbents. Those incumbents are laced with friction. They need to tackle it. Three distinct institutional frictions underpin this capability-absorption gap, and each one is structural rather than technological.
First is learning time. Historically, general-purpose technologies such as the typewriter required surprisingly long periods—often decades—to boost productivity. James Bessen, in his influential book Learning by Doing [ https://substack.com/redirect/f701fac3-d143-4fef-8d37-da2e2de2333d?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], showed that clerical output took about 25 years to rise significantly after widespread adoption of the typewriter.
Similarly, businesses today will need time to integrate AI into workflows, adjust managerial practices and refine user behaviors around new systems. The more temeritous, less ambitious and less deliberate they are, the slower they will learn. It is those processes—not the technology—that hold them back.
Consider Waymo: the core driving AI was mature years before large-scale deployment began. Waymo’s final hurdles were operational, not technical. They needed robust institutional knowledge: how to clean vomit from autonomous vehicles, how to handle distressed customer calls and how to negotiate regulatory approvals city by city. Once those operational hurdles were addressed, uptake accelerated. We reckon that Waymo’s fleet will capture 50 percent of all San Francisco ride-shares within three years of full-scale launch.
Second is the complexity of the technology. AI’s general-purpose nature amplifies organizational complexity rather than simplifies it. Unlike narrowly defined technologies—such as accounting software, which finance teams clearly manage—AI tools cut across several organizational functions at once. Even in a modest-sized business, adopting accounting software is clearly the finance department’s responsibility, with straightforward metrics for success. AI, however, could affect customer service interactions, employee onboarding, product design and data analysis at the same time. The managerial challenge is substantial: which team leads implementation? Who controls the budget? How do you measure success across such varied activities? Organizations struggle because managing these overlapping deployments demands entirely new competencies—or new organizational forms.
Third is non-determinism and reliability. AI’s greatest strength—its flexibility and generality—is also the source of its trickiest weakness. Terence Tao, reflecting on AI-generated mathematical proofs [ https://substack.com/redirect/7f401b96-7da6-47dd-a0ef-f91d0c6180d9?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], captures this vividly, describing their tendency to “look right but smell wrong.” Outputs from LLMs often look superficially convincing and pass initial reviews effortlessly. Even small error rates can compound across multiple AI-driven steps, making verification cycles costly and eroding organizational trust, ultimately slowing adoption.
Andrej Karpathy, a founder of OpenAI and former head of AI at Tesla, makes a similar case [ https://substack.com/redirect/b79a6fc6-d201-4093-ac68-9d9aaeae404c?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], describing the “cognitive deficits” of LLMs, superhuman in some areas but making “mistakes that basically no human will make.” One consequence is the importance, he argues, of ensuring humans can “audit the work of these fallible systems”.
Compounding this problem is what Simon Willison calls the lethal trifecta for AI agents [ https://substack.com/redirect/32f1edb0-55b2-43e4-97fe-c25ec61ee853?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]: private data, untrusted content and external communication. When these three factors combine, the risk of exploitation surges. Malicious actors can embed hidden instructions in untrusted content, causing AI systems inadvertently to access sensitive information and send it outside. It is reminiscent of the “Wild West” phase of the early internet—robust security practices and infrastructure are not fully in place yet and will take time to mature.
Closing the gap
A business can close the capability-absorption gap in three ways—by developing talent, redesigning workflows and upgrading infrastructure.
There’s a reason 70 % of Anthropic’s code [ https://substack.com/redirect/98ac1623-726a-4ee0-881d-3fb1029ab144?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] is AI-generated and yours isn’t. It’s not because they have better models—they have better people. Their teams understand how models work and how to embed them in effective workflows. These implementation specialists master critical details such as retrieval pipelines, evaluation frameworks and fallback logic. Hiring this expertise is essential—and worth paying a premium for. Mark Zuckerberg recognizes this [ https://substack.com/redirect/48f7c2c3-88ea-41fc-87d6-3458d1c6cde8?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], even if his super-intelligence hiring spree seems excessive. But while he’s chasing researchers, you need people who can map an LLM’s strengths and limits onto your existing processes. They’re cheaper, faster to hire and immediately impactful. As Amazon CEO Andy Jassy puts it, smart AI deployment slashes costs and reshapes teams [ https://substack.com/redirect/8133790c-de5f-4e2e-82dc-1ae53f792687?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]—so invest in the people who can actually deploy it.
But there’s little value in hiring top talent if you’re unwilling to redesign your workflows—fast. Electricity taught the same lesson a century ago: don’t just replace candles—tear up the factory floor and invent the assembly line. Electricity’s performance improved incrementally each year, yet it transformed the economy only after firms rebuilt their factories around it. AI demands an equally bold redesign. Incremental tweaks aren’t enough; processes such as verification, authorization and customer interactions must be fundamentally re-imagined.
Consider Morgan Stanley’s in-house DevGen.AI tool, which has already scanned 9 million lines of legacy code, turning them into plain English and reclaiming about 280,000 developer-hours. Such victories aren’t accidental—they come from teams that have painstakingly crafted new playbooks for integrating AI. Organizations can’t maximize AI’s potential by merely speeding up existing tasks; they must reinvent workflows, embedding new automation and decision-making deep in their core operations.
To achieve this, organizations need robust verification infrastructure. Given AI’s stochastic nature and current reliability limits, effective verification is essential. How do you position people so they spend as little time as possible checking AI outputs? Do your AI systems provide citations that link directly to ground-truth sources? Are tasks broken down so individual AI agents can focus on discrete subtasks? Do you have AI agents cross-checking the outputs of other agents? These are only a few emerging strategies—but the key point is that, today, no one knows the perfect approach; you’ll learn only by doing. Verification therefore needs to be baked into your workflows from the outset. Otherwise, you’ll spend more time checking AI-generated content than you would have spent creating it yourselves.
Five years, not five quarters
The capability-absorption gap will close—it’s only a question of when. Real fireworks are more likely five years away, not five quarters. Google took seven years to convert PageRank into an advertising powerhouse; Waymo’s trajectory looks even longer. Institutional know-how compounds gradually, even if raw AI capabilities plateau today. Expect the absorption curve to kink sharply upward around 2027–28.
Practical steps are clear:
Push forward faster. It’s unsurprising that large companies have yet to see double-digit growth from their AI efforts. That “yet” is critical. Because you learn by doing, easing off the pedal means you’ll do less and learn more slowly.
Redesign workflows; don’t merely accelerate them. AI demands a fundamental reinvention of business processes, just as electricity enabled the assembly line rather than merely brighter candles. Future winners will emerge from startups catalyzing industry-wide change, though transformative innovation still takes time. Google’s disruption of advertising, after all, unfolded over nearly a decade.
Build talent rapidly. Whether through cultivating AI-nativity internally or hiring externally, build talent now.
Plan for capabilities to improve. While it’s unclear when the next GPT-4-level leap will arrive, price-performance for existing models will keep moving in users’ favor. Benchmark scores will also rise—even if we don’t yet know by how much—so plan for the near future, not today’s static systems.
Ultimately, economic transformation appears inevitable. Amid the confusion, keep a few competing truths in mind. On the one hand, capabilities are racing far ahead of impactful deployments by industry. Prices for existing models are dropping fast. Most incumbents lag in adopting and absorbing existing capabilities, for reasons both understandable and not.
That capability-absorption gap is real.
Those who close it—incumbents perhaps, startups more likely—will win.
