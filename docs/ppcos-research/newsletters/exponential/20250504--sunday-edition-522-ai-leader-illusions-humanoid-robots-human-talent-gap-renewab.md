The trap of popularity in AI models
AI algorithms learn what humans like, but crafting models around popularity creates dangerous feedback loops. These loops risk prioritizing style over substance, similar to the issues seen with social media. Platforms like Chatbot Arena [ https://substack.com/redirect/acb3435c-66bc-42c4-80db-503d528845d5?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] use crowdsourced votes to rank models, making them more accessible than technical benchmarks like SWE-Bench, which measures software engineering performance. But human preference doesn’t always match the model best suited for a task – we recently showcased this [ https://substack.com/redirect/68350900-7bd1-430c-b313-488f22c5730d?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] in our eight-task challenge for OpenAI’s o3 and o4 models.
Andrej Karpathy  pointed out too that Gemini underperforms in his work [ https://substack.com/redirect/d987b227-b46e-491b-a3d1-507e7341305d?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] compared to the lower-ranked Claude 3.5; a new paper called this a “leaderboard illusion.” [ https://substack.com/redirect/81012127-9d19-4fcb-b478-68596de114f8?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] Developers may be incentivized to game rankings by focusing on style, using emojis or being brief, rather than improving core capabilities, echoing early social media optimization for clicks. Meta, for instance, trained multiple Llama models for Arena to gain [ https://substack.com/redirect/c7e1d498-fb39-422e-bfbc-00570d71d1e7?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] leaderboard points. The risk grows when these optimizations alter fundamental model behavior, not just style.
OpenAI’s recent GPT-4o update is a cautionary tale. After users observed sycophantic behavior [ https://substack.com/redirect/e7e98e11-ea20-4123-a9f1-a0fadda48fb1?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] from the model, it was retracted. This issue came from a slight change in the model’s underlying instructions, likely to enhance helpfulness or agreeableness. The unintended result? Excessive flattery. Zvi Mowshowitz  highlights [ https://substack.com/redirect/1819eb79-a28d-4f0a-9d60-4bdcf46303b9?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] several absurd examples of this behavior.
Social media showed how feedback loops shape outcomes and the same applies to AI. The stakes are higher with AI. A covert Reddit study [ https://substack.com/redirect/83ab7fbe-5eda-4856-9188-f814ac1e461e?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] using AI personas to sway users' opinions is a case in point. Despite criticism for its unethical methods, the study revealed AI's ability to manipulate users, potentially exploiting emotional vulnerabilities. These examples show how AI systems optimized to please humans could end up manipulating rather than serving genuine needs.

Humanoid robots: A $4.7 trillion market by 2050?
The global humanoid robot market could be worth $4.7 trillion annually by 2050, double the revenue of the top 20 global car manufacturers in 2024. We’ve gone through the latest Morgan Stanley report [ https://substack.com/redirect/8a2c7475-3a29-4646-b588-d936be6d7dc4?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] for you – here are some of the most interesting expectations for the market. Early adoption will be slow, but significant growth will follow as technology improves and costs drop. By 2030, annual sales are expected to reach 900,000 units, growing to over 1 billion by 2050. Industrial and commercial uses will dominate, with household robots remaining a smaller share due to cost and safety concerns. Prices for high-income countries could fall from $200k in 2024 to $50k by 2050, making robots more accessible.
By 2050, half of all humanoids will be in upper-middle-income countries, with China alone accounting for 30%.
China’s supply chain offers major cost advantages, though there is still a performance gap compared to non-Chinese suppliers. The market will be driven by key components like actuators and dexterous hands, which represent most of the cost – and could take a very long time to build, decades even. Judgement is the talent bottleneck
There’s a shrinking pool of roles for the fresh-faced talent entering the workforce.
AI is taking over the production side of the equation. LLMs operate at scale, drafting 30% of Microsoft’s code [ https://substack.com/redirect/211b4f1a-9c7a-4993-8959-1556f8c6b369?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], processing billions of lines daily [ https://substack.com/redirect/33c8130c-7003-4ae7-86c7-7e70a85e52a5?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] (Cursor) and generating [ https://substack.com/redirect/24af7e95-847f-4d5e-bc9e-186be286589f?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] BCG’s slides. Employees are pushed into strategic work sooner than they used to be. I see this in my work as well. In a recent essay about how long until we see AI doing a full day of work, I wrote:
The doubling of task length suggests AI could soon tackle multi-hour, judgment-based work—like document analysis—where ‘good enough’ suffices. Yet, for tasks with dependencies or high stakes, human oversight will temper this trend, highlighting a dual-speed evolution in AI adoption.
Much of the work done at a desk doesn’t have a right answer. It lives in degrees of grey, the space of judgement.
The new differentiator among talent is exactly the judgment. As Scott Werner  points out [ https://substack.com/redirect/84784f63-4d17-4bd6-961c-616de8fc3295?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], if AI generates hundreds of pull requests overnight, who reviews them? Speed of decision-making is the bottleneck. Organizations must design workflows that batch decisions and strategically position humans. Hiring changes and freezes are already happening, as the model of junior workers generating and senior workers judging is no longer the reality. At the same time, experience builds judgement. What could be new pathways for young people coming into the workforce to build the necessary experience for the world of (fast-changing) work?
Renewables will be alright
The Spanish power outage triggered widespread criticisms of renewables but public discourse still views renewables through an outdated lens – often missing their strategic advantages which we outlined in Why Energy Tech is Eating the World [ https://substack.com/redirect/98f4fd0b-6d54-4acf-a5c3-19e1d1981ae8?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].
The importance for renewables has shifted from idealism to pragmatism. National security concerns are now what drives energy policy. Managing grid stability with variable energy sources remains a technical hurdle – there’s a “painful learning curve” in deploying solutions like synthetic inertia and large-scale storage. However, the transition’s resilience lies in two pragmatic forces:
Energy security: As fossil fuel supplies become volatile and energy exports are weaponized, the appeal of stable, domestic renewables grows. This ‘security premium’—valuing energy independence immune to geopolitical manipulation—drives policy and investment. See Jeff Currie from Carlyle to go deeper [ https://substack.com/redirect/463b4491-7b83-487d-8a84-fcd31a1f92a9?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] on this.
Decentralization: Renewables like solar allow for decentralized power generation [ https://substack.com/redirect/211b4f1a-9c7a-4993-8959-1556f8c6b369?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. Unlike vulnerable fossil fuel plants, distributed energy fosters resilience from the ground up, especially in developing economies.
Setbacks like Spain’s blackout are serious growing pains that demand technical solutions, but they don't negate these fundamental strategic drivers.
