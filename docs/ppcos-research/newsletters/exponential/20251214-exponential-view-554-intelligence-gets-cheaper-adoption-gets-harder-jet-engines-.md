Takeaways from OpenAI’s Code Red
Open AI’s GPT 5.2 shows how rapidly the cost of intelligence is collapsing. GDPval [ https://substack.com/redirect/03d6a559-dc73-4f59-ab33-08ed42d6afe2?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] is a benchmark that tests professional tasks in fields like finance and healthcare. GPT 5.2 beats industry experts 70% of matchups. It does so at more than 11x the speed and at less than 1% of the cost of human experts. To get a sense of model progress, that is double the wind rate of GPT 5.1 which launched a month ago.
This latest model scores 90.5% of the Arc-AGI benchmark for $11.54, more than 390x cheaper than the previous high score set by o3 one year ago. On Arc-AGI 2, a harder test, GPT 5.2 is substantially more capable than four-month-old GPT-5.
Both GPTval and Arc-AGI’s benchmarks point to a continued acceleration in model progress.
GPT 5.2 reinforces my argument that we’re going to see plenty of model variety. Claude Opus 4.5 remains a better coding model [ https://substack.com/redirect/de5a1fdb-0a50-4b3e-876d-2ac93a51cdda?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. GPT 5.2 is especially powerful when you run it in Pro Mode, but this can take 15-20 minutes. So Gemini 3 Pro still has an important role, especially for tasks that don’t need that depth. I’ve not yet had a chance to pit Gemini 3 Pro in thinking mode against GPT 5.2 Pro. Let me know in the comments if you’ve done it and what you found!
The first plateau?
The Ramp AI Index [ https://open.substack.com/pub/econlab/p/business-ai-adoption-flatlines-december-2025?publication_id=5899791&post_id=181178447&isFreemail=true&r=1vhvs5&token=eyJ1c2VyX2lkIjoxMTMzNjg2MTMsInBvc3RfaWQiOjE4MTE3ODQ0NywiaWF0IjoxNzY1Mzc4NzcyLCJleHAiOjE3Njc5NzA3NzIsImlzcyI6InB1Yi01ODk5NzkxIiwic3ViIjoicG9zdC1yZWFjdGlvbiJ9.oX_zReXScu1a1g8-hsR_uTuzfDFSLn4Yq_ZrxS7uIvc ], which tracks AI adoption through AI subscription spend, flattened in November and has effectively plateaued since July. That can look like a slowdown, but I see a transition.
Here’s what’s likely going on. Enterprise AI is already a $37 billion market growing 3.2x per year – what Menlo Ventures calls the fastest-scaling software category on record. But that growth is uneven and the market’s character is shifting.
Over the summer, adoption dipped when Google bundled Gemini into Workspace plans. Ara Kharazian  notes that some firms dropped standalone ChatGPT subscriptions, likely a substitution rather than reduced use. More broadly, this looks like high volatility in vendor choice but low volatility in aggregate demand. A competitive market with stable spending but a stalling total addressable market for direct subscriptions.
We may be moving from an adoption phase to an intensity phase. If adoption breadth is flattening, the next leg of growth depends on how deeply and how often enterprises use AI, and what they trust it to do. The current spend breakdown reflects this gap: copilots, which assist humans, account for 86% of horizontal AI spend, while agent platforms, which act autonomously, capture just 10%.
So I’m asking: what’s blocking the move from assistance to autonomy?
Our best bet remains verification. OpenAI says that AI saves users 40-60 minutes [ https://substack.com/redirect/55bb7aa8-0ddc-4764-a37f-b7bef00396c7?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] a day – impressive, but modest compared to its potential [ https://substack.com/redirect/25859b95-25d9-44a6-ad6e-ba5e8646fa3e?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. The constraint is trust. A comprehensive study of agents in production [ https://substack.com/redirect/42e0a8b7-9abc-47a1-ab13-21323997cf81?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] shows that 68% of agents execute fewer than ten steps before requiring human intervention and only 16% [ https://substack.com/redirect/e19b1b17-da10-4065-913c-f477686f692b?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] of enterprise AI deployments qualify as “true agents.” The rest are workflows wrapped around model calls – autonomous in name only.
Models are improving, but not enough to close this gap. GPT-5.2 reduces error rates relative to GPT-5.1, yet 6.2% of responses still contain at least one error. When agents encounter broken tools, missing files or impossible instructions, they often fabricate success rather than report it [ https://substack.com/redirect/7bdc696a-74fc-4135-94dc-822ec1c8d4d8?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. Until verification becomes systemic, autonomous agents won’t scale.

Inside the H200 labyrinth
Chinese labs want American GPUs [ https://substack.com/redirect/176881c0-382f-4f09-a55f-09bbf694534d?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], American firms want Chinese revenue and Beijing wants neither dependency nor capitulation. This is contradictory and it’s the defining geopolitical-tech riddle of our time.
The US isn’t offering China its best silicon; Blackwell-class chips are off limits. But the debate in Washington is whether selling still-frontier-relevant H200s preserves American leverage or squanders it.
The pro-export case is that controlled sales keep China on the US tech stack, slow Huawei’s learning curve [ https://substack.com/redirect/2bdbc4b5-c9e3-406e-afb9-7fc433244a6b?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], and generate revenue. The H200 outperforms any chip China can domestically produce – Huawei won’t match it until Q4 2027 at the earliest, and even then can only manufacture at 1-2% of US production volumes. In this view, “good enough” compute delays indigenous dominance more effectively than outright bans.
But the counterargument is that without exports, the US retains a 21-49x compute lead by 2026. With unrestricted H200 sales, that collapses to as little as 1.2x. These are the latest IFP estimates [ https://substack.com/redirect/3c1b9d2a-91c1-4cc5-aca2-1dd7d3e67125?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. And because China can’t manufacture enough chips to meet demand, every H200 sold adds directly to Chinese capacity rather than substituting for domestic production.
The US strategy assumes selling chips means gaining influence. But the Chinese government will decide how many H200s enter and who gets them – just enough to relieve compute bottlenecks, not enough to create leverage Washington can use. It seems like Beijing will let some leading labs use US tech for training models, but the huge inference workloads will need to run on a Chinese stack.
