# 😵💫 The AI acceleration delirium: models, mergers, and mayhem

**Date:** 2025-04-19T05:23:17.000Z
**Folder:** exponential

What a week. The feeling isn’t just acceleration anymore; it’s delirium. You blink, and another model is released, another rumour flies. The pace is truly accelerating beyond all reasonable expectation. Welcome to artificial general confusion.
This week felt like a microcosm of the entire AI race. OpenAI dropped o3 and o4 Mini, their latest reasoning models. o3 is really quite impressive: it can use an array of tools from Python to web search and image analysis to do your bidding.
o3 has shown its mettle in my early tests. A particularly tricky one is my flight challenge. The test involves making a transatlantic booking with specific constraints (like my preference for particular planes). o3 did really well, beating all other models, but, for now, I’m still better. Another has been a multi-factor, quite complex, real-world strategic problem. o3 worked its way through the study like a master strategist: pulling out the key issues and addressing them with just enough detail.
One of the more formal measures I’m tracking closely is METR’s time horizon, which tests AI’s ability to complete long tasks. Mastering long tasks is a key unlock for significant productivity enhancements.
On this o3 does not disappoint.
Benchmarks offer a glimpse but we should be cautious about simplistic readings [ https://substack.com/redirect/21352c6c-4780-4b57-8495-dc0f34739664?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. The true, messier reality of real-world application shows a palpable rate of improvement, even as capabilities feel like jagged edges pushed into the market.
Predictably, there was a stampede online, people breathlessly declaring this “artificial general intelligence,” as if we’ve finally tripped over some obvious finish line. It’s a line that, frankly, remains stubbornly undefined.
Within a day, Google fired back, launching Gemini 2.5 Flash. This is the faster, more efficient sibling to the 2.5 Pro model that’s become my go-to [ https://substack.com/redirect/8ca82a1b-ae49-4fe2-b027-6545797705ce?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. At least on one benchmark, Google seems to be carving out a fascinating and potentially dominant space right now. Their price-performance frontier is helped, no doubt, by their hardware expertise [ https://substack.com/redirect/82838cbf-1c02-4ecb-b6c1-96b3e1bf5239?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].
Anthropic’s releases this week went rather unnoticed. Claude can now search your email, Google Drive, and calendar, although it is ponderously slow, and I use other AI tools [ https://substack.com/redirect/8ca82a1b-ae49-4fe2-b027-6545797705ce?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] on my email.
Then rumours started that OpenAI is considering buying Windsurf [ https://substack.com/redirect/76cdf8ed-1faa-4fa0-ba94-d28921512f6f?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], one of those increasingly indispensable code completion tools that software engineers use to magnify their output. The purchase price? A cool $3 billion. And what is more, rumours that Sam Altman might turn the billion-user base of ChatGPT into a social network [ https://substack.com/redirect/b03ad08d-cc31-4b93-98fe-f43c286f0e60?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] full of yeets.
How do we make sense of this four-ring circus of releases and more?
First, the pace is unsustainable for traditional product cycles. Products are being released faster than they can be properly described, product-managed, or even benchmarked in a way that’s useful to anyone outside the lab. The capabilities, while impressive, feel like jagged edges pushed out into the market. As Rohit Krishnan  says:
Second, the Windsurf rumour is a telling move. If true, it does at least one, possibly two, critical things for OpenAI. It gives them a vertical application, providing direct ownership of a key piece of the user interface for a crucial professional cohort (engineers). This is strategic, sitting alongside their stated ambitions in social networking. But perhaps more importantly in the near term, it floods them with data – high-quality code and usage patterns upon which they can dramatically improve their coding models. Coding, I’ve argued before, is one of the first complex cognitive tasks that will truly transform under AI because it’s eminently verifiable. Code runs or it doesn’t. Formal methods exist to check its correctness. This makes it fertile ground for rapid AI improvement and deployment.
Third, this week underscores an increasingly clear reality: our future AI world will not be dominated by one monolithic super-intelligence, but by dozens, if not hundreds, of different types of AI models. Some will be specialists, many will be generalists, but they will all embody different trade-offs.
As I wrote two years ago [ https://substack.com/redirect/857adbc3-28cc-4ee9-9451-dbe3bb76d9dc?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]:
These things are software, which means that there are a set of software-like trade-offs to contend with: privacy, cost, latency and ultimately performance.
The most obvious trade-offs are speed versus capability, and computational cost versus output quality. O3 might be incredibly smart in some ways, but like cutting-edge skis or a Formula One car, you need a certain level of skill and understanding to even formulate the right question to get value from it. But even the big, “general” models like o3, o4, and Gemini 2.5 have distinct, sometimes slightly weird, flavours to their intelligence and how they respond.
This expanding surface of capabilities makes the idea that one model is simply “better” than another across the board increasingly unlikely. The frontier is jagged [ https://substack.com/redirect/c150a17b-0b2d-4370-ae84-21b0ed107cfd?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], defined by context, task, and the user’s needs (and skills).
This brings us to this week's final critical point: these products are really confusing as hell. Which vendor is right for you? Which of those vendors’ services is right? If you’re using Grok, should you switch to “Deep Search”, and when do you use “Deeper Search”? What do the cryptic icons in Perplexity mean? Is Claude 3.7 Sonnet really better than 3.5, and when should you even consider “Extended Thinking”? And don’t get me started on ChatGPT’s bewildering dropdown menu of a dozen different options, none adequately explained.
A new test of machine intelligence
I gave o3 a practical test to gauge its vaunted intelligence. I asked it to come up with a better product architecture and naming convention for that very chaotic product line that is behind the hallmark of OpenAI.
o3 tried. What came back didn’t even merit the description of “word salad.”
Here was its first attempt. This is not even wrong.
I was reasonably kind to the AI. Here is my follow-up prompt where I give it some friendly feedback.
o4 mini is part of the reasoning model family. I have to confess this schema is a mess. I know you tried hard. but sorry perhaps this task is too hard for you.
It tried again. The final output is, if anything, worse.

The stakes are too high, and the mayhem will continue. Meanwhile, we have a candidate for the true test of “artificial general intelligence.” Can it develop a coherent, understandable, and appealing way of naming OpenAI’s models?
