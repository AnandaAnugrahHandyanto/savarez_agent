Canary in the bullpen
We now have stronger evidence that AI is biting into youth employment. Since late 2022, early-career workers have seen employment fall by 13% [ https://substack.com/redirect/c368bd6d-7d1e-4608-a1b5-8f682396ac95?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] compared to older cohorts in the same roles, whose employment grew by 6–9%. The decline in employment for the youngest workers shows up only where AI automates rather than augments work. It isn’t causal proof, but Erik Brynjolfsson , Bharat Chandar  and Ruyu Chen used ADP payroll data across tens of thousands of firms to rule out obvious alternatives: pre-AI trends, Covid-19, education gaps, and firm or industry shocks. The most likely explanation is AI.
This study adds some weight to what we’ve believed for some time [ https://substack.com/redirect/2ded33ff-9688-49c3-a466-15eac0637241?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] – as AI enters the workforce, experience and judgment are being re-evaluated and valued.
AI is taking over the production side of the equation. LLMs operate at scale, drafting 30% of Microsoft’s code, processing billions of lines a day (Cursor) and generating BCG’s slides. [...] The model of junior workers generating and senior workers judging is no longer the reality.
Erik and his co-authors refer to this as “a canary in the coal mine” for broader AI disruption of the labor market. It could be, but we don’t know whether these results point to lasting change or just a transitional pause as firms figure out where AI fits. In uncertain times, it’s easier to slow youth hiring and lean on experience.
So we need to read these results with nuance. It’s a canary, not a smoking gun. I explore how I think about it here. [ https://substack.com/redirect/c8bbef73-cfc9-4681-b407-0748b5306577?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]
Nevertheless, these findings should be a wake-up call for policymakers. AI and anxiety [ https://substack.com/redirect/b3afcf85-8000-441c-83d1-a41a8de03d45?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] around it are already affecting a large portion of the workforce. But, in many ways, we know what we’re working with. Usage-based assessments such as the Anthropic Economic Index [ https://substack.com/redirect/ecf29288-f831-494c-98f5-20d55563df78?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] now show where AI is automating tasks and hint at which occupations are most exposed. That makes proactive policy possible. The good news is that retraining works: a new study found AI-exposed workers who completed government-funded programs earned $1,470 more per quarter than comparable job seekers [ https://substack.com/redirect/fe4e072e-5634-426f-b433-26bdd3a9eced?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. With the right data and tools, governments can ease the shocks of transition.

The real DeepSeek moment?
DeepSeek made a fascinating move when it released its new model V3-1 last week. They revealed [ https://substack.com/redirect/b0cbe6a7-25ec-4bd9-b09e-4bd77680188e?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] to be using a stripped-down number system to make advanced AI training possible on the chips China may actually be able to produce.
UE8M0 FP8 is designed for the next generation of domestically produced chips to be released soon.
Normally, AI models use different ways of storing numbers that balance precision against computing cost. FP32 is the most precise way but is slow and expensive to use. FP16 uses half the memory but is twice as fast, with some hit to accuracy. It’s traditionally been a good middle ground.
By leaning into FP8, DeepSeek is halving memory requirements and setting a new industry standard. And they push it further: the UE8M0 variant DeepSeek supports uses all 8 bits just to track scale (powers of two), none for the in-between details. This would be similar to measuring distance only as 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, etc. – we’d lose granularity, but we’d cover ranges more cheaply.
This is a workaround for China’s hardware bottleneck. DeepSeek’s bet is that a coarser arithmetic system can squeeze more efficiency from next-gen domestic chips (in the 5-7nm range) and lay a foundation for scaling AI training without immediate access to cutting-edge fabs.
It’s a gamble – precision matters and fabs still struggle. But for the first time, there’s a plausible standard around which China’s ecosystem can coalesce. As Kevin Xu  writes [ https://substack.com/redirect/b0cbe6a7-25ec-4bd9-b09e-4bd77680188e?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], this may be “the real DeepSeek moment”: not a single model release, but the creation of a technical foundation for a sovereign Chinese AI stack.
[I]f FP8 becomes the default, and the UE8M0 variant can indeed train great models, SMIC’s 7nm and 5nm processes may just be enough to support an explosion of indigenous GPUs from Cambricon to Moore Threads to Alibaba and ByteDance’s in-house chips, not just Huawei. These could be general purpose GPUs or ASICs. They can support massive training runs – currently a big gap among Chinese GPUs – not just inference workloads.
See also, last week’s edition [ https://substack.com/redirect/786a3bdc-1e47-4564-9864-e56335032244?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], when we explored how China is reframing global competition in AI as a physics problem: converting energy into intelligence as efficiently as possible.
Africa’s rooftop revolution
Last year, we tracked Pakistan’s silent solar boom [ https://substack.com/redirect/111860bf-c364-4fa1-bbf3-5744ea9997a8?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. The country imported about 19GW of panels in 2024, nearly two-fifths of the grid’s formal capacity. I wrote then:
This shift in the energy system from a Rockefeller-Gulbenkian-Ibn-Saud commodity to a Moore-Gates-Musk technology has more than geopolitical implications. In poorly run countries, organising complex projects like large gas or nuclear power plants is impossible – too much grift and bribery. […] Yet, I argue that the new technologies of energy – essentially solar and batteries – could work in this context. Why? Local investment: by a householder or a business, with limited need to wait for top-down institutions to provide the public goods needed for big investments.
Now we are seeing the first signs of a solar takeoff in Africa [ https://substack.com/redirect/c1f6cd24-69a1-4f88-af17-74d132448960?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. Imports of panels from China are up 60% this year across the continent, with 37% of countries hitting record highs.
While Africa isn’t quite at the same level as Pakistan yet, the trend is similar and the economics are irresistible. In Nigeria, switching from diesel generators to two-decade-lasting solar panels can pay for itself in six months. The returns only improve as costs keep tumbling.
Solar will be central to Africa’s energy transition. The continent is the most sun-rich region on Earth and has chronically been bottlenecked by the fossil fuel politics. While there is no concrete data on how much of the imports are distributed rather than utility-scale solar, estimates suggest the share is significant. As we have long argued [ https://substack.com/redirect/25903f28-9a21-4a25-a14f-60f28e616368?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], this is systemically important: decentralising energy also decentralises political power.
Human agency
The more hopeless the world seems, the less power we feel to change it. Sam Gee calls this a culture of  [ https://substack.com/redirect/d6dece3b-7ded-493b-80f7-7dd27d9549cc?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]“ [ https://substack.com/redirect/d6dece3b-7ded-493b-80f7-7dd27d9549cc?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]crisis-thought [ https://substack.com/redirect/d6dece3b-7ded-493b-80f7-7dd27d9549cc?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]” [ https://substack.com/redirect/d6dece3b-7ded-493b-80f7-7dd27d9549cc?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] – the belief that constant vigilance against disaster is the only serious moral stance. Decades ago, critic Lionel Trilling warned that by fixating on catastrophe, we come to treat cruelty as more real than joy. Vigilance hardens into paralysis, and our agency dies away.
Yet paradoxically, we are living in an era of unprecedented human agency. Solar allows households and businesses to generate power independently, free from fragile governments or volatile geopolitics. AI is enabling the rise of the “one-person startup [ https://substack.com/redirect/6b298228-0894-47ac-a8e5-1507eb977754?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]”, where individuals can wield leverage once reserved for corporations. Our tools are expanding our capabilities at the very moment our narratives are contracting them.
Part of our work at Exponential View is to close this gap. And if you’ve made it this far, you already know that!
