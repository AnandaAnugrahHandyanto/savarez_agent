🔮 Why energy tech is eating the world
Originally published in September 2024 [ https://substack.com/redirect/f36d8a5d-9a4e-42ac-b801-e219cf1bada5?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]
Britain will unplug its last coal power station at the end of this month, 142 years since the world’s first coal power plant was built in London and 264 years since the beginning of the Industrial Revolution.
As the turbines fall silent, Britain will become the first G7 country to end its reliance on coal-fired electricity. Gone are the days of what English poet William Blake described in 1808 as the “dark satanic mills”.
For over three centuries, energy has been a commodity: finite, unevenly distributed and subject to the whims of markets and politics. But that paradigm has collapsed.
The “energy transition” often refers to the shift from a carbon-based energy system to a zero-emissions system. Though this change is occurring—and is crucial—the real transition we need to understand is from energy as a commodity to energy as a technology.
Unlike fossil fuels, which are extracted and burned, renewable energy is generated through innovations that improve and cheapen over time.
Energy has become a technology.
By examining the characteristics of this new energy paradigm, its key technologies and its broader implications, we can better understand and prepare for the radically different energy landscape of the future.
Energy as a commodity
Energy commodities are tradable resources like oil, natural gas, coal and uranium. And as commodities, their price is volatile and subject to “market forces.” In the case of oil, for example, those market forces have been outright manipulated starting at the dawn of the era. As early as the 1870s, John D. Rockefeller of Standard Oil used tactics like consolidating competitors and secret agreements with railroads to manipulate the supply and the price of oil. A century later, OPEC’s oil embargo triggered a huge spike in the price of oil and left long-lasting ramifications in geopolitics — we still feel them today.
This control over supply has meant that prices haven’t decreased over time. In fact, the only thing that is certain about fossil fuel prices is that they will be volatile. Coal costs roughly the same today as it did in the 1880s. Oil is about double the price. In a century, natural gas has doubled in real terms.
And yet this inflation occurred against a backdrop of ongoing supply growth. The substantial increase in oil production as the Middle East ramped up output in the 1960s briefly brought down prices. But in 1973, geopolitical events caused oil prices to surge, much like how Russia’s political actions led to a spike in natural gas prices in recent years.
Prices remain volatile despite the discovery of new fields and technological innovations reducing the cost of prospecting, extraction and transport.
Innovation certainly occurred, but it had its limitations. Energy historian Vaclav Smil estimates that across a whole slew of diesel engines (in cars and on ships), there was an annualised improvement in engine efficiency of around 1.1% for a 16-year period before thermodynamics limited the process. But thermodynamics imposes strict limits. The Carnot cycle [ https://substack.com/redirect/ccbd22f7-08ff-420f-97e3-9ae396996652?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] dictates that a heat engine’s efficiency depends on the temperature difference between the heat source and sink. For coal plants, material constraints further reduce achievable efficiencies. Modern plants reach 30-45% efficiency [ https://substack.com/redirect/819acdc0-43f7-4f28-992e-47deb032d22d?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] — below the theoretical maximum of 60-70%. Combined cycle gas turbine generators can, in the right circumstances, achieve around 60% [ https://substack.com/redirect/10a43e92-c87e-4df6-8ee9-37432a20f128?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. Physics will stop them from going any further.
So, yesterday’s energy system is at the whim of commodity prices, which depend on the mood of the local dictator. It’s no way to run a resilient future-oriented economy.
Energy as a technology
Fortunately, renewables change all that. They go beyond merely providing an alternative and competitive energy source. Much more importantly, the machines that turn wind and sunshine into electricity are technologies that behaves like the technologies of the digital world.
The underlying components, from solar photovoltaic systems, wind turbines and battery storage, are all technologies. These technologies benefit from learning curves, becoming more cost-effective as manufacturers accumulate experience.
Modularity [ https://substack.com/redirect/01e28342-15a0-435d-ad06-5eb36f102c2e?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] enables and amplifies the benefits of learning curves.1 [ https://substack.com/redirect/2e4a498a-41d6-444f-a3ed-0014c94b5aae?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] By sharing cost reductions across various market segments, it facilitates significant market expansion and nurtures an ecosystem of complementary businesses. The photovoltaic panels powering homes are fundamentally similar to those used in large-scale utility installations. This mirrors the computing industry, where the same processor might be found in both personal and enterprise-grade laptops.
But how can we be sure that renewable energy is a technology rather than a commodity?
One clear indicator is the trajectory of their prices. As technologies mature, they tend to become cheaper over time. This is especially true for technologies that are modular — composed of standardised units — and amenable to miniaturisation. Lucky for us, energy technologies have these characteristics.
What is bringing those prices down is (rapid) learning curves.
Solar and lithium-ion batteries all have learning rates above 20%. This means that for every doubling in production, prices decline by 20%.
Wind is slightly lower at 15% [ https://substack.com/redirect/f1775dad-8000-46bb-a29f-25218368d6c4?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. Some estimates put solar’s learning rate in excess of 35% [ https://substack.com/redirect/34b2b82e-07ec-4a66-958f-4a1a00e10e37?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ](learning has increased rapidly since 2009). These learning curves arise from continuous innovation across all the components and the systems design of the technology.
Now, let’s dive into the specifics. Solar power is leading the charge, so we’ll give it the most attention. Then we’ll look at wind and energy storage. Each shows how learning curves and modularity are slashing costs and boosting efficiency across the board.
Solar’s power
From 1980 to 2017, 90% of the cost reduction in solar system [ https://substack.com/redirect/d86ca92f-60d5-44b7-bbd0-743086ebbf90?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] installation was due to hardware improvements. Cell efficiency, a measure of how effectively a solar panel converts sunlight into electricity, has seen remarkable progress. The first practical solar cell developed by Bell Labs in 1954 had an efficiency of just 6%, while today’s commercial cells typically achieve 15-22% [ https://substack.com/redirect/9e965bfa-7cb0-480b-9622-1fa3a9977d7f?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. Most current solar cells are crystalline silicon, but promising alternatives are emerging. Perovskite-based cells can reach over 30% efficiency [ https://substack.com/redirect/0ef7349b-2322-4426-8a8d-318b9f6a7f2d?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], with commercial perovskite-silicon tandems expected within 1-2 years. Research aims for 35%+ efficient, stable, low-cost cells using earth-abundant materials by 2030. Quantum dot technology had a breakthrough in 2024 with efficiency of 18% [ https://substack.com/redirect/e31b8028-99a6-46df-9945-8f7b37e09106?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. Organic solar cells hit 19.3% efficiency [ https://substack.com/redirect/42baaee1-f51c-46af-bd1b-c03e8e86b3f9?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] in the lab last year. 2D materials like graphene enhance charge transport and self-healing polymers [ https://substack.com/redirect/6254b707-0d03-44e8-b822-722a0a88349f?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] boost durability. The industry is shifting towards earth-abundant materials to reduce reliance on rare elements.
Solar PV efficiency, like fossil fuel combustion, is constrained by physics. While the theoretical Landsberg efficiency limit reaches 93% [ https://substack.com/redirect/ce5ac404-c463-46bd-82c3-793d2f9f27ff?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] under special conditions, real-world cells face unavoidable losses from spectral mismatch, heat generation and material imperfections. Even with advanced designs, surpassing 50% efficiency remains a formidable challenge.
Efficiency isn’t the whole story though. As of this month, solar module prices rival the cost of plywood at $0.095/W [ https://substack.com/redirect/14873801-e87d-412f-a7dc-6909b681e02f?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. The solar industry has been revolutionised by sweeping advances [ https://substack.com/redirect/8cc35b1b-997a-4c1c-ae52-6f83ae3047c9?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] in manufacturing technology. Larger photovoltaic modules now capture more sunlight, while innovations have dramatically reduced silicon usage, a key cost driver. Tooling throughput has increased up to tenfold, speeding up the production.
Perhaps most striking is the growth in manufacturing scale. At the turn of the millennium, a facility with a 25MW production [ https://substack.com/redirect/94ed13bd-443f-4912-bb2c-7e592e95e6d8?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] capacity was considered substantial. Fast forward to 2017 and Tesla’s Gigafactory 2 boasted a 2GW capacity [ https://substack.com/redirect/741e6063-ba85-4fb3-b1c5-6191d9ec09fb?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] — an 80-fold increase.
Blades of glory
Wind power has demonstrated a 15% learning rate. Despite their large size, turbines are modular—assembled from standardised blades, nacelles and towers—which makes manufacturing economies of scale possible. Cost reduction is largely driven by increasing turbine size. Taller hubs and longer rotors capture more energy at higher altitudes [ https://substack.com/redirect/90112ae0-9225-4549-933a-368cdcde5b75?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].
Over the past decade, average onshore wind turbine capacity has grown 82% [ https://substack.com/redirect/031a652f-3a2e-4447-a219-9cea37e6660d?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. This growth in power output has been accompanied by a significant 35% reduction in turbine prices. Future designs will push the boundaries further, with potential capacities exceeding 15MW [ https://substack.com/redirect/22a1ff86-365c-43ac-ac06-767b3457cd79?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. These next-generation turbines may harness superconducting generators or innovative gearbox designs, innovations that will drive costs down even further.
Ohm my god
Renewable energy integration heavily depends on effective energy storage. The most prominent technology in this field is lithium-ion batteries, which have shown impressive progress with a learning rate exceeding 20%. So, for every doubling of cumulative production, costs decrease by over 20%.
Lithium-ion cell costs fell by 77% from 2010 to 2020 [ https://substack.com/redirect/c6dbd802-bd7e-476d-8105-5b838731cd92?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], driven by cathode material savings (16.4%), energy density gains (15.3%) and electrode manufacturing improvements (14%). This trend of diverse, component-level efficiencies suggests no immediate barriers to further cost reductions in the coming decade.
But researchers are also exploring new battery chemistries that could push the performance further. Solid-state batteries, for instance, could have higher energy density, which could lead to more compact and efficient storage systems. Another promising avenue is sodium-ion batteries, which use more abundant materials and could significantly reduce costs. The recent deployment of a large-scale sodium-ion storage station in China [ https://substack.com/redirect/6fb70022-0e1a-4b1a-9997-5873acdd15f3?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] is one sign of this technology’s market readiness. Even within the established lithium-ion battery family, new formulations like lithium iron phosphate are emerging. These variants are helping to reduce dependence on critical minerals, making battery production more sustainable and potentially less expensive.
Six ways to reimagine energy
Here’s where we are: energy is transitioning from finite, geographically constrained commodities to ubiquitous, improving technologies. A paper in Nature last week argued that we’re already past the tipping point where solar has won [ https://substack.com/redirect/3cdfb891-7a32-41fc-b20a-2010d66f19d0?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].
1. Electricity will become the currency
You’ll notice I talk about energy when I really mean electricity. And that’s what will happen. Nearly everything (perhaps not rocket launches…) will become mediated by electricity. Even hydrogen or ammonia, if ever used as fuel, will be derivatives of an electrified industrial process. Electricity will become the currency: dollars per megawatt hour will replace dollars per barrel.
2. Economies of abundance
Nearly every country in the world can become an energy superpower. The sun shines favourably on nearly all of us. The great game of the Persian gulf will lose its relevance. So too will dastardly pipelines that straddle continents.
The result is a fundamentally different economic model: one of abundance rather than scarcity, of distributed production rather than centralised control.
3. Seasons are back
Rooftop solar contributed 101.7% of South Australia’s energy needs for 30 minutes on New Year’s Eve in 2023 [ https://substack.com/redirect/e59d5555-cb3f-476d-ae74-06025807871c?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. One function of solar, in particular, is that to make sure the system can provide on dark winter days, it will over provide in high summer. Some of this variability can be helped with by storage and distribution networks. But there will still likely to be an excess production of free (or low-cost) energy during summer months. It’ll be a perfect time to run energy intensive industrial processes like arc-furnaces, direct air capture or foundation model training. Seasons may be back!
4. Buy now, enjoy later
If the fossil energy system is like a taxi, with its meter always ticking. Renewables are buy now, enjoy later. Their upfront costs disguise cheaper electricity over time. Finance needs to step up to support every new entrant in the market from large-scale providers to you and me, reducing the upfront capital cost.
5. Need for speed
This internet-like electricity system will need new rules because the technologies behave differently. Governments need to speed up the implementation of locational pricing [ https://substack.com/redirect/991ce2c6-c4af-4c09-95bd-f8d09d4d585e?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], demand response [ https://substack.com/redirect/380791ee-9aa9-4ffd-84f3-0aa4f2c7010a?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] and creating ancillary service markets [ https://substack.com/redirect/8ed14d10-3bf8-4b60-88c9-5b052c9a33ff?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] for grid flexibility. But even without those changes, as the German experience of the past decade has shown [ https://substack.com/redirect/286b7a31-9b0c-47d4-a289-f02df60727bf?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], a grid with greater solar and wind assets can be more stable than one with lower.
6. The right transition
Unlike fossil fuels, constrained by finite supply, renewables tap into effectively limitless sources – the sun and wind. The amount of solar energy hitting Earth in just under three minutes is equal to humanity’s entire annual electricity usage source. Those renewables are harnessed by technologies, behaving in ways that Moore’s Law technologies have, cheaper and more ubiquitous every year.
Energy is becoming a technology, harnessing that bounty. And technology is things getting cheaper. So the benefits of energy—prosperity, health and quality of life—are on the cusp of getting much, much cheaper.
To get those benefits quickly, we need to reframe our thinking and shake off centuries of entrenched beliefs. But our mental models must evolve from the extractive logic of commodities to the expansive potential of technologies.
For policymakers, investors and consumers alike, the imperative is clear: reframe your knowledge, recalibrate your strategies and welcome the age of energy as technology.
