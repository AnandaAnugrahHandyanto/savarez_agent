# 🌐 Tariffs are the tremors, systemic change is the earthquake

**From:** "Azeem Azhar, Exponential View" <exponentialview@substack.com>
**Date:** 2025-04-12T09:42:56.000Z
**Folder:** exponential

---

View this post on the web at https://www.exponentialview.co/p/tariffs-are-the-tremors-systemic

Globalization has grown exponentially. Our linear institutions, norms, values and systems could not adequately keep up.
It is a case of what I call the “exponential gap [ https://substack.com/redirect/5063be15-ce12-4c1a-a4c9-eeceb3b56635?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]”, the chasm that emerges between rapidly developing new technologies and the slower rate at which our existing corporations, institutions, employees, politics, and wider social norms adapt to these changes. In this case, the bundle of technologies, some digital, physical, social and legal technologies, is called “globalisation”.
This set of technologies refactors how the world operates faster than our existing institutions can adapt. At some point, something would have to give.
The last 30 years have since two parties spin the flywheel of globalisation faster. On the one, the US consumer, who has kept spending, is supported by common-sense decisions by businesses to reduce their costs. However, those short-term decisions have turned into long-term structural problems. Like plaque on teeth, they are layered and layered.
China is the best place to make an iPhone and forget the cost. The know-how, over 20 years, is there. The ecosystem, with its codes, cues and knowledge, is there. Hear Tim Cook on this. [ https://substack.com/redirect/4800b6c7-57eb-4544-9a2b-5f7837076b65?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] Mark Gurman goes into much more detail [ https://substack.com/redirect/2d09f5f7-c516-4ac8-9244-8302c994504f?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. Moving advanced manufacturing of scale takes time, longer than a Presidential term.
The other party, the Chinese, turned their economy into a supply-side juggernaut. It was fuelled not by domestic consumption, even though there was an enormous number of increasingly rich consumers, but through exporting to the rest of the world. The Chinese consumer doesn’t. And their relative unwillingness to spend has been recognised as a problem for a while [ https://substack.com/redirect/c1da1e69-c9c9-4219-b30e-5498eadb3988?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].
The Chinese kept exporting. Over successive decades, not years, leaders in neither country attempted to trim their sails. Certainly not in Beijing [ https://substack.com/redirect/c1da1e69-c9c9-4219-b30e-5498eadb3988?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]:
China’s structurally-high domestic saving rate is the result of a decades-long development strategy in which income is effectively transferred from households to subsidise the supply side of the economy — the production of goods and services. As a result of these transfers, growth in household income has long lagged behind productivity growth, leaving Chinese households unable to consume much of what they produce
Meanwhile, across the Pacific, Americans happily continued to import and consume, import and consumer. As Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick says, “We buy everyone’s stuff [ https://substack.com/redirect/e2bbbe91-64a3-4b72-9145-42961d10250f?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].”
These behaviours have had a cumulative effect. Chinese manufacturers are the most capable globally and lead across various high-tech sectors.  Successive American Presidents did not meaningfully—and coherently—address the dependency they were building on a strategic rival.
The distribution impact
This global dynamic, driven by the exponential gap, didn’t just reshape international relations. It has other profound impacts. Consider, for example, the uneven effect on wealth distribution within nations like the US.
If we go back just over 30 years to 1994, the start of NAFTA, and a year before WTO would replace the GATT, globalisation contributed to a vast increase in America’s wealth, growing more than six-fold to $160 trillion.
For the top 1%, garnered about a third of that $135 trillion increase in national wealth. Their wealth increased at a compounded rate of 7% as a cohort, with the richest amongst them, the top 0.1%, enjoying even more significant accumulation.
There were gains for the bottom 50%, the thin yellow sliver on the chart. They pootled in at 5.1% per annum on average. There is nothing so powerful as compound interest, and that delta of 2% yearly means a lot.
It’s unclear to me that free trade necessarily results in such a distribution of the gains. It seems like in-country distribution is more a case of fiscal, welfare and investment choices that a nation can make for itself. As Ray Dalio points out, “The [US] domestic political order is breaking down due to huge gaps in people's education levels, opportunity levels, productivity levels, income and wealth levels, and values—and because of the ineffectiveness of the existing political order to fix things.”
Whatever the reason, here we are.
Tariffs are the wood, systems change is the forest
What is going on is much broader than tariffs. It’s about technology, alliances and what people in their respective countries feel about their prospects. It’s complex mix of, often conflicting pressures.
In short, it’s about a systemic change in our geopolitical and technological regime, the type of change I described in my book [ https://substack.com/redirect/5063be15-ce12-4c1a-a4c9-eeceb3b56635?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. Tariffs and trade wars are merely visible tremors signalling a deeper tectonic shift – symptoms of a profound disequilibrium, not its root cause.
When the environment shifts so fundamentally, established survival strategies falter. In evolutionary terms, we’ve transitioned from a long period of exploitation to a necessary, often messy, phase of exploration.
During the ‘exploitation’ phase – corresponding perhaps to the peak decades of late 20th and early 21st-century globalization – the environment seemed relatively stable. Nations and corporations behaved like species perfectly adapted to their niche. They refined existing processes, optimized known resources (like global supply chains and established trade rules), and efficiently 'exploited' the predictable landscape for maximum gain. Success lay in efficiency and optimization within the existing paradigm.
But now, the environment – the geopolitical climate, technological ground, and distribution of resources and power – is changing rapidly. The old equilibrium is gone. This forces an ‘exploration’ phase, characterized by:
Increased Variation: Actors (nations, firms) must experiment with new strategies, alliances, and technologies. Think of mutations in a population facing environmental stress – some will fail, and some might offer a path forward. Tariffs, industrial policy, reshoring initiatives, the race for AI dominance – these are all forms of exploration.
Search for New Fitness Landscapes: The established paths to success (fitness) are eroding. Exploration involves searching for new sources of stability and advantage – perhaps regional blocs, technological self-sufficiency, or different models of economic growth.
Uncertainty and Risk: Exploration is inherently less efficient and carries higher risks than exploiting a known environment. As different strategies clash, there will be failed experiments, dead ends, and heightened competition.
Emergence of New Norms: Eventually, successful exploratory strategies will coalesce into new, stable patterns of behaviour and interaction – a new set of norms adapted to the changed environment. This phase seeks to discover what the next ‘stable state’ might look like.
Understanding that we are in this volatile ‘exploration’ phase, rather than simply tweaking the old ‘exploitation’ model, is crucial. Our posture must shift from optimization to adaptation, from efficiency to resilience, as we collectively search for the contours of the next geopolitical and technological era.
To make sense of what happens next and the different exploratory paths being taken, I want to share some interesting things I’ve read.
Ray Dalio: [ https://substack.com/redirect/f77781fc-96b7-4e4f-9f02-91f1628879b8?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] Don’t Make the Mistake of Thinking That What’s Now Happening is Mostly About Tariffs: “the far bigger, far more important thing to keep in mind is that we are seeing a classic breakdown of the major monetary, political, and geopolitical orders.”

Niall Ferguson [ https://substack.com/redirect/552bb722-f791-4ec0-bea8-32fd41b3ec49?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]: “[America’s] problems can’t be blamed on trade policy when they have much more to do with an unsustainable policy mix ranging from public finance to education.”
Ultimately, the fixation on tariffs misses the forest for the trees. As Dalio suggests, we’re witnessing a potential breakdown of established orders, exacerbated by globalization’s uneven impact and domestic policy shortcomings highlighted by Niall Ferguson . China’s strategic pivot towards innovation, which long preceded these tensions, shows the game is shifting.
Therefore, a simple ‘deal’ focused on old metrics is insufficient. The challenge lies in understanding the dynamics of this exploratory phase and developing strategies – national and corporate – suited for navigating prolonged uncertainty and constructing the next, likely very different, global order.
