Execution is cheap
Over the break, I spun up multiple agents in parallel — one building a fact-checker, another an EV slide deck maker and a third a solar forecast model. All ran simultaneously in the background while I did other work. I described the problems, LLMs create detailed product specs and the agents made the apps. In my first meeting back, I demoed two dozen working tools to the team. This follows my rule of 5x [ https://substack.com/redirect/83584d50-f897-4af4-b996-a91acb207532?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] – if I do something more than five times, I build an app for it. A year ago, each of those apps would have cost a developer weeks to build.
My friend Erik Brynjolfsson  calls the human in this arrangement the “Chief Question Officer [ https://substack.com/redirect/e4b856ab-7996-413f-8df4-40834a9299cc?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].” We ask, machines do, we evaluate. Erik’s framing is elegant, but I don’t think it’s true to the moment; we’ve moved even further. I used to check every output against a strict spec; now I mostly trust the agent to catch and fix its own mistakes – and it usually does. Before Opus 4.5, I had to rescue the model from dead ends. Now it asks good clarifying questions, corrects itself and rarely stalls.
This velocity changes behaviors. For instance, I used to frame briefs carefully; now I leave them a bit looser because the agent fills the gaps. I remain the Chief, yet the role feels like a pilot toggling autopilot ever higher. If progress continues, will I always occupy the cockpit? Would stepping aside, ceding the questions as well as the answers, actually increase what gets built?
Erik warns of the “Turing Trap,” the temptation for firms to use AI to mimic and replace humans. He frames this as a societal choice between augmentation and replacement. I agree we shouldn’t drift into replacement. But my holiday build sprint made it clear that convenience pulls hard. The pressure isn’t just from companies; it’s from us, users making choices. When each small handoff to the agent feels free, can we really resist going all the way to full automation?

Capital gains, labor pains
The US economy has decoupled growth from hiring (see EV [ https://substack.com/redirect/598b06e8-1a3b-4aa6-98e2-7b8119acb16c?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]#545 [ https://substack.com/redirect/598b06e8-1a3b-4aa6-98e2-7b8119acb16c?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]). While the Atlanta Fed projects a massive 5.4% GDP surge [ https://substack.com/redirect/bbc5f73b-20f7-402d-8220-039e10d3c34d?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] for Q4 2025, the labor market has effectively stalled, adding only 584,000 jobs all year [ https://substack.com/redirect/7e75ec9f-dc66-4b10-bb13-994b4817fc73?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. This is the worst non-recession performance since 2003. This divergence is driven by acceleration in productivity and back-to-back quarterly declines in unit labor costs [ https://substack.com/redirect/255aec49-e6d3-4978-aae7-3183cf700381?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. Is AI the cause?
Possibly. Rather than expanding workforces, companies seem to be “increasingly rely[ing] on technology to maintain output with leaner staffing”, says Bloomberg. Perhaps contrary to the narrative that an America that builds will hire tradespeople, blue-collar employment is declining as well [ https://substack.com/redirect/99af28f4-fd0b-45a1-af0c-5a36943b40f5?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], about 0.4% below its 2025 peak.
For policymakers, particularly at the Federal Reserve, this is a vital development; falling labor costs imply that wage increases are not translating into the kind of cost-push inflation that often forces central banks to keep interest rates high. This emphasis on productivity might have banished the inflation spectre, even temporarily.
But does it mark a deeper risk from AI — the decoupling of economic expansion and labor demand? AI and compute scaling could be a plausible cause, but in truth, you don’t need the frontier AI models that have made the headlines for this to happen. The data doesn’t yet uniquely identify AI as the cause; it could also reflect demand dynamics, cyclicality, or some other non-AI changes.
Amazon’s data shows a growing robot workforce and a static human one. And more tellingly, the labor share of US GDP has fallen to 54%, [ https://substack.com/redirect/4af0aba5-8795-4738-98fc-9dfa7efa8cd1?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] the lowest since records began nearly 70 years ago. A portent? Perhaps.
AI company = energy company
My New York Times piece a year ago argued that the US energy grid is the most significant physical constraint on the US AI build-out [ https://substack.com/redirect/d06dbe92-8cc4-4cce-960d-57a1a8823464?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. A year later, I’m still watching for signs that the bottleneck is clearing. It isn’t, but the labs are responding by vertically integrating into energy production itself.
In Texas alone, tens of gigawatts of data center load requests pour in each month, yet barely a gigawatt has been approved in the past year. AI infrastructure economics don’t accommodate five-year interconnection queues. A 400 MW data center coming online six months earlier means billions in revenue. The labs had to adapt by going backwards through the supply chain.
First, they bypassed the grid. xAI led the way, powering Colossus with truck-mounted gas turbines. Hyperscalers followed one by one. SemiAnalysis  now tracks 12 suppliers  [ https://substack.com/redirect/0cb8e3d2-fbff-4c2a-886b-9e1b428286fd?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]that have each secured over 400 MW of US data center orders for on-site gas generation. OpenAI and Oracle placed a 2.3 GW order in October. Today, only 6% of data centers have co-located generation. But of planned capacity, Wood Mackenzie finds that a third now incorporates [ https://substack.com/redirect/b4f76738-70ca-4407-b070-fd223eb2ff9f?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] on-site power. The data center is becoming a microgrid.
Then they hit the next wall, the turbine factories. GE Vernova and Siemens are largely booked through 2027–28 [ https://substack.com/redirect/fdce893e-cefa-48c7-9b74-a7544af8a78e?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], with conversations now focused on 2029-30 delivery. The labs outran the grid only to find themselves waiting on manufacturing capacity.
When that model hit constraints, they started buying the infrastructure builders themselves. Last month, Alphabet agreed to acquire Intersect Power, a clean energy developer, for $4.75 billion [ https://substack.com/redirect/7629cc1b-8fff-4c8d-8713-cb9900b7681a?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. If you can’t wait for the grid and you can’t wait for turbines, buy the companies that build the infrastructure.
This is the logic of AI scale that we’ve been pointing out for months: whoever controls energy, controls compute.
