# 🔥 Five forces set to upend the global order

When I look back on our recent history—from around 2014 to today—I see an era that future historians will call an inflection point. Much as the 1920s and 1930s fundamentally differed from the world of the late 1800s, we’re going through a similarly significant transition now. We have game-changing technologies and volatile geopolitics. In my view, this period will be studied as the moment when economic and political models were reshaped.
I wrote a book [ https://substack.com/redirect/cff9c7cb-42cd-441f-9d5e-541c002bc3c3?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] about this in 2022; if you’re new to my work you can pick it up here [ https://substack.com/redirect/cff9c7cb-42cd-441f-9d5e-541c002bc3c3?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].
I draw heavily on the work of economist Carlota Perez [ https://substack.com/redirect/6f105112-b0ce-4cb3-b68e-a556f5fe0fc5?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] who outlined how the changing underlying technological paradigm brings with it financial speculation, bubbles and wealth accumulation. It requires an institutional recomposition which fundamentally heralds a golden age.
Things are breaking apart, but also recombining in new ways. Yes, it is about tech but it’s also about deeper social, cultural and economic realignments.
I hosted a briefing for the Exponential View members community this week about my five-to-ten-year horizon scan. I’m sharing the recording today for those who missed it and detailed notes to help you get through the dense hour we spent together.
Thanks to all who joined me – I’ll soon post dates for the next community gathering in February. If you’re not a member yet, this is your chance to change that and join the community.
My five themes in focus:
Society of AI
Energy as technology
Rising domestic agendas
Geofragmentation
Social and cultural “vibe shift”
Let’s go through each…
1. The shift to a society of AI
My own AI usage has exploded over the last 18 months, and I’m not alone.
State of the art tools keep smashing benchmarks and my personal conversations with frontier researchers confirm they don’t see a near-term “wall” in AI progress. Yes, training is expensive at first, but engineers keep finding ways to optimise.
However, the bigger sticking point is productisation and integration. We don’t yet have polished workflows that allow complex, multi-agent setups to hum flawlessly. My team and I try using agentic systems for research, editing and argument-structuring—but it’s still cumbersome. Yet I have no doubt we’ll look back and wonder how we ever managed without AI handling routine tasks.
Ultimately, this reshapes organizations. When electrification replaced steam power in factories, people had to redesign workflows around smaller, distributed motors. AI is our “electricity of thought”.
At the same time, I’m aware of a growing cultural friction—people who embrace AI wholeheartedly could pull ahead [ https://substack.com/redirect/9416dd28-b3e1-4e50-917f-ca6553696da2?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] in productivity, while others might opt out for personal or ideological reasons. We may end up with a deep divide between “AI natives” and “AI abstainers.”
2. Energy shifting from commodity to technology
Whenever I assess the energy landscape, I compare it to the telecom world 30 years ago. We used to have expensive, centralised phone monopolies. Then the internet arrived—cheaper, modular and wildly innovative. Renewable energy is doing the same: solar, wind and batteries have moved energy from a commodity to a technology [ https://substack.com/redirect/5f4a8afd-1161-4666-9dca-768dc00e3626?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. That means costs can drop drastically over time and power generation or storage can be increasingly decentralised.
This shift holds profound implications.
Where previously only a few nations had real “energy sovereignty,” small communities and even individuals can now generate or store energy locally. Opportunities also balloon in software and market design—real-time pricing, virtual power plants, peer-to-peer energy trading and so on.
It’s crucial to realize this goes well beyond “defossilisation.” We’re not just replacing coal with solar. We’re building an interactive, technology-based ecosystem, much more akin to the internet than the old power grid. Over five to ten years, many wealthy economies will complete major steps in this direction, though big questions remain over things like regulation, permitting, and supply-chain friction (e.g. transformer shortages [ https://substack.com/redirect/7940d3d6-d56c-46c1-bdaf-e480c8c32adb?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]).
3. Make your country great again – the rise of domestic agendas
In the political realm, I see a reversion to the mean. After decades of globalism and cosmopolitan thinking, many countries—from the US to Argentina, Canada to India—are pivoting inward, focusing on nationalism or populist policies. That isn’t inherently surprising; if you zoom out, these pendulum swings happen – although we’re at the highest level since 1930s.
It explains a lot of our current political sentiment.
And you can see it when Mark Zuckerberg [ https://substack.com/redirect/50cfec84-d4fb-4293-bef3-b9f28eacea92?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] emphasized “American values” as Meta got out of the business of fact-checking. The really interesting moment though was when Zuck looked down the barrel of the lens and asked for help from Trump “to push back on governments around the world.”
They’re going after American companies and pushing to censor more. The US has the strongest constitutional protections for free expression in the world. Europe has an ever-increasing number of laws institutionalising censorship and making it difficult to build anything innovative there.
That’s a stark shift from the global regulatory negotiations Big Tech entertained even five years ago. In Europe especially, I sense a struggle to align national priorities with the broader EU framework. Given Europe’s layered governance and demographic challenges, it may find it tough to chart a unified, innovative path in the short term.
4. Rising geofragmentation
The “G-Zero” era, borrowing from my friend Ian Bremmer , is that of a multipolar world where no single power or coalition effectively sets global rules. The alternative narrative is “G2” (China vs. US), but it’s a more fragmented picture: many medium-sized powers, each asserting its own strategies, some challenging existing norms.
On a practical level, that fragmentation is palpable. Whether it’s export controls on semiconductors, countries demanding their own version of data sovereignty, or the fracturing of net-zero finance alliances, we’re losing the sense of a “tidy” global consensus.
Meanwhile, China continues innovating at an incredible pace, pushing through stealth fighters, advanced rail, the world’s largest hydropower plant and AI [ https://substack.com/redirect/f7d86ad3-0495-4ce6-8802-93ceecdf5771?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], partly spurred by external pressure. It drives home that each region is going to make its own rules and push for self-reliance. That puts more onus on business leaders, policymakers and investors to keep channels of communication open.
In fact, in this world of deep fragmentation, we need to talk A LOT.
5. The vibe shift
Where once we worried about digital divides — who has internet access and who doesn’t — the new divide is about who chooses to live augmented by AI and technology, call them the amplified, and those who don’t, call them the organic. Some of us will likely believe that life is richer through the support of these technologies and others will want to reject them as far as they can.
This social-cognitive split will be ideological and hard to bridge especially as firms and bureaucracies will increasingly rely on AI to do whatever it is they do. Neither side, the “amplified” and the “organic”, will really be able to persuade each other. Perhaps this is where classic liberalism will shine once more.
Q&A
Here’s a selection of questions members asked during the briefing and my answers:
“What about agentic AI—can we trust it?”
Interestingly, multi-agent workflows can be more auditable, because each agent’s actions can log precisely what they do. The difficulty is designing the right guardrails. Startups like Mission Control are building monitoring tools for organizations deploying these AI “teams.”
“Can Europe remain competitive?”
There’s no question Europe led on regulatory frameworks (e.g., GDPR), yet it is just moribund when it comes to innovation and growth. Fragmented decision-making and policy inertia are major headwinds. A real strategy to reward innovation, support entrepreneurial risk, and adopt advanced technologies at scale is still lacking.
More European governments need to wake up to the changing rules. A nation’s position in this new world, whether a maker or a taker, isn’t something inherited. It’ll be something earned.
“What about nuclear energy?”
I’m not opposed to nuclear. In fact, I invested in a company focusing on advanced uranium enrichment. The promise is there, but the timelines for new builds in the West are slow, especially compared to the pace of renewables. Over the next decade, nuclear’s share is likely to stay modest outside a few pockets (China, South Korea, UAE, France).
“Where are the hidden risks?”
The one that keeps popping up for me—outside the standard conversation about AI risk—is demographics. Birth rates in many developed nations are below replacement level. This is a big deal. This should be a huge conversation: does this matter and if it does, why is it happening? And what should be done?
The takeaway is that we’re not just witnessing isolated changes in technology, energy, or geopolitics. We’re likely experiencing the end of a old technological, political and economic, after which something new will emerge.
