# 🤔 How serious is Google’s ChatGPT problem?

**From:** "Azeem Azhar, Exponential View" <exponentialview@substack.com>
**Date:** 2025-06-28T03:45:18.000Z
**Folder:** exponential

---

View this post on the web at https://www.exponentialview.co/p/how-serious-is-googles-chatgpt-problem

Two years ago, I argued that Alphabet, which owns Google, faced a “GPT Tidal Wave [ https://substack.com/redirect/cf6ea47e-6140-40c9-aab8-66badaa1e46c?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]” because “the start page of the Internet is shifting further from the browser and Google.com, replacing dozens or more Web searches each day. ChatGPT is preferable to open multiple tabs from a Google search and continuously backtracking.”
I’m an early adopter. Early adopters are either canaries in the coal mine or we’re wrong. Two years on, the data are starting to suggest we’re right. Slides from the investment firm Coatue circulated last week, showing what many of us sense anecdotally: once you adopt ChatGPT, you use Google less.
Across a still-short observation window, heavy ChatGPT users have cut Google’s page views by about 8% a year. That may feel mild, yet if the 800 million ChatGPT users today grow—plausibly—to three billion within three years, and if the search deficit holds, Google’s core business could shrink by a fifth, lopping tens of billions of dollars off annual revenue.
In truth, that is the bullish scenario for Google. ChatGPT is fast becoming the generic verb for “finding stuff,” and its advantage widens on difficult queries—which may be the very ones that anchor Google’s pricing power. The products will only get better at serving users’ needs, so that 8% figure could rise.
Fresh data from Britain’s competition watchdog, the CMA, reinforce the picture. For long-form questions, 17% of Britons already default to ChatGPT; they still turn to Google for simple, local, “tree-surgeon-near-me” look-ups. We do not yet know where the money lies—complex, ad-light queries or transactional, ad-heavy ones—but user behavior rarely plateaus.
I’ve highlighted a couple of important factors in yellow. Once someone becomes an “AI user,” AI starts to eat into complex and shopping queries.
Personally, my use of ChatGPT has snowballed. It not only cannibalizes my Google and Wikipedia time but also coaxes me to ask questions I once deemed too fiddly to type into a search box. Digital history’s graveyard—who kept both MySpace and Facebook profiles running for long?—shows that once a superior interface hits critical mass, dual loyalties fade fast. Usage, in other words, accelerates away from the incumbent.
Mountain View’s gambit
Google is not standing idle. Its AI Overviews now headline many results pages, and Gemini already serves roughly 400 million monthly users (according to Similarweb).  And as the table shows, month-on-month growth is scorching.
That is serious traction and a textbook example of an incumbent launching a product that partially competes with its own cash cow.
Kudos. Yet the manoeuvre muddles incentives: every query answered by Gemini inside Google’s walls is one not monetised by the traditional ads-against-links model.
Moreover, there is a vast difference between how people use Gemini and how they use ChatGPT. Similarweb data also show that the average ChatGPT visit is 60 times longer than a visit directly to the Gemini app. (With a strong caveat that users can interact with Gemini embedded through Google’s products.)
Apple could further skew the calculus. Should Cupertino ship high-quality, on-device LLMs, a large chunk of iPhone traffic might leapfrog both Google and OpenAI. The result: discovery becomes local, private and ad-light.
Classic network effects do not favor any large-language-model chatbot. Unlike social networks, LLM utilities do not yet become more valuable because our friends join; nor, crucially, do websites optimize themselves for ChatGPT crawling. Google’s formidable moat arose because publishers tuned pages for its index, exchanging free content for human traffic. Today, LLM crawlers devour pages yet return vanishingly few visitors. The economic pact that funds the open web is fraying—and that, not mere query share, may be Google’s deepest vulnerability.
Users vote with attention
Retention data tell the tale: despite buggy UX and no social stickiness, ChatGPT keeps users unusually well. People like the conversational mode and the time it saves.
Back in 2023, with a much less capable product and less capable underlying models, ChatGPT’s one-month retention rate was 56%, which was well below that of Instagram or TikTok, but on par with Roblox. Paid-for retention [ https://substack.com/redirect/e0553a46-c20a-4165-8803-d86f7184a64b?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] after six months runs to 71%, roughly 9%-20% ahead of its competitors.
That preference stacks atop the quantitative erosion in search share, compounding Google’s headache.
It’s a new web
Google is the world’s most profitable firm. Even if its search—and ad-network—businesses are under pressure, it still enjoys an incredibly powerful franchise in YouTube and a decent one in cloud services.
To its credit, the company has adapted to the changing environment and is now centred around Demis Hassabis’s AI leadership. Demis was the world’s best competitive player of strategy games. And it shows.
Models are getting better; products are being shipped. Generative AI now sits inside Google’s eponymous search engine—the start page of the internet for a quarter of a century.
Google’s ambition was always to put AI at the heart of its business. Here is Larry Page discussing it nearly 25 years ago, back when Google’s enormous computing infrastructure amounted to 6,000 servers. Page said:
Artificial intelligence would be the ultimate version of Google…it would understand everything on the web it would know exactly what you wanted and it would give you the right thing
But the technology wasn’t ready. The world was all about pages of text connected by links. In those decades, computing has exponentialized, with processing six orders of magnitude cheaper than when Page said those words.
The grammar of the internet is shifting from links to language. Yet the company made trillions of dollars by being the best at making sense of that linky-web.
That web is going, going, gone.
Google is too big to disappear with it.
The question is: what will it become?
