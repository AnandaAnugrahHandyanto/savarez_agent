AI, jobs and productivity
The St. Louis FED estimates [ https://substack.com/redirect/e62d1071-a742-41da-9ce4-438cd465b357?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] that generative AI may have increased labor productivity by up to 1.3% – and has had positive effects on industry-level productivity. While that may sound modest, a sustained 1.3% annual productivity boost would be transformative.
We’re seeing the micro‑signals of this shift inside firms. A new paper [ https://substack.com/redirect/74ae7341-5da3-410f-a279-426bde273eec?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] found that AI agents increased code output by 39% without any decline in short-term quality. More telling: for every standard deviation increase in experience, developers accept 6% more agent‑generated code. Senior engineers are simply better at delegation, and delegation is a skill that compounds with time. As AI takes on more execution, the premium – particularly for those entering the workforce – will move from raw output to orchestration.
I dug into this question with Ben Zweig of Revelio Labs [ https://substack.com/redirect/dbe33e49-2d86-48d8-be8f-e33453d22116?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], which builds its own data-driven view of the labor market. Ben and I share the view that it’s probably too early to really see AI impact in the early hiring. Ben’s perspective is that softening in early hiring is coming from somewhere else:
there’s a lot of uncertainty in the labor market, partially due to AI, partially due to policy. As a result, employers have a high discount rate. Young workers are the workers that are more uncertain. So, it’s a kind of high-risk, high-reward type of bet, whereas more experienced hires are the safer bets.

Movers:
CoreWeave cut its 2025 revenue forecast to $5.05–$5.15 billion [ https://substack.com/redirect/a6388333-c385-428e-a213-2b66b458b5a6?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] after delays in building new data centers. Investors did not like that; its shares fell by nearly a quarter. The cost of protecting against a CoreWeave default, measured by a 10‑year CDS, has jumped more than 60% over the last six weeks to 700 basis points [ https://substack.com/redirect/5b37fba7-3cd3-4d2c-bd37-adc05a037876?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. Likewise, Meta signaled AI spending could exceed $100 billion next year [ https://substack.com/redirect/074b6dfa-a150-4291-b31d-b77e88621116?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]; shares fell 12.6%.
New details have emerged on the complex funding behind Meta, OpenAI and xAI’s data center deals [ https://substack.com/redirect/35f3065b-7835-42c2-b7e1-2f784bae3090?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. In the cases of OpenAI and xAI, the structures shift most financial risk onto project‑finance vehicles, bank syndicates and private‑credit funds. Meta’s structure is different: the debt sits off its balance sheet and protects its credit rating, but the make‑whole guarantee means Meta still ultimately bears the tail risk. The economic risk stays with Meta, even as the reported risk is dispersed across its creditors. Debt will continue to rise to pay for the infrastructure build‑out. Data center ABS is up 66% year over year [ https://substack.com/redirect/c639eebf-2c4c-4c5b-8a72-839218956861?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] to $19.3 billion, and further help from the credit markets will be needed to plug the $1.5 trillion global data center financing gap, according to Morgan Stanley [ https://substack.com/redirect/4ad1b44f-1d45-4719-bbd1-5dde89d2f52c?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].
Another point of tension is chip depreciation [ https://substack.com/redirect/03467e96-ee78-468a-bad1-20e35fb1f460?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. On paper, the hyperscalers depreciate their GPU investments over four to five years. The trouble is that for frontier training, those chips are truly competitive for only 18-24 months before they drop a generation behind. Quick run of the numbers: a $100 billion GPU build‑out depreciated over four years shows up as $25 billion of annual cost; if frontier life is closer to two years, the economic cost of that capacity is more like $50 billion a year. The chips do not go straight to landfill (seven‑ and eight‑year‑old Google TPUs still run at 100% utilization). But that does not change the core tension: the premium, frontier‑grade usefulness of a chip decays in half the time the accountants assume.
Anthropic projects to be cash‑flow positive in 2027 [ https://substack.com/redirect/b1fb040d-7b11-46fd-bc0e-b0785e416bff?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. Optionality beats scale
AI will need about $1.5 trillion worth of compute over the next three years [ https://substack.com/redirect/686fded8-ef6d-4297-862f-29355ca5995c?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. By next year, we will see the first 1 GW data centers [ https://substack.com/redirect/9a20b1b5-0f91-450c-8e46-a6d6e72d57a4?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], enough to power one million homes. And not just one data center; there will be five of those.
The speed of build‑out is extraordinary, but it’s colliding with physical and organizational constraints, like labor, grids, power density, cooling and planning capacity. Here are quotes Barclays analysts collected from a data center conference (as reported in the  [ https://substack.com/redirect/7b359074-7151-4e3c-a420-a164d32dc4f4?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]FT [ https://substack.com/redirect/7b359074-7151-4e3c-a420-a164d32dc4f4?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]):
There aren’t enough bodies and resources to do what we’re expected to do.
Some utilities can’t even manage the email inbox requests from data center developers.
Projects are singularly focused on what they need, not the greater impact on the grid.
Budget isn’t the limiting factor.
Most operators are still acting as if these constraints can be bulldozed with capex. One of the few companies behaving as if it recognizes the limits is Microsoft, as it quietly eased off the accelerator earlier this year [ https://substack.com/redirect/2288d501-9314-4ec0-9a71-f0105927fa0b?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. It is still betting on explosive training and serving demand but refuses to lock billions [ https://substack.com/redirect/b171d3c5-f5ee-4b01-8c56-543ff2d58175?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] into data centers sized for the wrong power envelope. Microsoft is trading raw scale for optionality –they are releasing some sites, reshuffling others and leaning on third‑party capacity where demand is clear. The risk is then taken on by neoclouds (Microsoft has committed $60 billion [ https://substack.com/redirect/2c924b1b-69a2-4307-9bda-1a692c15b457?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]) and by Oracle, which is building specifically for one big AI tenant (OpenAI).

Slopping in the free world
An AI‑generated song targeting asylum seekers (“We Say No, No, No to an Asylum Seekers’ Centre”) briefly topped Spotify’s Viral 50 this week before disappearing. Even so, it underscores how AI’s political footprint is widening fast. There’s pressure on at least three fronts:
Front one: Slopaganda wins on volume and Taiwan’s recent KMT chair race was a preview. Analyst Mandarin Peel  documented how influence operators  [ https://substack.com/redirect/61fec1d6-612a-47e8-ad1b-260f4fe17dcc?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]flooded YouTube with crude AI-generated videos, activated dormant channels suddenly, and designed an illusion of momentum. The goal was to overwhelm and make a manufactured consensus feel real. See EV#537 for our previous commentary on the firehose of distortion [ https://substack.com/redirect/a98774c9-da45-4fda-877b-912fffeb1d7a?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].
Front two: Russia poisoning AI’s training data. Intelligence suggests that Russian operatives are now building content farms optimized for AI [ https://substack.com/redirect/36f68ea6-c5d9-4ad2-9801-2d63d1b0c37e?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], systematically seeding the datasets that train language models. The objective is to shift what AI considers “normal” discourse and warp the baseline of truth.
Front three: AI agents are now operational assets. Anthropic disclosed the first confirmed case of a state-sponsored group deploying autonomous AI agents (Claude Code) for cyber espionage to breach multiple major institutions [ https://substack.com/redirect/356aa371-8162-40bf-8eff-e45abe2ad5c4?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. They estimate that 80-90% of tactical operations were executed by the AI with minimal human oversight.
