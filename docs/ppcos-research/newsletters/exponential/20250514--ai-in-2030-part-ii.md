# 🔮 AI in 2030: Part II

In 2020, the most powerful publicly-known AI model needed a small data center to train. By 2029, the same job will fit on a laptop because of an interlocking layer of exponential trends, gifting us more processing power and more efficient software.
When we put numbers to that stack of exponentials, the future stops being speculative; it becomes, to some extent, scheduled.
Azeem described what that might feel like in his essay over the weekend. [ https://substack.com/redirect/5166a40f-52c6-463d-8ae3-569d9a515d8a?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] Your phone running GPT-5-class models, accessible whether you are online or not. Agents will handle tasks that would take you weeks of deep work for less than a dollar.
But what gives us the confidence to say any of that? Words are easy to put on the page. To build greater certainty, we looked at the drivers of the underlying capabilities of AI systems: the availability of compute and the improvement in models and how they might match up.
How likely is it that the specific trends we’ve experienced in these different domains continue? And what happens if they do?
Here’s a detailed breakdown to help you understand the path from here to there.
The compute explosion
Capital is racing ahead of Moore’s Law. AI infrastructure, particularly data center packed with accelerator silicon, now represents the single largest slice of the capital expenditure budgets across hyperscalers. Microsoft currently leads the pack, having amassed 485,000 of Nvidia’s $25,000 Hopper chips. They are closely trailed by Meta’s ~350,000 H100s, scaling towards an installed base equivalent to nearly 600,000 H100s.
This build-up precedes even more substantial expansions like the Stargate project, a $500 billion AI infrastructure investment targeting more than one million GPUs by 2029, drawing 5 GW of power, about the same as Los Angeles at peak times. It isn’t just the US – Saudi Arabia announced Humain, an AI company [ https://substack.com/redirect/bf9c960b-12e2-4ebd-8a18-481c5b5baa17?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], with an investment of $15 billion from AWS and AMD. This complements a deployment of 18,000 [ https://substack.com/redirect/e9cfa419-68fe-42f5-b889-f65c7ad4a52b?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] of Nvidia’s state-of-the-art GB300s. The UAE has signalled its own ambitions [ https://substack.com/redirect/89d1b6ab-e737-4e6f-bf87-a9c3ae0e7ae1?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].
This accelerated growth of compute used specifically for training frontier AI models surpasses the trajectory historically observed under Moore’s Law. For context, Grok-3 alone was estimated [ https://substack.com/redirect/79518fa6-6be1-48db-885a-db1f6dc75867?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] to be trained with roughly 4-5 × 10²⁶ FLOPS – approximately 1,300-1,600 times the compute used by GPT-3. The pace and scale of recent advancements is extraordinary.
Epoch AI , a research group, found [ https://substack.com/redirect/4f9948b6-02c1-4a80-9ba8-598b96b21f7c?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] that compute training was doubling every 5.4 months, substantially outpacing the traditional Moore’s Law doubling interval of 18 months. This comes before considering the additional compute required for post-training of these models to fine-tune their abilities and “personality”.
Silicon economics also remains exponential. Each GPU generation nearly doubles performance every 18 months. Nvidia’s latest Blackwell B200 chips provide [ https://substack.com/redirect/a90db98e-15d4-4d4b-9988-15267a7858bd?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] up to 2.5x the throughput of previous-generation Hopper H100s.
Thanks to ongoing improvements in computing hardware and efficiency, AI systems will become much more energy-efficient. By the end of the 2020s, the number of floating-point operations per watt (FLOP/Watt) - a measure of how efficiently a system performs computations – could improve by a factor of 35 compared to 2020 levels. This projection compares the efficiency of Nvidia’s A100 chip (a 2020 benchmark) with expected advances by 2030, considering historical trends and potential unlocks with reversible computing architectures in the late 2020s.
We can trace this trajectory through key inflection points, such as the launch of Nvidia’s H100 in 2024 and the upcoming Vera Rubin and Feynman architectures. These successive GPU generations are expected to cumulatively drive a 2,000-fold increase in compute availability by 2030.
This rapid progress isn’t limited to data centers. On-device computing power in laptops and smartphones is also accelerating, roughly doubling every two years.
If current trends hold, by 2027 the computational power packed into a consumer laptop could rival the inference performance of a 2020-era A100 GPU and bring once-elite AI capabilities into everyday personal devices.
Algorithmic efficiency revolution
Alongside hardware gains, AI models themselves are becoming more efficient. Between 2012 and 2023, research showed [ https://substack.com/redirect/cbca88e7-2971-4cc8-9195-db8d87a41e99?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] that the compute required to reach a fixed performance level halved roughly every eight months.
More recent estimates from Epoch AI  suggest [ https://substack.com/redirect/4f9948b6-02c1-4a80-9ba8-598b96b21f7c?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] this trend is continuing, with efficiency now improving by a factor of three each year.
Just as Moore’s Law did not rely on a single set of optimisations, AI efficiencies are improving because of a portfolio of approaches, such as sparsification, low-precision arithmetic and model distillation.
Taken together, these advances are shifting the compute‑to‑accuracy curve downward. At this pace, by 2030 models could match today’s accuracy using roughly 32× less computation in a conservative scenario, around 100× if the historical trend holds, or up to 243× if Epoch AI ’s recent estimates prove correct. Either way, these algorithmic advances significantly amplify the performance gains delivered by hardware improvements alone.
More reliable bots
Frontier models in 2030 will expand the boundaries of what AI systems can handle. According to METR [ https://substack.com/redirect/b0548fbb-165c-4bc6-86f7-92762a19004d?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], another research outfit, today’s frontier models can complete 50-minute coding tasks with a 50% success rate. But capabilities are improving fast – AI task endurance appears to be doubling every 4 to 7 months [ https://substack.com/redirect/99778079-34d3-464f-930b-e311e0132339?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. By the end of the decade, we could see AI systems reliably executing complex tasks over multiple days or even weeks without human intervention.
Let’s put this into perspective. To achieve a 50% success rate on tasks lasting 200 hours, individual one-minute subtasks must exhibit extremely high reliability, at 99.994% per minute – we need a significant leap in per-task dependability as AI task complexity grows.
But even a 50% overall success rate often isn’t good enough for practical use; tasks typically require significantly higher reliability, closer to 90%, to match human standards.

These are then chained together to build more complex workflows with higher overall reliability.
The METR data focused on software-related tasks: ML research, coding and short activities designed to mirror a developer’s day. It’s worth noting, though, that many AI use cases, like analysing consumer research or summarizing documents, don’t always have clearly verifiable outcomes. Success often depends on human judgment, not a binary right or wrong. Arguably, on those types of tasks, such as desk research, today’s AIs are capable of several hours of good-quality work without intervention.
Context matters
Contextual understanding is expanding just as fast as task competence. GPT-3 launched with a 2,048-token context window in 2020. Today’s models routinely handle 125,000 tokens. Gemini 2.5 Pro supports up to 1 million, with 2 million already on the horizon. Meta’s Llama 4 Scout claims [ https://substack.com/redirect/0021454d-424b-4a3e-b9a5-807b831d256d?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] a staggering 10 million-token window, though reliability drops off after the first million, based on early tests.
Crucially, this growth hasn’t been steady – it accelerated sharply after mid-2023. From GPT-3 in 2020 to GPT-4 in early 2023, capacity doubled roughly every eight or nine months. However, more recent breakthroughs have since dramatically shortened this doubling interval to around four months, quickly bringing public demonstrations to the million-token frontier. Even if this rapid pace slows substantially, settling back to a more conservative annual doubling, context lengths exceeding 30 million tokens by 2030 remain entirely plausible. If the current faster cadence persists, context windows extending into the billions of tokens become a realistic possibility.
By 2030, it’s therefore plausible to expect context windows in the tens of millions of tokens – this would likely get us comprehensive, coherent and sustained AI interactions – a memory – the kind that Azeem outlined in his essay [ https://substack.com/redirect/5166a40f-52c6-463d-8ae3-569d9a515d8a?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].
Multimodal AI is rapidly maturing, integrating text, audio, images and video into unified systems. Paired with agentic frameworks that use iterative think–act–observe loops, these models are gaining the ability to work on complex, real-world tasks that once required constant human oversight.
The path from here to there
The trends that we identify in this essay are increasingly well-evidenced. While we can’t say when specific AI capabilities will emerge, and in what configuration, they do allow us to put some shape on what the next years might look like.
2026: 100,000-GPU Blackwell pods deliver computational scales far exceeding current benchmarks; consumer devices routinely embed GPT-4-level copilot capabilities.
2027: Companies with major AI infrastructure, including Meta, Microsoft, and xAI, collectively surpass one million GPUs, enabling pervasive, affordable cloud-based fine-tuning services and the proliferation of domain-specific AI.
2028: Stargate infrastructure deployment significantly boosts computational capabilities, further enhancing real-time inference speeds of SOTA models and substantially improving interactions across consumer and enterprise applications.
2030: Frontier models reach 50–100 trillion parameter sizes, enabled by scaling sparse Mixture-of-Experts architectures, while smartphones become capable of running 20–50 billion parameter models, extending current trends in hardware efficiency and quantization.
None of this considers radical new approaches. It assumes a continued evolution of the transformer architecture that underlies today’s large language models. But there are other approaches that might become relevant and might push us onto a higher curve.
We have to adjust to this tempo
The next surge is almost mechanistic, unless the real world says “no”. Of course, the physics or the power availability, could be a drag, but for reasons Azeem argued here [ https://substack.com/redirect/e98d0e05-d549-4107-8d3e-d71ae4ca699b?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], we think there might be routes through that.
Competition may be the driving force today, but concentration could slow the big players down. Half-trillion-dollar build-outs lock capabilities inside a handful of firms and states. The window for open competition narrows each quarter.
Talent and tools will start to matter. We can generate 90,000× more capability, but reliability still collapses over hours-long tasks, magnified exponentially as agents swarm the internet. Verification, not scaling, is the frontier. Compute, chips and data are becoming as strategic as oil. Export controls, subsidies and security incidents could kink today’s curves tomorrow.
So what? Betting against exponentials has been a losing trade for half a century, but betting on them blindly is worse. The real game is to turn these extrapolations into action: how do you build into this environment? How do you design incentives that spread not hoard benefits? How do you ensure AI remains aligned? How do you secure sustainable and reliable supply chains for the massive amount of physical stuff this will demand?
The calculations in this essay give us a high-confidence envelope of possibility. What we do inside that envelope – compete, collaborate, regulate – will decide whether 2030 feels like liberation or lock-in. The clock speed is set!
