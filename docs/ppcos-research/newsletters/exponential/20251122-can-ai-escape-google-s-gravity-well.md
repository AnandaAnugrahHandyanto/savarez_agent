# 🚀 Can AI escape Google’s gravity well?

**From:** "Azeem Azhar, Exponential View" <exponentialview@substack.com>
**Date:** 2025-11-22T12:39:16.000Z
**Folder:** exponential

---

View this post on the web at https://www.exponentialview.co/p/can-ai-escape-googles-gravity-well

Some two and a half years ago, Google faced a GPT tidal wave [ https://substack.com/redirect/feeef104-f32e-41f3-b8f8-35cd52e1a210?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. Sundar Pichai may (or may not) have declared a “code red [ https://substack.com/redirect/cc7325e3-167b-422e-9694-8f9eaa56c83e?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].” This week, words gave way to a physics lesson in vertical integration.
When massive objects warp spacetime, smaller things will either get captured by the gravity well or achieve escape velocity. Google is adding enormous mass to the AI field: $93 billion in capex, custom silicon, vertical integration top to bottom, coupled with the breakthroughs from the research team at Google DeepMind.
Does that mean game over for competitors? Well only if intelligence is a monolithic mass, one dimension where the biggest model always wins. But that “God Model” fallacy, the notion that intelligence is monolithic, doesn’t sit with my intuitions,  [ https://substack.com/redirect/fdab3a7b-6dcd-4c2c-b720-1778b421db52?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]as I have written previously. Recent research from Epoch AI  concurs, suggesting intelligence is more multifaceted. If that’s true, Google’s gravity well has a floor but not a ceiling. It’s tough to escape by matching their mass. You escape by achieving different densities in other dimensions.
Keep that frame in mind as we look at what Google just shipped this week.
Inside the launch
This week Google released a new flagship LLM, Gemini 3, which includes an advanced thinking capability called DeepThink. Accompanying the launch was a remarkable new image generator called Nano Banana 3 and some developer tools.
I’ve been running Gemini 3 through my usual battery of tests in the run-up to this week’s launch. It’s a noticeable upgrade on Gemini 2.5 – sharper reasoning and a real jump in dealing with complex, theoretical questions. It’s also a concise communicator.
Compared to GPT-5,  differences showed up quickly. GPT-5 tends to pile on layers of complexity; Gemini 3 gets to the point. That clarity compounds over hundreds of queries. I now default about a third of my prompts to Gemini 3. I’ve also moved a high‑stakes multi‑agent “council of elders” workflow, where several different prompts challenge and critique analyses, from GPT‑5 to Gemini 3. In that workflow GPT-5 worked noticeably better than Claude 4.5; Gemini 3 Pro is the best of the lot.
Model choice isn’t about novelty-chasing. You’re recalibrating the tone of a colleague you consult dozens of times each day. And for those who need numbers, Gemini 3 Pro tops Anthropic and OpenAI across a wide range of benchmarks.
If we just focused on Alphabet’s technical milestones, we’d miss half the picture. The unsung hero here is the firm’s deep infrastructure.
Markets are jittery about AI spend and the bubble chatter. It’s the tension of the “exponential gap” [ https://substack.com/redirect/fc06418a-d46c-4574-b9d5-7f941ed64239?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], linear financing pulled by exponential tech. Even Sundar Pichai has flagged elements of “irrationality.” [ https://substack.com/redirect/4b313b62-4590-46d2-966d-c44117833289?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]
Alphabet raised its capex guidance [ https://substack.com/redirect/1df18e7d-dd84-492d-96b1-008d2578191d?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] three times this year. It now expects $91-93 billion of capital expenditure in 2025, up from about $52.5 billion in 2024, and has already signalled a “significant increase” again in 2026.
So far, that splurge has not forced a retreat from the financial cosseting that Alphabet offers its investors. The firm authorised a $70 billion buyback in 2025, spent roughly $62 billion on repurchases and $7 billion on dividends in 2024, and is still retiring around $10-12 billion of stock a quarter while paying a $0.21 quarterly dividend.
The vast bulk of the capex is going into technical infrastructure, servers, custom tensor processing units and the data centres and networks that house them, with recent cycles funding the shift to the new Ironwood TPUs and the AI Hypercomputer fabric that ties them together. Nvidia will still get a slice. Google Cloud continues to roll out high‑end Hopper‑ and Blackwell‑class GPU instances, and the Gemini and Gemma families are being optimised to run on Nvidia hardware for customers who want them.
But the core Gemini stack – training and Google’s own first‑party serving – now runs almost entirely on its in‑house TPUs. And those Ironwoods are impressive. They are comparable to Nvidia’s Blackwell B200 chips, delivering similar amounts of raw processing (42 exaFLOPs FP8 for Ironwood) and the same 192GB of HBM3e memory. But Google’s chips only need to be good at one thing: “Google-style” house models, massive LLMs with Mixture-of-Experts inference, for high-throughput serving. And so Ironwood promises lower cost per token and latency for Google services.
Gemini 3 is the dividend of that spending.
Crucially, the new model also addressed a criticism thrown at this sector. For the past few years, foundation model labs have bet on scaling laws, which state that spending more on data and compute reliably produces performance improvements. Many outside commentators claimed that scaling itself had failed [ https://substack.com/redirect/9a4ccd9a-5626-46f7-aa99-6ce8202c53e3?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. My view, last year, was that while other innovations would be welcome, scaling still had some years to go [ https://substack.com/redirect/c4148228-08b9-4279-9168-34996e8ca01f?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].
In the words of Google researcher, Oriol Vinyals [ https://substack.com/redirect/6913cad2-6c03-4a20-95ba-37da321de582?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], on the subject of pre-training scaling:
Contra the popular belief that scaling is over—the team delivered a drastic jump. The delta between [Gemini] 2.5 and 3.0 is as big as we’ve ever seen. No [scaling] walls in sight!
And as for post-training, the step where a trained model gets further refined, Vinyal’s is even more explicit:
Still a total greenfield. There’s lots of room for algorithmic progress and improvement, and 3.0 hasn’t been an exception.
What Google has shown is that scaling still works if you have the vertical stack to sustain the burn: infrastructure, data, researchers. To what extent can Anthropic or OpenAI follow that strategy?
The shape of intelligence
To understand whether Google’s gravity well is escapable, we need to look at what kind of mass creates AI gravity in the first place. Epoch AI  has argued that  intelligence isn’t monolithic; it’s a composite of general reasoning plus distinct, hard‑earned capabilities shaped by targeted data and training. In practice, that broad reasoning acts like Google’s diffuse gravitational mass, bending the competitive field.
But there’s also a second category of contingent capabilities, spiky domain-specific skills like high-level coding, legal discovery, or proteomic analysis that don’t emerge by default. These require deliberate, expensive investment in specific data and training. Think of these as concentrated density in particular capability dimensions.
This changes everything about escape dynamics. In classical gravity, you can’t escape a larger mass, full stop. But in a composite system, you can achieve localized density that exceeds the dominant player’s density in specific dimensions, even while having far less total mass.
The customer choosing between them isn’t asking “who has more total mass?” They’re asking, “Who has the highest density in the dimension I care about?”
This is the key to the next five years. If capabilities are heterogeneous and orthogonal, you can’t build one “God Model” that maximizes every dimension simultaneously – even Google faces resource constraints.
Scale creates mass; specialization creates density. Pick one capability, invest hard, and win on price‑performance in that lane. That’s how you slip Google’s gravity – there will be no head‑to‑head fight.
Why Google owns “good enough”
Google commands three fronts simultaneously: (1) research labs rivalling OpenAI’s talent density, (2) infrastructure investment approaching $93 billion annually, and – most crucially – (3) distribution channels no startup can replicate.
The last of these, distribution, is becoming decisive [ https://substack.com/redirect/36e736d2-fcaa-49fd-abd2-9c922870393c?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. AI Overviews surface before two billion searchers each month, putting Gemini in front of more users in a day than most competitors reach in a year. Its assistant already claims 650 million monthly actives, second only to ChatGPT. Gemini doesn’t need to win every task; it just needs to meet the threshold of good enough inside products people already live in.
And that threshold is rising. Across enterprise workflows – email synthesis in Gmail, coding tools, slide generation in Notebook LM – Gemini 3 sets a new capability baseline of the minimum quality threshold at which “adequate” becomes “adequate not to switch,” especially when the tool embeds itself within applications employees already use hourly. Once Google reaches sufficiency at a given task, external challengers face asymmetric competition: users won’t abandon Gmail for a five percent better email summarizer, because the friction of switching (new login, broken integrations, retraining muscle memory) exceeds the marginal gain.
Startups like Fyxer [ https://substack.com/redirect/e2d0e1ef-4fd9-48e9-9b81-4b2b80d6adbe?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] or Shortwave [ https://substack.com/redirect/e03e9ae1-a818-403d-9da5-83fc43c380bf?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] survive this dynamic only by delivering step-change improvements and entirely new capabilities that Gmail cannot replicate which justify overcoming switching costs entirely.
The financials confirm this thesis. Google Cloud revenue hit $15 billion in Q3 2025, growing at 35% year-over-year, faster than AWS (12%) for the first time in five years, though still trailing Azure’s roughly 39%. Microsoft’s AI cloud business remains larger than Google’s for now. OpenAI’s voracious compute appetite tilts the scales but Google’s is accelerating.
All of this raises a plausible outcome: Alphabet becomes the ambient substrate of digital work. Gemini sits quietly in the background, assisting across tools, shaping habits, becoming invisible infrastructure.
But nothing about this is preordained.
Gravity wells don’t exist in isolation and Google isn’t the only massive object in this system. Microsoft retains enterprise relationships Google lacks; and can match the spending on infrastructure and power commitments.
For OpenAI, Google’s show of force presages “rough vibes [ https://substack.com/redirect/642ff884-43a7-4f3c-8dc6-6b059ea84195?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]” according to the startup’s boss, Sam Altman. Yet it commands mindshare amongst consumers: “I asked Chat!” ChatGPT’s Pulse feature is evolving into a compelling, persistent interface. I’ve started using it for iterative research, the kind of slow-burn exploration that gradually shapes my approach to thorny analytical problems. OpenAI will end 2025, having more than tripled its monthly revenues in the year, according to our forecasts.
This doesn’t mean much for Nvidia either. Google’s TPU are optimised to do one thing—serve large LLMs in a particular way. Today’s those are all Google’s models but from 2026, they will include some Anthropic workloads [ https://substack.com/redirect/ce9e31ae-4740-423e-a611-2d6fb8df11c8?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] as Google expands its Google Cloud business.
But the market for AI workloads is growing enormously. Amin Vahdat, Google’s head of AI Infrastructure, told employees the company needed to double its AI capacity every six months to meet demand [ https://substack.com/redirect/2754ddff-c6ea-4fd8-ab3c-205fc94e93cf?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. In such a rapidly growing market, Nvidia’s market share was inevitably going to erode even as its order book—currently sitting at $500bn [ https://substack.com/redirect/efc618f0-7a81-47d8-b288-e1ce442db8e7?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]—stays strong.
Distribution advantages compound, but they are not insurmountable. Nokia, BlackBerry, Yahoo all mistook distribution for destiny and forgot about innovation. Their gravity wells collapsed suddenly when a denser competitor arrived. For now, Google seems to have scrambled to the high ground – it has the resources to fund research, the infrastructure to deploy at scale, and the distribution to reach billions. But in a multi-body system, stable orbits are rare.
The specialist escape
The “contingent” nature of intelligence creates an opening for a different class of company: the deep vertical specialist. The mistake many observers made over the past year was to believe the model was the product and the most powerful model would have the most mass and win.
Customers actually don’t buy “the best model”. They buy the product that solves their problem. And their decision criteria extend far beyond benchmark performance.
Enterprises evaluating AI models care about specialist domain expertise, total cost of ownership, inspectability and explainability, and avoiding single-vendor dependence. This creates divergent strategies. Some companies fine-tune models for vertical-specific use cases and others prioritize open-source for flexibility and control.
Take Basis [ https://substack.com/redirect/083f8fd9-afc2-4206-a0ec-ddfd7be6fc16?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], which builds agents that can autonomously complete accounting tasks and report back to accountants in about a fifth of America’s top 100 firms. The UX is built specifically for accountants managing teams of agents, and it includes the integrations required for agents to do their work. For many accounting workflows, Basis is close to 100% accurate, whereas generalised models fall over at the 80% level. That is an error rate that even Enron’s auditors would have noticed.
Harvey [ https://substack.com/redirect/3d7e2c99-2015-41b9-b160-ef4dcff3ca3b?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], the AI platform for law firms, isn’t winning because it has a “better brain” than Google. It’s winning because the brain is wrapped in a legal‑specific IDE that understands discovery, privilege, and case law. Harvey recently hit $100 million in ARR. A generalist model in Google Docs can’t handle the liability frameworks of an M&A; Harvey can. (And many of these specialists will still use parts of Google’s stack– models for inference or Cloud for hosting.)
These “Ceiling” players will escape Google’s gravity well because their users – accountants, lawyers, geneticists, chip designers – don’t need a chatbot. They are buying a result.
The open-source deflation
There is one final force that complicates Google’s victory lap: the cost of remaining in orbit.
While Gemini 3 is cheap, it isn’t free. It’ll cost $2 per million input tokens and up to $18 per million output tokens. It’s about the same price as OpenAI’s GPT-5 models. You can access Kimi K2 Thinking for $4 per million output tokens at Together.AI. If you don’t need Gemini 3 Pro’s capabilities (and for many applications, you won’t), why would you pay more?
For enterprises running massive inference workloads, that API tax adds up. OpenAI recently promoted thirty customers who have used more than 1 trillion tokens. Last month, Google acknowledged 150 customers who had reached a similar number [ https://substack.com/redirect/9c62652a-4688-4c23-912f-1b48a7790cd8?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].
At today’s token volumes, it barely matters. A trillion tokens only run to about $10 million at the list price. M’eh, that’s not huge. But numbers are surely ramping up. Our friends at CLSA [ https://substack.com/redirect/a7202396-ee32-4de0-b4bd-660cd5e5530c?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] recently argued that token consumption might run to 80 quadrillion tokens a month within a few years. Token prices will have come down by then, but cost savings will matter even more.
This is driving a quiet migration to “good enough” open-source. On platforms like OpenRouter, running Llama 3.3 can be 25 times cheaper than proprietary heavyweights. And surveys on enterprise CIOs show growing interest, although marginal use, in open-source [ https://substack.com/redirect/1b552976-467b-4c81-a470-1558e032c804?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].
Airbnb’s CEO Brian Chesky admitted that his outfit, one of tech’s most sophisticated product organizations, chose Alibaba’s Qwen [ https://substack.com/redirect/53693d64-f452-4efd-bab4-92a411ba1ea9?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] models to power its AI customer service agent, despite Chesky’s personal friendship with Sam Altman. Qwen was “very good,” but more critically, it was “fast and cheap.”
We write more about this in tomorrow’s Sunday edition.
This puts a ceiling on Google’s margins. They can’t act like a monopoly rent-seeker when a free, downloadable substitute is just a GitHub repo away.
The bifurcated future
So, where does this leave us? Google has created a gravity well that has already sucked in a bunch of my usage. Kudos to the Google team for executing right across the stack, from their power innovations, their chips, their models and their applications.
But multi‑body systems rarely settle. The AI market will stay dynamic and competitive. New arrivals can also upset the balance, just as Google’s flex may have altered the fabric this week. Whether that mass is a new model from Claude, Anthropic, xAI or state-level compute, or a new paradigm entirely, remains to be seen. Until then, the only thing that matters is infrastructure. Infrastructure is destiny.
The gravity well is deep, and the escape velocity for innovation, specifically in those spiky, specialized dimensions, has never been higher. Go out and build.
