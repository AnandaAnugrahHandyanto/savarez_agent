# 🎄 What progress looks like

**From:** "Azeem Azhar, Exponential View" <exponentialview@substack.com>

**Folder:** exponential

View this post on the web at https://www.exponentialview.co/p/what-progress-looks-like

Progress is often depicted as a gentle, upward-sloping exponential curve - a smooth path toward better outcomes. This representation has become so ubiquitous in how we think about technological and social advancement that it shapes our expectations and, potentially, distorts our understanding of reality.
The truth is messier. Progress follows a turbulent, oscillating path around that idealized trend line. While the overall direction might be upward, the journey is marked by volatility, setbacks, and unexpected accelerations. We often lose sight of the underlying positive trajectory amid these dramatic fluctuations.
Consider recent examples. A 10% spike in lithium-ion battery prices in 2022 led to headlines questioning the viability of the electric vehicle transition. Yet this temporary disruption occurred against a backdrop of dramatic long-term cost declines in battery technology. 2024 prices today are 30% below 2022. [ https://substack.com/redirect/8333ba1a-83f2-4955-b97b-a991dac938e6?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] Similar wobbles show up solar capacity. It’s easy to mistake a bit of volatility for a break in the trend [ https://substack.com/redirect/a390c4d2-523f-4a4d-81e8-00a3c66f326c?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. I wager that the trend will recover, probably next year but certainly within a few.
Similarly, the semiconductor industry has experienced recurring supply chain crises, most notably during the pandemic, when chip shortages cascaded through global manufacturing. Despite these disruptions, the industry has consistently delivered on Moore’s Law and now, perhaps, Huang’s Law.
Fasten your seat belts
This pattern of turbulent progress isn’t limited to technology.
Building successful companies often looks deceptively smooth in retrospect. The clean hockey stick growth curves in pitch decks mask the reality of existential crises, pivot points and near-death experiences that virtually every successful venture encounters.
But we might experience a zoomed-in version of the curve. In which case, the turbulence and volatility is our every day. If you live at point A, below, you might feel worse, in the face of relative decline, than if you live at point B. A peasant living just as the Black Death arrived in England could well have experienced a shorter and more horrible life than normal (point A). One who grew up in the years after, could have experienced a minor golden age (point B).
Every startup founder recognises that curve. It’s called Tuesday afternoon. You swing from absolute elation, a sense that the product is working and customers are beating down your door, to fear of total disaster. Usually within a couple of hours.
But those swings are reflected at a wider-scale and over a bigger time frame as well. And yet, we tend to focus on the swing rather than the curve.
Part of our skewed perception stems from media dynamics. Journalism naturally gravitates toward disruption and volatility. “Smooth progress continues” doesn’t sell papers. And for many analysts, air time comes from the language of crisis. To misquote a famous adage, “It is difficult to get someone to stay calm, when their salary depends on shouting fire.”
Understanding this reality of turbulent progress is crucial for leaders and decision-makers. It helps build resilience against short-term setbacks and maintains focus on long-term objectives. That isn’t to say we can be blasé about setbacks, challenges and changing realities. There are bad outcomes: from pollution to exclusion; from the debilitation of the public space and the erosion of trust; from the increase in conflict to the accretion of unmediated political power. But it is important to understand these against that trend line.
The next time you encounter headlines about setbacks in climate technology, artificial intelligence, or any field of rapid advancement, remember: progress isn’t smooth, but turbulent waters don’t necessarily mean the ship is sinking.
After all, progress isn’t about avoiding turbulence - it’s about navigating through it toward our desired destination. And that takes intention, commitment and work.
