An update on the job market
Two more papers came into my view this week on AI and youth employment. First is a piece of research by Harvard economists that looked at 285,000 US firms and 62 million workers [ https://substack.com/redirect/72e2a418-f24c-4da7-8720-ae8b8ded76a8?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] to find that junior hiring among AI-adopting firms was down by 22% starting in early 2023 relative to non-adopters, while senior employment continued growing.
These findings align with Erik Brynjolfsson ’s research using ADP payroll [ https://substack.com/redirect/d921c7fe-fa0a-40b3-95ee-e5c8f4cd9978?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] data, which found that workers aged 22-25 in AI-exposed occupations experienced a 6% employment decline since late 2022, while older workers in the same jobs grew 6-9%.
The second study I’d like to highlight found that since 2021, sectors exposed to LLMs have seen higher wages and more jobs [ https://substack.com/redirect/4dfd6fde-ae4a-4a81-8cde-f0c577ea8cd1?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], especially for young and well-educated workers. But in areas where AI directly replaces people, jobs have fallen. This, again, aligns with Erik’s paper.

The fountain of youth
Putin and Xi were caught on a hot mic [ https://substack.com/redirect/db79b36d-5972-43fa-9eae-499ca1be2ece?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] musing about organ transplants and immortality. Odd talk, but biotech is starting to make the idea less fanciful. We are now developing both the atlas and the search tools to discover how human aging might be reset – and this matters hugely beyond wrinkles: aging is the single biggest risk factor for cancer, heart disease, Alzheimer’s, and most of the conditions that undermine quality of life.
A new epigenetic atlas maps [ https://substack.com/redirect/e0759a1c-7cde-4983-a46b-e4e62377a8e9?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] how the chemical tags on DNA change with age across 17 human tissues. It shows where change is fastest and highlights the patterns that could guide future drug targets.
These chemical tags form part of the epigenome — the regulatory layer that helps cells decide which genes to use. With age, some tags fade and others appear in the wrong places, making gene control noisier and, in some contexts, eroding a cell’s ability to hold its identity. (I discussed the importance of the epigenome [ https://substack.com/redirect/1623def2-54a5-455c-84a7-02ba0d2be506?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] with David Sinclair back in 2020.)
Crucially, in cells, the epigenome can be reset. Shinya Yamanaka [ https://substack.com/redirect/ad6f548e-1dff-452b-92d1-c2270fae634b?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]’s Nobel-winning 2006 breakthrough showed that four transcription factors (OCT4, SOX2, KLF4, MYC) could rewind adult cells all the way back into stem cells, resetting their identity entirely.
The new frontier is figuring out how to rejuvenate cells without wiping them clean or triggering uncontrolled growth. This is a combinatorial puzzle with astronomical possibilities, precisely the kind of search space where AI might excel. OpenAI and Retro Biosciences have already engineered improved versions of Yamanaka’s factors [ https://substack.com/redirect/ce902f2a-7f6f-4a1d-a57f-d39aae070a90?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] that can rejuvenate cells more efficiently, speed up marker expression in vitro and restore DNA integrity. For now, these results live in the lab; the challenge is safely porting them into therapies that work in whole organisms.
Reflections on autonomy
Technology extends us in different ways. It can stretch our reach, structure our choices, or even decide outright on our behalf. Each step down that ladder may give us greater convenience, but it also carries a hidden cost. The less friction we feel, the less practice we get in the habits of judgment. We reflected on this when GPT-5 [ https://substack.com/redirect/8820df57-ddbf-49c2-8c16-cadbb1fb7fd4?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] launched:
When [GPT-5] anticipates needs and makes decisions for you, you do not feel the friction it removes; you just experience the smoother path. But that invisibility makes it risky: the more the model guides you, the more your own curiosity and agency can atrophy.
Harry Law  wrote a beautiful essay [ https://substack.com/redirect/423cd26e-bf85-4b6b-b573-4e7a28ab568a?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] this week about this very conundrum. He argues that the real danger is not when technology obviously overrules us, but when it quietly simulates us:
To deliberate wisely, one must encounter situations, weigh alternatives, and learn from the consequences of one’s choices. Autonomy, in this sense, grows as habits of reflection are strengthened and withers when they are not. Technologies that augment or configure us can support this process by extending our capacities or freeing us from thinking about the minutia of everyday life. But when technologies begin to simulate or replace us, they risk undermining the very practices through which phronēsis is formed.
That’s precisely what we see in the way teenagers use AI companions. A third of teenagers using chatbots use them [ https://substack.com/redirect/97215912-0bed-4133-b434-9a5042f1bb82?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] for serious conversations. Many are drawn to validation, not challenge. As one teen put it [ https://substack.com/redirect/253133ab-c0ca-4479-96f8-1c6aac1fa42e?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], “When you’re talking to AI, you are always right. You’re always interesting. You are always emotionally justified.” Neuroscientist Dr. Dominic Ng  warns that in vulnerable users, the same loop can tip from bias reinforcement into pathology and amplify psychoses by mirroring delusions [ https://substack.com/redirect/208b3019-db0c-48df-a6f9-f5dd1909fdcb?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].
These tendencies are not intrinsic to AI – they are products of design. Red-teaming [ https://substack.com/redirect/3e71e122-67a8-40d7-9d64-af8b15697167?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] has shown that some models indulge delusions, while others push back and refuse to play along. Companies are testing new features such as “study mode [ https://substack.com/redirect/d3fcf6fa-2e14-4201-b01d-5e2018edb162?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]” to prompt users to deliberate rather than simply accept answers.
