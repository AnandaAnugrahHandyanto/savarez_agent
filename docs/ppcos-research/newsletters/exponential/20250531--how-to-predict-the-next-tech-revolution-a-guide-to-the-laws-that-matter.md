# 🔮 How to predict the next tech revolution: a guide to the laws that matter

Physics has laws. Gravity doesn’t care about your business model. But “technological laws” like Moore’s Law are really just observed patterns — and patterns can break.
That said, dismissing them entirely would be a mistake. These patterns have held up remarkably well across decades and understanding them helps explain some of the most important economic shifts of our time. Why did solar power suddenly become cheap? Why can your phone do things that supercomputers couldn’t do 20 years ago? Why does everything seem to be getting faster, smaller and cheaper all at once?
The answer lies in these technological regularities — Moore’s Law, Wright’s Law, LLM scaling laws and more than a dozen others that most people have never heard of. They’re not iron-clad rules of the universe, but they’re not random either. They emerge from the fundamental economics of learning, scale and competition.
Today we have a slow, special weekend edition for you about what important technology “laws” actually tell us, why they work when they work and what happens when they don’t.
Whether you’re trying to understand the AI boom, the energy transition, or just why your laptop doesn’t suck as much as it used to, these patterns are your best guide to making sense of our accelerating world.
We prepared a scorecard to help you evaluate these technological laws based on their empirical basis, predictive accuracy, longevity, social and market influence, and theoretical robustness. This is only a snippet—for the full scorecard covering seventeen laws, read on!
The core of computing
Most readers know about Moore’s Law, coined by Intel co-founder Gordon Moore [ https://substack.com/redirect/ec4ad67c-7a8d-47bc-a6a3-c575a89af396?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] in the 1965 when he was asked to predict what would happen in silicon components over 10 years. He predicted that transistor counts would double roughly every two years, driving exponential leaps in computing power.
Yet each anticipated limit, whether from physics or economics, has sparked new waves of innovation such as GPUs, 3D chip stacking and quantum architectures. Moore’s Law was never a single forever-exponential; it is better viewed as a relay race of logistic technology waves. The baton is still being passed –just more slowly, and with ever-higher stakes – so the graph still looks exponential even though the underlying sprint has become a succession of S-curves.
For decades, making computer chips smaller let engineers simply turn up the speed without using extra power – a trend called Dennard Scaling [ https://substack.com/redirect/1bb9dd38-3141-4fb8-bdab-869207835e6c?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], which worked hand-in-hand with Moore’s Law. But physics pushed back. Smaller parts started to overheat and by the early 2000s manufacturers hit a ceiling: raising the clock speed any higher would melt the chip. The solution was to add more “brains” (cores) that share the work instead of asking one core to sprint faster and hotter. That shift to multi-core processors, explored in detail here [ https://substack.com/redirect/d1afcefa-39a3-4025-9dad-3236943a065d?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], let performance keep climbing without turning your laptop into a space heater.
Still, innovations continued, reaffirming the adaptability of these foundational laws.
Collateral exponentials: storage, LEDs and solar
Raw computing muscle doesn’t matter if you can’t afford a place to keep all the data it creates. That’s where Kryder’s Law [ https://substack.com/redirect/740ce61f-8416-48f5-9b57-e5b8dcc6ee11?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] comes in: the cost of storing information has fallen so fast that we’ve gone from guarding a few megabytes on floppy disks to tossing terabytes into the cloud for pocket change.
Cheap, roomy drives unlocked everything from YouTube and Netflix to the photos filling your phone. But the parts we use today—magnetic platters and flash chips—are bumping up against hard physical limits. New ideas, like packing data into strands of DNA, could be a thousand times denser than the best solid-state drives, potentially restarting the price-drop roller-coaster.
If that sounds familiar, it should. Just as Moore’s Law kept shrinking transistors, storage has advanced in overlapping waves: fresh materials, clever techniques, better manufacturing. Up close each wave looks like an S-curve that flattens out, but zoom out and the combined effect is a long, steady surge that keeps powering the next frontier.
Similarly, Haitz’s Law [ https://substack.com/redirect/99b186d9-1cbc-4238-829d-d208043e16c0?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] has done for LEDs what Moore’s did for microchips.
Every few years, LEDs get brighter for the same amount of electricity while their price per lumen keeps falling. It’s the result of a thousand tweaks: better semiconductor materials, smarter chip designs, improved heat-sinking, and streamlined factory lines.
For those interested in a longer historical lens, the cost of lighting has plummeted by orders of magnitude since the 14th century, when tallow candles made with cow or sheep fat provided meager illumination at a high price.
Gas lamps in the 19th century and Edison’s incandescent bulbs in the 20th were both significant leaps, yet neither revolution can match the accelerating pace of modern LEDs.
Swanson’s Law [ https://substack.com/redirect/e48c29d8-b364-4528-8a3f-7dfa120a8978?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] is the solar-power version of a volume discount because each time the world’s total solar capacity doubles, the price of a panel drops by roughly 20%. Over a few short decades those repeated cuts have worked like compound interest in reverse – turning solar from an expensive science-fair project into, in many places, the cheapest way on Earth to make electricity [ https://substack.com/redirect/d3194471-982f-4156-9195-43d7c64919c0?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. It’s a textbook example of how steady, exponential cost declines can flip a market on its head.
Connectivity is unstoppable
Connectivity has had a profound impact on all our lives and there’s a set of laws that can tell us what’s going on. Nielsen’s Law [ https://substack.com/redirect/09a355f3-e3c5-4e78-99c9-fcb5bcb19382?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] is the “speed limit” that keeps jumping. Top-tier internet connections get about 50% faster every year, a journey that has taken us from the screech of dial-up to whisper-quiet gigabit fiber and made streaming, cloud gaming and video calls feel effortless.
Edholm’s Law [ https://substack.com/redirect/3b86f115-b8da-4978-83ca-c5ccb58a600b?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] says the walls between wired, wireless, and optical links keep crumbling. Today’s Wi-Fi 6 and Wi-Fi 7 routers pump out gigabit-class speeds that used to require an Ethernet cable, showing how quickly those once-separate lanes are merging.
Throw in Gilder’s Law (which boldly claimed total bandwidth in telecom networks might triple yearly) and Butters’ Law [ https://substack.com/redirect/396889cf-ff53-4ba0-9bac-a5648264dc43?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] (the cost of transmitting a bit over optical networks halves every nine months), and you get a picture of nearly unstoppable expansions in capacity and falling network costs.
Even the airwaves have their own rule: Cooper’s Law [ https://substack.com/redirect/6071f19c-953b-415d-9930-e948853c8462?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], courtesy of cell phone pioneer Martin Cooper, states that the number of simultaneous wireless calls in a certain spectrum doubles about every 30 months.
In 1901, when Guglielmo Marconi [ https://substack.com/redirect/6d7320e9-aeb9-4511-9422-97332cdf7e97?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] first transmitted Morse code across the Atlantic, the technology was so primitive that his signal used a significant fraction of the world’s radio spectrum. Today, more than one trillion radio signals can be simultaneously sent without interfering with each other.
That’s why we can cram more and more people into the same frequencies without meltdown – assuming we keep improving our spectral efficiency with tech like MIMO, 5G or even future 6G. If Cooper’s Law continues at its current pace, by 2070, each person on Earth could theoretically use the entire radio spectrum without interfering with anyone else’s signals. Of course, practical deployments must still respect physical and regulatory limits, but the trend of maximizing capacity continues to hold.
This is great news for networks themselves. Metcalfe’s Law [ https://substack.com/redirect/e99e4a1a-3d11-4b12-b993-f28ffcf732eb?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]  [ https://substack.com/redirect/e99e4a1a-3d11-4b12-b993-f28ffcf732eb?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]suggests that the value of a network is proportional to the square of the number of users. This was the core of social media growth – each new user exponentially increased the connections and value of the network itself.
It has also been applied [ https://substack.com/redirect/3fca6172-8526-4067-9e9b-0f037e2c9b6b?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] to make bitcoin and enterprise valuations. This is a reminder that many of these laws matter most when enough people adopt the technology. In other words, scaling is crucial to turn a neat idea into a major change.
The cost of bits
Of course, with all these bits whizzing around, efficiency is paramount. Koomey’s Law [ https://substack.com/redirect/91202c9d-a212-422d-907a-19489b89402b?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] is the power-efficiency counterpart to Moore’s, tracking how many calculations we can wring from a single kilowatt-hour of electricity. From the dawn of electronic computing in 1946 through 2000, that figure doubled about every 18 months, allowing machines to grow both faster and thriftier. In recent years physics has started to push back, so the doubling pace has eased to roughly every 2.6 years, yet the underlying story is the same – generation after generation, new hardware still squeezes more work out of the same amount of energy.
This explains how we can run entire AI data centers, crunching billions of parameters, without blacking out entire cities (though energy concerns remain real).
Researchers tracking crypto mining hardware have found that roughly every nine months, new rigs use only half the electricity to produce [ https://substack.com/redirect/596deb0a-6d97-4360-8d9f-5f4c8249ee64?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] the same cryptographic work. In other words, the energy cost per “hash” keeps falling almost as fast as the hardware improves, echoing the wider march toward leaner computing.
Parallel power & AI acceleration
Amdahl’s Law [ https://substack.com/redirect/bf34fab3-557c-4c25-b164-550ce3b27974?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] warns that serial bottlenecks limit parallel gains, but Gustafson’s Law [ https://substack.com/redirect/b3fc2d36-4147-4f32-933d-da9fe8c584f1?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] counters by highlighting that complex, data-intensive tasks – such as AI – benefit greatly from parallel architectures. This dynamic explains the surge in performance we’ve observed in high-performance computing (HPC) and AI training, where enormous datasets and neural networks leverage massive parallelism to outperform traditional computational limits.
This explosion of data-driven and AI workloads explains the rise of GPUs. Nvidia’s CEO Jensen Huang noticed that GPUs were getting roughly 25× faster in five years. On the back of an envelope, that works out to a doubling every 13 months, far quicker than the ~24-month cadence of traditional Moore’s Law for general-purpose CPUs. This is Huang’s Law: when the tasks are massively parallel and the hardware is purpose-built, progress can out-sprint the classic curve.
Huang attributed this rapid advancement to a synergy between hardware, software, and artificial intelligence innovations. He emphasized that the progress wasn’t only about chip improvements but involved the entire computing stack.
A study from Epoch AI  attempted to track this prediction for GPU performance, finding that:
Performance per dollar has improved rapidly, and hardware at any given precision and fixed performance level becomes 30% cheaper each year. At the same time, manufacturers continue to introduce more powerful and expensive hardware.
They also found that machine learning hardware performance has increased at a rate of 43% per year, doubling every 1.94 years, not quite as quick a doubling rate as suggested from Huang’s law at 1.1 years.
Regardless, this progress in ML-purpose GPUs has enabled the rise of scaling laws relating to large language models.
These AI scaling laws further highlight the nuances of exponential progress. Initially, larger datasets appeared to improve generalization. However, DeepMind’s Chinchilla scaling Law indicates [ https://substack.com/redirect/e6a1ba88-ba21-4397-a8c0-255c23343101?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] an ideal ratio of roughly 20 tokens per parameter optimizes performance, balancing model size with data and computational resources.
While this has effectively guided model training, recent studies suggest [ https://substack.com/redirect/883f4aa2-499f-4f8c-b79a-f395d2e83f3e?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] training on even larger datasets continues to yield incremental improvements, challenging previous assumptions about diminishing returns. This scaling of compute to performance has continued with the latest models as we’ve seen previously in SNL#512 [ https://substack.com/redirect/75b55755-c419-47eb-9ab2-a56830a002bc?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], SNL#513 [ https://substack.com/redirect/1ad2d926-8a04-4251-a874-c124feb2e982?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].
Scaling laws also matter when the model is actually generating answers. If you allow a smaller network extra inference time, it can sometimes out-reason a larger one that must reply instantly, highlighting a trade-off among accuracy, speed and model size. Techniques like self-play and data augmentation create fresh examples from existing ones, stretching available data and postponing saturation. Meanwhile, architectural tweaks and smarter optimization – DeepSeek-R1, for instance, achieves a reported 45-fold efficiency boost [ https://substack.com/redirect/1115e96b-f7f8-4e56-925e-dc37acbf2857?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] – squeeze more performance from the same silicon. Finally, distillation lets a large “teacher” model train a small “student,” putting advanced capabilities onto everyday devices and tighter budgets.
Because hardware, algorithms and training tricks keep evolving, these scaling rules will keep evolving too. For a practice-oriented deep dive into how data volume, model size and compute interact in real systems, Cameron R. Wolfe, Ph.D.  has an excellent overview [ https://substack.com/redirect/a2461fc3-e322-43f8-80db-112b2af3b261?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].
More complexity isn’t always better
Pollack’s Rule [ https://substack.com/redirect/2fe59a88-d232-431a-9977-d591765e2f51?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] reminds us that making a CPU more complicated pays off much less than we might hope: if you double the circuitry, you gain only about √2 ≈ 1.4× the speed, not a full 2×. In other words, every extra layer of hardware ingenuity delivers diminishing returns.
On the software side, Wirth’s Law [ https://substack.com/redirect/f1fafb1a-e0de-4cb8-956a-e3c85ca6d837?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] flips the spotlight, warning that ever-bulkier programs can erase those hard-won gains because “software gets slower faster than hardware gets faster.” Anyone who has watched operating-system updates balloon in size only to feel their laptop drag knows this first-hand. Together, the two rules argue that clever coding—trimming excess features, tightening algorithms, and avoiding bloat—can matter as much as–or sometimes more than–the next leap in silicon.
The overarching insight
Wright’s Law – originally from aviation in 1936 – complements this big-picture view by explaining how, with each cumulative doubling of production, costs fall by a constant percentage. Wright’s law underlies much our writing and analysis – including some of our earliest essays:
Investing in disruptive innovation [ https://substack.com/redirect/a954a76e-e752-4b8b-bc6e-fec15f098446?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] (2019),
The exponential march of solar energy [ https://substack.com/redirect/0327b8e4-2fb7-4ffa-a35e-acde4573ef81?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] (2020),
The great computing stagnation [ https://substack.com/redirect/8015985e-07e1-4845-9c70-4e5305ab1acc?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] (2023).
Wright’s Law underpins cost declines in everything from solar panels to electric vehicles, all the way to Lithium ion batteries for which prices drop by an average of 19% for every doubling of capacity [ https://substack.com/redirect/fbd24119-0d49-46c9-8059-84ff1a1b6385?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], fueling the takeoff of new markets. Nagy et al., (2013)  [ https://substack.com/redirect/5c8acd42-23f2-4efe-b619-943eac2843be?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]found that Wright’s Law is superior in determining future costs over Moore’s Law across 62 different technologies.
Both Wright’s Law and Swanson’s Law exemplify the power of learning curves: as production volumes climb and experience accumulates, costs predictably drop. Each emphasises the self-reinforcing cycle – greater adoption lowers costs, which in turn accelerates adoption – making these laws invaluable for anticipating industry shifts.

We’ve highlighted Wright’s Law at the top, for its impressive implications as a predictive scaling law that has covered many fields!
Navigating the road ahead
These technological laws offer strategic signposts and can serve you as mental models for where technology might go next.
But no law is absolute – physical limits or policy decisions might disrupt these trajectories. Yet, innovators continuously pivot, whether towards quantum computing or AI breakthroughs, resetting the exponential clock. Such paradigm shifts remind us that laws are ultimately grounded in current technology and may be redefined when a disruptive platform emerges.
If we continue pushing boundaries and learning from past curves, the pace of innovation will likely continue to surprise us – and that, for many of us, is the most exciting possibility of all.
