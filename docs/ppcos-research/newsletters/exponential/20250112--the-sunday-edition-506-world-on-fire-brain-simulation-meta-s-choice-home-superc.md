👋🏼 Hi, it’s Azeem. As Los Angeles grapples with devastating wildfires and global temperatures breach the critical 1.5°C threshold, we face stark reminders of our changing world. The insurance industry’s retreat from climate-vulnerable regions signals a broader crisis of adaptation. Yet, alongside these global challenges, we’re seeing remarkable advances: scientists have achieved a breakthrough in brain simulation, matching the scale of human neural networks with 86 billion digital neurons. Today’s weekend edition explores how we navigate our planetary boundaries and technological frontiers.
The uninsurable frontier
Wildfires tearing through Los Angeles are on track to be among the costliest in US history. One natural disaster after another begs the question: who will bear the cost? Lex Sokolin  estimates that although $50-60 billion in damage looms [ https://substack.com/redirect/2d05b752-cfc8-4ab2-a7f4-ada8ab3069df?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], only $10 billion of private coverage plus $25 billion from California’s FAIR Plan stands ready – leaving tens of billions unaccounted for.
Adding to this misalignment is Proposition 13 [ https://substack.com/redirect/243d2f4d-0631-4ef9-999a-d88b66f47794?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], which locks in artificially low property taxes until a sale triggers reassessment. Recent research finds that once a wildfire forces turnover – through destroyed homes and rebuilding – local governments can see a 10.5% overall revenue boost within five years, including a 21.2% jump in property tax receipts. On paper, that surprising surge offsets some short-term losses. However, it underlines the broader irony: local authorities may inadvertently reap fiscal gains from periodic destruction, while homeowners and insurers bear the brunt of rebuilding costs.
Meanwhile, Proposition 103 still largely prevents insurers from using forward-looking climate models [ https://substack.com/redirect/abe1d897-3c54-4970-ab37-b9e24c17cf9d?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], forcing them to rely on outdated or incomplete data. Though California regulators have begun easing that rule, the real impact is yet to be felt. In the meantime, insurers will continue to withdraw from fire-prone zones rather than write unprofitable policies, leaving the FAIR Plan (effectively the taxpayer) as the insurer of last resort.
Taken together, these top-down constraints distort prices and dampen incentives for more robust risk mitigation. If insurers were allowed to price fire risk fully, prospective homeowners in at-risk areas would face higher premiums and either build to higher fire-safe standards or move to safer zones.
See also: Climate-tech investor Yishan Wong  provides more insight into the home insurance market in southern California [ https://substack.com/redirect/81277b2d-f8ca-4864-b59b-5d260c5513e1?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], including the $105,000 deductible his policy mandates.
Matrix brain
A new platform can now simulate 86 billion digital neurons [ https://substack.com/redirect/a04a68a7-73b2-4030-a95c-6867b71ac695?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] and 4.76 trillion synapses – roughly on the scale of an entire human brain – by tapping more than 14,000 GPUs to model up to 47.8 trillion synapses. Researchers call this the “Digital Brain” – a mesh of spiking neuronal networks calibrated using MRI, diffusion-weighted imaging and PET scanner data.
The engine behind the model enables the simulator to approximate blood-oxygen-level-dependent signals observed in real fMRI. That correlation coefficient of 0.93 at a voxel level is particularly impressive. The simulated brain was also moderately good, with a correlation of 0.575, at predicting whether the subject would find an image pleasant or unpleasant.
This is not a full-brain simulation. But, it is a useful step on the path to modelling the brain at near-human scale with all sorts of applications in medical, AI and neuroscience research.
See also: More companies are looking to simulate the physical world with AI. Google is building a team for this [ https://substack.com/redirect/af7afc26-15b1-4265-bc31-20ac317bc9fe?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] and Nvidia has released their world model, Cosmos [ https://substack.com/redirect/1945af9f-4aae-480a-af4c-32c6db67bb51?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. While robotics training is one potential application, these models will benefit self-driving cars, video game creation and other fields requiring physical world simulation.
Freedom or facts?
Mark Zuckerberg has moved to remove fact-checkers [ https://substack.com/redirect/87e3a2ab-c624-4295-96f1-e2a161aa2fc2?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], heralding a renewed emphasis on “freedom of expression”—though Meta still moderates the most severe policy violations. Leaked training materials also show dramatically relaxed hate-speech rules, including allowing statements such as “Mexican immigrants are trash [ https://substack.com/redirect/2172a539-f066-48e8-88c0-cdb2b80d524b?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ].”
Yet the larger question is whether certain groups—particularly women and minorities—will quietly opt-out if the environment becomes hostile. If participants feel harassed or view the platform as toxic, do they really enjoy free speech? According to a recent survey by the Anti-Defamation League, 52% of American adults report experiencing hate or harassment online [ https://substack.com/redirect/bd74125b-1484-4371-abcd-eea726dff283?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]—up from 40% in 2022 and the highest share in four years. I’ve certainly found discussion on X more aggressive and unpleasant than it ever was on Twitter.
A “public square” is hardly free if many users feel too intimidated to speak in the first place.
Finding a balance between free expression and decent public squares is hard. For many, Meta had gone too far since 2016 [ https://substack.com/redirect/5aa1981b-3786-47f5-8e1c-e70f8b27212e?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]. As The Economist [ https://substack.com/redirect/5aa1981b-3786-47f5-8e1c-e70f8b27212e?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] points out the “definition of hate speech has expanded in a way that limits debate about subjects such as transgender rights. Automated filters are so strict that even Meta says 10-20% of the content it removes is taken down in error.”
In reality, every moderation strategy, however tight or loose, is a product, and ultimately, political decision. Yes, even Elon’s. Platforms could make sure their decision-making is transparent and observable, and give users tools to better shape their experience online. Such an approach would help them balance the impossible task of determining good and bad with providing a service their users would enjoy.
Related: Henry Farrell  of Programmable Mutter  makes an excellent case that [ https://substack.com/redirect/449b89d4-7fdf-4636-9d45-0cc62761b8a9?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]
many of the problems that we are going to face over the next many years will stem from publics that have been deranged and distorted by social media in ways that lower the odds that democracy will be a problem solving system, and increase the likelihood that it will be a problem creating one.
AI & capitalism
In a community briefing for members [ https://substack.com/redirect/e595cdd5-9d9f-441b-a3c0-f913660c0560?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] of Exponential View this week, a member asked me to talk about how I see the impact of AI on capitalism. I’m still thinking through what I really believe. I revise my view quite regularly. In the spirit, I’ll share a couple of interesting essays on the question which I read this week.
The impact of AI on capitalism is a complex issue, with some fascinating ideas emerging. One is the concept of “autocapitalism [ https://substack.com/redirect/74b68fc4-7ae4-4575-b96f-b96298a40eed?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]”, a new phase where AI and robotics drastically reduce the need for human labour in production. This could lead to huge economic abundance, but also shift the focus to capital ownership as the key to economic participation. The focal question here is how can individuals adapt to an economy where human labour is no longer central?
Another idea from L Rudolf L  at No Set Gauge  is that AI could fundamentally alter the balance of power [ https://substack.com/redirect/ed405f67-ffbb-4d93-8404-645b68a55753?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] across many registers: institutions, individuals, capital, labour, incumbents and upstarts. With AI acting as a substitute for labour, the incentive for institutions to care about humans could diminish. The ability of money to “buy results” will increase as top AI talent can be cloned, making it harder for those without initial capital to compete. This may also lead to a more static society where entrenched power structures are hard to disrupt. So, what measures, if any, can be put in place to ensure a more equitable distribution of power and resources in an AI-driven economy?
We’ll unpack these questions over the coming weeks and months.

Video games of mass destruction. The US Department of Defense designated Tencent, the owner of WeChat and the world’s largest company in the video game industry as a “Chinese military company [ https://substack.com/redirect/9e167d99-5a6f-4903-89e5-c05519386f54?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]” that is “operating in the United States”. The company appears in a longer list of firms published on 2 January 2025, in a ‘Notice of Chinese military companies operating in the United States’, via Bill Bishop .
A supercomputer at home? Nvidia has announced Project Digits, a $3,000 desktop-sized AI supercomputer [ https://substack.com/redirect/1df77475-67a9-4bf6-b26b-f19f097ab575?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] slated for May 2025. Powered by the new GB10 Grace Blackwell Superchip, it can handle models of up to 200 billion parameters – yet still runs off a standard power outlet. We now live in a world where anyone can run their own LLM.
Rodents in the metaverse. Neuroscientists track brain activity in animals including mice to study complex behaviours. Now, a team at Cornell has created MouseGoggles [ https://substack.com/redirect/72685232-1e61-4cd8-a057-18605a9c07f7?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ], a pint-sized VR headset designed specifically to be fixed to the head of mice. Unlike older panoramic display systems, MouseGoggles delivers binocular visual stimulation and tracks eye movements, resulting in stronger behavioural responses (for instance, a robust startle reflex to looming virtual objects).
Collective antelligence. In a new “piano movers puzzle [ https://substack.com/@didierhope/note/c-83042233?r=2inn&utm_medium=ios&utm_source=notes-share-action ]”, researchers pitted ants against human volunteers to see how each moved T-shaped loads through a maze. Humans outperformed ants individually – thanks to strategic thinking – but once ants formed large teams of 80, they surpassed human groups by using collective memory and consistent effort. Meanwhile, humans struggled with suboptimal strategies, especially when communication was limited. Humans need a better hive mind.
Pushing the medical frontier. Sana Biotechnology has demonstrated that transplanted islet cells – pancreatic cells that produce hormones that regulate blood sugar – can survive and function without immunosuppressive drugs [ https://substack.com/redirect/93347f6b-67f0-43fc-907e-1d15c1a6d609?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ] in a type 1 diabetes patient, thanks to their hypoimmune (HIP) technology. If this approach scales, it could redefine how we treat type 1 diabetes and potentially other autoimmune diseases.
