Hey folks,
Over the weekend, the 'evals’ debate really took off on Twitter. Debates like this have a ton of “is a burger a sandwich?” nonsense, but the big question is: should you, as an AI product builder, have a testing process for how well your product performs on certain tasks? Of course, the answer is yes, and of course, the usual caveat applies: both too little or too much of this testing are bad. I plucked some snippets from people who wrote good stuff about evals this weekend:
Shreya (In defence of evals [ https://substack.com/redirect/4976290c-1a55-4aa2-b86d-535d95829e0f?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]): Expertise lets you avoid static metrics. Dogfooding (using your own product) as an expert regularly and updating it based on the vibes is evals. To paraphrase this: “we don’t do evals” is mostly a misnomer like “our sci-fi movie has no CGI.”
Alex (Evals are a scam) [ https://substack.com/redirect/c4a1ce10-fdd9-4199-886f-8e5080f9f90a?j=eyJ1IjoicXd0ciJ9.0tEpqnOrcfuKYRYnEOurmlOZ_pVIUnS06konjQA-bCs ]: You don’t outsource evals to someone with zero expertise about your product. You can use their tooling to measure something, but don’t let them tell you what to measure. Most evals companies sell logging, observability and complexity beyond that.
