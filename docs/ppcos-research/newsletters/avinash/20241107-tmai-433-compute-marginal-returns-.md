TMAI #433: COMPUTE MARGINAL RETURNS!

[ Web Version [2] ]

A simple but powerful concept today, to get closer to your CFO.

Before we get going: I have expressed reservations about ROAS as a
metric. It hides more than it illuminates. Ex: It does not take into
account any of the 15 dimensions of profitability from the dollar
spent on acquisition. It does not apply to all work we do in
Marketing. Ex: What’s the ROAS of SEO? Email? And so much more.
I’m setting all that aside today, in service of a higher cause. :)

Consider this scenario for a Facebook campaign:

SPEND: $100,000 | REVENUE: $150,000 | AVG. ROAS: 1.50
In the limits of what it is doing, pretty good return.

We are super excited.

We even did something to make me happy: We did a bit of extra analysis
to realize that we are making a profit at 1.5!

Yea!

Let’s spend more.

So, we increase the budget by 25% for Facebook, and measure:

SPEND: $125,000 | REVENUE: $168,000 | AVG. ROAS: 1.34
Yea!

Ok, fine, a bit less ROAS. But, look more than 1.

Yea!

No.

No, yea.

This is what the extra budget we spent on Facebook delivered:

SPEND: $25,000 | REVENUE: $18,000 | AVG. ROAS: 0.72
Ouch.

This humble exercise of figuring out what did that extra spend get me
is called MARGINAL ANALYSIS.

In our example above, 0.72 is the MARGINAL ROAS.

You spent more, but got a lot less for that additional spend.

The challenge is when you get reports from your agency, who will get
it from ad platforms like Facebook Ads or tools like Google Analytics,
you will see 1.34 – the AVERAGE ROAS.

It is not that they are trying to be evil. It is more that Avg. ROAS
is easier to compute, and doing marginal analysis is, while easy,
complex.

Computing Marginal ROAS is crucial, as obvious above because it
quickly helps you see at what point you start losing money by doing
more Marketing.

In an ideal scenario, assuming your Agency, as we do at Croud, is
doing deep meta-analysis, giving you good advice, what would be a
confirmation is to see this, let’s say your YouTube spend…

SPEND: $100,000 | REVENUE: $150,000 | AVG. ROAS: 1.50
Now we spend the same incremental amount as we did above with
Facebook, $25k:

SPEND: $125,000 | REVENUE: $180,000 | AVG. ROAS: 1.44
So, it looks like things got worse.

But, now that we've learned the lesson, we compute marginal analysis:

SPEND: $25,000 | REVENUE: $30,000 | MARGINAL ROAS: 1.20
What initially seemed like “bad,” is “good” as you got a
positive return.

(Assuming we check profitability at 1.20…) In this instance, an
incremental investment of $25k in YT was a better idea than an
incremental $25k in FB.

One obvious lesson is that you should have an inherent distrust of
Averages (ask for distributions wherever they apply).

The second, less obvious, lesson is that when you use Ad Platform
features like “Target ROAS,” realize that the Platform is running
off averages. At some point, while achieving your Target ROAS, could
actively be losing you money on your next incremental dollar spent.

The third obvious lesson is that in doing marginal analysis, you are
bringing the concept of diminishing returns into the conversation.
This is a very good thing.

In the first phase, the zone of increasing returns, for every dollar
you spend, you will make multiple times more. High Avg ROAS.

In the zone of decreasing returns, measured on a marginal basis, every
dollar will spend you deliver increasingly lower returns, but even on
a marginal basis it will be positive.

In the zone of negative returns, measured on a marginal basis, will
deliver less or significant less than your investment.

It is entirely possible, measured as normal Avg. ROAS in your Agency
report or Ad Platform or Analytics tool, all of this looks like
“positive ROAS.”

By now you’ve figured this out by now, but just in case :)…

TRADITIONAL ROAS = TOTAL REVENUE / TOTAL AD SPEND
And..

MARGINAL ROAS = INCREMENTAL REVENUE / INCREMENTAL COST
Marginal ROAS computation provides a more accurate picture of a
campaign’s success, as it accounts for the reality that every
additional dollar, beyond a point, will deliver less revenue (/profit)
than the previous dollar.

Among other investment decision benefits, your Agency will use
marginal ROAS in:

A. bid cap strategies to optimize spend (ex: in your Facebook ad
campaigns),

B. allocate incremental budgets optimally across channels (not FB in
our example above, and yes in YT),

C. identify that new channels might have an initial lower Avg ROAS,
but higher marginal ROAS

And numerous other cool activations.

[HELPFUL LINK: Google, much credit to them, has a detailed article on
B: Power More Conversions through Cross-Channel Bid Optimization [3].
See the last image in the article.]

Looking at incremental investment and marginal ROAS from it will make
these metrics look, potentially, dramatically different:

Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC).

Cost Per Acquisition (CPA).

Return on Investment (ROI) – whatever your definition of it is.

BOTTOM LINE.

From someone who obsesses about incrementality [TMAI #411 [4]], it
should not be surprising that I’m a fan of marginal computations.

When you and I go visit your CFO to prove that we in Marketing are
investing money wisely, delivering results No Other part of the
company is delivering… They respond to marginal thinking.

Carpe diem.
