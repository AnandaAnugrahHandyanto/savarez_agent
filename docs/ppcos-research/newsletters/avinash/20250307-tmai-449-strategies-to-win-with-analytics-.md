TMAI #449: STRATEGIES TO WIN WITH ANALYTICS.

[ Web Version [2] ]

When asked _how do you pick from 200 cartoons the 18 or so that end up
in the magazine,_ Bob Mankoff the Cartoon Editor of the New Yorker
answered:_ IT'S NOT THE INK, IT'S THE THINK_.

_ _Those close to me will tell you just how much I adore this quote.

The _ink_ (tactics, tips, best practices, how-tos) are all important,
but the thing that truly delivers non-normal outcomes is the _think_
(strategy, frameworks, _higher-order-bits_).

When it comes to building a data-influenced org,
intelligent_ think_ is necessary for exceptional_ ink_. A
collection of strategies form the belief system that powers
my_ think_. I go back to them again and again.

I wanted to share them with you today, in the hope that they'll become
a part of your belief system - and give you direction.

1. ALWAYS BE KNOWINGLY WRONG.

Plenty of Marketing decisions are based on "experience," and at times
just a "feeling."

Oftentimes, I find us in situations where we actually have data that
would say otherwise.

_Don’t do this. Don’t allocate that much budget. Don’t spend
money on this channel again, we have failed for 2.5 years already.
Don’t agree to that target, we can predict that even if everything
goes right you’ll get to 60% of that target._

And, so on and so forth.

In cases where the Analytics team lacks influence, the tendency is to
just let_ experience_/_feeling _decisions happen, to let things just
flow. After all, it is not like you can change the outcome.

I believe this is the wrong strategy.

If a leader has decided to do X, and you have analysis that
demonstrates Y is the right path, make the recommendation. It might
mean require courage, show them the work.

The leader can be knowledgeable about Y, but still choose to do X. It
is ok.

If it turns out X was the right decision. You - Analyst/Data team -
learn something new, important, of value.

If it turns out Y was the correct decision. The Leader/Company learns
something new, important, of value.

Win-Win.

If you never say anything. If the Leader does not know Y, there is no
possibility of learning.

Always be knowingly wrong.

A real example of the principle in action:

Before every campaign, we look across six dimensions, and we issue a
CS3 (Campaign Smart Start Summary). In the last row, it predicts the
campaign's % LIKELIHOOD OF SUCCESS.

Often, the plan is rated as “_Low Probability of Success_.” The
CMO has a chance at that point - before start of the campaign - to
change campaign elements. Some decide to plow ahead anyway. They are
being knowingly wrong. No matter, because of the CS3, there will
always be a lesson learned.

2. Data influenced, not data-driven.

I’ve come to recognize that it was a mistake in the past - in my
writings - to use the phrase _data-driven organization_.

That thing does not exist. It might even be impossible.

In part, because it is rare that all senior leaders will listen to
data. In a group of five, maybe there is one. Then there are two
who’ll be happy to listen politely. The last two are so high up in
their ivory tower where all they see are mirrors reflecting their
opinions.

In part, because it is hubristic of _data people_ (you, me) to think
that we can actually understand everything necessary to make sound
business decisions.

You can certainly use only data to decide the best landing page for
people from Boston shopping for Yankees gear. It is much, much harder
to use only data to decide how aggressively we should go after the
mobile market on your company’s behalf - there might be many
unappreciated unknowns.

Hence, the principle: Aim to build a _data-influenced organization_.

A real example of the principle in action:

When we do this right, after a string of being only partially
data-influenced (or not at all) the team/leader gets the message,
since the result is more consistent failure.

For our most crucial initiatives, we’ve arrived at a harmonious
balance: 80% of the budget will be extremely data influenced. 20% of
the budget will be _experimental / vanity / just do something crazy to
see if it works_.

So. Not entirely data-driven. Mostly data-influenced (guaranteeing a
certain amount of success for sure, and some lottery entries - the
20%).

3. OPTIMAL ANALYST DISTRIBUTION: 50 | 30 | 20.

I’ll often ask: _WHAT IS THE SIZE OF YOUR 30?_

What I’m asking is: _HOW MUCH TIME ARE YOU SPENDING ON REPORTING
DATA?_

So many in our community spend an inordinate amount of their time
either creating reports (often, but not always, _glorified data
pukes_), or consuming reports. If reports could change the world, we
would all be working four hours a week and world peace would be
here.

I believe:

*
50% of an Analyst’s time should be spent on analysis (IAbI)

*
30% of the time on reporting (a necessary function, sadly not
sufficient to have impact)

*
20% of the time on experiments (with an eye to learning - self,
marketing, company)

In stressful situations, this principle has been a helpful _true
north_.

_Have I gone astray? Have I taken the team I’m responsible for in
the wrong direction? What is the quality of the 20% time spent on
experiments? Are we learning anything of value or just messing around?
What about Analysis? Have we explored the latest statistical
approaches or new ML algorithms? What can we do to make the 30% even
better? Do our reports have a care-do-impact storytelling framework or
just loads and loads of why you should care?_

And so on and so forth. Crystallizing questions through a simple
framework for existence. You can see how it is helpful in both
reorienting in stressful times AND helpful in building out a stronger
future existence.

Two honest admissions:

*
It is incredibly difficult to hit this balance early in your career.
You are probably reporting 70% of the time and spending 30% of the
time chasing data for your reports. It is ok. Do it as well as you
can, but have a plan to get to 50 | 30 | 20.

The sooner you get there, the more your career will accelerate.

*
Embracing Analysis can be challenging. Reporting is a comforting
existence because there is not a lot of hard thinking required. People
want data pukes, supply them with data pukes.

Analysis requires a lot of thinking, knowledge, and self-improvement.
This is hard. Experiments require extensive business understanding,
leadership connections, and being ok with failing. This is hard - and
also a dead end.

4. PAIN PERSUADES LEADERS, MORE THAN UPSIDE.

_Loss aversion is a cognitive bias that describes why, for
individuals, the pain of losing is psychologically twice as powerful
as the pleasure of gaining. _

_Loss aversion refers to an individual’s tendency to prefer avoiding
losses to acquiring equivalent gains. Simply put, it’s better not to
lose $20, than to gain $20._

In the context of influencing a leader to take action on
data-identified insights, I’ve found loss aversion to be an
effective ally. People always react a little faster, a little more
commitment, and with a little more resources, if we can show them what
they stand to lose.

*
_If you switch over to a Shopify style single page checkout
experience, you will improve the conversion rate by 1.8 points._

*
_For every day that we don’t have a Shopify style single page
checkout experience, we will lose $13.8 million dollars in revenue._

In statement A, you could even have said _you will improve revenue by
$13.8 million dollars_, and will often be less effective (or often not
effective at all).

Statement B, on the other hand, leverages the loss aversion cognitive
bias that evolution has wired into us. Hence, it tends to be
significantly more effective.

When you find yourself in a stressful situation, where you feel you
don’t seem to have much influence to get the company to do the right
things… Ask yourself the question: _Have I leveraged loss aversion
to frame the value?_

An honest admission:

There is a type of leader who wants only good news to flow, their
worldview is to focus only on good things, and at times, this
individual will be incredibly allergic to any negative slant - no
matter how valid.

You will know this leader because they will almost always be
overseeing an organization defined by mediocrity.

In these instances, it will be difficult to leverage _loss
aversion_.

My approach is to mix it in with other approaches that are approved.
Over time, the number of chances increase as it becomes clear the
technique is working.

A real example of the principle in action:

I’ve emphasized that to truly win Brand Marketing, you need to
pre-test your Creatives with Human Made Machine [3] (where I'm
CSO!). TV. Facebook. YouTube. Billboards. Creative Concepts. Creative
Executions. Everything.

In companies, some will embrace this effective strategy and others
will resist (because they believe they represent the users better and
there is no need to ask actual users!).

To ensure everyone takes advantage, use a loss aversion inspired KPI:
For each VP report the _% BUDGET ON PASSED CREATIVE_. It shows just
how many dollars were “wasted” by spending them on creative that
had failed pre-testing OR not pre-tested at all.

It is a massive motivator of change.

5. A PONY CONSISTENTLY BEATS A CATMELEON.

This is about KPIs. This is about Marketing strategy.

Many frustrating conversations regarding data come down to the fact
that Marketers/Senior Leaders are expecting a fish to both swim really
fast and fly really fast.

Fish swim.

Birds fly.

When I visit clients, it is not uncommon for a CMO to tell me: _We
are running this inspiring brand campaign to improve Unaided Awareness
and drive short-term Sales_.

Guess what happens: NO IMPACT ON UNAIDED AWARENESS. NO IMPACT ON
SHORT-TERM SALES.

Why? The creative you need, the targeting you need, the audience focus
you need, the call to actions on landing pages or in-app or in your
retail stores, is entirely different for those two goals/metrics.

I’m not saying either one of those two goals is better or wrong.

I’m saying it is a CATMELEON strategy.

It is very, very difficult for one campaign to accomplish multiple
goals. Almost all Marketing teams are really (often really, really)
bad at executing Catmeleon strategies.

So, be humble. Work to build out a marketing strategy called fish,
focus it on swimming as best as it possibly can, and measure success
at swimming. Build out a separate marketing strategy called birds,
focus it on flying as best as it can, and measure success at flying.

When I’m really unhappy in some discussion, I take a mental pause
and ask myself _wait, are we judging a fish’s ability to fly_? It
does not solve the problem if the company has no strategy and such
poor quality thinking… But, it brings me calm. I take advantage of
that calm to plot a smarter response.

A real example of the principle in action:

In one of my favorite examples, the client was trying to execute a
Catmeleon. Use Google Search to drive Brand Impact. A mix of a cat and
a chameleon. Ugly. Not possible. Stressful.

With a calm discussion, I managed to persuade the client to switch to
a pony. Humble, sauntering pony. Use Google Search to solve the
long-tail conversions. Pretty. Possible. Not stressful.

Six months later, we earned the confidence to execute an
ADDITIONAL strategy to use a combination of Facebook and TV to solve
the Brand Impact problem. Pretty. Possible. Not stressful.

[Bonus: In case you are curious what a Catmeleon looks like [4].]

6. YOU ARE NOT AN ANALYST. YOU ARE A CHANGE AGENT.

I believe this truly, madly, deeply: _Life is too short not to do
something that matters_.

It is not uncommon for Analysts to feel they are in the data providing
business. Let’s collect all the data, let's process it, produce
reports and dashboards, and ensure everyone who needs to see the data
sees it.

That’s necessary, but not sufficient.

It won’t drive change.

It will take a combination of super strong insights, process
integration, the psychological persuasion of people who often really,
_really_, don’t want to be persuaded because the status quo is so
appealing, and a thousand more things, to actually have an impact with
data.

This takes hard work. This takes understanding humans as much as you
know data. This takes persistence. Most of all, it takes being that
barnacle on the side of the shore that never gives up no matter how
many strong waves thrash you.

This is crucial because just providing data/reports/dashboards - no
matter how well done - is not going to drive business impact.

Hence, think of yourself not as a Business Analyst, Data Scientist, or
Data Systems Integrator. Think of yourself as a Change Agent.

And, do not rest until you are an agent of change.

A real example of the principle in action:

In stressful situations, I stop and ask myself the question: _Is this
in service of delivering business impact? _

If yes, plow on firmly and respectfully to, as they say, do _God’s
work_.

If no, step back, take any lessons worth taking and live to _fight
another day_.

I’ll be the first to admit that living this principle is not easy
— in fact, it actively makes life hard — but the stress it
eliminates is waking up 5, 10, 15 years into your career and realizing
nothing you did actually mattered.

7. “DATA IS FAR LESS IMPORTANT THAN ANALYSTS BELIEVE. DATA IS FAR
MORE IMPORTANT THAN LEADERS BELIEVE.”

I'd created this note as a reminder for me to be humble.

Every analyst, and I, believe that the data we have, the analysis we
did, is the MOST CRUCIAL EARTH-SHATTERING IN THE WORLD, and everyone
should stop and listen to us and take immediate action.

:)

Data is of course essential, but there is so much more to running a
successful business. There are so many more variables a senior leader
has to balance for. In the context of that, sometimes what we consider
MOST IMPORTANT is really not. Sometimes, there is a reason to waste
$25 million dollars on an initiative, regardless of what the data
says. So on and so forth.

When I find myself in a heated situation, or even sitting all by
myself reviewing the actions we are driving and ones we are not, I
remind myself of the above quote.

It calms me down a bit.

Then. With an open mind, I go seek context for what seems like
anti-data decisions.

Sometimes, I discover a context I was not aware of, and now I’m a
more informed employee. Sometimes, the decision is indeed as terrible
as I thought it would be.  Either way, I find that I can handle it
more calmly.

So remember to be humble about your work.

[Data is way more important than leaders believe, especially in large
companies. But, that’s a story for another day.]

BOTTOM LINE.

It is invaluable to have a core set of _first principles_ that you can
identify as a part of your core belief system. (Professional or
personal.)

If you are just starting your first principles journey, you can use my
seven above to get started.

If you are advanced in your first principles journey, I would love to
learn from you. Please share.

I wish you a calmer, more fulfilled professional life.

Much love.
