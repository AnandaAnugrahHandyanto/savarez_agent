TMAI #465: DETECTING BULLSHITAKE IN BRAND MEASUREMENT. – P2 | BMBM.

[ Web Version [2] ]

With enough experience, filled with failures and some successes, most
humans end up with a _BSDAR_ – a radar to detect the _bullshitake_
being peddled in the professional space they occupy.

While a _bsdar_ can be tuned qualitatively (_this just smells bad_),
it is especially accurate when tuned based on quantitive patterns
observed in data (_there is no way that small a sample is
significant_).

A good _bsdar’s _key benefit: Sparking a combination of skepticism
and curiosity.

A _bsdar’s _purpose is not to dismiss. It is to not accept things
at face value. It is to put skepticism to positive use.

Here’s a recent example: Data presented by a huge brand painting
HUGE success on a super huge ad platform – at a major conference
organized by the super huge ad platform…

My _bsdar _immediately went _womp, womp, womp._

In TMAI #464 [3], I’d shared four crucial assumptions I’m making
when assessing the three different channels being compared above.
I’d also outlined for your future use a dozen reasons clustered in
four groups as to why the data presented is fishy.

The dozen reasons for skepticism are sourced from my experience
analyzing a few billion dollars worth of Marketing – across
countries, company types, and campaign purposes (KPIs).

I want you to TRUST me, but I also want you to be able to VERIFY my
insights.

Better still, as a Premium subscriber, I want you to have a model that
you can use in your work to detect potential _bullshitake_ from your
internal teams, agencies, ad platforms, and consultants.

Today, let’s understand all that powers the new simulation I’ve
built for you. Next week, I'll build out six simulations using the
model, which you can use to verify as well!

THE BRAND MARKETING _BSDAR_ MODEL. “BMBM.” 😊

Here’s the question we are trying to answer:

CAN THIS BRAND LIFT BE REAL? / ARE DOUBLE-DIGIT BRAND LIFTS A THING?

Brand Marketing has _unknowns_, _unpredictables_, and _unclears_. The
BMBM model simulates as many as possible, and includes variables and
multipliers to account for the three U_s_.

The BMBM is powered by NINE VARIABLES to come closer to simulating
real-world behavior & outcomes.

1. TARGET AUDIENCE IN POPULATION.

_What is the size of your target audience?_

Some companies do _gen pop_ targeting, considering it inefficient.

[NOTE: I’ve come to increasingly disagree with this perspective.
The much-vaunted digital micro targeting can be incredibly
inefficient, while also being ineffective! I’m a believer in letting
the _creative find your audience_. Don’t look down on broad
targeting.]

Let’s say your campaign’s target is _millennial men interested in
gender-fluid fashion_. Great. Then, your Target Audience in
Population, staying in the UK: 20% of 36 million = 7.2 million.

You might put more caveats, or have fewer, to arrive at the number for
your campaign.

2. REACH OF TARGET ON AD PLATFORM.

_What percent of the target audience can the ad platform reach?_

Let’s say the ad platform we are using is Snap.

Not all the _millennial men interested in gender-fluid fashion_ our
campaign is targeting will be on SNAP. Identify how many are.

In my research, approx. 45% are on Snap.

3.2 million is your Reach of Target on Ad Platform.

3. CAMPAIGN REACH.

_How much of your Target Audience on Ad Platform will your campaign
reach?_

While there are 3.2 million _millennial men interested in gender-fluid
fashion_ on Snap, there are diminishing return curves at play to
reach all of them.

Identify how many your campaign will reach (in part this will
determine your budget as well).
In my experience, to get to sufficiency you want to be around 50% else
effectiveness is low. For your _spike _campaigns, you’ll go as high
as 60%, even 70%, because you want that big bang – even if your
efficiency suffers a bit.

In this case, we choose 50%. Our Campaign Reach hence is 1.6 million.

4. SUCCESS KPI BASELINE.

_What’s the starting level of the KPI being measured, I.E., the
control group level?_

Let’s say the campaign is looking to lift Unaided Brand Awareness
with _millennial men interested in gender-fluid fashion_. That’s the
success KPI.

If we ran a survey on Snap today, without any campaign/ads, what
percentage of people would mention your brand unaided when asked about
brands selling gender-fluid clothing?

For my brand, the baseline for the Success KPI is 35%.

[NOTE 1: The higher the baseline, the harder it is to get any brand
lift. Harder = more money, more exceptional creative, more reach, more
frequency, more duration… more cash.]

[NOTE 2: Brand Marketing campaigns delivering long-term business
impact solve for only one of three KPIs: Unaided Brand Awareness,
Consideration, (Purchase) Intent. That’s it. You use any other KPI
at your own peril. (Ex: Ad Recall, Brand Love [4], Engagement
[5] etc.,) You can absolutely have Metrics or Influencing Variables
you track, but choose one of three KPIs mentioned if you want to pass
CFO scrutiny.]

5. % FREQUENCY 3/U/WK.

_During the campaign, what percentage of the audience was exposed to
your ads 3 times Per User Per Week?_

The second Most Causal Factor delivering success is Frequency.

[The most gigantic causal factor, of course, is the creative – the
reason why without creative pre-testing [6] you are dead –
AI-powered or human-powered.]

Most ad platforms, and nearly all agencies, report and plan _Average
Frequency _of your campaign. This is _bullshitake._

Why? The average net worth of you and Bill Gates is $55,000,500,000.
Factually true, but misleading to the max when it comes to you.

You want to ask Snap, in this case, to report the distribution of
Frequency by humans. _How many individuals saw my ad once? How many
saw my ads twice? How many saw my ad three times? _Continue beyond 3
so you can capture the full glory of how many people you’ve annoyed
like heck (and wasted your budget).

For OPTIMUM BALANCE of delivering influence while ensuring
effectiveness and efficiency, you want the maximum individuals in your
Target Audience to receive 3 PER USER PER WEEK for the duration of
your campaign. If your budgets are huge, and you can waste some, total
the individuals with Frequency of 3 and 4 Per User Per Week.

In our case, Snap delivered a Frequency of 3/U/Wk to 10% of the
individuals who saw our ads during the campaign.

[NOTE: It is not unusual for most of your Target Audience to receive
1/U/Wk or 2/U/Wk – this is because modern digital ad platforms were
not really built to do _frequency shaping _(like good old TV!), they
were built for _frequency capping_. 1 and 2/U/Wk are proven
ineffective. Beyond 4/U/Wk will be your long-tail, could be a material
percentage in total, and an inefficient spend.]

6. CAMPAIGN BUDGET.

_How much are you spending on your campaign?_

There are a hundred variables that determine your campaign budget.
Some of them are above. Others include seasonality, country, Ad
Platform, deals your Agency might have in place with the Ad Platform,
the actual mechanism you use to buy advertising (recall, of every
$100k budget you spend on non-search digital, only $30k reaches the
publisher!), and a lot more.

The BMBM model estimates the budget required in each scenario based on
accounting for the audience size, reach, frequency profiles, and
more.  Oh, and a realistic CPM.

When you use the model to detect if the Brand Lift reported to you is
_bullshitake_, please just type in your actual campaign budget.

There are three _key campaign variables _that influence the
simulation of the BMBM model to make it more real _worldy_…

7. KCV: 1 FREQUENCY.

_How many individuals are delivered a frequency of 1/U/Wk?_

This variable DOES NOT impact the brand lift prediction, rather it is
used to compute Impressions delivered, which in turn is used to
estimate the Campaign budget in the BMBM model.

It is set at 50%, something we normally see.

Different campaigns can be wildly different depending on the spend and
the size of the target. Small targets and big budgets can lead to
higher average frequencies, pushing down the 1 Frequency.

8. KCV: 3+ EFFECTIVENESS.

_How many individuals who see our ad the optimal 3/U/Wk but do not
change their mind?_

Not everyone who sees the ad an optimal 3 times will remember the
brand. Or, if the Success KPI is Consideration, not everyone who sees
the ad 3/U/Wk will start to consider the brand.

It depends a lot on the quality of the creative – a common failure
is running a UBA creative and expecting Consideration or Intent
outcomes! (One more reason why you MUST pre-test your creative with
Human Made Machine’s AI or Pro solutions [6].) It depends on the
dynamics of your audience – just one example: I have seen 1.7
billion GEICO ads, I like a lot of them, but I will never ever
consider GEICO for my insurance needs. It depends on the actions of
competitors.

It is why the entire audience exposed to your ads does not change
their mind – ever.
The 3+ Effectiveness value is set at 0.5.

9. KCV: LOST AWARENESS.*

Any attribute associated with a brand faces_ decay_. Humans have
other things to do than remember brands. Ex: While all of us watched
it, and talked about it the next day... Do you remember the highest
rated ad from 2025 Super Bowl? I bet you don’t. Neither do I.

Lost Awareness in the BMBM model captures the _decay rate_, though in
our simulation in the context of the campaign (not the SB example
above).

How many people who were aware at the start of the campaign no longer
could remember the brand at the end of the campaign (or at least when
they were surveyed)?

These are people who did not get enough or any impressions to refresh
their memory during the campaign.

The Lost Awareness value is set at 0.02.

To reflect the rate at which people forget a brand (a higher
probability for people who recently became aware of a brand). It also
can be the rate at which people stop considering a brand for the
Consideration question. Etc.

* While we call it Lost Awareness here, this key variable represents
the Success KPI – which can be Unaided Brand Awareness,
Consideration, or (Purchase) Intent.

TWO KEY FORMULAS.

Brand Lift is properly computed by building Test and Control cells on
Snap, delivering surveys to both sets, and measuring the delta
between, say, Unaided Brand Awareness. Test minus Control equals Brand
Lift.

Since we can’t do that in the BMBM, there’s a different approach
to help you simulate Brand Lift in a real-world scenario.

The Success KPI score in the Test cell, called “SUCCESS KPI |
CAMPAIGN EXPOSED” in the BMBM, is calculated using this formula:

((Number of Reached _ Baseline) + (Unaware) _ (The Percent Reached
3+) _ (The Percent Impacted by 3+) _ (The Number Reached) * Lost
Awareness)

The Success KPI score in the Control cell, called “SUCCESS KPI |
CONTROL GROUP” in the BMBM, is calculated using this formula:

(Control Group Size _ Baseline - The Decay Rate _ The Baseline * The
Control Group Size)

Then… One minus the other, and we are in business!
