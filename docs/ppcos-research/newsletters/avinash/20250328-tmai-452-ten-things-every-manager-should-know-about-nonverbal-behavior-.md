TMAI #452: TEN THINGS EVERY MANAGER SHOULD KNOW ABOUT NONVERBAL
BEHAVIOR.

[ Web Version [2] ]

At various points in my life, I’ve been a people leader. Teams small
and large.

One common theme over the last couple of decades is that they have all
been _Challenger _teams. The objective was to deliver
_transformational change_;_ _hence, I’ve crafted the teams to be
uncomfortable with the status quo.

Accelerating profitable change is not an easy task. Organizations are
designed by evolution to reject such teams. Throw in a portion of
leaders unsure of their incremental value add…
_Transformational _change is a million times easier said than done.

To make things (relatively) easier for the team, for myself, I have
consistently attempted to invest in my leadership style, to be
self-aware, and change. _Inspirational _leadership is easier said
than done, and I’m a work-in-progress.

One key lesson, that came later than ideal, is the value of NONVERBAL
BEHAVIOR. As I’d shared in my _user manual_ [3]_,_ I am
a _low-power distance human_ and operate in a _low-context
culture._ It took effort to understand and modify my nonverbal
behavior.

When I’ve done so successfully, the rewards have been positive.

I believe the same benefits can accrue for you.

One of the very best reads for me was research by DANA R. CARNEY. (Ex:
The Listening section, and Detecting Deception section below.)

Today, I’m quoting from an article published in the CALIFORNIA
MANAGEMENT REVIEW.

The full 19-page article is worth focused reading, and
re-reading. Please download it here [4]. To encourage you, from each
recommendation, I’ve pulled out excerpts that capture the essential.
All the words below are Ms. Carney’s.

While intended for Managers of people, I’m confident any individual
engaged in communications will benefit immensely – even in sections
where you’ll be like _wait, really, that sounds odd!_ There will be
parts that will be difficult for the human you are, they are for me.
Still, knowing is helpful.

(Ex: I strongly believe discussion is good. I actively practice
extreme transparency with available information. But. I EMPHATICALLY
believe solving for consensus is a high coefficient root cause
explaining corporate mediocrity. Still, I’m glad to have a toolkit,
below, to achieve consensus when I have to flex that muscle.)

Please read, please practice… We are all leaders, regardless of our
actual title.

Over to Ms. Carney…

TEN THINGS EVERY MANAGER SHOULD KNOW ABOUT NONVERBAL BEHAVIOR.

#1—AVOIDING MICRO-AGGRESSIONS.

Behaviors such as smiles, looking at others, speaking to others,
leaning toward others, and standing or sitting closely with others are
all nonverbal signals of “I like you,” “I want to include
you,” “I feel close to you,” “I hear you,” “I understand
you,” “I am engaged by you,” and so on.

The absence of these positive behaviors are extremely powerful
indicators that _you do not value some people, while valuing
others _at whom you do smile and speak and sit close to.

If you want people to feel equal to one another and you want to create
an environment of inclusivity, your nonverbal behavior matters a
lot—especially if you are the boss. To summarize, micro-aggressions
can be acts of commission (behaving in a negative way) or acts of
omission (withholding positive behavior). So what do you do to make
everyone at that oval table feel included? The toolbox below lists
simple, immediately actionable behaviors you can use toward to goal of
making sure everyone feels equally included and has an equal right to
be “at the table.”

TOOLKIT:

*
Look equally at people while you are speaking.

*
Speak to people for an equal amount of time.

*
Smile equally at people.

*
Sit or stand (within your comfort zone) equally across people.

*
Orient your body an equal amount toward all people.

2. LIKING AND VALUING OTHERS.

Behaviors that signal liking and valuing others are synchrony,
mimicry, listening, and ensuring conversational turn-taking.

SYNCHRONY is the simultaneous action of two or more people at once.
You can accomplish this by, for example, having chairs that are all
the same height. You can set norms such that during a meeting all must
make eye contact with the speaker and nod when they are inspired by
something, agree with a statement, or are feeling connected to what is
being stated. The norm of nodding and looking will be synchronous as
all members of the group will be doing it. All of these are different
versions of “doing the same thing at the same time.” And when we
are “in step” with others, they feel liked and valued by us and,
likewise, they like and value us more, too.

MIMICRY is similar to synchrony in that both are about “sameness.”
When others are appropriately imitating our nonverbal behavior,
whether it be a hand gesture, crossing one’s arms, touching one’s
hair, or any contextually appropriate behavior, we like them
more—and when we do it, it makes others feel likewise liked and
valued—“seen and heard.”

Listening—both actually and being perceived as listening—promotes
our colleagues, team members, and workers to feel liked and valued.
Finally, conversational turn-taking in a group allows everyone to add
their unique value.

TOOLKIT:

*
Look at people while you are speaking.

*
Simple at people.

*
Sit or stand (within your comfort zone) closer to people.

*
Orient your body and lean in toward other people.

*
Synchronize your behavior with others.

*
Mimic others (not too much) within 5 seconds.

*
Show that you are listening through eye contact, head nodding, and not
interrupting.

3. BUILDING TRUST AND CONSENSUS.

There is a psychological phenomenon lurking below the content surface
that can significantly contribute to consensus-building—even when
people do not agree on that content. That psychological phenomenon is
trust. Trust can sometimes be easy to achieve. Sometimes it is
sufficiently broken so that it is hard to resurrect.

Signaling that you trust and can be trusted by others is easy. You
should express an open body posture (which signals I trust you not to
hurt me and you can trust that I am open to what you are saying). You
should look at people while you are listening to them (to signal “I
am listening and I hear you”).

You can also use eye contact to suggest to a colleague or group member
that it is their turn to contribute—this is especially effective
after it is you who has just spoken. It is like using your eyes to
“pass the [conversational] ball.” This is also particularly
effective if you are the boss or leader of the group.

TOOLKIT:

*
Open body posture, arms uncrossed, limbs expanded appropriately.

*
Look at people while listening to them.

*
Use gestures and eye contact to facilitate conversational turn-taking.

4. LISTENING.

First, you need to make eye contact while listening to others. More
importantly, you need to make eye contact when you are
speaking—often easily forgotten.

When we speak we are thinking hard, we are often unconsciously darting
our eyes around the room, maybe looking at a wall. We must
deliberately look at the person (or people) we are addressing or at
each individual at the table.

When others are speaking, we can engage in two nonverbal behaviors
that are called “backchannel responses.” These include nodding
your head up and down as if to say “yes, I get it, I understand, I
hear you” and uttering affirmative paralinguistic cues such as
“uh-huh” and “mmm-hmm.”

TOOLKIT:

*
Look at people while listening to them.

*
Look at people while speaking to them.

*
Nod your head as if to say “yes, I heard you and understand.”

*
Utter affirmative paralinguistic cues such “uh-huh” and
“mm-hmm.”

5. AVOID RESTING “CRANKY” FACE.

Many of us look judgmental or upset as we sit there listening to
others. Some people tend to squint their eyes—for whatever reason.
Some people merely narrow their eyes and furrow their brows when
paying really close attention to what someone is saying.

The problem is not what people feel inside. The problem is in what
their facial physiognomy/expression is signaling. They may seem
intimidating, judgmental, and disapproving. Most of us are unaware
that we do this.

The data showed that narrowed eyes and furrowed brows make you seem
meaner, dumber, less thoughtful, lower on self-control, more
judgmental, and people would not much like to have you as their boss.
However, by merely putting your hand up to your chin, you are able to
transform how others perceive your facial expression. They perceive
you to be nicer, more thoughtful, less judgmental, higher on
self-control, and more intelligent.

TOOLKIT:

*
Cranky face” is hard to change; it is difficult to stop engaging in
behaviors such as this, but simple to add one.

*
Adding one simple behavior (hand to chin), radically changes others’
perceptions from “my boss is mean and judgmental” to “my boss is
nice and thoughtful.”

6. POWER (ACCESS TO AND CONTROL OVER PEOPLE AND RESOURCES).

Conveying power through nonverbal behavior is easy to do—whether or
not you actually have it.

Having an upright posture is perceived as more powerful. This means
sitting with your back relatively straight and erect.

Expanding your body to take up a little more space also leads to
others thinking you are more powerful. This should be used
judiciously.

Tilting your chin slightly upward is perceived by others as more
powerful (and more prideful —not in a bad way, but as someone who
has self-respect and is proud of their accomplishments).

Longer speaking time is associated with perceived power. Be careful
with this behavior as it is also associated with people who can’t
self-regulate (i.e., they don’t know when to shut up and they overly
dominate conversations and important discussions).

Having something to say is critical—if you are the type to stay
quiet, this can lead to perceptions of lower power. You will want to
contribute something—even if you merely reflect back to the group
what you just heard as a way of summarizing a discussion toward the
goal of moving forward.

TOOLKIT:

*
Upright posture.

*
Expansive posture that takes up space.

*
Chin up.

*
Longer speaking time.

*
Only speak if you will not be successfully interrupted.

7. STATUS (RESPECT AND ADMIRATION OF OTHERS).

Power is most often defined as access to and control over people and
resources. Status is defined as the respect and admiration of others.

There are two notable additions to power nonverbals that appear to
almost exclusively convey status. The behaviors that can boost
others’ perceptions of your status are: moving with highly
controlled and deliberate nonverbal behavior such as gestures, and
using laughter—both by laughing heartily and out loud and by
successfully making others laugh.

TOOLKIT:

*
Moving with highly controlled and deliberate nonverbal behavior such
as gestures.

*
Laughing heartily and out loud.

*
Making others laugh successfully.

8. INTELLIGENCE.

Conveying intelligence has both direct and indirect effects—both of
which are desirable. There are some easy-to-express nonverbal
behaviors you can employ to increase others’ perceptions of your
intelligence. These behaviors involve engagement.

When we appear engaged with, stimulated by, and connected to what
others are saying and doing, it tends to reflect our intelligence and
it shapes others’ perceptions of how smart we are.

Specifically, the more you lean in (literally) to another person as
you are talking, the more intelligent you seem (and tend to be). In
addition, nodding your head “yes” as people speak is another
indication of your interest and engagement and thus your intelligence.
And finally, the affirmative paralinguistic utterances such as
“mmhmm,” “yes,” and “ah-hah” also signal and reflect
intelligence.

TOOLKIT:

*
Leaning in to signal engagement.

*
Nodding.

*
Affirmative paralinguistic utterances such as “mm-hmm,” “yes,”
and “ah-hah.”

9. DETECTING DECEPTION IN STRATEGIC AND ORDINARY ENCOUNTERS.

They are not clear signals that a person is lying, but rather that
something may be awry. Use them as a guidepost to tell you to dig
deeper, because there may be more to the story.

If you notice that a person shifts their body around in their chair a
little (or a lot) and this is atypical for them—that is a time to
take notice. Or you may notice a person become slightly less warm or
less  positive than normal. Perhaps they will seem more nervous by
touching their hair or face when they usually do not, or they may push
back from the table when they are usually leaning in— engaged and
alert. These are examples of a person who is acting a little
differently and this is a change in baseline behavior and thus a
possible red flag.

Fake versus real smiles. A fake smile uses just the mouth—the
“apple” of the cheeks do not raise up, there are no crinkles
around the eyes (what are commonly referred to as “crow’s
feet”), and there is no small pucker below the eye, and the smile is
often closed mouth. In contrast, a real smile uses the mouth, the
apple of the cheek raises up, and there are crinkles around the eyes
and a pucker below the lower eyelid.

When we tell the truth, there is coherence in emotion (i.e., on the
positive-negative continuum) expressed across the various channels of
communication including the words we say and the nonverbal behaviors
we express in face and body—and all of those match the context.

Facial expressions that are inconsistent such that the top half of the
face is expressing one emotion and the bottom half a different
emotion.

TOOLKIT:

*
Changes in baseline behavior.

*
Fake vs. real smiles.

*
Emotional coherence across different communication channels (words,
face, body).

*
Emotional coherence across lower vs upper face.

10. USING THE WISDOM OF THE CROWD TO DETECT OTHERS’ THOUGHTS,
FEELINGS, AND INTENTIONS.

Making sense of others’ thoughts, feelings, and intentions can take
up quite a bit of mental bandwidth.

As you practice your people-reading skills, you will improve your
judgments: Are they powerful? Do they have the respect and admiration
of their peers? Are they lying to me? However, right now (before you
have time to practice) you can leverage the wisdom of a group of
people immediately to help make more accurate assessments about other
people.

TOOLKIT:

*
Devote 2 or more people at a road how to determine whether a CEO is
being honest (pool information).

*
Use more than one analyst to listen to a conference call to detect
signals of risk or low confidence (pool information).

*
User committees to make hiring and promotion decisions and pool
information before rendering a decision.

Managers’ nonverbal behavior has a profound impact on others—how
others feel about themselves, how well workers and colleagues perform,
and whether or not managers’ teams feel validated and included.

In addition, the cues listed here can help managers make sense of
other people—especially nonverbal behaviors that are associated with
both perceived and actual liking, listening, power, status,
intelligence, and deception.

Finally, pooling perceptions from two or more people can lead to more
accurate judgments of a manager’s team or a particular individual
that is being considered for hire, promotion, or even the
all-too-uncomfortable punitive moment about which many managers fret.

BOTTOM LINE.

When I first read this invaluable analysis, I was overwhelmed. _OMG,
am I doing anything right?!_

And, perhaps as a defensive mechanism, _there is no way a hand to the

Then, I started to use some pieces of the toolkit… Then more… And,
I could see the impact. There are elements I find challenging, others
I admit I’m suspicious of. All that said, the impact has been
immensely positive.
