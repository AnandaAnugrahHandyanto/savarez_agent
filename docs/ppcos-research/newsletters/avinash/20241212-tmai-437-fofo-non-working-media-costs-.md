TMAI #437: FOFO: NON-WORKING MEDIA COSTS.

[ Web Version [2] ]

You all know the more common _fear of missing out_ (FOMO).

An extraordinary amount of human behavior is driven by FOMO. For
better, or for worse.

There is one other phenomenon that we appreciate less, _fear of
finding out_ (FOFO).

This occurs when we actively avoid learning of a reality because we
sense/worry/fear that reality is terrible, and we are better off not
finding out. Assuming good intent all around, FOFO exists as an
evolutionary self-protection instinct.

FOFO is everywhere.

SIMPLE EXAMPLE: Your Performance Agency is deeply reluctant to move to
Multi-Touch Attribution (MTA) to analyze true impact of your Paid
Search – because 86% of the spend is on Brand Keywords. They will
fight you to death if you ask for CLS studies (incrementality). FOFO.

COMPLEX, OMNIPRESENT, EXAMPLE: Your boss INSISTS that the Analytics
team surround the _Analysis and Implications_ with so much
“context”, infuse the write-ups euphemism, bring up and highlight
every small gray, prohibits the use of Red, Yellow, Green colors
(“_they are judgmental!_”), and more, until everything data is
saying is neutered. He is afraid of the company’s Marketing will be
discovered to be a fraud. FOFO.

In both cases, and fifty-seven more that are on the tips of your
tongue, FOFO helps mask low quality of thinking, lack of effective
thinking, preserving the existing state of affairs, keeping the ride
easy, etc. etc.

It also harms the company/Non-Profit’s competitive spirit,
innovation, talent pool upgrades.

I am a fan of finding out (FFO).

It is why I ask for feedback all the time (_what worked, what should I
do/say less,_ and _what was missing that would be helpful_). It is why
my personal life mantra is _suck less, everyday_ – to do that you
have to be a fan of finding out. It is why I love data – warts and
all. I WANT MY TOMORROW TO BE BETTER THAN MY TODAY.

The choice to find out is always harder, and at times incredibly
painful. It is also not well received in FOFO cultures.

But. If you want your tomorrow to be better than today, choose to find
out. Life is short.

Now, a special case of overcoming FOFO.

WHAT’S THE REAL ROI?

On this flight, I’m reading an industry paper that postulates that
_ON AVERAGE_ the short-term ROI of $1 spent on advertising is $1.8.

It also postulates that _on average_ the long-term ROI, t_aking into
account sustained effects delivered over two years post activity_, the
ROI is $4.11.

[Immaterial for this newsletter, but… 4.11? I’m afraid it triggers
my suspicion - because that would require a level of sustained
commitment, to a consistent practice of Marketing, that is as rare as
a Northern river terrapin.]

Their formula for ROI is very good: (PROFIT CONTRIBUTION/MEDIA COSTS).

You get $0.8 of Profit, for every $1 spent on advertising.

It is not clear in the paper how Profit Contribution is computed.
I’m going to assume that at the very minimum they are taking
Advertising-Driven Revenue and subtracting from it the Cost of Goods
Sold (COGS).

I want to elaborate on the thing the paper did acknowledge not
counting in computing Profit.
The general bucket referred to as: NON-WORKING MEDIA.

To put Marketing into the real world, in addition to paying Facebook
or StarTV or The New Yorker to show your ad, numerous other costs are
incurred. These costs reduce the amount of money you can spend on
reaching current/prospective customers, i.e. they reduce _working
media_.

Let's dig into four large clusters of _non-working media _I want you
to compute to get a sense of your Real ROI.

The largest bucket of costs excluded from Marketing’s ROI
calculations?

CREATIVE DEVELOPMENT COSTS (CDC).

Usually between 20 – 30% of the entire campaign budget for DR
(Performance), and between 30 – 40% for Brand Marketing campaigns.

20% TO 40%!
Why?

Here are the elements that fit into that budget (thank you
Perplexity):

PRE-PRODUCTION COSTS

Concept development and ideation
Scriptwriting
Storyboarding
Planning and consultations

PRODUCTION COSTS

Equipment rental (cameras, lighting, sound)
Crew wages (director, cinematographer, sound technician)
Talent fees (actors, models, voice-over artists)
Location fees and permits
Set design and props
Wardrobe and makeup

POST-PRODUCTION COSTS

Video editing
Sound mixing and design
Color grading
Visual effects and animation
Music licensing

ADDITIONAL COSTS

Project management
Travel and accommodation (if applicable)
Insurance
Contingency budget for unexpected expenses
Obviously, not all of these costs will be incurred on all your
Campaigns.

Many items above will also depend on the size of your company (goes up
with size), the channel you are producing the ad for (more for video,
less for display), and the talent you are using (Mr. Clooney is
expensive, I am, let’s say, affordable).

Here’s the key point:

Your ROI now is [($0.8) - (20%*Campaign Budget)]
Unfun.

[Please change the 20% to whatever your actual number is for complete
Creative Development Costs (CDC).]

[In an interesting twist, CDC are a higher percentage for a smaller
company and smaller for a larger company – a function of the size of
the Campaign and amortization possibilities.]

Next big contributor to your _non-working media: _AGENCY FEES.

Often companies hire (multiple) Agencies to manage their planning,
buying, and campaign execution.

There can be multiple Agencies for large companies, none free, of
course. Lumping them all into one bucket, your Agency fees range can
be:

8% - 30%.
[Please hire Croud for all your Marketing & Analytics needs!]

Not included above are specialized agencies you’ll hire to do your
Organic Social, Influencer etc. Typically, these are not considered
“campaigns”, so likely coming out of your Marketing Budget. Be
aware of them.

Your ROI now is [($0.8) - (20%*Campaign Budget) – (8%*Campaign
Budget)]
The $0.8 is looking a little fragile.

On to the next big bucket.

Between all parties involved, internal teams, agency teams,
third-party providers, consultants, etc. “RESEARCH AND ANALYSIS”
can be a material amount of spend. To do effective Marketing, you want
all the tools and cool technology to measure if it was actually
effective. The range for total fees:

5% - 10%.
For larger companies, I suspect I’m underestimating the top of the
range (based on my experience) and it can go beyond 15%.

Your ROI now is [($0.8) - (20%*Campaign Budget) – (8%*Campaign
Budget) – (5%*Campaign Budget)]
Is there enough Profitable ROI left in that $0.8 for your Campaign to
still be a wise investment?

Our last big bucket holds a collection of items, without which your
Campaign is not going live. OVERHEAD COSTS include:

General operating expenses (office space, utilities, admin, free
lunches, etc.).

Company employees working on the campaign (salary, bonus, stock,
healthcare, 401k, etc.).

Fractional people costs allocated to the campaign (CMO, VP Strategy,
middle management etc.).

Fractional costs allocated to the campaign from conferences attended,
travel & expenses, etc.

Costs for special experiences needed to be produced for the campaign
(landing pages, retail store experiences (1P and 3P), mobile
experiences, new games/toys (digital, real), etc. etc.).

Legal.

The range for Overhead Costs:

4% - 10%
As in all cases above, please compute your position in the range (and
if you discover you are an outlier, please do reply and share any
insights re why).

The important bit…

Your ROI now is [($0.8)
- (20%*Campaign Budget)
– (8%*Campaign Budget)
– (5%*Campaign Budget)
– (4%*Campaign Budget)]
That lops off approx 37% of your Campaign Budget (or, if you are
keeping score, up to 90%!).

The Profitable ROI no longer looks as attractive as it did at the
start (and it looked shaky then at 0.8!).

A hundred small decisions you made along the way, re creative or
agency (/many agencies) or process or tools or staff, decisions that
seemed ROI consequences free, came back to have an existential impact
on your value to your company.

Worth reflecting.

Look, I get it… Without _non-working_ budget, your advertising
campaigns won’t have anything to work with.

My experience has led me to a strong recommendation that you should
compute your non-working costs. At a recent client, the Global CMO was
shocked that they were 52%.

Fifty Two Percent of the Campaign costs (!).

Imagine how quickly, in her mind, everything she had ever presented to
the company CFO re the impact of Marketing collapsed.

Every dollar you spend non-working is a dollar you could have spent
reaching more prospective or current customers with a message that
could increase the size of your company’s bottom line.

So.

Calculate.

1. Creative Development Costs

2. Agency Fees

3. Research and Analysis

4. Overhead Costs
If you find your _non-working media_ costs are above 25%, look
closely.

Ask hard questions. Cut where judicious.

Yes, yes, yes… Varies by industry/company/geo/yada/yada… But
imagine if you had to add _non-working media_ costs to computing ROI
(expressed as Profit). Would your advertising still be worth doing?

And… We are not even looking at any of this based on incrementality
– if you do, the ROI died at 5% _non-working_ costs!

BOTTOM LINE.

The real “enemy” is not non-working costs.

As the incrementality point above illuminates… The real “enemy”
is the fact that our advertising is delivering such meh results.

If instead of starting with $0.8 Profit for every $1 spent, we started
with $80 Profit for every $1 spent… Who cares about _non-working
_costs?!

Not me.

So, keep the passion on high incrementality [3], or, even better, high
marginal incrementality [4]!

But.

If either is unknown, or not high, dig into your non-working costs –
ideally before your CFO comes calling.

Carpe diem.

Avinash.

PS: Two of the most effective strategies to control _non-working
media_ costs:

A. Do not launch brand-new campaigns, with brand-new creative, with
brand-new goals, chasing the latest shiny object
(business/marketing/channel/thing), every three months. _Reduce,
Reuse._

B. There is no such thing as wear-out for an effective creative
concept. _Reuse. Recycle._
