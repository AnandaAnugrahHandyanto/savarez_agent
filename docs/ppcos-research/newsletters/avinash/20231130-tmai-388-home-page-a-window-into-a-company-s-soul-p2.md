TMAI #388: HOME PAGE: A WINDOW INTO A COMPANY’S SOUL – P2

[ Web Version [2] ]

This is a special time of year when we say thanks for our blessing, we
reflect on extremely major holidays for our respective religions,
ponder our higher purpose in life.

Of course.

But...  Also… It is shopping time!

[Coach Outlet Home Page]

Shopping time in Q4, also means heavy promotions – both to drive
(hopefully, incremental) sales AND clear out excess inventory before
the year ends.

Black Friday and the upcoming heavy sales season were also a trigger
for TMAI #387 [3].

With a sharp focus on just one page on your website, and only
above-the-fold experience at that, I’d share the six influencing
factors in delivering a magnificent home page:

1. Good Taste.
2. Self-Segmentation Capabilities.
3. Kill Useless Links.
4. Zero Pop-ups/Begging.
5. Speed!
6. Meets Basic Quality Standards.
In Part 2, let’s apply the influence factors above to solidify the
lessons via real-world analysis of what makes for an effective brand
and performance home page.

Since (like you?) I’m a sucker for deals, all the businesses we’ll
look at today, except one, are in the same type of business: Offering
delightful deals via their Outlet presence. More than any time of the
year, in Nov., Dec., they will be applying SALE SALE SALE as the
primary expression on their home page.

To keep things slightly simple, I’ll only look above the fold
(reminder: very few users scroll). All the screenshots were taken on
Nov. 14th, just as these companies were gearing up for Black Friday.

To make this a little entertaining (and useful) I’ll assign a score
to each on a 10-point AKHP6 scale. It will be the aggregate rating
assessing how they are doing across the six influential factors I’d
identified.

PLAY ALONG: I would LOVE to get your ratings for these home pages.
Email me your six scores, let’s learn from each other!

Ready?

#1. SAKS OFF 5TH.

AKHP6 Score: 3/10

[Off 5th Home Page.]

Admitting that _good __taste _is a matter of…. _taste... _for a
site selling brands like Ferragamo, Versace, and Moschino, the vibe
above feels very much like: _Let’s chuck good taste associated with
our brands out of the door, and go for the ugly Christmas sweater
vibe._ Oh, and while we are at it, let’s use the most garish greens
you can find.

Nothing about this whispers luxury, much less scream it.

The top 30% of the page, from the gray promo bar to the Dec 20, could
have been compressed to perhaps half the size, or less. We could then
pull up the Markdowns 35% above the fold. This would be especially
helpful on mobile.

I took the screenshot on Nov. 14th, it is odd that the first thing you
see is _order by Dec. 19th._ Perhaps a better use of that real estate
would be to use it to push a Black Friday, _buy now before we run out
of your favorites message._ No?

I do appreciate the options in the middle for self-segmentation. Her,
Him, Shoes... Good.

The top nav is also well done, with further self-segmentation options.
The red for Holiday Gifts, very nice.

Let's talk speed.

The PageSpeed Insights tool reports four scores: Performance,
Accessibility, Best Practices, and SEO. For each page in today’s
newsletter, I’ll share the first one. Here’s precisely how [4] it
is calculated. 0-49 is Red. 50-89 is Orange. 90-100 is Green.

The home page speed score for Off 5th mobile home page is: 31/100.

If you prefer, you can use other page speed test tools like Pingdom
[5]. Off 5th received a Performance Grade of 68, which is a D.

BONUS: A really great way to grab attention of your leadership team is
to show them the video DebugBear [6] captures. The 18.39 seconds to
wait for Off5th page to become interactive is painful.

NORDSTORM RACK.

AKHP6 Score: 3/10.

[Rack Home Page.]

Half the page is wasted.

Half.

: (

It is pushing so much valuable content below the fold – including
the self-segmentation links in the third box from the bottom, or the
More In (what!).

In a moment, after you see Kipling, scroll back up to Rack – and
you’ll have a concrete example of how wasteful Rack’s visual
design is.

Also, on an outlet site, with every pixel being precious real estate,
what makes the tagline, _more to rack, easier and faster, _important
enough to take up all that space?

Why is the first thing I need to do is Sign In or Create an Account?
Useless links?

I appreciate that they picked UGG and are going for it.

The imagery is certainly better shot than Off 5th. Who does not like
real smiles?

I invite your reflection on this... What does candy pink have to do
with Black Friday, Christmas, Winter, or the Nordstrom brand color
pallet? It feels improper here, like a garnish of garish.

If the desktop site is sub-delightful, it is typically a safe
assumption that the mobile site will crush your heart.

And, Rack’s does.

AKHP6 Mobile Score: 2/10.

[Rack Mobile Home Page.]

Why is the first call-to-action I see: Get our credit card? [Yes, yes,
I know they are profitable, but on Black Friday, SALES SALE, as soon
as I landed?]

Following that potentially questionable use of real estate, there is a
pop-up at the bottom to get their app. #zeroinstantpopupsandbegging

If you reflect closely, you will notice: 60% of the home page is
simply layers of navigation.

The actual shopping is that strip in the middle [which changes every
couple seconds, Marc Jacobs then UGG then…].

The links, under the middle image strip, could be used for
self-segmentation, but after Gifts and Location Finder, the agency ran
out of patience to type anything else – even though there was space
left. :(

The lovely _skinny nav_ on the desktop site was lovely, and could be
used for self-segmentation on the mobile site, I was heartbroken that
it disappears into the hamburger icon on the top left.

If they would just remove the main mobile call to action at the bottom
– _Sign In or Create an Account _– I might be able to see what
Ends 11/20.

The home page speed score for mobile is: An excellent 92/100.

KIPLING OUTLET.

AKHP6 Score: 5/10.

[Kipling Outlet Home Page.]

Team Kipling approach feels like:

_Hey, sure, this is our discount, excess inventory, site, but it does
not have to look and feel terrible. It can still reflect our lovely
brand identity - that we've spent millions crafting._
That’s a good feeling to send into the world.

There’s just one clear call-to-action on the home page: Shop Right
Now.

[Not, Sign In or Create an Account!]

Refreshing not to see any funky Christmas or Candy Pink color business
going on.

The focus is on the products. Good.

You’ll notice everyone today used a skinny bar for their top promo,
it is weird that Kipling Outlet uses such a thick one. A waste
(magnified on mobile).

You can still make your _skinny bar_ stand out, and communicate
clearly. See Premium Outlets below.

If there is one thing I’ve learned in advertising, it is that people
have banner blindness. The 50% and bags look exactly like a banner ad.
I worry, this will cause people to overlook it.

As a Mechanical Engineer, I like harmony and balance in my lines and
boxes. Maybe it is just my OCD. I’m not fond of the maroon bags in a
gray background, next to a rotating promo that is much smaller and
seems to be floating in the air. It all looks lopsided, as if it is
going to tip over to the left.

[For a contrast, see the harmony and balance below, Premium Outlets
home page.]

Nit-picking: These days it is so easy to geolocate a User, in the US
for sure. Why not use that instead of Select Your Location on the top
left. Why make me do the work? Oh, and if you have even approx.
geolocation, on the product pages you can automatically show me if the
product is in stock at a store near me!

The home page speed score for mobile is: 35/100.

PREMIUM OUTLETS.

AKHP6 Score: 6/10.

[Premium Outlet Home Page.]

Premium Outlets (PO) is also using up a lot of the real estate for
navigation – as much as Rack.

Still, notice how much better they are doing it.

PO’s challenge is that they are a massive shopping mall experience
(in the real world, and digital), and they manage to tastefully bring
15 of their big brands, to allow you to self-segment and choose your
path quickly.

I LOVE the Coach promo - Clean, elegant, enticing.  [The element
rotates across promoted brands.]

It is clean. Readable. Clear CTA. Memorable promo code. And, the bag
looks pretty enticing to boot.

The Search bar is not straddling the site like Rack and Off 5th, it is
tasteful.

I don’t know who many people will notice it, but I love the little
camera in the search box – teaching me that there are more ways to
search AND that the PO team is advanced in its thinking/execution.

I don’t see any useless links, do you?

They’ve managed to cram a lot into that top nav bar, stopping at the
very edge of overwhelming.   Well done.

The home page speed score for mobile is: 31/100.

FOSSIL

AKHP6 Score: 7/10.

I’ve found in my analytics work that simple works better re getting
people down the right next steps, that clear contrast visual designs
have lower bounce rates.
You see that in action with Fossil.

Even on Black Friday, they have decided to go with less.

There’s a clear promo, bang in the middle of the page, with a bit of
self-segmentation built in. Combined with the _skinny nav_, you can
find yourself to anything within the top 20% of the home page.

The branding on the home page reiterates what you are likely to see in
other places with Fossil, including the boxes in which they sell their
products.

Good, clean, photos.

I would have shaved off the "Fossil Outlet" up top, unnecessary
duplication. That would result in the Bestsellers peeking up a little
more - yum!

There is enough to entice me to scroll (and this is key… most people
will not scroll, but if you do your visual design well, you create the
temptation/need).

The home page speed score for mobile is: 25/100.

WALMART.

AKHP6 Score: 10/10.

[Walmart Home Page]

I am a fan of Walmart stores, and do visit them when they are close to
where we are.

I’d not been to the Walmart website in forever. And, I was
surprised.

Walmart’s website, in a lovely twist, looks and feels better than
their stores (which let me reiterate are imminently functional).

This is so rare.

Can you sense just how pleasing it feels to me to have boxes, and the
entire visual design, have harmony and balance? #love

You are not going to mistake this site for any other company’s, that
is how emphatic and clear the brand experience of the site is. Blue.
Gold. WALMART!

This is self-segmentation to the max. There are numerous choices on
the home page, but the visual design is so remarkable that it does not
feel cluttered or overwhelming.

Walmart is all in on Black Friday, they have a count-down, and the
only call to action on the site is tied to it. _Explore Deals, that is
why you are here! _

I don’t know if this was intentional, but it sends a tingle in my
heart that the hero image is “Gather ‘round,” this beacon of
capitalism, on a festival of capitalism, manages a little reminder as
to what’s more important.

Sweet.

Real estate in the two layers of the top nav are used smartly. In the
choices I see in the body of the page, it becomes pretty clear that
Walmart sells everything.

As I mentioned above, if you do the desktop experience well… Chances
dramatically improve that the super crucial mobile experience will
likely be good as well.

True for Walmart.

Let's consider the Walmart mobile site.

AKHP6 Mobile Score: 9/10.

[Walmart Mobile Home Page]

It is a very good mobile experience.

Notice, again, the consistent, clean and emphatic branding.

My small worry, one point docked (!), is that half the page is promos
and perhaps there was a way to have more options for
self-segmentation.

Perhaps Walmart has research that would indicate that the three Shop
Now links for food, clothing, and electronics cover a vast number of
use cases. That would be nice.

This is out-of-scope for our topic today, but do checkout the rest of
the Walmart experience.

There is so much you and I can learn about how to do digital
experiences right – regardless of our industry, business type,
customer.

I want to emphasize this: I am NOT saying you should copy Walmart's
digital experience (though, do consider it after you look at yours).
Their home page says: We care this much, we can set aside our
internal politics, complexity, and everything else to do right by our
customers. Copy that about Walmart. It is hard.

The home page speed score for mobile is: 37/100.

BOTTOM LINE.

Take the lessons from TMAI #387, and the examples above, and go ask
your UX team:

_How come our x on our home page looks like y and not like
Walmart/PO/Fossil?_
Pick taste, self-segmentation, useless links, pop-ups, speed or some
visual element.

Rejoice when they say:

_Wait. That does make a lot of sense. Especially since Walmart likely
spent $1.2 million designing and testing. We are inspired, and will
act. Here's what will be live in __two weeks._
If they give you a list of excuses and/or blame other teams in your
company and/or drag out a VP for a verbal flogging and/or point to a
six-month review to be executed by an external entity who will take
four months to hire… It would be fair to summarize, you don’t have
a home page problem, you have a culture and accountability problem.

Either way… You are smarter, having pulled the home page thread.

Carpe Diem.
