TMAI #461: BRANDING LESSONS FROM TACO.

[ Web Version [2] ]

TW: This is a post that draws lessons from politics. We live in a
fraught environment re politics, globally. If you are avoiding
politics, it is perfectly fine to skip this edition.

The current American President is a unique individual. What works for
him, businesswise, politically, is unlikely to work for other humans.

Still, there is a lesson we can all learn related to branding.

A decade from now, with enough distance for real reflection re
causality and nuance, many books will be written about this success
with branding. I want to draw one out today.

The President, as a Candidate and now, has demonstrated a knack for
understanding this critical fact of life:

*
A VAST MAJORITY OF AMERICANS DO NOT PAY ANY ATTENTION TO POLITICS,
POLITICIANS.

Yes, there are the political news hyper-caffeinated folks like me.
And, the journalists on Twitter and TV. Also, groups protesting each
week (regardless of who is president). Total all of them, and it is a
tiny minority vs. citizens in this blessed nation.

To achieve his success, the President has skipped needing to solve for
the news hyper-caffeinated, and instead has focused on solving for
that deficit of attention.

As a candidate, and now, has executed the following two-part tactic:

*
To break through the attention deficit, you need to DRAMATICALLY
SIMPLIFY the message. Ideally, to one word. Can be controversial,
mostly just needs to not be boring same-same.

*
You need to keep REPEATING IT AND REPEATING IT AND REPEATING IT AND
REPEATING IT. Unchanged.

You have seen active demonstration of the above for the last 15
years.

Most clearly in how he has dealt with opponents (in his party, in the
other party, with other world leaders).

He has given them “nicknames,” always disrespectful. Because,
heartbreakingly, that does help solve for #1 above.

Ex: _Liddle Marco_. For Senator then and now Secretary of State Marco
Rubio.

He created the nickname in 2016. He has used it consistently, and
constantly, since then. Including in May 2025 (!) in the White House,
partly as a joke, pointing to Secretary of State Rubio. Repetition. He
not only uses it unchanged, he often clarifies: “_And it's not
Little. It's Liddle. L-I-D-D-L-E._”

You’ve seen the impact.

The “nicknames” for everyone are here [3].

It is a measure of his successful execution of #1 and #2 above that,
even without clicking, you can probably name 10 of the “nicknames”
– independent of your political affiliation.

From him, the perfect example of #1 and #2?

The four-letter acronym you will not forget until the day you die. It
is an accomplishment of the President’s execution of branding that I
don’t even need to tell you what it is.

THE OPPOSITION’S FAILURE.

The individuals in political campaigns against the President,
political parties in opposition to him, have made uncountable mistakes
– including on branding.

*
They have not understood the attention deficit challenge.

*
They’ve not managed to come up with anything dramatically simple.

*
They’ve certainly never ever stuck to the magic of repeating and
repeating and repeating anything.

The results demonstrate that they have not just sucked; they have
royally sucked.

Oh, and it was not a Media budget problem. They’ve blown through
tens of billions of dollars on failed brand executions.

[A billion is a lot. If your salary was $1 million per month, and you
saved it all, it would take you 83 YEARS to save up $1 billion!]

In a break for the opposition, last week something dramatically simple
was created. It could be repeated and repeated and repeated to break
through the attention deficit challenge, and be super effective (in my
professional reflection).

TACO.

T ALWAYS CHICKENS OUT.

So good.

Short. Devastating. The President absolutely hated it.

For the opposition: Win-Win-Win.

Given that currently there is ample evidence of it being true, a
win-win-win-win.

It was created by Financial Times columnist ROBERT ARMSTRONG,
describing a “TACO trade.”

Some investors anticipate market rebounds when stocks drop
dramatically when the President imposes steep loudly publicized high
tariffs, only to pull back a few days later. Stocks go down on
blustery announcements, traders buy. Stocks go back up on the pull
back, traders sell. Traders derive immense profits.

As an individual politically opposed to the President, and someone who
loves/lives clever branding, TACO made me smile. Those in the
opposition finally had something that solves #1 and would surely break
through the attention deficit!

Ten seconds later, the balloon deflated.

The opposition has never demonstrated the intelligence or discipline
to stick to anything. Despite its potential, TACO is going to wither
and die in 48 hours.

And. It has.

YOUR BRANDING SUCCESS.

TACO for me was then a moment to remind myself, remind you all,
especially the VPs of Finance and VPs of Marketing, what delivers
incremental business impact via effective branding.

Humans have other problems to deal with. Many. Many. Many. Many.

*
NO HUMAN WAKES UP WANTING TO SEE ADS.

*
NO HUMAN CARES ABOUT A BRAND.*

Understand, internalize, tattoo this reality: We live in an attention
deficit world.

That translates into something devastating.

*
ALMOST NO ONE CARES ABOUT YOUR BRAND PURPOSE,* OR COMPLEX
MULTI-LAYERED BRAND STORIES.

Not because your complex multi-layered hidden messages brand stories
were not hard work. No. It is because you are offering such little
value and relevance and in exchange you are asking for something the
audience can’t offer: ATTENTION.

Simple wins.

Dramatically simple wins profitably.

*
_When it’s got to be clean, it’s got to be Tide._

*
_The ultimate driving machine._

*
_What happens here, stays here._

*
B_ecause, you’re worth it._

Though I’ve not named three of the brands above, the chances are
high that you’ll recognize them.

Simple is mandatory.

The reason you recognize the above, or the President’s nicknames and
four-letter slogan, is not just because of the simplicity demands very
few brain cells from you to process. The impect comes from:
REPETITION.

That is why this is your kiss of death:

*
EVERY THREE, SIX, NINE, MONTHS… A NEW CREATIVE, NEW TAGLINE, NEW
EXPENSIVE CAMPAIGN.

Failure of repetition.

Question: When did you see an ad you really, really, really liked
[4], and wished _omg, I hope it shows up 30 more times in my Netflix
queue!_

Incremental results from a long-term commitment to spending a ton of
money and time repeating and repeating and repeating… Unchanged.

_Open happiness _is one such brand’s choice as a simple cut
through. I bet you can’t name the brand. First, not simple enough to
tie to them. Worse, a lack of consistent repetition (not for a few
weeks or months, but over years, even decades).

Chief Marketing Officers overestimate the value of being clever re
branding. Then a lot of money is committed to unproductivity.

There is nothing clever about _Jake from STATE FARM, _but, if you
live in America, you can picture him, his colors, and some of his
shenanigans. Repetition. CAPITAL ONE? Repetition. GEICO? Repetition.
NY TIMES? Repetition.

SHISEIDO, HEINEKEN, MONTBLANC, NIVEA, TATA repeated repetition over
fifty years!

EXPERT ANALYSIS: GREAT. DATA: MAGNIFICENT.

Let me live up to the high expectation that a newsletter on Analytics
should back analysis with data.

Here are a handful of pictures that make the case, from The Institute
of Practitioners in Advertising [5]. It is sourced from an analysis of
4,000+ ads from 56 brands across 44 categories, spanning 5 years.

A. Brands that are creatively consistent produce higher quality
creative.

[Avg. Creative Consistency Scores.]

B. If the brand is disciplined about this, over time the business
impact (Market Growth) incrementally improves.

[Market Growth from Consistent Creative.]

C. And, this one’s the key, the real key to the expression of the
above discipline is to let your simply expressed branding by a SINGLE
CREATIVE to get somewhere close to wear-in!

Instead of pulling an ad as soon as it's been seen a few times (the
"wear-out" fallacy), consistent creative allows consumers to become
more familiar, comfortable, and even fond of the advertising over
time. THIS FAMILIARITY BUILDS BRAND RECOGNITION AND TRUST.

Here’s what happens when you get to 713 days of repetition!

[Impact from long campaign life: Non-Normal!]

See the problem with a new campaign with a new set of creatives
carrying a new set of taglines every three months? Or six months? Or
nine months? Or even two years?!

No one is sparing you any attention. No one has time to parse through
complex creative. No one can reprioritize their life to commit to
memory your ever changing messages over months, much less years. You
and I can. Because we have seen them on our slides. Repeatedly.

The IPA does not define what consistent creative is. Let me fill that
gap from my experience. Here's a sketch:

CONSISTENT BRAND ASSETS INCLUDE:

VISUALS: Logo, color palette, typography, imagery style (e.g.,
photography vs. illustration, realistic vs. stylized).

AUDIO: Brand jingle, sound effects, voiceover style, music themes.

TONE OF VOICE: Is your brand humorous, authoritative, empathetic,
edgy? This should carry across all communications.

CHARACTERS/SPOKESPEOPLE: If you use a consistent character, celebrity,
or mascot, they contribute significantly to consistency.

SLOGANS/TAGLINES: A memorable and consistent tagline reinforces the
brand's identity.
All of that _stuff_, so much to imagine, manage, and create, needs to
be as simply expressed as possible, and not change every quarter.

Oh, and one more absolutely critical bit from me... Ensure...

CROSS-CHANNEL COHESION:

The look, feel, and message should be recognizable whether a consumer
sees your ad on TV, social media, a billboard, interacts with your
app, or receives an email. A truly unified brand experience
(because… remember... I’m not paying attention to you).

D. Show me the money!

Here you go… In the IPA Study [6], the business effects between the
Top 20% (gold) and Bottom 20% (purple):

[Long Term Impact from Consistent Branding.]

And, pin this SUPER IMPORTANT POINT on your bulletin board:

*
To achieve the same business impact as the gold companies (high
consistency & repetition), purple companies would have to spend 2.32X
HIGHER.

#omg

If you have the ear of your CFO, be sure to bring this up.

UNCOMFORTABLE QUESTIONS.

With experience, I've come to appreciate that there are nuances that
define critical differences between businesses. There are incentives
and business structures in place that demand paths different from
optimal. Acknowledging this reality, I encourage you to explore these
five potentially uncomfortable questions with your CMO, and their
direct reports:

*
Is your Marketing’s expression survivable in an attention-deficit
world?

Can it breakthrough like “_Liddle Marco_” (but without any
disrespect in the expression)?

*
To overcome the attention deficit, is your branding simple, and
consistent over time?

*
What percent of your media targeting ensure relevance of that simple
and consistent creative to the human at the other end? It is just
_spray and pray_?

*
Why do you create campaigns every quarter?

What makes you special enough to deliver any ROI from the People,
Process, Agency, Talent, Time costs incurred – against such
overwhelming data that that does not work?

*
How long did your last five Pre-Testing passed Creatives [7] live
before being killed for the next “campaign”?

Why only 180 days? Why not 713 days? Or, 18,250 days?

BOTTOM LINE.

Effective branding demands SIMPLICITY, REPETITION, and the ability to
BREAKTHROUGH an attention deficit.

I believe you can deliver that.

_Carpe diem._
