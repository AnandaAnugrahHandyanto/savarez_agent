TMAI #405: A SIMPLE DASHBOARD.

[ Web Version [2] ]

The purpose of a Dashboard is to evoke curiosity.

The purpose is not to answer every question in the known universe.

An effective dashboard provides just enough about crucial business
indicators to cause our leaders to ask us intelligent questions.
That’s it.

Solving for this refined purpose This purpose creates an
exciting/scary proposition for some Analytics teams as they can’t
hide behind data puking AND they get a seat at the table to answer
hard questions from Senior Leaders.

The best Analysts can't wait for that day, as they come to the table
ready with the IAbI! [3]

The outcome is joyous: My Analytics peers and I ready to engage by
offering Insights [_why does the performance look the way it does_]
and recommendations [_here’s what we recommend as actions_].

Dashboards can have insanely complex and super-high-impact KPIs [some
of which I've created!]. You’ll be surprised how often they can be
simple, and have an outsized impact.

THE SIMPLE DASHBOARD.

I’ll share one such recent dashboard, with KPIs and segments
you’ve known for decades. Incredibly, it still provokes great
questions from leadership.

[When you do the math with the numbers below, you’ll quickly realize
that the numbers are not real. You’ll appreciate that I can’t
share a real dashboard for any company I work with. My assessment is
that that won’t reduce the quality of the lessons below.]

Here’s the _scorecard strip_ in the dashboard...

[Simple Dashboard | Scorecard Strip.]

The objective is to give a topline view of the business to the CMO.

You’ll notice the KPIs cover _acquisition_, _behavior_, and
_outcomes, _from my ABO framework.

I usually advocate a balance across the three. This one is heavier on
outcomes because we want to adjust to the current business reality
(and we cannot be dogmatic about how things should be!).

And remember my Number One rule for dashboards: It should be crystal
clear if the performance is good or bad.

[I appreciate some don’t like the word “bad,” with all the
confusion and deliberately inserted FUD in the business world, I am
for clarity. I use “bad.” I do use orange sometimes – like a meh
–, though I try and avoid it as much as possible for the sake of
extreme clarity. Please adjust to your political environment.]

You can see just how clear it is above what’s red (bad) and what’s
green (good).

My preference for adjudicating the performance (red, green) preference
is in this order:

PREFERENCE 1: PRE-SET TARGETS.

I LOVE them because it shows pre-planning. It is an indicator of an
organization that has robust analysis and processes in place. I also
see Targets when the Marketing and Finance teams work together –
this I adore.

PREFERENCE 2: INDUSTRY BENCHMARKS.

I find these set higher standards. Ex: The industry benchmark for
Ecommerce Conversion Rate is 2%. That is good context for the 1% you
see above. Some companies are happy to have high standards to achieve
(hurray!), in others external benchmarks trigger a defensive instinct,
occasionally political responses. Adjust accordingly.

PREFERENCE 3: YOY (OR QOQ) RELATIVE PERFORMANCE.

Every so often you don’t have targets, and you can't use industry
benchmarks, in these cases, the fallback is to choose same quarter
last year, or quarter over quarter. For seasonality reasons, I prefer
the former.

It is perfectly ok to start with 3, graduate to 2, and get to 1 as the
practice of strategic analytics matures in the company.
Once you have the scorecard; you want to provoke smarter questions.

My strategy for this: _Just segment, baby!_

To prevent data puking, we pull out three of the most crucial KPIs
that are presently important re business concerns, and segment them
into four slices that prevent myopic thinking by looking across Owned,
Earned, and Paid initiatives.

The simplicity of the approach is clear,_ no?_

Paid, Direct, SEO, Email. Four big _horsemen_. Each provoking good
questions.

_Do you mean we make as much revenue from direct as 80% of our
Marketing Employees working on Paid?_ Followed very quickly by…
_Wait, what do you mean by Direct? _That is such a great question
because it leads to conversation around multitouch attribution (MTA),
the consumer journey, all great and very welcome.

You can easily see the questions that will arise around yield - effort
to outcomes, just look at Email.

And, probably five or six more that will jump out at you. Like for me,
the decline in session and rise in revenue was a big one –_ did we
lose all the crap traffic, and that meant better things for us?_

Notice my tricky act: COST PER ACQUISITION is not actually on the
scorecard strip, but I believe as the Analyst that it is something
crucial that will help the CMO make better decisions about where to
invest their budget.

The question I’m hoping to provoke is: _Are we certain all the Paid
Media acquisitions are truly Incremental? They better be if the CPA is
$23!!_
_
_Furthermore, please, reflect on the choice to be restrained in
sharing the segmentation in just three of the KPIs. It would be easy
to puke them out for all six. But. Focus. Focus. Focus.

Crucially, above and beyond all else, I hope you’ll notice that
important questions jump off the dashboard above. Yet, the magic of it
is that while the KPIs will remain, the questions will go from SMALL
[you’ll answer quickly, they’ll never arise again] to MEDIUM
[you’ll answer will some effort, some might arise in the future] to
MASSIVE [you’ll struggle to answer, not a bad thing, and will remain
the focus for quite some time, also exactly what you want].

THE SIMPLE DRILLDOWN.

Senior Leader dashboards often don’t succeed because they are
missing the supporting structure around them.

It is good that our Senior Leaders can ask smarter and smarter
questions over time - triggered by the Simple Dashboard. To respond to
those questions, it is helpful for the layers below to also have a
line of sight to understand what’s happening in their area of focus.
This way, getting to that before the Sr. Leaders, they can understand,
identify, and be prepared with IAbI.

My approach is to attach a simple dashboard for three areas that are
typical in any company: _Acquisition_, _Behavior_, _Outcomes_. Roughly
speaking, these tend to be the “Paid team,” the “Site team,”
and the “Ecommerce team.”

As an example, here’s the Simple Drilldown I would attach to the
Simple Dashboard, for the Behavior team...

The scorecard strip remains nearly identical, except I choose to
insert Cart Abandonment Rate because it is a better KPI for the
Behavior team to help identify why Revenue is up or down. [In this
instance, CAR goes down 10%, you can see why Revenue would go up. One
important explanation down!]

I want the Behavior team to care about Acquisitions, hence the
Sessions remain. Subsequently, Revenue and Cost Per Acquisition are
less relevant to explaining their team responsibilities; hence I’ve
swapped them out for Bounce Rate and Cart Abandonment Rate.

My choice in this instance is to preserve the segmentation as in the
Simple Dashboard, the continuity made sense, it gave an extra ability
to answer leadership’s curiosity questions. But, on occasion,
reflecting business priority, insights I want delivered, I might swap
the segmentation out in the Simple Drilldown. So, do not feel
constrained – deep relevance to influencing change against business
priority is paramount.

Here’s proof of having made the right choices in this instance: You
can see the Drilldown explaining and underlining similar themes as the
Dashboard – Paid Media, what the heck is going on. Email could use
more love and focus. Etc. Etc.

You also clearly see hope for a continued bright future – getting
both Bounce Rate and CAR down by 5 points each would double Revenue!
[Ok, ok, maybe not double. But, loads of sweet new Revenue, without
more expense on Media.]

I have similar Drilldown versions for Acquisition, and Outcomes. Four
pages, one for the Senior Leader, three for each Sub Leaders to
understand and explain.

NOTE: FORMATTING.

I don’t know if pink will be the jam in your company. But, give it a
whirl. It just might surprise and delight.

Note the care and attention on little things, re formatting. Large
numbers. My fav font. Aligned rows and columns. Reds and Greens. Etc.

Lots of data above, but I’m trying very hard for it to not look like
a lot of data.

[A big missing piece in the Simple Dashboard and Drilldown are trends
over time. I almost always have them, even if just sparklines. This is
why we’ve covered data viz so much in TMAI. In this instance, this
simpler approach made more sense. I come to each of these situations
enriched by my experiences, but with no dogma. I find it to be an
optimal approach.]

REMINDER: IABI.

Data does not create a data-influenced org.

It is the IAbI [3] that creates a data-influenced org.

The Simple Dashboard is not a get out of jail card. :) If anything,
its simplicity ensures that more, a lot more (!), will be demanded
from the Analysts. This is a Very Good Thing.

BOTTOM LINE.

Complexifying is easy.

Simplifying is hard.

Choose simple.

Carpe diem.
