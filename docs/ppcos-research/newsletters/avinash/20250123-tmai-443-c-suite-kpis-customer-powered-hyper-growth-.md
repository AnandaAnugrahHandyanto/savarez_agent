TMAI #443: CUSTOMER POWERED HYPER GROWTH C-SUITE KPIS.

[ Web Version [3] ]

The CEO of a company looks across clusters of activity as different as
HR, Sales, Support, Product, Engineering, and more. They do so while
worrying about the company's present and future, and the drag from the
past. They inspire risk taking with innovation initiatives, while
limiting activity that risks current large revenue streams.

It is a complicated job.

I offer that in that job, beyond building differentiated products that
actually delight customers, a super high-priority task is to drive
business growth. Ideally, :), hyper growth!

Growth itself is a complex topic. Is our company in _growth mode _if
Revenue is increasing 20% YOY? Is it Profit? Or, perhaps, the number
of employees? Could it be entering new product categories, or new
geographies? Something else?

When the topic is hyper-growth,  acquiring new customers quickly
becomes an overwhelming obsession. All resources head to Marketing,
all the focus now is Paid Media. 😒

Look, sure, new customers are important. An overwhelming obsession
with them is a mistake.

I have delivered hundreds of millions of dollars of hyper-growth to my
clients by focusing on something profoundly unsexy: EXISTING
CUSTOMERS!

Hence, today... Let me persuade you of the value of Existing
Customers, the massively sexy benefit of predictable revenues, and
converting them into engines of hyper-growth. I'll do so by sharing my
Big 4 KPIs, an obsession with them will balance where growth comes
from AND you'll discover it might even be more efficient than the Cost
Per Incremental Sale Driven By Marketing.

Let's start by reflecting on a KPI that had so much promise, and is
widely recognized to be a _false God. _Then, let's dive into the Big
4.

I'll also assign each of the Big 4 hyper-growth KPIs to a C-Suite
member - the individual who has the max power to improve it.

Ready?

NET PROMOTER SCORE (NPS) | THE DEATH OF POTENTIAL.

If it is new to you, the measurement of NPS is sourced in the answer
to this survey question answered by existing customers:

_On a scale from 0 to 10, how likely would you be to recommend our
company to a friend?_
Proposed by Fred Reichheld, in 2003, the scores are categorized into
the following buckets:

9 – 10 Promoters
7 – 8 Passives
0 – 6 Detractors
The formula for NPS is:

(((Count of 9, 10s)/Total Respondents) – ((Count of 0 – 6s)/Total
Respondents)*100)
= NPS

The idea behind NPS is super, incredibly, awesome.

Cause: Deliver products and services that delight your customers.

Effect: Delighted customers will give you the greatest gift imaginable
(no, not LTV, though that too): They will tell others! Hence:
Increasing NPS = Leading Indicator of Future Growth!

Action: We measure NPS, everyone rallies around insights we find
around things broken about our product, our innovation pipeline, our
customer service, our walk – talk gap as a company.

Ex: We say we are a brand that _loves _our customers, and we send
them numerous promotional emails EVERY DAY. Lower NPS. Fix it. Only
one relevant propensity modeled email/week!

The fly in the ointment: Very few company cultures are built for:

A. that level of deep love for the end-to-end customer experience, and

B. that level of transparency about what's broken inside the company -
with _hide any and all bad news _being the default posture in most
companies.
So what happens when you activate such a potential filled metric, and
it runs into A & B?

Let me directly quote [4] Mr. Reichheld, from a recent article:

_Unfortunately, self-reported scores and misinterpretations of the NPS
framework have sown confusion and diminished its credibility.

Inexperienced practitioners abused it by doing things like linking Net
Promoter Scores to bonuses for frontline employees, which made them
care more about their scores than about learning to better serve
customers.

Many firms amplify the problem by publicly reporting their scores to
investors with no explanation of the process used to generate them and
no safeguards to prevent pleading (“I’ll lose my job if you
don’t rate me a 10”), bribery (“We’ll give you free oil
changes for a 10”), and manipulation (“We never send surveys to
customers whose claim was denied”).

No details are provided about which customers (and how many) were
surveyed, their response rates, or whether the survey was triggered by
a specific transaction.

Reports rarely mention whether the research was performed by a
reliable third-party expert using double-blind methodology. In other
words, some firms have turned Net Promoter Scores into vanity
statistics that damage the credibility of NPS._

You frequently hear this from employees in companies using NPS as a
CxO KPI:

“CAN YOU PLEASE _SCORE US 9 OR 10 ON THE SURVEY, IT IMPACTS MY
BONUS_.”
The employee _really _cares about the score, vs. learning to better
serve customers.

NPS is a "bad" metric not because it is poorly defined. It is a "bad"
metric because it ends up incentivizing undesirable behavior in
employees.

NPS lives up to none of its potential [building great products,
solving customer problems]. The measurement of NPS incurs cost related
to wasted time, effort, talent, in chasing the score.

Hence, heartbreakingly, NPS going up (or down) does not indicate
anything about the company's current or future growth rate.

HYPER-GROWTH KPI 1: NET REVENUE RETENTION (NRR).

My sense is this is indisputable: Your best customers are the ones you
already have!

Retaining an existing customer is significantly cheaper than acquiring
a new one.

Focusing on a Product strategy, a Go-To-Market strategy, a Care
strategy (me explaining [5], while wearing Google Glass!), to shorten
the repurchase cycle, nudge the AUR up (Average Unit Retail - thanks
Alex for teaching me this!), SHOULD be a part of your hyper-growth
strategy.

The KPI that helps you measure success with existing customers is
called Net Revenue Retention (NRR).

A simple version of NRR's formula:

(Sum of 2025 Revenue from 2024 Customers) / (Sum of 2024 Revenues from
2024 customers) = NRR.
Expressed as a percentage, it is a simple comparison of growth across
years of customers from the previous years.

For SaaS companies, you can also compute this by month. Obviously, as
Analysis Ninjas, segment it like crazy, no matter how you compute it.

Computation example: Our family placed 176 orders on Amazon in 2024,
and 141 orders in 2023. I won’t embarrass us by stating that in
revenue. Suffice it to say, Amazon’s analytics team is smiling at
our NRR. :)

When you focus on NRR, it becomes a great starting point for deeper
dives into upgrades, downgrades, x-sells, up-sells, churn, and other
core business metrics.

NRR is at such an aggregate level, it is less open to active
manipulation like NPS.

In the C-Suite, I recommend assigning NRR to the CFO.

HYPER-GROWTH KPI 2: NEW REFERRED CUSTOMERS (NRC).

This one has NPS vibes, except... It is all about the money [vs.
feelings.]

The simple computation:

% of New Customers referred to by the company's existing customers.
If you've followed me on _da socials_, you'll see me come back to the
same brands that I love - from personal use. Red Bull. Mammut. Rapha.
Coach. REI. Sony. I talk about them again and again and again. Guess
what, some of you listen, and you choose those brands for yourself.
Then, you talk about them. NRC.

I've expressed NRC as a percentage above. You can express it as a raw
count, you can express it in terms of Revenue. Or variations.

Identifying if a new customer was referred by existing customers is
via the use of a survey of all new customers. Simply asking for
_primary influence on buying a product/service from our company?_

Surveys have their issues. In my experience, in aggregate, as long as
you follow simple survey best practices, you will be able to get an
accurate enough read. [Premium members, pelase see _TMAI #377:  Do
Surveys Right: Lessons From The Frontline_. Ping me if you can't
locate it.]

In the C-Suite, I recommend a joint assignment of NRC to the Chief
Product Officer, and the Chief Marketing Officer.

HYPER-GROWTH KPI #3: EARNED GROWTH RATIO (EGR).

Another computation wisely advocated by Mr. Reichheld.

It is the combination of:

Returning Customers (RC): Customers who spent across consecutive time
periods. [My Amazon example above.]

NRC: New Referred customers.
If RC is throwing off strong NRR vibes, it should!

We compute Earned Growth Ratio (EGR) in two steps.

Earned Growth (EG):

(Returning Customers + New Referred Customers) = Earned Growth (EG).
A simple hop, skip, and jump to Earned Growth Ratio (EGR):

(Earned Growth : Total Growth) = EGR.
Measuring EGR consistently provokes strategic conversations. _Is our
Marketing team only obsessing about "adding net new customers" into
the funnel, or they are also helping us retain and convert current
customers into evangelists. How much Revenue do we have "in the bag"
already__? Are there ways to increase it? _And, so on and so forth.

It will also, in a Marketing context, force the conversation away from
Paid Media - which often sucks all the oxygen out of the room - and
Acquisition, to Owned and Earned Media, and solving a broader set of
problems with them (not just Do, but also See, Think and Care).

If you discover your EGR is low, that will be a weeping moment. Most
companies would find it extremely difficult to sustain a profitable
existence with a low EGR.

In the C-Suite, I recommend a joint assignment of NRC to the Chief
Product Officer, and the VP of Customer Service.

If you can only report one hyper-growth metric to your Board of
Directors, I recommend EGR.

HYPER-GROWTH KPI #4: CUSTOMER LIFETIME VALUE (CLV).

You'll hear both CLV and LTV in conversations. Both mean the same
thing. I grew up around people who used LTV, so I use LTV. :)

If you are going to retain a customer by delivering products they
love, being there for them in the _first moment of truth _(when things
go wrong) by delivering excellent customer service, it stands to
reason that the value of each customer is not represented by the value
of their first purchase, rather it is more accurately reflected in
their first, second, third... purchases.

There are 72,000 formulas for LTV.  Here's the simplest one:

(Average Purchase Value × Average Purchase Frequency) × Average
Customer Lifespan = LTV.
For SAAS businesses:

(ARPU x GM)/Churn = LTV
If you want to go all crazy with financial accuracy, go to town:

😮

Start with the simple one. :)

At a company level, LTV is an invaluable KPI. Among its many benefits
are:

Identifying valuable customer segments (the "whales!").

High-priority ideas for product development.

Annual resource allocation across all the company teams.

Elevating investments in Customer Delight/Service, Loyalty programs.

Churn mitigation strategies (including ML predictive churn models).

Informing pricing and discounting strategies.

Helping Investors to assess company's financial health, and future
potential.

+ 75 more company level tactics and strategies.asdf

As is clear above, few things fuel hyper-growth via existing customers
like an obsession with LTV.

In the C-Suite, I recommend assigning LTV to the CEO.
