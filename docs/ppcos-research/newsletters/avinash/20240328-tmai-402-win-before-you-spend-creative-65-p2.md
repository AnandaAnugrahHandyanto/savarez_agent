TMAI #402: WIN BEFORE YOU SPEND: CREATIVE = 65%! -P2

[ Web Version [2] ]

One more time… _It’s the Creative, dummy!_

Last week [3], I’d outlined why an obsession with the Creative is a
wise investment.

From extensive research, myself, industry practitioners, vendors, has
highlighted that an ad’s Creative is responsible for determining 55%
- 70% of the overall success. #omg

There’s also the fact that widening the aperture from _what Creative
does our CMO love_, to include _what might resonate with our target
audience _you are delivering relevance and delight – that’s got
to feel good (in addition to being significantly more effective!).

Here’s the best part… The universe has already provided a magical
tool called _Creative Pre-Testing_. The very best ones work with TRUE
TEST AND CONTROL groups, and identify:

A. how likely is the Creative to deliver positive business results
(vs. sucking), and, super helpfully,

B. identifies performance along 8 – 14 diagnostic metrics that help
you understand why the Creative will deliver positive business results
(or, why it is royally sucking)
This gives you a superpower: You can know BEFORE you spend a single
yen/dollar/peso, how likely it is that you are putting in market the
_thing _that will contribute 55% - 70% of the success of the
campaign!
_
Win before you spend._

Every CFO will love you. I don’t need to tell you what that does for
your career.

PROFIT DELIVERING CREATIVES | WHY SO RARE?

Creative includes the video, text, animation, display, and other ads
that your company creates. It also includes your emails, web pages,
logo, t-shirts, and any surface that a customer interacts with.

Kantar estimates that a vast majority of Creative in-market is
ineffective. In my experience, that's a result of these three
patterns:

1. The prevalence of the Creative Director and/or the CMO deciding
what a good creative is. This is less effective than ideal, but our
ability to Creative pre-test, at scale, to add data to the CMO's
opinion, is only five years old.

2. The mismatch between the PURPOSE of the creative – Ex: _Let’s
make mini-movies as the Creative Agency, so our client will be wow
’ed and pay us more _– and what the BUSINESS STRATEGY demands –
Ex: _To earn Market Share, we need to move Consideration by +7 points
this fiscal year._

3. The technical mechanics of the marketplace mean Creatives have to
work so much harder now.  Ads have to be _Skip-Click_ proof. There is
no inventory to run 7-minute mini-movies. 47% of all attention
you’ll earn is in the first 3 seconds. AVOC for Facebook Video is
approx. 1% (!!). Voice-over is magnificently effective, even if your
VP of Global Creative & Beauty deeply dislikes voice-over. So, on and
so forth.
The smartest companies in the world solve for _win before you spend_
by ensuring they accept fully the above three realities.

Sometimes, even the best of intention they end up delivering
ineffective creative because it trips up #2, and/or, #3.

Here’s a great example. Imperfect You [4], by Nike Japan.

_When the intensity of life becomes too much, a mysterious moon
appears and invites one teenage girl to embark on a journey across
time and space.  _
_She encounters women from different eras of the past who provide
clues to overcoming the pressure to be perfect._
A Japanese person will get the full cultural nuance of this
mini-movie, but even without that benefit, I LOVED this story. I
suspect, you’ll love it as well.

Setting aside that Nike is Nike… It is unlikely that this would
deliver Brand Lift in the real world.  Why? Except for rare inventory
like YouTube Mastheads, it is impossible to deliver this to the target
audience with the frequency required. It would be challenging to cut
this complex and multi-layered story into a 6s or 15s and capture the
lovely beauty of the story. You could cut it into smaller Part 1, Part
2,… Part 5, but sequencing in the ads ecosystem is crap shoot.

Hence, Imperfect You likely remains a fantastic mini-movie…. With no
distribution. Unlikely effectiveness. It still has a purpose. It is a
wonderful motivational tool for Nike Japan employees. It is a great
entry for winning creative awards for Nike. Furthermore, it also gave
employment to a mix of Creative team employees, an Agency (or more),
the talent, and more.

I believe we can solve for the tension between Creative VPs,
Agencies, CMOs on the one side, building Creative assets that showcase
of their individual super coolness, and the business imperative to
deliver Profit over time. Understand the reality outside the office
building + Let the target audience have a voice (not the entire voice,
a voice).

In TMAI #401 [3], I’d shared three tips that help ensure business
outcomes:

TIP #1: Make it clear who paid for the ad!

TIP #2: Ad Length: Your secret weapon.

TIP #3: Creativity matters.
Please read that #401, if you have not. With four to six examples
each, I’ve shared in-depth reflections on why each is important.

Let’s look at three more today.

TIP #4: RESPECT THE INTELLIGENCE OF YOUR AUDIENCE.

We know when you, company, creative, are faking it.

Here’s an example [5] from a brand with a reputation for being #1 in
the solar system. They have so much money, they can get an
award-winning Creator to direct it. They can hire any human on the
planet to star in it.

You will want to turn it off, please watch it to the end.

The story in the ad is a tired trope, but it can retain your attention
at least once. Did you notice, the gratuitous product placement? The
story has nothing to do with the product at any time (except once in
towards the end). Multiple products by the company are stuffed into
the ad, with the director zooming in to make sure you see them.
Nothing has anything to do with anything. If the tired trope was
interesting to you, it is unlikely you would have noticed the
products.

This ad was hailed as the _first cross-team collaboration by the
company, all product entities came together in a rare occurrence, what
a massively awesome thing!!!_ You mean random placement of random
products with no connection to the storyline?

The entire ad assumes we are brain-dead automatons who can be tricked
so cheaply.

Disrespectful.

[Let’s look at data as well. Not the be all and end all metric, off
65,000,000 views, this ad has 304 likes. An Applause Rate [6] of
0.00047%]

Here’s another one [7]. The car company is paying a prestigious
magazine to make mini-movies to earn the car company prestige by
association.

The prestigious magazine does not want to sell cars. So, they end up
making complex, layered, "stories"  that might be entertaining to
some humans but will clearly struggle with accessibility to most.
Setting that crucial point aside, you’ll sit there watching these
movies and wondering: _Why the entirely random car shots?_

Do they think we can’t notice one (the story) does not have anything
to do with the other (shilling the car)?

Disrespectful.

Exactly the same approach, _borrowing some brand equity_, except in
this case an advertiser, hotel, with a magazine, Vogue. Through a New
Lens: Osaka [8]. New Orleans [9].

Who really is winning with these creatives? Certainly not the
business, or the customers of the company who paid for these.

I provocatively offer that all five examples above are expressions of
hobbies of a Senior Leader in Marketing. They should be expressed as
such in the Marketing budget, and not as Creative funds meant to
deliver business impact.

To learn from an unlike source... Save the Water, by the state of
California, is at least imparting teachings along the way, I respect
that: Full story [10] (47 secs).

And, as my fellow carer of delivering results, you’ll be delighted
that it works perfectly well as a 15s [11]. [11]

TIP #5: SURPRISE, AND DELIGHT.

Depending on whom you ask, an average human will "see" between
thousands to a bazillion ads in a single day.

Add to that the fact that our attention is fragmented across
exponentially more activities, devices, media, when compared to even
20 years ago.

At a red light, which only stays red for 60 seconds, I often have to
honk the person in front of me – because the moment the light turns
red, they are on their phone, the light turned green 30 seconds ago
and they are still on their phone.

We are the generation that swipes left or right after a quick two
seconds of attention - that is all we can tolerate!

It takes a lot for something to grab our attention, and hold it. Even
for 30 seconds.

In an advertising context, it helps if your Creative delivers surprise
and delight.

Here’s an example [12] of doing neither. You will be bored after
the first three seconds, but please watch it all the way to the end.

Did you make it?

It was difficult, no?

This is from one of the most brilliant papers in the country. And, the
entire thing is self-indulgent, and utterly boring. They spent all
this time telling me they have things even a baby knows they have. The
kiss of death, if they get five seconds of attention it will still be
from “old people” like me. Nothing in this Creative execution will
cause a younger person to pause and make not – and that’s the
audience they need to survive/thrive.

Heartbreaking.

Like all other humans on the planet, I am a fan of Mr. Driver. Despite
that, it feels painful to make it beyond the first five seconds of
this [13] – and it is 97 seconds! Then, I think of the connection to
the product, and is this the best way to get the USP out, and what a
waste of talent, and…

Heartbreaking.

Surprise can literally be, _grab their attention from the first
second!_ And, you have to hold it.

Laundry Sauce demonstrated that in a compelling manner. Check out
Egyptian Rose [14].

Grabbed you by the ears, and eyes, right?

Do you care about detergent? No, why would we? And yet, here’s me
forwarding their ad to thousands of people. There are multiple layers
of smart creative tactics at play.

Note the clever use of text upfront – making the ad work for ad
formats where sound is off by default.

[My daughter watches everything with captions, even movies that are
in English and Chinese – languages that she reads, writes, speaks!
She says her entire generation is like that. We need to adapt to this
reality.]

Note that the product, both actual product and brand name, show up in
second 12 of the ad – early is better. And then, again, and again.

Note that the USP is underlined repeatedly, over 42 seconds.

Note a clear call to action, for five seconds vs. the more common, and
ineffective, one second.
The sign of every great concept is that it can power numerous
additional Creative executions. Laundry Sauce’s Creative VP/Creative
Agency accomplish that. Here’s Sandalwood Dreams [15], and Siberian
Pine [16].

The attitude expressed in the ads will work more with some of you and
less with others. That is ok. We can still learn from it. And,
you’ll agree, it is a surprise, and it retains attention.

You can be on an entirely different end of the emotional spectrum, and
still surprise and delight.

Here’s an example from Icelandair: The Polite Bouncer [17].

The conceit: _Iceland is so incredibly beautiful*, we have to have a
bouncer to get people to leave._

[*It really is!]

A softer pace. Relevant. Instead of the usual tourism approach of
come, come, come, Icelandair’s choose_ please leave at the end of
your trip_. Nice. In an execution that "coincidentally" captures the
beauty of Iceland.

Most Creatives by large, rich companies fail because the Concept
starts with a mini-movie of between four and forty minutes, and then
it fails gloriously in shorter duration formats.

_Easy to stop, difficult to leave_ also passes my multi-format test
very well: 30s execution [18]. A Shorts version [19].

Works on a flat Instagram surface just fine as well.

[BONUS INSIGHT: Imagine any example in Tip #4 could possibly pass the
multi-format test - without which you will fail the basic need to
deliver Profit: effective frequency.]

[Icelandair.]

Even if you are a rich large company, with a rich large marketing
budget for your latest Marketing indulgence, you will need 6s and 15s
but build frequency. And, please, please, please make note of this,
the secret to delivering Brand or Performance lifts is frequency,
reach comes third.

One more travel example, just because it delighted me [perhaps only
because I’ve flown two million miles on United!].

United, big game next year. [20]

If there is one thing that brings America together, it is the NFL.
Most people watching the SB will not be cheering for their team. The
ad taps into that in such a lovely manner, at an emotional level
that’ll resonate. And, so, so, so hard and rare, goes from that
emotional story to a product benefit seamlessly!

[United.]

What did you think?

Staying on the theme of ensuring concepts can work across multiple
executions… Same concept above, different executions that stay in
the same space: Cleveland [21]. [21] Houston [22].

inspire you: LinkedIn CRM [23].

TIP #6: BIG WIN: CLOSE THE LOOP!

My absolute favorite among the 200 or so creatives I assessed for the
awards.

Challenges:

A. There are Brand ads that get so off the deep end into the creators
crafting non-resonant mini-movies, that only slightly hint at the
product, USP, or even the company name.

B. There are Performance ads that are IN YOUR FACE buy now, BUY NOW,
sans any hint of emotional connectivity or even _why_ buy now.

C. Most Creatives executions, at best, demand passive consumption.

D. We can all agree: Nearly all business to business ads are boring,
and the 1% of the time they are not, they don’t know how to connect
to a large not business to business audience.
The Creative Agency, SVP of Global Creative, the internal teams at
Heinz solved all these problems spectacularly.

Watch: Tip for Heinz. [24]

In less than three seconds [remember, that’s 47% of all attention
you’ll get], they grab your attention, and you want to know where
this story is going.

At the eighth second [fast!], they’ve demonstrated product features
AND gotten to the brand’s name [most Brand ads are so shy about
this].

So far, a good ad.

Then, a surprising twist.

In 16 SECONDS (!!), they’ve pulled you in to considering doing
something weird: Leave an extra tip at the end of your meal in a
restaurant _that did not serve Heinz!_

[Tip for Heinz.]

At this point, I’m like: _wait, why would I leave my good money for
Heinz?_

Over five seconds, they solve that problem: Take a picture of the
receipt, tag us on the Socials, we’ll pay you back!

Now, they have all generations creating social _buzz_ for them because
when I post my receipt, a whole bunch of my followers will see it –
in addition to Heinz who will pay me.

I thought this was such an effective way to get the Heinz brand name
out there, and even if I did not _tip for Heinz_, the ad itself
reiterates that I want to ask for Heinz at restaurants and non-Heinz
ketchups are lame [as the first five seconds wonderfully demonstrate].

[CREATIVE TACTIC TIP: End cards are massively underrated. Heinz does a
fantastic job of the end card, over the last four seconds.]

I was already really impressed at everything they pulled off over just
35 seconds.

And, then they really got me – as someone who insists that Brand
Marketing should deliver business impact!

It turns out this is not a B2C ad. It is a B2B ad.

The Heinz case study shares [25] what happens after the “B2Cs”
like you and me sent in all those receipts – they were not just fore
reimbursements.

The team at Heinz input the contact information of all these
restaurants into their CRM system. From there, it because a classic
B2B story.

Their Sales team called the restaurants, sent them receipts customers
had shared expressing a preference for Heinz, and offered the
restaurant a deal to switch to serving Heinz.
_Cha ching!_

250+ restaurants – largest number from any Marketing campaign. 2%
growth in share [huge].
That is what I mean by _close the loop_.

I deeply dislike the concept of “funnels.” That was a helpful way
to think 60 years ago – funnels have not existed in the last
decades, it is simply not how customers behave if you analyze data to
any degree.

Funnel thinking also creates fragmented imagination. _This is an upper
funnel ad. We are building mid-funnel creative. Make sure to tell the
agency this is lower funnel_. Siloed thinking. Even if you escape that
trap, _this is a B2C, oh this one’s not B2C, we have to be utterly
boring and uncreative because it is B2B_. So on and so forth.

Pieces of the loop.

I’d developed See-Think-Do-Care as a modern Marketing framework
[26], where the same human can be in any intent cluster at a given
time. Go from See to Do or Care to Think or be in See and Do and Care
all at the same time – given their situation and time of the day. It
is a framework that challenges industry orthodoxy AND liberates the
imagination of Marketers.

Hienz’s ad is a demonstration that it does not matter what intent
your behavior is expressing, you can _hop on the train_ and go for a
lovely ride.

They build a “Brand” ad that does not focus “Performance.”
Thus, closing the loop – in 35 seconds. That it solves so cleverly
for a B2B outcome is truly worth admiring.

Joy!

Are your ads _closing the loop_? Or, do they continue to be
_fragmented execution?_

BOTTOM LINE.

If you solve audiences, targeting, day parting, Pmax, upfronts,
frequency, reach, inventory, delivery, and a thousand more variables,
it is very helpful in delivering results.

Yet, doing all that work, work we spend a majority of our time doing,
is kaput if you don’t have an effective creative.

Incredibly, if you have an effective creative, you can get some of
those thousand things wrong, and still deliver business impact!

_It’s the Creative, dummy._

Carpe diem.
