TMAI #481: WHY MMMS FAIL TO DELIVER IMPACT?

[ Web Version [2] ]

Media-Mix Models (MMMs) are fun intellectual exercises for company
Analytics teams (my natural home!), who spend considerable time and
money on them.

MMMs are often very profitable for the vendors who build these MMMs,
because a considerable time and money is spent on them. 😊

It then comes as a surprise that MMMs rarely deliver the hyped value
to the Marketing teams who commission them (and pay for them!).

Let me share an example.

During my time in London, I had the privilege of working with an
undisputed industry leading company. An external Agency has been
providing MMM analysis, for five years. They were an insane amount of
work, done well. Yet, in strategic conversations, the three most
senior leaders could not tell me of a single consequential Marketing
decision powered by the MMM/s.

It was all a charade. An exercise by the Analytics team in checking
off a box on their to-do list.

[In moments of weakness, I felt jealous of the Agency supplying the
MMMs. I so wished this was our MMM client so we could make the no
consequences easy revenue!]

Take any provider of MMMs (here's a list [3]). Go to their Clients
page. I’m sure you’ve interacted with Marketing from some of them.
Did you think the Marketing was relevant, contextual, intelligent? Go
to Google, type in a relevant non-brand query, do you see the company,
is the ad any good, what position is it in? See what I mean? What are
they doing with those MMMs?

WHY DO MMMS SUCK AT ACTUAL IMPACT DELIVERED?

Look, I get GIGO. Sure, Agency/Vendor x or y are working more or less
smart. And, of course, on the margins Meridian vs. Robyn vs. your
custom MMM has strengths and weaknesses. None of those bits are why
your MMMs suck at delivered impact.

Having observed companies across three continents, I’ve identified
five _barriers to activation_ for MMMs.

Often internal teams ignore these patterns: Unconsciously since they
are new to MMM value activation, or consciously because fixes demand
immense courage.

Often MMM providers ignore these patterns: They believe they are in
the _business of analytics_ and not _analytics of the business_ [4] -
which would demand more human investment from vendor.

Let me lay out the patterns so you can recognize them in your company,
and then the fixes to ensure the focus of your MMM is on new/better
business decisions!

1. THE ASSUMPTION OF INTELLIGENCE AT THE CLIENT END IS FLAWED.

MMM providers, have good experience at their end. They work as smart
and hard as possible. That delivers 70 to 140 slides of _analysis_
that are presented to the client – everything the vendor could think
of as important, and everything the client could dream up. The
presentation is often repeated to the CMO, many other teams. Larger
vendors also provide access to all the MMM reports in an online tool,
anyone at the client can have a go at it.

So. Much. Data! The flaw?

The assumption that across a 20/200/20,000 Marketing org there’s
enough intelligence to close the last mile gap: Identifying actions
based on MMM and driving actions.

It is rare that Marketers (Affiliate Lead to CMO) can read any of the
70-slides accurately. It is rare that the company Analytics team has
the expertise to look for biases, limitations of methodologies, and
recognize which 52-slides are irrelevant (_that is something MMMs
answer, oh this other thing, you get the answer from CLS and not an
MMM_, etc.).

The reality: A non-intersecting Venn diagram of business expertise and
analytical knowledge.

The result: No brave impactful business actions.

For internal or external MMM providers, the assumption of intelligence
and experience in people who will consumer MMM data is fatally
flawed.

2. THE MMMS OFTEN ARRIVE WITH BAD NEWS.

The first three to five MMMs you receive invariably have bad news.

And, that is absolutely ok. You did not have access to
incrementality-centric analysis. You were not doing
incrementality-centric Marketing. Of course, Meta’s incrementality
is 8% and Google Brand is at 7%. You. Did. Not. Know.

The flaw?

Most company cultures are not built to receive bad news with an open
mind.

Bad news = _OMG, I’m going to get found out. _= _My budget is going
to get cut_. = Defensive weapons raised. = ATTACK the MMMs!

And… Because MMMs are good at what they do, but not good at
everything else… It is not all that hard to attack an MMM to sow
FUD.

Rather than taking what is good about MMMs, many company cultures take
what is slightly imperfect and use it to discredit the whole exercise.

The result: No business action.

The company keeps doing non-incremental Marketing. ☹️

3. THE MMMS OFTEN ARRIVE “TOO LATE.”

A typical MMM might arrive in, say, July 2025, covering all the
Marketing activity from Jan 2023 – March 2025. That is pretty good
and, IMHO, timely.

Especially when the MMMs have bad news (in aggregate or in some rows),
it is really easy for _concerned _Marketer / Leader: _Oh, that is not
relevant to us anymore. You don’t have the last three months._

I also hear this variation from big budget holders: _Oh, you are
missing March 2025 through June 2025, we made a ton of changes to our
budget/campaigns/tactics, your analysis is no longer as relevant._

Both of these reactions are wrong. But, they are very common.

The result: No business action.

4. MMM VENDOR PROVIDES LITTLE TO NO _TRUST, BUT VERIFY_.

This one’s on the MMM vendor, and your internal analytics team.

If you combine #2 and #3, you can see a big trust deficit.

This makes it worse: Some CMOs have an outsized sense of _what works_
re Marketing. Then, when the MMM shows low incrementality from, say,
_upper funnel investment, _the focus is not on the low
incrementality… It shifts to the MMM is bad at measuring.

The MMM will show low effectiveness of Promotions, these words are
likely spoken: “_I have 29 years of experience in this business, you
don’t know what you are talking about._”

My favorite frustration: “_The MMM does not account for the heavier
cyclone season in Indonesia, how is it possible your analysis of our
underwear sales in Ohio is accurate?_”

The result: Data will lose all these arguments.

In part because the deeply knowledgeable person is full of doubt, and
the less knowledgeable person is often stuffed with confidence.
(Dunning-Kruger effect.)

5. THE COMPLETE LACK OF AUTOMATION.

This one’s tricky.

MMMs don’t lend themselves to tactical decisions. Ex: _What changes
can I make to my bids for insurance ads on Pinterest for GenZ in
Chicago? Which version of this creative drove the highest
incrementality?_

MMMs require interpretation, addition of context, and then they power
big decisions. Ex: _What happens if we shift 75% of our budget from
Meta to YouTube? _(Or, viceversa.) _At what point along the
diminishing return curve is our TV budget vs. incremental revenue?_

MMMs power shifting focus of your retail strategy, 1P and 3P media
redistribution, internalizing activation required for a 28-point shift
from Performance to Brand. Really, really, really cool and HIGH impact
decisions.

The flaw? It is very difficult to automate this decision-making.

(Contrast: It is easy to plug your MTA results into SA360 and automate
your bidding – eliminating all humans from decision-making!)

The result: Fewer/no business decisions.

We know we need to remove humans from the MMM analysis,
decision-making, so that trust, but verified action can be taken. It
feels so hard to think of automation for MMMs.

WINNING WITH MMMS | INCREMENTALITY AS A SUPERPOWER.

Now that we really understand the challenges, let’s get on with the
courageous and thoughtful fixes that’ll ensure our expensive MMMs
drive business actions.

FIX #1. LACK OF INTELLIGENCE.

To address this pattern, you need to hire a deep Advanced Analytics
expert (AAE) in your internal Analytics team. Ideally with experience
in algorithmic approaches (since MMMs are headed to AI).

This individual will invest in time to take the vendor
reports/analysis, add the context from your business, and convert the
90 data pukes into 12 relevant (pretty!) slides with 4 BIG decisions.

You also need to hire an Analytics Storyteller who will be able to
tell those ten or twelve stories in an accessible manner across your
organization and ensure the four decisions are made. Then, this
storyteller, your AAE, your key influencers will work with the
eighteen teams that will implement those four decisions.

Then, in three months, an extrovert in the team. 😊, will close the
loop with key leaders in, tell glorious stories of the impact, and
give those leaders all the credit for the impact.

Did the MMM make the company smarter? No. The deep expert and the
storyteller did.

Did the MMM need the credit? No. The people did. That’ll ensure they
come back and use the MMM.

FIX #2. ONLY BAD NEWS.

There are entire books written on how to have an optimal company
culture.

Read them.

Build a culture where:

*
Blame the messenger is not the default.

*
Bad news is an opportunity, not an attack.

*
(Counter balance) There is accountability for consistent non-action.

*
This is the how leaders lead: _Tough on ideas, soft on people_.

No one is perfect. Individuals, teams, companies, will always get
bad news. Including from the MMM.

A, B, C, D actions will ensure your MMMs are actionable, and that will
be 1% of the magnificent impact from the culture change.

FIX 3. IT’S TOO LATE.

Anyone trying to give you a daily MMM is trying to con you – MMMs
operate at a very high altitude, they are for very high-level
quarterly budget allocation type decisions.

IF your daily marketing activity is changing your MMM
results/recommendations every day it is “rerun”… You are doing
it wrong.

If you change your allocation between ASC and DABA by 15% and it shows
up in material MMM changes the next day… The MMM is not an MMM, it
is your daily Meta report which does not measure incrementality!

Your “daily MMM” is not even allowing the consumer journey to
complete before foisting Incrementality or No Incrementality!
Arrrgggh!!

Educate your leadership team.

Being three months old is normal, even desirable so that you won’t
overreact before the _impact horizon _is crossed.

Making daily/weekly/monthly major decisions about Marketing strategy
sifts is an indicator that you don’t have a strategy. Put the focus
on that, fix that vs. ask for a “daily MMM.”

If your MMM is six, nine, months old - that is a problem, fix it.

FIX #4: NO _TRUST, BUT VERIFY._

If your leadership doubts the MMM, the solution is not arguing more,
or puke out even more _here’s the proof _drilldown reports. You,
data, is going to lose all those arguments (and I’m assuming you are
super smart and did everything right.)

After the first MMM is delivered, and no action will be taken, that is
ok. Your immediate focus needs to shift to identifying the accuracy of
the three biggest recommendations.

_Allocate 50% of the budget to Meta ASC Attribution7D. Cut TV by 88%,
move funds to YouTube. Paid Search has 4% Incrementality_. Etc.

These are hard things to hear. Immediately set up experiments where
you can validate these recommendations from the MMM – thus verifying
the accuracy of the MMM, start to earn trust.

Cutting TV by 88% delivered a 40% lift in sales, as promised by the
MMM analysis. YEA! Good news, more trust in the MMM.

Allocating 50% to Meta ASC delivers less than 20% of what your MMM
predicted. YEA! Bad news, but now we can calibrate the MMM, work on
reanalyzing the data, which will build trust.

On and on and on.

Your MMM is doing a very difficult job, in an uncertain, complex
world. The rewards of that job done well are astronomically awesome.
If your accuracy is above 75%, celebrate, send bigger contracts to
your MMM provider.

Without Trust, but Verify… Why even have an MMM?

FIX #5: AUTOMATION CHALLENGE.

It is difficult, but we can do a few things.

The best MMMs have a Predictive Budget Allocation Model built on top
of all those sweet diminishing return curves. Why not build a simple
automated analysis that is emailed to the CFO each month with the
latest recommended budgets for each channel?

Same different: Automate the prediction of incremental revenue from
each decision the MMM is recommending, use it as an agenda item for
the CMO’s first staff meeting each quarter?

For some channels, Search, why not integrated with Google’s existing
tools and, with permission of the CMO, automatically change budgets,
bids, targeting (depending on the depth of data in the MMM)? Or,
integrate with someone like Smartly and kill the budget on Meta
In-feed ads because they have super low incrementality?

Something we are working on at Human Made Machine [5]…

MMMs can be confusing. Why not use the new LLM horsepower to add
_conversational analysis _on top of your MMM? Marketers, indeed the
CMO, CFO, can ask _what if _questions, they can ask for sensitivity
analysis, and the LLMs have already proven they can do this to an
acceptable degree of quality today. The quality will be awesome in six
months, and this approach begins to solve the interpretability
challenge that MMMs face.

While getting to 100% automation is not possible, the above ideas take
you from zero to 40. That’s better than zero. 😊

BOTTOM LINE.

Companies often choose MMMs based on the pomposity of the methodology
trumpeted in the RFP. An immense amount of time is spent on _look mine
is bigger than yours_.

This is a mistake.

Tools are the 10 in the famous 10/90 rule.

Focus on culture, people, process.

Fix what needs fixing.

You’ll shift to 24% incremental impact via Marketing – tens of
millions of dollars of new profits.

Carpe diem.
