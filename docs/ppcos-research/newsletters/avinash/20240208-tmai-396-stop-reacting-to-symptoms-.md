TMAI #396: STOP REACTING TO SYMPTOMS!

[ Web Version [2] ]

In a study published a while back, the results identified that married
men lived four years longer if the couples kissed goodbye every
morning (when leaving for work).

[NOTE: The research was for women-men couples, though it is hardly a
stretch to imagine that the results would be similar for couples of
all types.]

I was like, that is all it takes? “_Jennie sweetie (!!) we are
kissing goodbye starting tomorrow!”_

: )

While the study had a large enough sample, and it followed
participants for long enough of a duration, something about the cause
and effect bothered me. _It cannot be the kissing!_

It was not the kissing.

Upon diving deeper into the material, following the threads in the
citations, and further research, my discovery was that the data
applied to couples who continued to be in love –_ with each other._

Pulling that thread led to the discovery that couples who were in love
were in that state because of a number of factors, with the ability to
transparently communicate being a factor of outsized importance –
especially being able to communicate about elements considered
negative.

The kiss was a SYMPTOM. [A good symptom!]

The ability to communicate honestly with each other was a primary ROOT
CAUSE.

Taking actions based on the symptom rarely yields expected results
because, doh, it is fixing the wrong thing.

[NOTE: If I may… From a veteran of a loving 26-yr union, and
counting, a path that eases is to default to: _Yes, Jennie, you are
right_. Admittedly, a sample of one. Still, give it a try for two
weeks. Marvel at the joy that flows. Then, come back and thank me!]

THE ROOT CAUSE FANDOM.

It is perhaps now not difficult to imagine why I’m a massive fan of
root cause analysis.

Not everyone is.

Overreacting to symptoms is far more common. Symptoms are everywhere.
Easy to spot. Not all that difficult to find someone to yell at about
them.

Root causes are challenging to find. They demand persistence. They
require asking hard questions. They often need threads pulled you
might get warned not to pull.

Fixing root causes can also prove to be a challenge because they tend
to be complex, nuanced, and demand courage [as they are often people
oriented].

Here’s a recent example: I was digging into why Bounce Rates for a
company were so much higher, creating a drag on Revenue and Profits.
Symptoms included "bad traffic," "poorly designed landing pages,"
"seasonality," and more.

The root cause turned out to be the way the organization is
structured!

That might seem like a long way to go to fix a metric like Bounce
Rate.

Being persistent in getting to the root cause, gets to the root cause.
And, fixing the root cause to solve one issue, will typically fix
numerous other issues along the way - as was the case with the client
above, fixing fundamentally how the marketing team functions.

BOOM!

A STORY ABOUT FASTER IMPACTING TESTS.

Let’s reflect on a simple use case, to highlight the value of root
cause analysis.

If you and your organization are doing any testing at all, it is
likely that you are doing A/B or Multivariate Testing (MVT).

If you are in a larger organization, or one that has a conservative
culture, you likely run loads and loads of A/B, MV tests. There is
always a jostle for space, after all, there is only so much traffic
you can slice, and only so many people in the web and creative team to
do all the work.

Over time, you will notice that since there is an incentive to test
everything (you can see how that happens in a conservative culture),
and there is constant angst about how all this testing has become a
bureaucracy in of itself.

An Internal or External _agent of change _comes along and does an
assessment of the testing program, they will point out:

The testing program, team, is all gunked like an uncleaned carburetor.

Everything is taking a really long time to test.
To unclog the difficult reality, with absolutely the best intent,
you’ll declare:

_All tests will only run for a week._
_
Look at the results between the variations being tested, take action!_

_Move on to the next test!_
This is an excellent example of reacting to the symptom.

In theory, addressing the duration of the test will ensure more tests
go live at your company.

It would also be a gigantic mistake, throwing off false positives and
false negatives. You see, in that duration, the test may or may not
have reached statistical significance.

To ensure that if you repeat the experiment you’ll see the same
results (winners again producing higher profits), the results have to
reach 90% Statistical Significance. [3]

For tests that are testing high-risk hypotheses, they should reach 95%
statistical significance! [3]

To get to faster test results, while ensuring stat sig, you can dig
deeper into the root causes.

When you do, you’ll discover two super insightful root causes that
impact test duration.

1. The difference between the variations being tested.

This is huge.

The bigger the difference, the faster you will get results. For the
simple reason that they are dramatically different, people will
love/hate them quickly.

Test minor differences between colors of a button [the infamous
testing 19 shades of blue], the test will have to run for a long time
while you accumulate the minor impact of that minor variation.

Ditto, if you are testing something inconsequential in the digital
user experience, like variation in a sub nav in your mobile hamburger
menu. Sure, for some of the very small percentage of users who will
end up there, possibly there is a minor lift, but it will take you a
very long time to figure out that minor consequence.

Test a form that has four fields vs. the current sixteen… The
results will come quickly.

Test the current version of your home page that’s scoring 3/10 [4],
against a new version that is scoring an 8/10 [4]… You’ll get to
stat sig significantly faster – remember, that does not guarantee
that your 8/10 is necessarily going to win, just that you will get the
answer from your site visitors faster.

Conclusion: A. Kill small ideas. Or, save them until you have the
luxury of having fixed all the big things first. B. Have the courage
to push back on your User Research team, your Analytics team, to come
up with meaningfully big variations.

Neither is easy, root causes rarely are.

The payback is massive.

2. POUR MORE TRAFFIC INTO THE TESTS.

The higher the number of participants in the test, the faster you will
get the results. For the simple reason that you’ll read the
preference of the test participants faster.

If for a test, you take 5% of the site traffic, split it between A/B/C
versions, that will take many weeks to get to P <0.05.

If for that same test, you take 90% of the site traffic, split it 30%
each between the same A/B/C versions, you'll get the results faster.

It helps if you have high site traffic as well. A million visits a
month site, split 30% - 30% - 40% will yield faster results, than a
site with a hundred thousand visits per month.

You might feel this one’s easy, but it really is not. Risk-averse
cultures tend to want to go with _why don’t we try this on 5% of the
site traffic and see what happens_.

You’ll have to overcome that ingrained cultural drag – as root
causes often demand.

Now, here’s the fantastic news: Recommendation #2 works
exponentially better when combined with recommendation #1, above.
Though, if you fail to elicit big ideas to solve for your hypotheses,
#2 will still make things better. [Colloquially: You’ll suck less.
And, in my book that is a worthy objective!]

Obviously, this works like _da bomb_ if you combine it with
recommendation #1 above. But, if, for some reason, you fail to elicit
ideas that are dramatically different, recommendation #2 will still
help!

BOTTOM LINE.

To increase the impact of your testing program, and the speed at which
that impact is delivered: Encourage the sourcing of a few very big
ideas that are bigly different, and pour lots of traffic into those
tests.

I’ve used testing as an example to illustrate the significantly more
important point: Avoid reacting to symptoms, be brave and dig for the
root causes. They will lead you to fixing people, process, structure,
the three things that change the business world.

Carpe diem.
