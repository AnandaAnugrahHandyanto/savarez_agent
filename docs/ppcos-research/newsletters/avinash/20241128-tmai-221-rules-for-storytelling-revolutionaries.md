TMAI #221: RULES FOR STORYTELLING REVOLUTIONARIES.

[ Web Version [2] ]

Every one of us is in the business of persuasion.

We have to persuade our co-workers. We have to persuade our bosses.
And clients, and spouses, and kids, and people who don’t share our
political perspective, and our moms, and… Honestly, I don’t know
what else we do all day other than to persuade someone to think
differently.

Most Analysts think they are in the business of providing data, or
analysis, or even insights.
Nope.

They are in the persuasion business.

If your data efforts are not persuading your senior leaders to Take
Action, by making different, sometimes difficult, decisions, then all
your data efforts are worth _diddly squat._

All Salespeople surely realize they are in the persuasion business.

A few years ago, I spent several years as a part of the Sales
organization at Google, and I had the privilege of teaching
storytelling (often with data) in a sales context. I’d built
frameworks to aid with clarity of thinking, to more effectively
understand the industry and competition, and - my favorite part - how
to persuade through effective storytelling.

For meetings that required using presentations as a tool of persuasion
(to sell a product, to sell a new way to do analysis, to sell an
entirely new way of thinking), I’d developed a collection of
recommendations I called _RULES FOR STORYTELLING REVOLUTIONARIES_.

I wanted to share with you today what 3,500 people at Google have used
to win persuasion “battles.”

My custom crafted _storytelling ideation_ sessions would expand each
of these with deeper guidance, examples, and practice. I hope, even in
a summary form, you find them to be helpful in being more effective at
persuasion.

Next time you are off to persuade in a presentation/slides/keynote
context, apply these _rules for storytelling revolutionaries_:

1. Be ruthless. Only one data set per slide, making a single point.

2. Don’t repeat information. It is irritating - and distracting.

3. Visual treatments beat text. Paint. Doodle. Draw.

4. Never use a graph when a table will do just fine.

5. If you must puke, one mini-puke per slide, please.

6. Sweat the tiny details. Images (clear). Font size (big).
Backgrounds (nothing).

7. Reword everything to focus on the client, not your employer.

8. Your presentation is not a legal document.[If you have too many
caveats… you have the wrong data, bad data, or too much data -
address that first.]

9. If something is going to come out of your mouth, it should not be
on the slide.

[People can read faster than you can talk.]

10. If you decide to create dense slides because they'll be shared
post-meeting and need ALL the context, you've pre-decided to suck
twice.

First, when you present (people will hate you, and your slides).
Second, when non-attendees read your slides (they'll have none of the
critical value you added with your words).

11. Whenever possible, skip blatantly obvious facts. Respect the
other person’s intelligence. They will respect yours.

12. Have one focal point on the slide. Identify it. Hide everything
else. Speak to it. Make love to it.

[If each slide makes seven points, and you have 35 slides.... death.]

13. Have connective tissue between your slides/key points.

_This, because of, therefore do y._

14. Large, relevant, well-cropped screenshots rock! They quietly
demonstrate you did the homework.

[For each of my keynotes, end up taking an average of 350 screenshots!
With all that research, connected patterns and clusters form in my
head. Around 20 - 30 screenshots make it into the final keynote.
Persuasion requires hard choices!]

15. Random data points/graphs make babies cry.

[It is a mistake to believe data can persuade all by itself. If it
did, we would not have global warming. Data helps, but stories
persuade people. Be humble about the effectiveness of your emphatic
proof with data.]

16. Think for the client. Prioritize your recommendations. Rank order
them!

[BONUS READ: TMAI #255: Business Life Hack: Ordered Lists FTW! [3]]

17. Splicing multiple decks into one (random) deck is extraordinarily
ineffective.

18. Clients can smell a lack of confidence a mile away. Speak only
what you know.

If you can, only tell stories from work you actually did - confidence
will come naturally.

19. If it takes you five minutes to read the slide, it probably
sucks.

Revisit.

20. Sweat the metaphors. No one remembers numbers, metaphors plant
memories.

_First-click attribution is like giving my first girlfriend all the
credit for me marrying my wife._

_Bounce rate represents: I came, I puked, I left._

21. An efficient path to losing credibility is to use irrelevant
examples in your stories.

It is also a display of intellectual laziness.

22. You sell not by showing how great your product is, rather
demonstrating the compelling value that your client will get.

Outcomes trump activity! :)

23. Magic time-management formula for a 60 mins presentation:

3 stories max, 20 mins for honest Q&A.
24. Almost all of the time you spent crafting a great story will not
be in PowerPoint.

This is what it takes to craft a persuasive story:

50%: Learning about the audience/client’s business.

20%: Learning about their competitors.

10%: Identifying the higher-order bits in your head.

20%: In PowerPoint, crafting slides.
25. If your client has not picked two big holes in your story, they
did not really get what you were saying.

Note clients being able to pick apart some of your story as a badge of
honor.

I’ll end with an insight I got from Al Gore:

THE SLIDES ARE NOT THE PRESENTATION, AVINASH, YOU ARE.
So many, many, many storytellers get this wrong.

Your passion. Your commitment. Your knowledge. Your ability to
challenge. Your credibility. Your competence. Your challenger-mindset.
Your… That is the magic.

Here’s the surprise: Everything I recommend in the list above, all
the hard work and love I want you to pour into identifying and
crafting great stories is not about the client/audience... It is all
to showcase you - the persuader.

You are the story.

Not the presentation.

Knock ‘em dead!

-Avinash.
