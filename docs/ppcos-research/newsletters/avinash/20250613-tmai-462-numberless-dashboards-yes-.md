TMAI #462: NUMBERLESS DASHBOARDS? YES!

[ Web Version [2] ]

[Half-way through, I throw down a challenge for you. I look forward to
your responses!]

Do your Sr. Executives cringe a little when you mention _dashboard_?

If your experience is like mine… Yes, almost always cringe.

When I say dashboard… All of you, all humans, think… Data Pukes.

Not just data pukes, but Sr. Leaders immediately think of numbers that
don’t reconcile, they think of numbers that change from period to
period, they think of LC & MTA & Incrementality, and all the confusion
about not being able to explain _why_ for anything!

Confusion. Arguments. Frustration.

Heartbreaking. Reality.

A lot of work goes into creating dashboards. Often smart. Certainly,
well-intentioned.

I still believe, and please come after me: A half-baked dashboard,
used to make decisions, is better _I feel, therefore let’s spend
more on TikTok._

Even if, God forbid, you are using ROAS instead of POAS (see next
week’s TMAI), you are going to make better decisions with data.
Emphasis on better.

Even if you only use Bounce Rate, hardly even a Metric, barely an
Influencing Variable (See: TMAI #448. [3]), noticing that Meta is at
59% and YouTube is 43% is enough to make a better decision re where to
spend more. I would rather you use a KPI, but BR is still better than
your opinion nine times out of ten.

In order to make better decisions, we just need to get past arguing
about the 2% imperfection or a poorly created chart or that we said
Feb 2025 ROI was 3 but now four months later we redid it, and it is
now 2.7.

Still… Are we, high-minded digital analysts, picky CFOs, pedantic
CSOs (me!), making our life harder than it needs to be?

I saw something in the New York Times [4] that made me
think.. _YES. For sure, yes._

CONTEXT: LOWER MANHATTAN TOLLS.

On Jan 2, 2205, the government implemented Congestion Pricing to
access Lower Manhattan. From 60th Street to Battery, most vehicles
would have to pay $9.

This change was not well received. The level of bluster before and
while was sky-high. Even the NY Governor could not make up her mind,
swinging back and forth with political winds.

Somehow, the program went live. So: Has the program achieved the two
main goals?

*
REDUCE CONGESTION.
*
RAISING REVENUE FOR TRANSIT IMPROVEMENT.

They seem like straightforward questions to answer.

The second one is simpler.

In March 2025, Congestion Pricing raised $45 million in NET Revenue,
putting the program on track to generate approx. $500 MILLION in its
first year!!

The first question, _reduce congestion_, is complex and complicated. A
close cousin of _what’s the return for our investment in Meta
advertising?_ Well… So much to think through.

This is where dashboards go south.

Are fewer cars coming into Lower Manhattan? Congestion also comes from
buses, are they moving faster? If everyone’s moving faster, that
also means less congestion, is that happening? If more people are
taking Citibikes (I LOVE ‘em!), that’s a great thing as well, is
that happening? In reducing congestion, have we harmed businesses in
Lower Manhattan, fewer tourists showing up or people going to
restaurants here? That would be bad. New Yorkers have a reputation for
being loud complainers, what’s happening to that now?

In total 19 dimensions (!) that need to end up in a dashboard that
captures the richness of “Reduce Congestion,” beyond _are fewer
cars coming in?_

The data for these dimensions is in fragmented locations, and of
varying altitude. The cameras can do that last one, _cars coming
in/out_. For the other bits you would need to go to various NY MTA
teams, the Fire Department, restaurant booking platforms, yellow bus
companies, _yada, yada, yada._

The perfect recipe for a complicated numbers stuffed dashboard!

THE NYT GOES NUMBERLESS.

Emily Badger, Stefanos Chen, Asmaa Elkeurti, Winnie Hu, Francesca
Paris and Ethan Singer at the New York Times solved the data
collection, processing, analysis challenge.

And, then, they created this numberless dashboard…

[NY Congestion Toll Impact.]

It brought me immense joy.

Worth emphasizing that for all the rows, the data and detail exist.

For the first row, the NYT identified that 76,000 fewer vehicles per
day (!) in April entered Manhattan’s central business district, than
probably would have without the toll. That equals 2.3 million fewer
cars (#OMG!) for April, that would have been expected given historical
trends.

But.

In the dashboard, it simply says: Fewer.

Why do we need the 2.3 million?

Someone in the NY City government does, for sure. And, the number is
available to report, by multiple dimensions. But the Mayor?
“Fewer” is enough.

It allows the Mayor and his team to look beyond data and explore
nuance. No more getting stuck on if it was 2.3 million or 2.26594
million or some other silly imprecision that makes no difference to
the big picture.

It is my hypothesis that the leadership looks at this numberless
dashboard, and quickly jumps to discussing the biggest opportunity in
the data:

A. WHAT PUBLIC RELATIONS EXERCISE DO WE NEED TO UNDERTAKE TO ENSURE WE
SOLVE THE SUPER-DUPER IMPORTANT PROBLEM THAT THE PUBLIC OPINION IS
STILL NOT GREAT?

Nearly everything is going right re key expected outcomes, but it
really matters in public policy that the citizenry at large has an
accurate understanding of reality. [The last US President likely lost
his job because of failing to understand this.]

And, it is nice that numberless dashboard contains the answers to
provide the public: _Lower Car crashes, fewer traffic complaints,
reduced fire response times, more kids getting to school on time,
transit ridership “up, up, up”!_

From there, the discussion moves to the second most important big:

B. HOW CAN WE REDUCE THE IMPACT ON LOWER-INCOME COMMUTERS? WHAT’S
THE CHALLENGE? WHAT HELPFUL INITIATIVES CAN WE PUT IN PLACE?

That’s it.

NINETEEN + ONE dimensions to look at, but the numberless dashboard
ensures that instead of confusion, arguments, and frustration about
the data, the leadership team (I hope) moves to the two important
things that need addressing.

I appreciated that nuance in the numberless dashboard was captured
using:

*
Faster
*
Faster still
*
Faster, less delayed

Or

*
Down
*
Slightly down
*
Fewer
*
Too soon to say
*
Not great, but improving

When making decisions, occasionally it is helpful to know that
Conversions from Meta are down from 160,000 to 145,000. But, most
often, in making big budget decisions, with Sr. Leaders only, why not
just use “Down.” Move the discussion to the Analyst explaining
_why_ and the VP of Paid Media deciding _what_ to do?

I want to emphasize, the NYT had data for everything… Ex…

[Impact on bus speed | Toll]

But, it was not necessary in the big picture dashboard for nearly
everyone reading the paper.

Please see the NYT article [4] for all the details, it makes for an
impressive read.

NUMBERLESS MARKETING REPORTS & DASHBOARDS? REALLY?!

I really think it can work marvelously.

Shift the focus away from: Arguing about numbers, small differences
that are really not that statistically significant, and arguments to
distract from poor performance by arguing methodologies.

Shift the focus to: Asking questions. A conversation about actions.

Let’s try it.

Below are two reports and my favorite simplest dashboard. Numberless.

All of them are for VP+ meetings, high altitude.

I WANT YOU TO CHALLENGE YOU: Reflect if it would be more or less
successful in shifting our VP/CMO focus away from data arguments and
towards curious questions re _why_ and actions to take.

To help you not be biased, I’m not showing you the numbers version.
But. They are all at the very end of this newsletter. If it helps you
answer my challenge, compare them. But. Really let go of your bias,
really give the challenge a try, without scrolling down.

And. Write back! Will a numberless version work for VP+ meeting?
Why? Why not?

Let me be clear about the KEY to numberless working:

*
The individual sharing the data has done real deep analysis of the
data.

*
The individual is ready to answer the question: _Why did this
performance occur?_

*
The individual has identified IAbI to drive the actions conversation.

If the data individual in a _Reporting Squirrel _mode has only
gathered and regurgitated the data, a numberless approach will fail.
They need to be in _Analysis Ninja _mode.

EXAMPLE 1: HEADROOM ANALYSIS | BRAND MARKETING.

Before you run off and start spending money on Brand Marketing, it is
critical to answer: _What problem are you trying to solve? UBA?
Consideration? PI?_

To answer that question, most will compute baselines using a Brand
Tracker or look at the last Meta BLS results to find the baseline.

The secret to my success in driving profits via Brand Marketing?

Identifying _HOW MUCH HEADROOM EXISTS FOR EACH KPI!_

Here’s the output of that analysis, for a major bank, for the KPI
Consideration…

[Headroom Analysis | Numberless]

Does it work numberless?

Someone around your VP+ table will want to know exactly what the size
of the small black box is or the larger yellow. I would have a hard
time resisting. That is ok. Habits die hard.

But. I suspect you’ll agree, without scrolling down to see the
numbers version (!), that the above report works just fine.

It answers two questions really fast: We have a small amount of real
headroom (green bars in the middle), and an expensive to solve
headroom (yellow bar).

From there, the smart VP/CMO is on to: _Should we pick the green or
the yellow? Green? Great. What do we need to approve as next actions?_

Data fades away. Decisions being made.

Success.

EXAMPLE 2: TASK COMPLETION RATE | VOC.

If you’ve read either one of my two best-selling books, in any of
the dozen languages 😊, you will know that if I could only get one
digital analytics report, it would be:

*
Primary Purpose by Task Completion Rate.

Why do people come to our app/site? Always full of surprises, vs. why
we built the site/app.
In their view, not ours (!), how many were able to complete the task
they came for?

Here’s the numberless version of the report:

[Task Completion Rate | Numberless]

Does the numberless version work?

Does it help quickly identify what the discussion should be about, and
cause the VPs to decide actions to improve failure rate?

The report clearly identifies that a small % of the site visitors come
to conduct ecommerce – the reason for the site’s existence.

The discussion should focus on a single problem:

*
We suck at helping prospective future buyers, online and offline,
figure out what we are all about and decide to buy from us.

Will it do that without numbers?

In less enlightened organizations, I suspect the VPs might start
arguing about the density of red between Make a Purchase and Other,
and what’s in the Other – both less important, if not a
distraction.

If the report is being presented, numberless, by an Analysis Ninja who
is ready with IAbI, I suspect it will work.

Yes? No? In your company?

[If you want to compare, version with numbers at the end.]

EXAMPLE 3: WORLD’S SIMPLEST DASHBOARD | ECOMMERCE.

Let’s take on a big challenge, can you remove all the numbers from
your monthly CMO’s dashboard?

I can see the blood draining from your face!

Let’s try it.

A few months ago, I’d shared the World’s Simplest Dashboard,
[5] with three different slices, as a way of reducing clutter,
improving context, and speeding up decision-making.

Here’s the numberless version:

[World's Simplest Dashboard | Numberless]

Does the numberless version work?

I’ll admit: I struggled. I live my life in numbers. It was hard to
see a dashboard without any!
But. Once I was done making the above for you. I was like… _I think
it can work._

What do you think?

I’ve used a simple scale: Super Improved, Improved, Flat, Down,
Worse.

There is a performance range, using Actuals vs. Target, that power
that scale.

In this edition of the dashboard, there are only three important
questions to bring up:

*
POSITIVE: What did we do on our site to dramatically improve
Conversion Rate?

*
NOT POSITIVE: Why did our Targeting via Paid Media become more
inefficient?

*
NOT POSITIVE: Did the change in our Promotions strategy drive the
lower Average Order Value?

Nothing else is important. Sure. Things to smile about, a couple to
frown about, but only three significant conversations to have.

Will these three conversations be easier to have in your company using
the numberless dashboard (above) or you’ll get crushed and have to
use the numbered dashboard (below)?

Company culture and your CMO’s trust in the analytics organization
are two critical variables at play in the answer.

I’m curious about your answer. Please write back.

BOTTOM LINE.

In all four instances (NYT + my 2 Reports + 1 Dashboard)… I hope you
notice the numberless versions have a lot less on them. Just visually.
Especially, compare the Dashboard above and below.

That’s a big win.

In all four instances… I hope you sense that there is a real
possibility that there is less to argue about and be distracted with
re small difference in Metrics or KPIs.

All four instances… I hope to inspire you to give numberless a try.

Remember, MORE intelligent work is demanded when you go numberless.
That’s an incredible cultural incentive to create in your analytics
organization.

_Carpe diem._
