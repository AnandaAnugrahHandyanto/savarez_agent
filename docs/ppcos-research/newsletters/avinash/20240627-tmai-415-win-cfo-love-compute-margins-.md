TMAI #415: WIN CFO LOVE: COMPUTE MARGINS!

[ Web Version [2] ]

One reason we can't protect our CMO from a CFO's scrutiny is that in
Marketing, we declare success much too early.

In the best-case scenario, we measure Revenue, compute ROAS and pop
off to Cannes on a self-congratulation soirée.

[I'm not even going to quibble today that nearly 99% of the companies
don't measure Incremental Revenue!]

As I'd emphasized in TMAI #279: Most Important KPIs. (Spoiler: Not
Conversion Rate!) [3], Revenue is just the beginning of figuring out
what's Marketing's impact on the business:

[Most important KPIs.]

To actually figure out what Marketing's real impact is, to pass the
CFO's sniff test, we have to keep going all the way to the right, and
identify our real impact.

A key part of that is getting to the CFO loved KPI: PROFIT.

If for no other reason than that if you don't know how much company
Profit you are influencing, your job will get tagged as expendable
first in slightly tough times.

And, when it comes to Profit, there can be a measure of confusion.

Let's look at the three types today.

LEVEL 1. GROSS MARGIN.

This is the easiest Profit you can compute.

Here's the definition:

GROSS MARGIN = (TOTAL REVENUE - COST OF GOODS SOLD) / (TOTAL REVENUE)
Let's assume I work at Samsung, and am responsible for Marketing their
wonderful phones. (I'm a big fan of the Z Flip.)

I sold $100,000 worth of phones (that's my revenue after discounting,
coupons, and all other shenanigans).

It cost me $50,000 to make those phones.

Gross Margin = (100,000-50,000)/100,000 = 50%
Well, that's super cool.

At 50%, Samsung could be in business for a very long time.

LEVEL 2. NET MARGIN.

The COGS is just one component of the cost.

Your company employs you, and thousands more in the case of Samsung.

Your company pays for office space.

Oh, and all the amenities: Free food. Free bus service from the office
to your house.

Don't forget, Marketing and Advertising is not free. From your Agency
fees to Creative and Talent costs to other vendor bills to paying for
your Bing Clicks and TV GRPs to even thee costs of the 27 specialized
Marketing and Analytics tools you've deployed.

This entire collection of items are called Variable Costs.

You now have a significantly higher bar to cross - no more hiding your
value (or lack thereof).

NET MARGIN = ((TOTAL REVENUE) - (COGS + OTHER VARIABLE COSTS)) /
(TOTAL REVENUE)
Back to my employer, Samsung.

Revenue from phones: $100,000.

COGS: $50,000.

Salaries, total Marketing costs, sales team salaries, agency fees,
free meals, office cubicles, R&D, patent fees, and all the rest total
up to a variable cost of $45,000.

Net Margin = ((100000)-(50000+45000))/(100000) = 5%

If this were true, Samsung is making less Net Margin selling phones
than Whole Foods is making selling tomatoes!

By the way, what are the chances that the total Marketing costs is
just $45,000? Low. It is more likely to be $450,000.

Now, what happens to your Net Margin?

Do you know what the Net Margin delivered by your Marketing campaigns?

BONUS TIP: Measuring your Net Margin as your revenues go up is a
fascinating way to bring incrementality into view. Ex: Plot the Net
Margin curve along $100,000 revenue vs $500,000 vs. $5,000,000.
Surprises await.

LEVEL 3. CONTRIBUTION MARGIN.

Remember my famous quote about _why segment?_

_All data in aggregate is crap!_
Hence, when it comes to Margin, I love segmenting by product. I love
the brutality of bringing into sharp focus how much profit each unit
of product is bringing to the company.

CONTRIBUTION MARGIN =
((Price Per Product) - (COGS + Other Variable Costs Per Product)) /
(Price Per Product)
I sold an S24 for $900.

It costs me $420 COGS plus $225 in Marketing costs plus $175 in all
other variable costs.

Contribution Margin = ((900)-(420+225+175))/900 = 9%
A Contribution Margin of just 9% is not so good for a
_flagship _phone!

Also, once you compute this... Good questions arise as to _should we
spend $225 in Marketing Costs to sell one phone? _Or, _what else can
we do with $175 in variable costs?_

Good questions.

Contribution Margins become super-valuable when they are computed for
your entire product portfolio. They can help you set campaign budgets,
sales incentives and quotas, create smarter bundling strategies, and a
million other things.

Let's take one more step into Samsung's product line to really
understand this.

At Samsung, I also sell a mid-range smartphones like the A55, for
$400.

It costs me $250 COGS plus $210 in Marketing costs plus $175 in all
other variable costs.

A55 Contribution Margin = ((400)-(250+210+175))/400 = -59%
OMG!

Step One: I need to stop Marketing the A55!

Eliminating the insane $210 in Marketing cost will immediately lower
my CM to -6%. It sucks so much less.

Step Two: Cut other costs, or eliminate the phone from the product
line.

See what I mean by obsessing about Contribution Margin across your
product portfolio?

I'll be the first to admit that measuring Contribution Margin takes
some effort. You have to ensure your costs are being categorized
right, for other bits you might need help from your Finance team.

But, that is precisely why it ends up becoming a true competitive
advantage if you can compute it. It can inform more intelligent
marketing strategies.

The love of your CFO is a nice cherry on top.

BOTTOM LINE.

One line: _Give me profit, or give me (professional) death!_

Carpe diem.
