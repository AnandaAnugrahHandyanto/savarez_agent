TMAI #422: COLLECT MORE DATA.

[ Web Version [2] ]

Yep, you read that right.

With all the worry and concern and regulation on data collection, am I
sending you down a dark path?

No. No. No.

We respect all the laws. We ensure full transparency when it comes to
our TOS, and write one that is understandable vs. lawyer speak.  We
keep our data hyper secure to prevent leaks.

But.

I believe it is a mistake not to think about how we can collect more
data from our customers. Data that they share with us. Data that would
be helpful in intelligent customer segmentation, a more accurate
assessment of their intent (bidding, targeting etc.), to improve our
ability to deliver relevant content (which can sometimes be offers),
and a whole host of invaluable insights.

We try to, often painfully, guess from clickstream (digital), camera
reels (offline), what our customers want, why they were there, how did
we disappoint/delight them? Occasionally, we can… just ask. Won’t
work for every use case, but you’ll be surprised how frequently a
little more data can be transformative.

I was reminded of this by www.beyondyoga.com [3], where I went looking
for their super comfy travel pants.

All of us request an email address from our website visitors.

Often, to make the process as frictionless as possible, we just ask
for the email address.
This is Beyond Yoga asking…

[Beyond Yoga | Mobile]

Email address, yes. For sure.

Additionally, they are taking the opportunity to get to know you
better by asking you for your product preference: Maternity. Men’s.

Notice they did not go all crazy, just two additional bits.

Notice also their assumption is, as a business primarily with women's
clothing, they skip asking if your product preference is women. If you
don’t click a box, they would assume you are, and be right most of
the time.

The two choices they offer are information they could really use.

Finally, notice it is opt-in.

Brilliant.

Smart, customer-choice providing, data collection.

This data can be used for better content targeting for the emails and
SMSs they send out. It can be used in the digital analytics tool to
analyze site behavior, for their product merchandizing, and so much
more.

They could probably get to this via the guessing route. _Let’s see
how many emails were signed up on the Maternity page, we will classify
all those people as Maternity customers._ Not optimal. Why not just
ask?

SPARKS FOR PERMISSION-BASED DATA COLLECTION.

To add to your passive data collection (GA4, video cameras), here are
some active data collection tactics that I’ve used with our clients
over time. I hope they spark your imagination.

1. USE THE ORDER CONFIRMATION EMAILS.

If it was a Guest Checkout, in the bottom third of the Order
Confirmation email, after ensuring you have simply communicated all
the order details, ask the customer to convert into opening an
account. Share the benefits like being able to track order history in
one place, any other perks you have like loyalty programs etc.

If it was an existing customer, consider adding one or two super
crucial form fields to the Order Confirmation emails to get a bit more
insight into the customer. Their company size and job title. Their
shoe size and favorite color. If they had shopped in the Retail Store
before buying using the mobile app. Whatever minimal high-value data
that would allow you to understand the customer better and deliver
relevance.

For someone who has purchased twice, offering an opt-in nudge to sign
up for your loyalty program. Typically, companies ask for a bit more
data during this process, ensure you communicate that clearly and what
benefits the customer gets. This data can be super helpful for your
Propensity Modeling initiatives.

[Premium member bonus: TMAI #379: Unlocking Machine Learning:
Propensity Models [4].]

2. IMPLEMENT DRIPS TO COLLECT DATA THOUGHTFULLY OVER TIME.

This newsletter comes to you via Email Octopus [5]. They have a thing
called _drips_. You build a workflow with emails sent to customers
using time triggers (every few days, every few weeks, etc.) or
triggered by specific actions. In these emails, you share information
normally helpful to your customers, say product onboarding process,
along with a series of ever useful tips. Take the initiative to ask
for a little more data around your customer’s preferences.

This is particularly useful for B2B. Long sales cycles, multiple
decision makers, complex needs.

[Clarification: You can, of course, also analyze a customer's clicks
or other interaction elements. These are _INFERRED DATA POINTS_.
Everything above will be _EXPLICIT DATA POINTS_ – no guessing.]

3. TIE ONLINE DATA TO OFFLINE BEHAVIOR.

When customers are checking out in your Retail Store, ask them if they
already have an account with you.  At my Sainsbury’s in London, my
Nectar card plays that role. When my kids are checkout out at
Chipotle, their account numbers play that role. You might use phone
number, email address (at my REI) or other primary keys to solve for
the same purpose.

Do this in a way that there is a benefit to your customers, and not
just you. Sainsbury’s gives me discounts on some stuff. With other
companies, I get a priority customer service phone number in exchange
for the _primary key_. You catch my drift.

The challenge is to incentivize your Store Associates to ask, and to
make it a part of their workflow.

4. Offer personalized experiences.

A while back, looking for the right bra, I used the Bra Finder on
Victoria’s Secret. I had to measure my band size, my bust size, my
shape type, my style preferences (wireless, lightly lined, etc.). In
the end, not only I get my bra size, I also got an entirely customized
store just for myself.

They got data that provides them with invaluable insight, and me a
custom bra store every time I come back.

If you are Salesforce, you can emulate this. If you are David Yurman,
you can emulate this. If you are Stick With Me Sweets, you can emulate
this.

5. Contextualize quantitative data with qualitative data.

A bit more complicated, but you are likely using surveys (Primary
Purpose by Task Completion Rate is my absolute favorite digital
analytics computation). With a little bit of creativity, after the
main short survey, you can ask for a piece or two of highly valuable
customer data that will help you classify and understand the data
better.

I’m currently having a painful customer service thread with Samsung.
But. At the end of the chat, they ask for a rating for the chat Agent.
Then, they ask a question that gives them more data about me. My
problem is not solved, but I filled it out because I appreciate them
doing what I’m saying in this email!

If surveys are a healthy part of your Analytics practice, and it
should be, you can put your survey data into your Analytics tool. Now,
even without any additional data from customer, you have a dataset to
help you understand _why _behind the Customer and also the _what _they
did. Deeply insightful.

3Q! [6]]

6. GAMIFY DATA COLLECTION.

You’ve seen loads of examples of this.

_
You’ve completed 5 of 7 levels of your Account completion.__

Reach Gold level of by doing…_

View top Customers Leaderboard in your area.

Your use of the Nest Thermostat saved 1,200 trees already, customize
your preferences and save even more.

One completed Customer review is read by 145k Users on average on our
website._
_You’ll have other ideas to create quizzes or fun challenges that
reward your customers with points or badges.

The challenge is knowing when you’ve reached diminishing returns,
and then stop collecting data. If you don’t know when to stop, it is
when your fair assessment indicates the additional data is only
helpful to you and not the customer.

7. STITCH YOUR COMPANY DATA TOGETHER!

The hardest, yet simplest, way to “collect more data” is to join
up all the data you already have about your customers.

The data from your website, CRM, ERP, Email, Ad Platforms, Stores,
Product, and numerous other sources. It can help you understand far
more than Marketing effectiveness.

The challenge, of course, is that the data is likely spread across
multiple systems, teams, politics, and priorities. Still. If there is
a central senior enough owner who will be an advocate, using data you
already have is the smartest data collection of it all. It should all
be disclosed up front in your privacy policies, obviously.

A WORD OF CAUTION.

Across 421 editions of TMAI, across nearly one-million words written
on Occam’s Razor [7], one consistent pattern is that I have rarely
focused on data collection.

My global experience, across companies large and small, has taught me
that companies rarely full advantage of the data they already have!
And, I’m sorry, Analysts obsess way too much about collecting more
and more and more data – instead of doing their job: Analysis and
IAbI.

It is easy to slip into_ __the business of analytics vs. the business
of analytics [8]._

So. Please do reflect on my advice in this newsletter. Do, please act
on it thoughtfully, favoring the value you deliver to the customer by
collecting a bit more data. Don’t let data collection become the
main purpose of your life in Analytics.

BOTTOM LINE.

It can be immensely helpful to get explicit data points, to add to all
the inferred data points we have (in tremendous amounts).

Listen carefully to the questions your C-Suite is asking you. Reflect
thoughtfully on the patterns you are unable to identify. Consider the
most frequent dead ends in your analysis.

Then. Put a transparent plan to have your customers contribute just
the data you need.

Joy follows.

Carpe Diem.
