TMAI #398: UNCOOL METRICS: “SHARE OF SEARCH.”

[ Web Version [2] ]

The KPI _SHARE OF SEARCH_ gets bandied about left and right, by
Practitioners and Gurus.

I’ve rarely seen it measured completely or consistently.

Then, you can imagine why I get distressed when it shows up on CMO
dashboards or reports for the Search Engine Marketing Director of
Glorious Glory.

Pause.

A step back.

In TMAI #398 [3], I’d shared my concerns about using the metric
Count of Brand Queries on Google. It is used as a measure of the
effectiveness of brand Marketing.

My tactical concern was the methodology used to measure it, often the
suboptimal Pre-Post [NO!] or plotting it as a stand-alone trend on a
graph with a non-zero y-axis [NOOO!].

My strategic concern was the ignoring of the foundational shifts in
butterfly effect of how brand Marketing works, and how humans seek.

From…

To...

The metric, Brand Queries on Google, represents what now?

With that as our springboard, I want to explore the other popular
Search related KPI: _Search of Search._

SHARE OF SEARCH | WHAT?

What’s your definition of _Search of Search_? Please hit reply.

[NOTE: Ask yourself the same question, if you are using KPI called
_SHARE OF VOICE_ – another highly, highly, suspicious KPI that seems
to mean everything to some.]

On occasion, I bump into _Share of Search_ as:

PAID SEARCH CLICK SHARE VS. COMPETITOR A
Any variation of defining Share of Search with the narrowness of Paid
Search is irresponsible to the max. Even if you are using _click share
for organic term abc_ vs. _competitor A_. If you are using any PPC
variation, you need to stop. It is a _spend more on Search_ exercise
of the worst kind.

Usually, Share of Search is measured as:

% OF OUR QUERIES ON GOOGLE
What’s “our,” and as a % of what?

% OF QUERIES FOR A BASKET OF OUR BRAND TERMS ON GOOGLE
Better.

Who came up with the basket? What’s in and not in? And still, % of
what?

% OF BASKET OF OUR BRAND TERMS COMPARED TO A BASKETS OF BRAND TERMS OF
KEY COMPETITORS ON GOOGLE.
Good, much more specific.

Worth worrying about still:

How much search behavior does this represent for our company? Does our
Basket of Brand Terms (and misspellings and language variations etc.
etc.) represent 90% of all search on Google? 10%?

What about our competition?

And, you know, I’m not going to let this one slip: How do we
understand cause and effective if we see our or other baskets shift?
Ok, let’s set these worries aside [though, now you know what tickles
my ‘nogging].

Here’s what often, but not always, people mean when they say _Share
of Search_…

Lot of people are searching on Google…

What is our share of these queries…

[NOTE: Sizes meant to be illustrative, not actual data.]

It is super crucial to point out that the share illustrated above is
not a representation of ALL queries in the computing category that
Dell, Acer, Apple, and Lenovo (DAAL) occupy. It is a _SHARE OF THE
BASKETS_.

Not _voice_.

Not _search_.

There could be 1,400,000,000 weekly queries in the DAAL occupy. Of
that, the _brand baskets_ could total, say, 1,400,000 weekly queries.
On a good week.

These are the kinds of things I worry about when I’m applying a few
tablespoons of of critical thinking when asking _wait, __why are we
measuring that?_

: )

Ok, let’s also set this aside and move on.

SHARE OF SEARCH | WHY?

You work at Dell. When each week you report to the CMO your _Share of
Search_ is 30%... What does that 30% mean?

Forget cause and effect. [Actually, NEVER forget cause and effect if
you are an Analysis Ninaja, but, for this moment, let's set it aside.]

What’s that 30%?

Is it good that across competitive baskets you are a 30%?

Given your business model, is it bad that it is not 75%?

Apple is illustrated as, say, that thin sliver. Is that bad? For them?
For you, good?

What if they jumped to 20%, and you went down to 20%, is that
good/bad/irrelevant?

See what I mean by really stress testing what a KPI computation is
including, excluding, representing?

I’m usually upset when a metric is this confusing and
meaning-deficient. I try as hard as I possibly can not to use it –
you know that if you are a Croud client [4].

If none of the rest of the baskets are truly relevant, or at least we
have no idea what those numbers mean in context of each competitors
business model and business activity, should we report on _Share of
Search [_sometimes also, frustratingly, called _Share of Online
Voice_]?

SHARE OF SEARCH | BRAND MARKETING USE CASE.

CMO: _Half of our $20m brand Marketing campaign is done, has our Share
of Search gone up?
_
We now bring together Brand Queries on Google [TMAI #397 [3]] and
_Share of Search._

You should clarify to your CMO that there are significantly better
measures of brand Marketing that you have put in place (BLS, CLS,
mlMM, SEM), but perhaps there is power asymmetry with the CMO and you
are compelled to answer.

You’ll plot the before _Share of Search_ [% of Basket of Our Brand
Terms Compared to a Baskets of Brand Terms of Key Competitors on
Google]…

And, get the data for the same set of _baskets of brand queries _for
the period post the campaign, and plot out the _Share of Search_ for
each brand…

[We've discussed that Pre-Post is not a sound methodology to
establish causality. Set that aside for now.]

Dell’s share went up!

Looks like Acer and Lenovo, as illustrated above, went down.

Success of the $10m brand Marketing spent thus far?

Since we only know of one variable, our spend, it is really hard to
say. I’m reluctant to because here there is no way to control for
everything else going on with our brand, and obviously, we have little
idea here what everyone else is doing.

You could push me a bit, if what we saw was that everyone else stayed
the same but the number of brand queries in our basket went up AND
that increased the total queries across baskets.

Like so… Notice the fatter blue POST bar compared to above...

Life is rarely this perfect.

It is more likely that as none of Dell’s competitors are sleeping,
and all four of these companies tend to operate in a seasonal/launch
harmony, what we see, in the BEST CASE scenario is that the queries in
our basket went up, but so did those for others, and there are just a
lot more queries across competitors.

Like so…

Interesting on paper.

Cleanly calculated KPI, and results of the performance.

Still, so challenging to tie it to our $10m in spend in a way that
builds competitors.

Oh.

And does the fact that Acer stay the same across all scenarios now
imply to Acer’s CMO that they need to immediately approve a brand
Marketing campaign and/or increase the PPC budget?

I don’t think so.

Not based on _Share of Search.__
_
You see just how frustrating it is to take this KPI that so many push
for, and make anything out of it?

A lot of Marketing Analytics is like that.

The measurement in each of the POST view is clean, it has lots of data
behind it, percentages or raw counts show up, you feel really good
about the whole measurement of it.

Except.

It all means nothing.

It can’t stand the simple test: _So, what does this mean?_

Or even worse, reality: _So, can we do anything with this robust
measurement?_

SHARE OF SEARCH | THE ACTUAL KISS OF DEATH.

I wanted to walk you through all of the above simply to share with you
how I approach torturing every KPI to ensure there is something clean
and useful that comes out of measuring it. Please take active notes on
the _think _applied above.

The main reason_ Share of Search_, however you choose to define it, is
entirely value deficient now is that how humans seek, how influence
flows, has foundationally shifted.

Like so…

The definition we were working with was: _% of Basket of Our Brand
Terms Compared to a Baskets of Brand Terms of Key Competitors on
Google._

The last two words now imply that you have an incomplete picture.

Amazon. TikTok. LLMs. All sorts of new and amazing apps, experiences,
inventions, googles, that form how the _world's information is
organized and made universally accessible and useful._

All of these are truths: People use Google loads and loads and loads.
Lots of queries have shifted from Google to other destinations. There
are an innumerable set of new use cases that have added to the total
volume of queries (though, so many are not queries now and still, they
are _search_).

What does the KPI Share of Search represent now?

Nearly nothing helpful.

Certainly not as a reflection of your brand's "strength" or the
effectiveness of your brand campaigns.

There are a lot of new analytical possibilities across those vertical
fragments, of the horizontal slices.

But. Not, Share of Search.

It was time to move on in 2019.

Now’s a good time too.

SHARE OF SEARCH | SHARE OF VOICE.

An unpopular, hate-mail inducing, point of view…

I’ve deeply disliked the metric _SHARE OF VOICE_. In the past, it
was defined as _the number of GRPs you have_ vs. _your competitor _x
(or basket of competitors).

It literally said: _You should spend more because your competitor is
spending more_. I work at an Agency, you can see why Agencies would
love it.

The problem is… There is no accounting for outcomes. Just, activity.
And, just money being spent activity. Not even brand lift or some
terrible brand tracker you could easily create to measure the
effectiveness of the campaigns of your client and their competitors.
It was just, activity.

All this was backed up by research that suggested _share of voice_ was
tied to “financial outcomes.” It was galling that no concept of
effectiveness and efficiency curves was ever included. If you had 150
GRPs for 50 out of 52 weeks, sure it would do something – if nothing
you would annoy your brand into Unaided Brand Recall, and sure a
fraction of that annoyance would convert to Sales.

Could I do so spending a lot less money, having significantly more
effective pre-tested creative, and a _spike and sustain_ media plan?
Good God Yes!

Ergo, my dislike for the old _Share of Voice._

Some have advocated that _Share of Search_ is “the new _Share of
Voice_.”

I disagree.

Bigly.

BOTTOM LINE.

Smart analysis is complex.

In these deep dives into Brand Queries on Google and Share of Search,
I hope you’ve learned two things:

1. Don’t be sheep.

2. How to pick and prod and look under the rock and apply your high
standards before you greenlight a KPI or a methodology.
It is difficult to do #1, especially when you see a stampede. It is
extremely fun to do #2 – there’s a mystery wrapped in an enigma,
you get to unpack it!

Carpe diem.
