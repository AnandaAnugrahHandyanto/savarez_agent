TMAI #450: VISUALS THAT TORPEDO DATA STORIES.

[ Web Version [2] ]

I usually unpack complexity into simple actionable stories, today
something (that should be) simple… Made simpler!

An extraordinary amount of work drives Data Capture to Data Reporting
to Data Analysis. A TON!

After that last stage, the best _Analysis Ninjas_ will attempt to
unpack the complexity in the analysis by using data visualization.

With heartbreaking frequency, things go off the data viz rails
[3] during this phase.

As if data visualization is not challenging enough, Analysts
underappreciate two additional challenges:

*
A ton of executive data visualizations are consumed by Extremely
Senior Leaders (ESL) via PowerPoint.

This creates issues with interactivity. This limits the types of
visuals you can use.

*
ESL meetings tend to be _data pukey_, and visual after visual after
visual after visual after visual (and often five of ‘em crammed into
one slide – the kiss of death!).

This creates emotional fatigue, which means the visuals have to be
even simpler than simple as each visual will get five- or
eight-seconds max.

It is wise to appreciate these challenges in advance, and adapt to
them.

At the end of impressively smart human work recently, this data visual
came up on screen… And I cried…

[For a higher resolution version: Right mouse click and choose open
image in new tab.]

In the few seconds available for each slide, I could not figure out
WTH was going on.

I darted my eyes around to figure out what the x-axis was. The font
was as small as are seeing above (if on your desktop). Frustration set
in.

I was sad.

Then, mad.

It was an important meeting. I’m sure someone worked hard on this.
Yet, all that work was lost because the audience could not understand
what this visual was doing.

Let’s see if we can fix this. It is less important that you make the
exact versions I do below, my hope is that the _think_ will be helpful
as you reflect on your visuals.

Two things are excessively painful in the graph above:

*
There is no reason for the x-axis to appear as a legend on top of the
graph. Makes everything so much more complicated.

*
A key principle of mine is that if any word, line, text is repeatedly
repeated… It is clutter easily eliminated.

Both of these are easy fixes. Two seconds… Bada, bing, bada boom…

Easier to see, no?

In my obsession to reduce clutter, especially when the data is going
out in slides, I overlay the x-axis and I find that it visually works
better…

No need for the y-axis. Less clutter.

Another one of my key principles: Don’t make the audience do the
math, they don’t like it.

In choosing to do the cumulative graph, you are making the audience do
the math.

_How many orders were in the one-week range? 73% minus 47%._

Why?

Let’s eliminate that need.

From a visual perspective, time to insight perspective, I find the
above view to be an upgrade over the cumulative version.

While the math for four weeks would say 3%, in the original version it
is the tallest graph and when time is of the essence, it does not
_feel_ smallest.

Is that your reflection as well?

If you can get to the _what_ in the data faster, you can start to
focus on the _why_. That is when the real magic starts. IAbI.

INSIGHT: _Why did this performance/pattern occur?_

ACTIONS: _What should we do to take advantage of (prevent) the
identified pattern?_

BUSINESS Impact: _What will be impact in Revenue/Profits if we take
each of the recommended actions?_

Would you hazard a guess if we discussed IAbI when the original graph
(among a bunch more visuals) was presented?

BOTTOM LINE.

As a good Analyst, you’ll constantly be pressed for time. There is
always too much data to sift through, too many avenues to explore.
Making time to obsess about every visual you are putting into the
world can have a very high ROI. Please make time.

When you simplify, when you take the clutter away, you will really
_see_ the data. That enables you to get to the optimal IAbI. The
benefits of that are immense.

_Carpe diem._
