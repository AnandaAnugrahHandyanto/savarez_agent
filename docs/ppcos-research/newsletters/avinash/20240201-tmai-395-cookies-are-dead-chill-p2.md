TMAI #395: COOKIES ARE DEAD!! CHILL. -P2

[ Web Version [2] ]

_Never let a good crisis go to waste._

One of a handful of quotes that form my life-value-system. :)

In a professional context, the most recent _crisis _ in the Marketing
Analytics ecosystem was the next step in the (IMHO, necessary) death
of the third-party (3P) cookie. Firefox, Safari set 3P cookies off by
default years ago. Chrome just joined the party, starting with a 1%
turn off – potentially reaching 100% off, by the end of 2024,
perhaps.

In TMAI #394 [3], I’d recommended a _let’s chill_ vs. a _let's
FREAKOUT_ strategy. Two primary reasons:

A. Unlike what the headlines would have you believe, the crucial
first-party (1P) cookies are not going anywhere. 1P cookies power a
vast ecosystem of analysis you currently undertake.

B. Both major use cases for 3P cookies fell well short of acceptable.

Users deeply disliked the irrelevance stuffed “personalized”
display and, some video, ads.

_View-Through Conversions _used to justify these ads, rarely, if
ever, stood up to validation via simple Geo Experiments.

I will not miss 3P cookies. Neither should you.

SILVER LINING.

That this poor tracking mechanism, 3P cookies, which most humans saw
as “creepy,” got Analysis Ninjas to start using Geo Experiments
was a wonderful outcome. We now use Geo Experiments to answer a
million super valuable questions. [Premium Members: TMAI #374. [4]]

We can do a lot more!

There have been other legit challenges impacting our ability to
understand Marketing’s impact. GDPR. CCA. Apple’s changes (to try
and spite Facebook! :)). Consent Mode [5] (particularly in EU – if
you don’t have a clean, smart implementation of CoMo, you are losing
up to 30% visibility to your Conversions). The issues that will arise
if you are not prepared by the end of March for the DMA [6] ruling.
[If you need help with DMA or CoMo, call Croud [7]!] [8]

The US is considering changes. As is Nigeria. Antarctica. And,
everyone else who can.

Both because of the above changes AND opportunities to glean richer
insights, over the last decade, my practice of Analytics has
dramatically expanded to include:

A. Direct data collection. [A la Adobe Analytics, CRM, etc.]

B. Panels. [A la opt-in panels used to measure incrementality of OOH.]

C. Modeling. [A la modeling effectiveness of ads in the Apple let’s
spite FB world.]

D. Experimentation. [A la what’s the incrementality of Google
Search?]

E. Pure ML. [A la, you know!]

F. Guess work. [Ok. This one’s here just to see if you are paying
attention!]

Online, and offline. Customer love, and profits. What, and why. Code,
and custom.

It has been so much fun to learn, to grow, and do meaningful
work. Let me share a bit more detail about these initiatives, to
inspire you to continue to evolve your data collection / answer
providing initiatives.

FUTURE PROOFING YOUR ANALYTICS PRACTICE!

When people, Extremely Senior Leaders (ESLs], are worried about DATA,
a crystallizing question to ask is:

WHAT ARE WE DOING TODAY, THAT WE WON’T BE ABLE TO DO TOMORROW?
Often it will turn out that the worry is misplaced because what’s
going away mostly impacts the _hobbies_ of our Data Collection
obsessed "Analysts." We acknowledge this, we shed fake tears, and we
move on – saving enormous angst, and budget.

In some instances, it turns out that we lose important business
abilities: H_ow much should we cut our budget on Facebook for ads
targeting Apple devices? How many conversions from Europe did we get
from our Affiliate campaigns? How many people have turned off Cookies
altogether, and buy in our retail stores?_

For these questions, and move, you will use E, D, C, B, among other
techniques. They will require hiring ANALYSIS NINJAS, buying new
tools, forking Machine Learning algorithms, juicing on Statistics, and
other super exciting undertakings.

B. PANELS.

Panels are collections of people who’ve opted into sharing their
behavioral data, to filling out x number of surveys per day/week,
etc., in exchange for a benefit [sweepstakes entries, fixed payments
of small sums of cash, other rewards].

Think of the granddaddy of them all, the Nielsen panel for tracking
TV. Now add to it someone like BeatGrid that’s got the largest
single source panel in the US.

Over the last decade, tracking and participation will be via a mobile
app – like Google Opinion Rewards [9] (GOR) which is on my Samsung Z
Flip5. It is used by Google to collect my feedback on topics, and
confirm my online to offline purchase behavior. Which then gets
aggregated, modeled, supermodeled into analysis for Google's
Advertisers.

[NOTE FOR ANALYSTS: Obviously having GOR on my phone is quite a bit of
tracking to hand over. But, if you trust the source, I do recommend
using it. It is my way of learning how data collection works with
these new mechanisms,. It also stretches my ability to imagine new
approaches to answering business questions – for me, measuring
_half-life of brand lifts_ came from my use of GOR. It feels slightly
uncomfortable to share data, even when I trust Google. Sometimes you
have to do slightly uncomfortable things in your quest to learn and
grow.]

We use Panels, like BeatGrid’s [10], to identify campaign-level
brand lift for our brand marketing campaigns – both because channel
surveys only identify silos of impact, and they are impacted by cookie
rejection.

I’ve used the ComTech panel [11] in the past to model out the
entire US smartphone ecosystem. The resulting simulations allowed us
to do budget what-if scenarios, identify impact from Apple improving
feature x or Samsung introducing model y or either or both changing
their foundational media investments.

We have done analysis of Marketing effectiveness (conversions, carts,
revenue) using Panels to _get close to_ real answers, if 1P or other
solid identity mechanisms weaken in the future. Currently, in the
_decent _state, we are trying new approaches to get to _good enough_
quality.

A lovely benefit of Panels is getting deeper, better, demographic,
psychographic data – within the limitations of the panel size. Also,
they have higher precision in being able to go to the same
micro-segments to identify changing preferences or longer-term
implications of marketing. A cookie-based world has other benefits,
but not these.

C. STATISTICAL MODELING.

When Apple implemented ATT, in part to spite Facebook (because if you
buy advertising from Apple, all sorts of intrusive tracking and data
are at your disposal), some data to help you identify Marketing
effectiveness went kaput. But. Pretty soon, you were able to see that
performance, more clearly, in Facebook and Google tools. How come?
Conversion modeling [12] powered by statistics! :)

When you open Google/Adobe Analytics and see Age, Gender, and other
demographics of all the visitors to your mobile app or site, that data
is created using statistical models. A small fraction of the people on
the web provide that data – often using a survey -, and platforms
extrapolate from that data. The best ones verify the quality of these
models using experiments.

When you and I are using oMMMs or nMMMs, that is all modeling – old
school ad stock, priors, statistical modeling or newer machine
learning based ground truth and channel-interaction models. The
answers these models provide (iSales, CPiS) are the single biggest
best approach to getting more budget from your CFO during the annual
planning process!

Models – old school statistics, new school ML – do not require
cookies, or even individual user-level data, to assess impact. In
part, that is because they are answering higher altitude Marketing and
User Behavior questions.

Less _how much money do we need to give off to Amaya to convert _and
more _are promotions more effective when we offer a discount vs.
offering a bundle of products_.

Less _how can I create the perfect site for Minyi, _and more
_what’s the optimal landing page for the micro-segment that has
visited our site three times and returns via the keyword “cashmere
scarfs.”_
It is Extremely Crucial that you invest in growing your internal
capabilities to do advanced statistical modeling, and more - it has
been my primary approach to being ready for a fully cookie-less world.

One of my absolute favorite aspects of Modeling-centered analysis is
its ability to not only comprehend the past, but also to anticipate
and plan for the future, utilizing sufficient historical data.

Try doing that in Google Analytics.

[NOTE: To ensure zero misunderstanding: GA is not going anywhere!
This is an AND situation, not an OR situation.]

D. LARGE-SCALE EXPERIMENTATION.

From the birth of civilization, we have used Experimentation to
advance human knowledge. How do you think we figured out which
Mushrooms were edible and which ones killed? ;)

Experimentation for those of us who were born with browsers meant A/B
and MVT tests to optimize web pages. Still a profitable exercise in
some cases, though more often I’ve shifted to leveraging behavior
targeting solutions (ex: cloud-based Amazon Personalize) to create a
unique experience for each visitor.

We are using Experimentation to answer harder questions: _x-Stack and
portfolio i_ncrementality, diminishing return curves, multichannel
impact, validating audience/region/seasonality/partnership
hypotheses, and more.

This might sound like a circular reference but… In the last decade,
one of my most bleeding edge uses of Experimentation is to verify
answers provided by my Models!

Increasingly, as I’ve shifted from _o_ models to _n_ models, they
are operating at a level of sophistication that my simple brain
can’t quite comprehend what they are doing. In these instances, our
team does _trust, but verify_ using experiments. Real-world proof that
our models are not _hallucinating_, or to glean persuasive proof, as
in... _I’m truly sorry Adam, I know you are an extremely experienced
CMO for a massive brand, but the Portfolio Incrementality of your
Marketing really is just 3.2% - as the nModel said in the first
place._

Nearly all the time, you do not need cookies, individual identity
tracking, and other user-level data. Occasionally cookies are helpful
(say to control cross- contamination in some cases), but, in a
Marketing context, as long as you can control Media/Content delivery,
you are all set for some of the most glorious insights you'll ever
discover.

E. MACHINE LEARNING.

In our strategic marketing analytics work, this is perhaps the
coolest work we are doing.

An example could be to leverage _Classification Decision Trees_ to
understand what variables are most important, and in what order, when
it comes to truly making advertising on Facebook work. It is super
cool, and yields surprising results which have perhaps had more impact
on our channel effectiveness than any of the terabytes of cookie data
we have.

[Premium Members deep dive: TMAI #283: Work Smart: Decision Trees
Rock. [13]]

This is just one example. At the moment, the sky the limit when it
comes to the application of machine learning algorithms to our
analytics work.

Other examples include using Random Forest, Belief Networks, and
more to answer super sophisticated entire marketing portfolio impact
level questions.

All of us in the marketing analytics ecosystem - and dare I say
audience targeting efforts - have barely scratched the surface in
terms of how to use machine learning. There is so, so, so, so, much
promise on the horizon.

These algorithms don’t need as much - or sometimes any - user-level,
PII-type, data as we human analysts currently need. They can see
patterns we can’t, at a scale we can’t imagine. Consider giving
them a whirl, if they are not already a part of your analytics
practice.
