TMAI #467: THE DELICATE LIE OF STANDARDS.

[ Web Version [2] ]

Like countless other teams, we are are working through optimal
assessment of influence of marketing campaigns on incremental
profitable results.

Medium-term measurement of brand campaigns will come from true test
vs. control Brand Lift Studies (BLS). Long-term measurement will come
via Marketing Mix Models (MMM). The former is important; the latter is
critical.

With MMM’s, a particularly pernicious problem (PPP!) is
_cookie-bombing_.

[Premium Subscribers Bonus: TMAI #459: Impressions Suck! [3]]

A trillion gazillion (usually 3P) cookies are placed each day as
Display, Video, “Dynamic” ads are delivered to desktops, mobile
devices,, dynamic OOH, and car dashboards.

Unsmart MMMs simply take in your quarter of a billion
_impressions_ delivered, and assess that as a signal of influence.
This grievous error in modeling is discovered when the recommendations
to _PUT MORE MONEY INTO META & TIKTOK _fails to deliver the predicted
massive lifts in profits.

Smarter approaches to MMMs, other models, attempt to separate the real
from the fake. The challenge is it is hard to do, and no one is
helping you.

Ex: Facebook’s MMM solution, ROBYN, does not do anything itself,
leaves you to deal with _cookie bombing_. (If you are building an MMM,
I still recommend starting with Robyn [4].) Google’s MERIDIAN does
not disclose the internal mechanics but seems to use some _Invalid
Traffic Filters (IVT)_ for cleaning impressions for just Google’s ad
stack.

To work through this problem, without A. large scale experimentation,
B. _trust, but verify_ initiatives, C. x-channel time-spanning
meta-analysis… You are going to do the best you can. This is
absolutely ok.

Let’s look at the benefit of one such approach, to use easily
accessible _good enough_. I then want to add an important lesson:

*
THE CRITICALITY OF DEEPLY UNDERSTANDING YOUR COMPROMISE, BEFORE YOU
MEET THE CFO.

THE DOUBLEVERIFY OPTION.

One _good _option to separate the fake _impressions _and _video
views_ from potentially real ones is to use DoubleVerify’s quarterly
benchmarks.

As an example, please download their Q1 2025 benchmarks: Click here
[5] to fill out the lead gen form, and download. Or, click here
[6] to directly get the PDF.

There is a ton of impressive-looking data in the report. Here’s the
summary slide (#3)…

Here’s how it can be instantly helpful in assessing real vs. fake:

*
We are trying to figure out how many of our quarter of a billion ad
impressions were viewed at all. You have an answer above, 72%.

*
We want to know the impact of brand suitability or fraud blocking.
3.8%.

*
We buy lots of ads, what’s the video completion rate (VCR)? 30% (it
is on slide 4 of the above download).

There is more, but you can see how it becomes easier for the
developers building Mix Models (or other long-term impact analysis) to
separate the real from fake.

You could now multiply your ad platform reported 100,000,000,000
Display _impressions _by 0.72, and on paper get closer to reality.

Likewise, you could take your 500,000,000 daily video views, multiply
that by 0.8 and get to a “productive views” number to feed your
MMM.

Better still, you could embrace a higher standard for video ad
effectiveness and use AVOC as a measure of a “productive view,” in
which case you would multiply 500,000,000 by 0.1 (Audible and In-View
on Completion Rate). Now you’ve fed your MMM a real quality boost.

Accepting those _cookie-bombed_ insanely inflated impressions and
views for your MMM was _sub-optimal._ You do the math above, and you
instantly suck ness.

I want to sincerely emphasize this: THIS IS A VERY GOOD THING.

Go do your MMM. Find the insights hidden in incrementality. Cut your
Search Brand or Meta DABA budget to zero. Increase your CMO’s value
to your CFO.

[NOTE: In case you are seeking other options, DoubleVerify does have
competitors. IAS is a big one. Zefr, Moat, and Nielsen also compete in
some areas very strongly.]

THE DELICATE LIE OF STANDARDS.

If you are an _Analysis Ninja_, as soon as you saw the image above,
this question would have gone through your head… A sign you are
digging deeper vs. accepting benchmarks at face value…

*
HOW DID DOUBLEVERIFY GET THIS DATA?

AKA Methodology.

It is a good question to ask when you use your own data, and insanely
critical when you use third-party day.

It turns out DV collects data when their customer implement their
javascript tag inside the ad creative – which tracks impressions,
viewability, etc. They also have SDKs for mobile apps, and
server-to-server integrations with DSPs, SSPs, and ad servers.

DoubleVerify states they “serve over 1,000 customers,” which feels
small, but their integrations with ad platforms provide them with a
good heft.

The next question an Analysis Ninja will ask… Another sign of
digging deeper…

*
HOW DO THEY DECIDE WHAT’S “VIEWABLE,” ”VIDEO VIEW,”ETC.?

Essentially if they say a video was viewed, a display ad was viewable,
what does that actually mean? Because, you used their 30% and 72%
numbers above.

It turns out DV uses the standards set by the Media Rating Council
(MRC).

Before you read the standards, it is worth inquiring who the MRC is,
who funds them, and what their incentives are to set standards.

I won’t dwell on this too much, you have access to ChatGPT, 😊,
but it is worth mentioning that the MRC is largely funded by the
industry it regulates. Platforms like Google, Meta, and Amazon are
both clients and funders and board members of the MRC. This creates an
immense conflict of interest for the MRC. It is not all that hard to
see why there is risk of going _soft_ on influential
vendors/platforms.

Oh, and those standards… The MRC formalizes and enforces standards
created by the IAB. Another industry body funded by dues from
publishers, platforms, and ad tech companies – the people who
benefit from those standards being… well… _soft_.

This “cabal” / “cartel” solves for everyone in the industry
except the companies who spend money – though they ostentatiously
exist to help the companies!

You are going to decide how to spend millions or tens of millions of
dollars just by yourself based on the MMM, it is critical to
understand the incentives driving the standards you will use (from DV
via the MRC via the IAB funded by ad industry).

[NOTE: It is important, and worth admiring, that the IAB is extremely
transparent about its advocacy, and that it is the voice of the
digital ad industry, not a neutral party.]

That should make you the Analysis Ninja curious about… Digging
deper…

*
WHAT ARE THE ACTUAL STANDARDS FOR EACH METRIC IN DV’S BENCHMARKS?

Viewability: 50% of the ad pixels in view for 1 second.

Video Views: 50% of the pixels in view for at least 2 seconds.

😢

When DV says the “Authentic Viewable Rate” is 72%, what they are
telling you is that 72% of the time half of your ad at least was in
view for one second.

When was the last time something that quick and potentially partially
in your view caused you to increase Unaided Awareness, Consideration,
or Purchase Intent for a Brand?

It is why in my extensive experience across the US and EU, Viewability
as proposed by the IAB, formalized by the MRC, and used by
DoubleVerify has never correlated with Brand Lift or Conversions (and
hence, never causally either).

Go through the same investigation for a Video View. Same concerning
conclusion: A very soft standard, friendly to the ad industry,
ineffective from a client’s view re delivering impact.

Keep digging… My favorite AVOC? Sadly, only needs 50% of the video
to be in view and volume over 0%!

Video Completion Rate? Not actually 100% viewed. It is counted to the
end OR a predetermined completion point such as 75% or 95%.

Attention? Unknown definition and unknown standard. “A composite
built from 50% exposure and engagement signals processed with
proprietary models.”

😟 😟 😟

NOW WHAT?

If you are like me, cry a little at the state of affairs. It can be
cathartic. (Be careful not to dip into cynicism.)

Recenter yourself.

Building an MMM and establishing Incrementality is an absolute
imperative if you spend a material budget on advertising.

Understanding and not falling for the awfulness of impression
inflation, views abuse, cookie bombing, is also an imperative.

As much of a house of cards as a source like DoubleVerify is, or its
competitors, start there.  The data is easily available, and YOU WILL
SUCK LESS.

Suck less is good.

Then dig deeper. Understand the layers above for the source you are
using for sucking less.
Internalizing that makes this an imperative:

*
TRUST, BUT VERIFY YOUR MMM BEFORE YOU MAKE REALLY BIG DECISIONS.

Make small ones, sure.

Ex: There will be such a massive delta between your MTA and MMM for
some channels and tactics, the suck less approach is enough to kill
those investments or double down on some.

But all the material ones…. Trust, but Verify your MMM results
because the standards you used were solving for the ad platforms and
not you.

A common approach to Trust, but Verify is to use real-world large
scale experiments.
These will yield your own standards for Viewability, Video Views,
Fraud, Attention, whatever else you are using.

Slowly you strip out the DV/competitor benchmarks from your MMM, and
you are, as they say in America, _cooking with gas!_

BOTTOM LINE.

Don’t let the difficulty of the challenge stop you from seeking the
truth.

It is ok for the first version of the _truth_ to be a bit _truthy_.

For some decisions, you don’t need perfection.

But.

When it really matters, dig deeper, understand, and get to the
truth.

Because you are worth it.

_Carpe diem._
