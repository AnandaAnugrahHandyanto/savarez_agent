TMAI #464: DETECTING _BULLSHITAKE _IN BRAND MEASUREMENT. – P1

[ Web Version [2] ]

At a big event organized by an ad platform, as is common, a client
presented the result of their Marketing campaign on three different ad
platforms.

The objective, as is common, was _SPEND WITH PLATFORM X, LOOK AT THESE
INCREDIBLE RESULTS, THEY ARE AMAZING!!!_

TikTok, Meta, everyone organizes these events. They are disguised as
_educational events by experts_ and, in reality, are Sales events. The
“case studies” presented are always positive. Every initiative is
a success. Everyone in Marketing is a hero.

All good. Sincerely.

I am confident you’ve seen me delivering these talks around the
world – though I hope you’ll remember not seeing me present any
“case studies,” just frameworks.

With enough experience, filled with failures and some successes, most
humans end up with a BSDAR. Especially in the field of data, with a
lot more quantitive proof of various phenomena. (Not that that worked
out too well for Galileo.)

If you have a modicum of experience in the field, especially the
benefit of having analyzed lots of data… You sit in the audience
with a _bsdar_ – with a combination of skepticism and curiosity.

When I saw the below “case study” being shared by a client of the
conference organizer, my _bsdar _was like _wait a minute, that sounds
entirely like bullshitake, how did that make it on to such a big stage
by such a big brand_?

Here’s the slide for your assessment…

[Client case study presentation, data.]

In case the image is difficult to read, here are the results presented
by the big brand:

[Campaign impact results.]

Does that trigger your _bsdar_?

As I only have the above image to assess the impact shared by the big
brand, my analysis today is based on the following assumptions:

*
The MEDIA spend across YT, itvX, Netflix was roughly the same, OR at
least funded to Media Minimums on each channel. Else, it would be
immensely irresponsible to make a table comparing the results.

*
The campaign DURATION across the channels was roughly the same, OR at
least met the Duration Minimums for impact on each channel. Else, it
would be immensely irresponsible to make a table comparing the
results.

*
The CREATIVE used in the ads across the channels was roughly the
same, (ideally Pre-Tested with Human Made Machine, Pro or AI [3]),
and optimized for each channel’s ad formats to give them a fair shot
to win. Else, it would be immensely irresponsible to make a table
comparing the results.

*
Brand Lift was MEASURED using roughly the same methodologies. Ideally,
true Test & Control groups for a brand lift study that was conducted
for the campaign’s duration. Else, it would be immensely
irresponsible to make a table comparing the results.

If you are making such tables, please carefully reflect on the above
four critical dimensions, before you present the analysis. Else, you
are comparing Apples and Cucumbers and Spiders. Very uncool.

PROLOGUE: HOW IS BRAND LIFT MEASURED?

Lift for a brand metric is reported by showing the ads to a
considerable number of the target audience (say, _GenZ in the UK,
males, affinity to gender-neutral clothing_), this is the test group.
On the same platform, a large enough sized group is created, of the
same campaign audience, and not showing them ads. This is the control
group. Then you offer surveys to both sets with questions connected
to, in this case, Brand Awareness, Consideration, Inspiration.

The score in the control group, who did not see the ads, is the
BASELINE. _How many people who have not seen our ads are aware of our
brand?_ The score for the same question asked to the test group, is
the IMPACT of your ads.

The control group, let’s say, had 25% of the audience unaided aware
of the brand. The test group had 28%. The lift in Uanided Brand
Awareness = +3 Points.

Brand Lift is sometimes measured using Pre campaign and then Post
campaign surveys, by computing the delta between the two. This
methodology should be avoided like the plague. ☹️

Last week, I saw a stunning new way to measure real-world Brand Lift.
It did this by showing the ad in a lab to 100 people in “test”
group and 100 people in the “control” group! That’s like
declaring a fighter as a world champion based solely on their flawless
performance against a punching bag in a training gym. 🤒

Let’s look at the “success” results again:

[Campaign impact results.]

Here are four reasons that my _bsdar_ went _womp, womp, womp_:

1. NEGATIVE “LIFTS” ARE USUALLY NOT A THING.

For you to get a negative lift, let’s say for the Inspiration metric
for Netflix above, the control group was at, say 25, and the test
group who saw the ads was 14! [Or, if the control group was at 50,
minus 11 would give you 39. Etc.]

That means your creative was SO BAD, that a very large percentage of
the audience saw your ads and said _Jebus, that is horrendous, we now
hate this brand._

Having looked through the data for a few billion dollars of brand
marketing… That rarely happens.

Humans rarely pay any attention to advertising. Even exceptional ones.
We all have complex lives to lif. Observed research identifies that
when humans run into bad ads, it usually does not register (they
expect ads to be bad, and you know what... totally fair). If they run
into ads that_ royally suck_, humans usually just ignore them.

If your company has a big scandal, you might get a _negative lift_. If
your chief spokesperson turns out to be a criminal, you might
get _negative lift_. Assuming it was not something like that for big
brand above (because itvX was -3, YT was a highly suspicious +20), it
is very difficult to deliver negative lift.

Ex: Here’s a cringey ad by Nespresso [4]. With George aging, the
brand understandably needs to appeal to GenZ, but this is an odd
attempt on so may dimensions. Is it going to drive negative brand
lift? No, it just gets ignored.

Bad ads annoy you for a second, but it is extremely rare that they
vehemently turn a large mass of people against the company/product –
which would have to happen to get a Minus 11! Most of the time, humans
just stay at the baseline.

In this instance, it is particularly bizarre that the same campaign
(implied by a comparative table) would deliver -11, -3, +20 in a
small, concentrated media market like the UK, targeting the same
audience. Sure, itvX ad targeting platform could be horrendous at
finding audiences,  perhaps Netflix could as well… Still, the
negatives that big would be rare.

It is possible different tactics were deployed on itvX and Netflix, in
that case it is unfair they are on the table.

If you see negative brand lift, it is time to ask questions re the
four assumptions I've shared above. Remember: It is rare for pigs to
fly.

2. DOUBLE DIGIT BRAND LIFTS ARE USUALLY NOT A THING.

Another lesson from analyzing several billion dollars of brand
marketing across countries, companies, budget sizes, and purpose (KPI)
types:

*
An uncomfortable percentage of brand marketing campaigns fail.

*
Most barely deliver a point or two of lift (with a sad, wide
confidence interval [5]).

*
Some manage to do well.

Getting +26, +21, +22 as implied for YouTube above is… Suspicious.

Before you swallow the case study, before you run to your CMO
reporting double-digit Brand Lifts, extend your curiosity to validate
if these three things are true:

*
THE BASELINES ARE VERY LOW.

I.E. Very, very, few people in your target audience have heard of you.
Ex: Your KPI's baseline is 5. If that is true AND you knock every one
of my four assumptions above out of the park, AND everyone loves it,
AND your campaign makes it to the front pages of tabloids… It is
possible your baseline moves from 5, with a gain of + 26, to 31!

[Honestly, even that would be very, very, very, hard to do. You are at
5, you could get to like 9 or 11 or something, but 31… I’m sorry
to burst your bubble, God herself would have to be in your ad.]

This is what makes it even harder to believe the double-digit lifts:
I've hidden the brand's name on the slide, but it is a brand everyone
knows. I knew it years before I lived in the UK, I know it now, many
of you, across 75 countries, would know it. What does that mean? They
have a high baseline. A double-digit brand lift from a digital ad
campaign for a high baseline... Should spark urgent curiosity.

*
THE CAMPAIGN WAS VERY LONG (DURATION), AND THE BUDGET WAS MASSIVE.

I’m afraid, I don’t know if this is true.

I encourage you to check both, before reporting results publicly.

It is possible to throw A LOT of money to saturate Reach, Frequency,
and Duration to get a double-digit lift, with exceptional creative (90
or higher HMM score on all dimensions).

*
THE CAMPAIGN'S AUDIENCE WAS PETITE.

Another lesson from data: If you target 25 million humans, with
exceptional ads, hit all the Media Minimums, you might lift 0.75
million humans. A Brand Lift of 3%. If you target 0.5 million humans,
with exceptional ads, hit all the Media Minimums, you might lift 0.1
million humans - a Brand Lift of 20%.

Small campaigns done well can, in percentage terms, drive bigger
lifts.

If instead of targeting, say, GenZ males in the UK (approx. 7 mil),
the big brand targeted GenZ males in NW1 9DN (my post code in
London!), there is a higher probability that the 20 percentage point
list could be real.**

My hope is not to persuade you to be suspicious of this big brand. It
is to ensure you understand how difficult it would be to get a
20-point lift for any company, in any country, for any purpose (KPI).
I want you to know which questions to ask.

For a Fortune 1,000 company, for a “big” campaign, any brand lift
above five points AND a narrow confidence interval is highly
suspicious. It would take doing every dimension of a campaign
exceptionally well and getting lucky. Perhaps you are that good. Just
make sure you question-proof it before reporting it to your CMO (or
presenting it at a major conference).

** This is an important reason why I am ANTI reporting brand lift (as
a percentage, or even points of lift). In Brand Measurement
newsletters, I’ve emphasized measuring: KPI 1: Number of Individuals
Lifted. KPI 2: Cost Per Individual Lifted. Harder to hide
mediocrity.

3. METRICS MOVING IN OPPOSITE DIRECTIONS IS USUALLY NOT A THING.

Effective Brand Marketing campaigns are conceived to solve a clearly
focused problem. Usually, either Unaided Brand Awareness,
Consideration, or (Purchase) Intent.

[Premium member bonus: TMAI #426: Brand Marketing Metrics Simplified
| 3 x 3 [6]]

The reason this focus is critical is that each of these often requires
extremely different creative. [_Here's a great story to remember our
brand and its ethos._ UBA._ Here's why our product is exceptional,
and stands out vs. the "other guy's."_ Purchase Intent.]

And, creative is approx. 60 - 70% of the success.

With that context, hold these two contradictory thoughts in your mind
at the same time:

*
It is rare for a custom-built for a clear purpose creative to move
multiple KPIs at the same time in one campaign.

If you were trying to lift (Purchase) Intent, done well, you'll move
it. It would be unusual to move Consideration and Unaided Brand
Awareness at the same time. The creative was built for Intent, the
audience intent selected was for Intent, the targeting was precise for
Intent. It moved Intent - success, even if others stayed at the
baseline.

*
It is rare for a custom-built for clear purpose creative to lift the
target KPI, and at the same time destroy other KPIs!

While delivering (Purchase) Intent brand lift, it would be rarer than
spotting a snow leopard walking in Manhattan for the campaign to move
the other KPIs negatively. Or for (Purchase) Intent to be flat at the
end of the campaign, but lift Unaided Brand Awareness. Read the second
para of point #1 above.

Over a long period of time, delivering consecutive successful
campaigns with pre-testing passed creative, it is possible that moving
UBA also moves Consideration up a bit. But, in one campaign? Extremely
difficult.

Over a long period of time, or in one campaign, moving UBA moves
Consideration negatively? It would be very challenging to accomplish.
You could really, really have to try hard.

The pattern you see on YouTube, everything double-digit… Snow
leopard in Manhattan. The creative was so gosh dammed good, so
gloriously magnificent that it persuaded its audience to be Aware of
the big brand AND AT THE SAME TIME ALSO move Consideration AND AT THE
SAME TIME ALSO be the source of Inspiration ALL IN THE SAME
campaign.

And while doing this rarest of rare magic on YouTube, that the
campaign sucked at doing it on itvX and sucked even more on Netflix.
That’s a snow leopard in Manhattan with pink fur.

If you see it, ask questions. Poke and prod from a safe distance.

4. DECIMAL POINTS ARE USUALLY _A TELL_.

One last thing that caused my _bsdar_ to go _womp, womp, womp_…
FALSE PRECISION.

Independent of the large budgets being spent on the campaign, usually
Brand Lift is reported based on a sample of just 150 – 300 survey
responses (and even fewer for offline campaigns).

Experienced Analysts know that survey data inherently contains errors
due to sampling variability, imperfect matching between test and
control populations, external market factors, and non-response bias.
Hence: Sampled survey results are always estimates.

When you report a brand lift of +5 points, the real number is anywhere
between +2 to +8. Or, if you have a small sample collected, the real
number is anywhere between -1 to 12. [Download the XLS tool [5] to
compute your CI.]

So, when the YouTube column above says +16.1 or the itvX column says
-2.8… It is a case of _false precision_. It occurs when a number is
reported with more decimal places than the data or the methodology can
reliably support. Thus creating a misleading sense of accuracy.

A margin of error of PLUS or MINUS three points is common with
approximately 300 responses. Hence, the three of the numbers in the
table are indistinguishable from noise. And, all the decimal points…
Potentially deliberately deployed to imply the illusion of accuracy OR
the work of an inexperienced Analyst. Either way… Arouses questions
when seen by a CMO in a high-stakes meeting (or when presented at a
major conference).

BEST PRACTICE: Round up or round down.

TRUTH PRACTICE: Round up or round down, and show the Confidence
Interval.

[IF YOU NEED A METAPHOR FOR DECIMAL POINTS: _Reporting a brand lift of
2.8% from a small survey is like measuring a sidewalk crack with a
laser, and ignoring the fact you’re standing on a trampoline_.]

BOTTOM LINE.

As any _data person_ would emphasize: Black swan events do occur. It
is possible that this client achieved exactly these results on these
three platforms.

It is still helpful to bring your_ bsdar_ when things look_ black
swany_.

A good _bsdar_ takes time, experience, experimentation, and lots and
lots of failure. It is helped by holding common sense close. A
good_ bsdar_ is never done, there are new mistakes waiting to be
made, new lessons waiting to be learned.

I hope today, you have four solid head-starts to tune your _bsdar_
when it comes to Brand Measurement – a field incredibly hard to
measure, filled with so, so, so much low-quality analytics.

Don’t take these four as gospel. Apply them to your data, explore
the results of asking these hard questions, trust but verify to tune
your own _bsdar_.
