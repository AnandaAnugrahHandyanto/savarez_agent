TMAI #486: ANALYTICS: 7 LIFE PRINCIPLES TO LIVE BY.

[ Web Version [2] ]

Data without action is an expensive hobby.

In the quest to drive action,_ meaningful action_, I've constantly
come back to a core set of _first principles_ [3] accumulated from a
lifetime of losses and wins.

1. ALWAYS BE KNOWINGLY WRONG.

In cases where the Analytics team lacks influence, the tendency is to
just let decisions happen, to let things just flow. Don't.

If your data analysis recommends action X and the Extremely Senior
Leader (ESL) decides to do Y, speak up. I know it takes courage, there
is an immense power balance. Show them the work, speak up for action
X.

Let's say the ESL sticks with Y, and it turns out Y delivers results.
Great. You as an Analyst learned something of value. Let's say Y fails
to deliver results. Great. They learned something.

Let's say the ESL changes their mind and does X. If it delivers
results, or does not deliver results, a valuable lesson will be
learned by someone (and the organization).

If you never say anything... If you never give data, your smart
analysis, a voice... No one learns anything. The cycle of uninformed
decisions never stops.

Always be knowingly wrong.

A real example of the principle in action:

Before every campaign, we look across six dimensions and issue a CS3
(Campaign Smart Start Summary). In the last row, it predicts the
likelihood that this campaign will succeed.

Often, the plan is rated as “_Low Probability of Success_.” The
company/leader has a chance at that point - before campaign spend
starts - to stop or change the campaign. Some choose to plow ahead
anyway. They are being knowingly wrong. That is ok. After the
campaign, we present the CS3 and the campaign's failed results.
Lessons are learned.

2. DATA INFLUENCED, NOT DATA-DRIVEN.

I’ve come to recognize that it was a mistake in the past - in my
writings - to use the phrase _data-driven organization_.

With experience, I've learned there is no such thing.

It is impossible to find a collection of senior leaders who will all
listen to data. Some do. Others have very strong opinions, and don't
care about data. Others still are afraid to make bold decisions data
might recommend.

I've also come to appreciate that data is imperfect, Analyst could
miss important context, the analysis might be making leaps that make
recommendations questionable. Analysts have to be humble that they
might not know everything.

Hence, the principle: SHOOT FOR BUILDING A _DATA-INFLUENCED
ORGANIZATION_.

Have a sophisticated, muscular, data collection, analysis, and
insights finding machine (people+process+tools). Earn the influence
required to ensure your data stories are understood, data is allowed
to have a say, and it is carefully listened to.

Then, sometimes, walk away. Aim for data-influenced, not data-driven.

Combined with principle #1, you can see how over a long period of time
the org still gets smarter and increasingly data influenced.

A real example of the principle in action:

For our most crucial initiatives, we’ve arrived at a harmonious
balance: 85% of the budget will be extremely data influenced. 15% of
the budget will be _experimental / vanity / just do something crazy to
see if it works_.

So. Not entirely data-driven. Mostly data-influenced (guaranteeing a
certain amount of success for sure, and some lottery entries - the
15%).

3. PAIN CAN PERSUADE LEADERS MORE THAN THE UPSIDE.

Loss aversion is a cognitive bias that describes why, for individuals,
the pain of losing is psychologically twice as powerful as the
pleasure of gaining.

Loss aversion refers to an individual’s tendency to prefer avoiding
losses to acquiring equivalent gains. Simply put, it’s better not to
lose $20, than to gain $20.

In the quest to drive change, I've found loss aversion to be a helpful
ally. People always react a little faster, with a little more
commitment, if we can show them what they stand to lose.

*
_If you switch over to a Shopify style single page checkout
experience, you will improve the conversion rate by 1.8 points._

*
_For every day that we don’t have a Shopify style single page
checkout experience, we will lose $13.8 million dollars in revenue._

Loss aversion.

In statement A, you could even have said you will improve revenue by
$13.8 million dollars, and it would be at least a smidgen less
effective (or often not effective at all).

Statement B on the other hand leverages the cognitive bias that
evolution has wired into us.

When you find yourself in a stressful situation, where you feel you
don’t seem to have much influence - on behalf of data - to get the
company to do the right things… Ask yourself the question: _Have I
leveraged loss aversion to frame the value?_

A real example of the principle in action:

A key part of your _win before you spend_ strategy will be creative
pre-testing (with Human Made Machine! [4]).

When you get the data, you can report _% of Ads Pre-Tested, % Pass
Rate, % Ads Pre-Tested Pre-Launch_, and 15 more metrics.

The KPI I recommend using in the CMO review comes from _loss
aversion_: % BUDGET ON PASSED CREATIVE. AKA, how many dollars were_
wasted_ on failed creatives!

It is a huge motivator of change.

4. A PONY CONSISTENTLY BEATS A CATMELEON.

Many frustrating conversations regarding data come down to the fact
that Marketers/ESLs expect a fish to swim really fast AND fly really
fast.

Fish swim.

Birds fly.

When I visit clients, it is not uncommon for a CMO to tell me: _We are
running this inspiring brand campaign to improve Unaided Awareness and
drive short-term Sales._

You can guess what happens: Neither Unaided Awareness nor Sales go up!

Why? The creative, targeting, audience, bidding, calls to actions,
landing pages, etc. etc. etc. for those two business outcomes are
entirely different.

Trying to do two incompatible things at the same time is a_ Catmelon_
strategy. It. Always. Fails!

And.. Often measurement gets the blame._ Something's wrong with your
measurement._☹️

It might feel uncool to push for simplicity._ Let's focus this
campaign, and measurement, on driving Unaided Brand Awareness_. That's
a Pony strategy. Simple. It knows what it needs to do, and the chances
are high it will.

Ditto, if the desire is short term sales. Different pony (channel,
creative, targeting, etc.), higher chances of success, cleaner
measurement.

This mindset also applies to analytics: MMMs can't measure
effectiveness of individual creatives (or even brand campaigns
individually), why try? Adobe/Google Analtyics can't measure
incrementality. In large companies, Experiments, as holy as they are,
can't answer complex questions (due to scale). Etc.

A real example of the principle in action:

In one of my favorite examples, the client was trying to execute a
Catmeleon. Use Google Search to drive Brand Impact. A mix of a cat and
a chameleon. Ugly. Not possible. Stressful.

I managed to persuade the CMO to switch to a pony. Humble, sauntering
pony. Use Google Search to solve the long-tail conversions. Pretty.
Possible. Not stressful.

Six months later, the client was back wanting to do a _next-level
up _that would use a combo of Meta and TV to solve the Brand Impact
problem. Pretty. Possible. Not stressful.

[Bonus: In case you are curious, this is a Catmelon [5].]

5. WIN BEFORE YOU SPEND! DATA IS MORE POWERFUL BEFORE THAN AFTER.

You've seen hints above why I adore _win before you spend._

My hypothesis is that if you and I counted the data output from your
Analytics team, a vast majority of it, over 90% of it, is focused on
showing what happened in the past - after the money was already
spent.

This can potentially help improve the future. Of course. But, you know
what would be better? Using data before the money left the company in
the form of campaigns, new site, email blasts, expensive creative
shoots, etc. etc!

When you focus on using data to drive decisions BEFORE the money is
spent, you are basically saying: _If you ignore data-identify
recommendations/changes to the campaign, then your current campaign
that is going live next week will fail (/be less successful)._

Now, there is an immediate cost to ignoring you/data. Even if the
Leader does not care about the impact on their salary/bonus, no human
wants to fit in this category:

*
“_I knew it would fail. I still did it._”

Using data before the spending starts, combined with specific fixes,
is very hard to ignore. You will feel a strong pull for you/data from
the business leaders.

A real example of the principle in action:

In a previous role... Our team analyzed years of data, results of post
campaign analysis, experiments, algorithmic meta-analysis for each
channel, and developed what I named_ the Minerva Check.*_

Pre-flight, before the money is spent, we would apply the Minerva
Check to the media plan and predict likelihood of success for the
campaign.

When we first started, nearly all the plans failed the Minerva Check!
They were being built by Agencies with insufficient knowledge of media
minimums. Or, ESLs were running small campaigns on TV to mostly boost
their profile.

Once the Minerva Check came out, before the campaign budget was
final-approved by Finance, these campaigns got asked the hard
question:_ Are you going to change it to meet the minimums required,
or it makes sense to eliminate it? _

It soon became extremely rare that a VP, Marketing Director, ignored
data. As the percent of the Minerva Check passing campaigns rose,
Marketing's incremental impact on the business shot up dramatically.

The most powerful impact was the foundational shift in culture: Plan
to win.

All from using data to win before we spend.

* Naming our pre-flight check for the Roman goddess of wisdom [6]
seemed appropriate.

6. OPTIMAL DISTRIBUTION OF AN ANALYST'S TIME: 50 | 30 | 20.

So many in our community spend so much of their time either creating
reports (often, but not always,_ glorified data pukes_) or consuming
reports. If reports could change the world, we would all be working
four hours a week and the world have zero conflicts.

I've come to believe:

*
50% of an Analyst’s time should be spent on active data analysis.

*
30% of the time on reporting (a necessary function, sadly).

*
20% of the time on experiments (with an eye to learning - self,
marketing, company)

In stressful situations, this principle has been a helpful true
north.

_Are we sending data out or the IAbI (Insights, Actions, Business
Impact)? What is the quality of the 20% time spent on experiments?
Are we learning anything material? Have we explored the latest
statistical approaches or new ML algorithms? What can we do to make
the 30% even better? Do our reports have ABO
(Acquisition-Behavior-Outcomes), or a partial view?_

And so on and so forth. Having a 50-30-20 framework forces you to
reflect in a way that can be super productive. Both for your career
today, and job promotions you seek in the future.

Two honest admissions:

*
It is incredibly hard to hit this balance early in your career. You
are probably reporting 70% of the time and spending 30% of the time
chasing data for your reports. It is ok. Do it as well as you can, but
have a plan to get to 50 | 30 | 20.

The sooner you get there, the more your career will accelerate.

*
Analysis requires immense effort. Reporting is a comforting existence
because people want data pukes, with a little effort you supply them.

Analysis requires a lot of thinking, knowledge, and self-improvement.
This is hard. Experiments require extensive business understanding,
leadership connections, and being ok with failing. This is hard.

But. There is no alternative. 50 | 30 | 20.

7. “DATA IS FAR LESS IMPORTANT THAN ANALYSTS BELIEVE. DATA IS FAR
MORE IMPORTANT THAN LEADERS BELIEVE.”

I’m the author of this quote.

I created it as a reminder for me to be humble.

Every analyst believes that the data we have, the analysis we did, is
the MOST CRUCIAL EARTH-SHATTERING IN THE WORLD, and everyone should
stop and listen to us and take immediate action.

😀

Data is of course important, but there is so much more to running a
successful business.

There are so many more variables a senior leader has to balance for.
In the context of that, sometimes what we consider MOST IMPORTANT is
really not. Sometimes, there is a reason to waste $5 million dollars
on an initiative, no matter what the data says. So on and so forth.

When I find myself in a heated situation, or even sitting all by
myself reviewing the actions we are driving and ones we are not, I
remind myself of the above quote.

It calms me down a bit.

Then. With an open mind, I go seek context for what seems like
anti-data decisions.

Sometimes I discover a context I was not aware of, and now I’m a
more informed employee. Sometimes the decision is indeed as terrible
as I thought it would be.  Either way, I find that I can deal with it
more calmly.

So remember to be humble about your work.

And, keep evangelizing inventive uses of data to inform ever more
decisions by your leadership team.

BOTTOM LINE.

It is invaluable to have a core set of first principles that you can
identify as a part of your core belief system. (Professional or
personal.)

If you are just starting your Analytics first principles journey, you
can start with the seven I've shared.

If you are advanced in your first principles journey, I would love to
learn from you. Please share.
