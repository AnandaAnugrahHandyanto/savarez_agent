TMAI #389: CARE, DO, IMPACT: A PCA STORYTELLING FRAMEWORK – P1.

[ Web Version [2] ]

Every fantastic practice of Analytics asserts its profitable influence
across four smart clusters of activity: Smart Start (_win before you
spend_), Smart Execution (_win while you spend_), Smart Lessons (_win
causing reflections_), and Smart Future (_win the future_).

Here’s a summary of what makes up _analytics on the bleeding edge_
[3]…

[Analytics on the Edge, Smart Clusters Framework.]

Most Analytics teams, at most companies, expend most energy on the
Smart Lessons cluster – getting engaged with data after the money
(campaign spend, site update, app launch) is already all gone. Hence,
they have very little impact on _winning before_ and _winning during
the spend_.

If this is true for your company, you should, of course, rectify this
by resetting the scope of your Analytics team, hiring the optimal
staff for Smart Start, Smart Execution and Smart Future. 10/90 rule
FTW! [4]

POST CAMPAIGN ANALYSIS| WHAT?

In Smart Lessons, most of the time of most Analytics teams at most
companies is spent on that green box in the middle: Post-Campaign
Analysis (PCA).

Typical PCA questions:

The Marketing team has just concluded a $100,000 brand campaign on
YouTube, how did it do?

It is Feb 10th, 2024, how did our Bing Paid Search campaign perform?

We just launched our first-ever TV + CTV buy to drive $25m in
Incremental Sales, did it?

How did all our investments in media in Q4 2026 perform?
Without any further explanation, I’m sure you get the importance of
these questions and why they take up so much of the executive mind
share. Budgets have to be justified. Promotions have to be earned.
Poor performance “contextualized” to ensure the VP of Marketing
escapes accountability. (Oh, no!)

Hence, it takes up so much time of your internal Analytics team or
your external Agency.

BONUS TIP: The start of the PCA is not a good time to figure out what
the Success KPI was! A heartbreakingly common occurrence.

POST CAMPAIGN ANALYSIS| THE FLY IN THE OINTMENT.

Most PCAs are presentations. Massive 80 – 120-slide presentations.
If I’m being honest, more like 80 – 120 slide dense meandering
data pukes.

They have this confounding existence: EVERYONE WANTS THEM. NO ONE
LIKES THEM.

The client feels like they are necessary, they often ask for them,
_cover every little detail, do not leave anything out, I can figure
out what’s important_. She/he most absolutely cannot (they are
human, after all). They are checked out on the “Executive Summary”
slide.

[Bonus read for Premium subscribers: TMAI #227: Die Executive
Summaries, Die [5].]

The internal team/agency hates putting them together for being the
unique combination of boring mindless work where the entire emphasis
is on the _ink _(prettification of the slide) and none on the _think
_(what the heck are we saying, and why is it important?).

CMOs dislike them because after saying 80 slides of words and
pictures… PCAs typically say nothing at all. There is a lot of
"discussion" about the data, but no meaningful action.

How could something that takes up so much time, talent, money, be so
spectacularly useless?

Five big reasons:

1. Without a framework to guide their work, the Analyst’s objective
morphs into a need to prove that they worked very hard. Hence, they
end up including every little table, graph, segment, representing
random side paths they took along with what might be materially
important.

2. The Marketing Manager, Account Director, Slide Prettification
Intern, working on the PCA does not actually understand the core
business (products, competition, service, industry) and/or business
priorities and/or the campaign’s _reason for being_ (Revenue? Brand
lift? Internal PR? Employee employment scheme?).

3. The recipients of the PCAs (typically Directors of Marketing, VPs
of Marketing) confusing quantity of work for quality of work. They ask
for pukes and pukes of data, under a misplaced confidence in their
personal analytical skills. Just bring me everything, I’ll figure it
out. :(

4. Lack of Analysis Ninjas in your internal analytics team / at your
agency (forgot 10/90 rule!).

5. The campaign actually performed poorly; it happens. The 80-slide
data puke PCA, in this common failure use case, is an active effort to
deliberately obfuscate reality (/not so subtle effort to create an
80-slide excuse making machine).
The common outcome at the end of an often sincerely executed (1-4)
massive effort:

INFORMATION RICH, INSIGHT POOR COMPANY THAT IS BOUND TO KEEP REPEATING
ITS MISTAKES.

POST CAMPAIGN ANALYSIS| ROOT CAUSE.

When we bump into such disappointing outcomes, the lazy thing to do is
to blame the Analyst or Data. Symptom. The root cause is leadership.

Primarily Directors and VPs of Marketing. They set agendas.

In this day and age, I expect them to know better than to ask for data
pukes when they are paying for recommendations tied to data-identified
insights. It is the Senior Leader trading off discussions of insights
with high predicted business impact, with building cultures where
pennies worth of arguments about data are considered to be high-value
activity.

I am not going to absolve my peer Analysts (internal, agency,
consultants). I expect them to realize the futility of data pukes,
and, to the extent circumstances and their skills permit, push back.
Or, even as they deliver what the client wants, throw in some of what
the client needs (but is not asking for).

Start at the top.

POST CAMPAIGN ANALYSIS| A GLORIOUS FIX.

It is extremely difficult to fix cultures.

I’ve found success using two levers: Constraints. Incentives.

When I can wrap those two into a framework, I know I’ve struck gold
because frameworks fix the _think_!

Around 2011, I’d built a storytelling framework for the Large
Customer Sales (LCS) team at Google. In that instance, my goal was to
have our top clients get sales material that was persuasive, specific,
quantified, and, most important, in a short story!

I call it the Care-Do-Impact framework. THE IMPACT WAS TRANSFORMATIVE.

We in Google Sales still spent just as much time on our analysis as we
did in the past, except now the client got our recommendations set
inside three short stories – delivered via conversations over
coffee, or documents/emails or formal slide presentations.

When I wanted to fix the 80-slide PCAs that assessed Marketing, I had
a proven framework!
In adapting the Care-Do-Impact framework for PCAs, my goals were
similar:

Encourage hard choices by constraining the number of stories.

Create incentives to encourage smarter analysis.

Focus the _think _on the recommendations of what to do vs. a focus on
data/visuals.

Shift attention to the future, vs. the past. Spend more time looking
out of the windshield, vs. looking out of the rearview mirror the
whole time.

Make it impossible to ignore the data, by clear quantification of
benefits of fast action.
Bottom line: More think, less ink. More discussion, less presenting.

[Care-Do-Impact Storytelling Framework.]

In Marketing, the framework can be used to tell stories over
conversations, in documents, or in slides.

When the format is slides, it totals: 13.

Exactly 13.

Not 80.

The result is truly glorious. For VPs, Agency peers, Analysts,
everyone.

It is very, verry, verrry hard to pull off.

The expected standards are high, the skills required are high.

It is so much easier to produce an 80-side data puke.

CARE DO IMPACT FRAMEWORK | BIG PICTURE, CONSTRAINTS.

1.
Each Post Campaign Analysis (PCA) can only have three stories.

Not three and a half. Not five. Just three.

That does not imply that the Analysts and I do any less analysis. In
fact, we will do more analysis, because the framework is forcing us to
find really big things. It is forcing us to be _PURPOSEFUL_.

[Imagine going to a VP/CMO and you only have three stories to tell,
and the total impact of those stories is 300k at a 350m in revenue
company!]

Now, when you start at the data reporting stage, in Google Analytics
or your favorite BI tool, you are quickly triaging to separate the
minnows from the sharks from the whales.

Three also because in my work across dozens upon dozens of clients,
I’ve found that by the time you cover three big things, you’ve
covered 80 to 90% of what’s important. Is the name is the long tail
of fragmented actions. Possibly, still worth doing by some junior
individual at the company, and rarely requiring the expertise of a VP
or CMO to decide the best path forward.

The long tail is the_ just do it_ category.

2.
The Big Open Plan is to fix a very important element that often gets
overlooked: What were the campaign goals? And, how did we spend the
money?

On one simple slide, it will contain:

Objectives: Success KPI, and Target.

Budget/Flight: Channels, spend, flight duration.

Audience: Media delivery audience (not audience used for creative or
strategy).

Creative Recap: Concept themes, % of Budget on Passed Creative.
Having this on slide one in the PCA centers everyone. Along with
reminding anyone who’s forgotten why we are doing this, it also
enables everyone to look at the next 12 slides through a common lens.
This is crucial (and reduces frustration).

Now for something exciting, WDIGFmM!

[What did I get for my money, slide]

3.

The WDIGFmM slide is named for Google’s inimitable CMO Lorraine
Twohill. For campaigns that reached her review, she was curious about
the details but, like any effective CMO, first wanted a clear, honest,
answer to this question: _What did I get for my money?_

Simple, no?

Go open the last PCA you received or delivered… Is the WDIGFmM
honest & clear? I bet you $1, it is not.

On slide two in the PCA framework:

On the left half of the slide: The Primary KPI for the campaign (the
diagnostic metrics puke can wait). Ex: # of People Lifted. Cost Per
Individual Lifted. All stated against pre-set targets.

On the right half of the slide: A visual with something like…
Channels on x-axis, their performance on the y-axis. Each bar color
coded G, Y, R depending on results vs. target.
Like the example I’ve shared above.

A clear answer to _what did I get for my money_, and who delivered and
who did not!

It is exceptionally challenging to do this. We are afraid to admit,
but most Marketing does not deliver much incremental value (especially
if executed without the concept of incrementality in mind). This
slide, the profound limitations on the _ink_, limit insertion of
“context” and outright manipulation.

This slide demands courage, and the willingness of a _blameless
post-mortem _where the objective is not to find who/what to blame,
rather it is to understand, learn, and take action that makes your CMO
even more precious.

The CMO could stop the deck on slide two and have a strategic
discussion. Or, jump from slide 2 to slide 13 by asking: _So, what are
we doing differently, better?_

4.

On that note, your leadership team’s favorite will quickly be slide
13, Big Close.

In most PCAs of the data puke nature, the actions, if any, will be
strewn across lots of slides. It will be rare if you see the business
impact computed. The team, leaders, are left figuring out which of the
random things to activate.

The Big Close format delivers rank ordered recommendations AND the
predicted impact of each action.

[PCA: Do Now, Do Later.]

30% of the time spent during the PCA should be on this slide. The
attendees and discuss the pros and cons of various recommendations,
senior leaders can add their unique strategic context, and greenlight
action.

What the Big Close emphatically makes clear is that the company is
going to lose money/value, every day, by the amounts in the Impact
column. Hard to then sit around and do nothing.

BOTTOM LINE.

If there is one complaint about Data and Analysts: _We have all the
data in the world, every tool there could be, but we don’t know what
to do to improve revenue/brand lift/profit!!_

The Care-Do-Impact storytelling framework, and dare I say mindset,
helps address exactly what question. And, it does so while making an
Analyst’s life simpler and VPs happier. A nearly impossible
accomplishment.
