TMAI #444: INCREMENTALITY FOR OWNED MEDIA, HOW-TO?

[ Web Version [2] ]

We love the measurement of Incrementality here.

DEFINITION:

Incrementality: Business results from marketing tactics that would not
have occurred otherwise

TRANSLATION:

What happens to Marketing’s claimed business impact, if you do no
Marketing?

THE ANSWER:

Almost always… For nearly all the companies… If you shut down all
spend on Marketing, the company’s profits will go up in the short
term.

Most Paid Media has low incrementality, especially if the
company/their Agency has never executed campaigns based on
incrementality. It is also true for Owned and Earned Media, which
tends to be typically targeted to existing customers or people who
already know the company.

In the long-term, or super long-term, the company’s profits will be
harmed.

EXISTENTIAL QUESTION:

How long before your company misses Marketing, if they shut it all
down (people, budgets, buildings, agencies, free snacks, everything)?
Nine months? Two years? Longer?

Effective measurement of Incrementality helps answer this question.

INCREMENTALITY FOR OWNED MEDIA.

In our ecosystem, in this newsletter, we apply the lens of
Incrementality measurement primarily to Paid Media. Much of our budget
is often being spent on Facebook, TV, Billboards, TikTok, it is
natural that we over index.

To measure the true incrementality of Paid Media, we leverage:

*
Conversion Lift Studies (for Channel-Silo Incrementality)
*
Large-Scale Experiments (for x-Stack Incrementality)
*
Next-gen machine learning based Mix Models (for Portfolio
Incrementality, in turn_ trust, but verified_ via experiments)
*
Causal AI based solutions (for Sustain portion of Brand Marketing).

In a lovely note, Lorenzo asked:  _How do I measure incrementality of
Owned Media?_

Email. SMS. Push notifications on mobile. Whatsapp. In App Messages.
More.

Great question!

Email is a bedrock of communicating with new and existing customers,
across See-Think-Do-Care [3] clusters of intent. There really is
nothing quite like Email. What’s the Incrementality of Email? What
about changes being made to Emails… New templates, different
content, and, the gift from God to your customers: Fewer emails from
you?

Push Notifications can be helpful to customers. _Your balance is low,
did you charge $1,900 at Marks & Spencer to your credit card, that
loan you applied for has been approved, we have lowered the APR on
your credit card, did you know you can check your credit score for
free in our app, _etc. These are just some creative uses of Push
Notifications by a Bank. Depending on your industry, there are
more.

Based on the above, you can imagine the uses for In-App Messages. If
you have the Citibank app, you have seen sub-optimal expressions of
these. If you have the Chase app, you have seen some helpful versions
of these.

Investment in these Owned channels is needed to drive the
possibilities above. To facilitate that, how do you prove the
incrementality of such initiatives?

INCREMENTALITY FOR OWNED MEDIA.

In short: It is hard. 😊

The instinct in these cases is to use good old A/B Experience Testing.
Sadly, there are 11 reasons that tactic is either minorly effective or
entirely counterproductive.

[Premium Bonus: TMAI #442: Stop A/B Testing [4].]

Instead, here are four analytical strategies, three still based on
experimentation, that I have used to prove the incremental impact of
Owned channels.

#1. PERMANENT HOLDOUTS.

In an experiment, you temporarily put a group of people in different
_treatments _(“spam them with 4 notifications a day”, “spam
them with 3”, yada, yada). Then you assess behavior.

If you are a large enough company, with a big enough customer base, a
magnificently effective strategy is to do a _permanent holdout. _

Take 10% of your user base, in this use case, do not change anything
about their mobile app or push notifications, icon, whatever else.

Take 90% of your user base, release everything shiny and new and bold
to them. Not just once, but over time as you improve (or "improve")
results to be incrementality worthy.

Then... Measure two levels of incremental outcomes:

*
What was the local impact? Specific to the change you made.

*
What was the global impact? In aggregate to desirable company level
behavior.

So, we were trying to push more notifications to influence customers
to use the Sharing feature in the app... _Did that happen?_

[This is the gorgeous bit….] DID THIS CHANGE TO YOU USING SHARING,
ALL BY ITSELF OR, EVEN BETTER, COMBINED ALL OTHER CHANGES YOU'VE
MADE… INCREASE THE LTV OF THE CUSTOMER?

Often, we preen like peacocks when the first outcome occurs, the local
impact (with A/B testing or even my highly recommended Large Scale
Experiments). But, a lot of those changes might have zero impact on
the overall value of the customer to the company. So… What was the
point?

The second bit, you can only do with permanent holdouts.

You get_ local maxima incrementality_ and _global maxima
incrementality._

An example of this for a large bank is that we built a permanent 5%
representative hold out group of customers of their mobile app.
Allowing us to PROVE the impact from making additional investment in
the Mobile App Development team/tools.

Another example of this is for a large consumer electronics company,
where we have a permanent hold out of an entire US state. It never
received any updates for two different Owned Media channels.

Depending on the company-specific factors, we reset the holdout every
nine months / year / as dictated by clean local and global maxima
needs.

[BONUS NOTE: You all know my obsession with trust, but verify. Trust
models, trust black boxes, trust AI, but verify the outcomes.
Permanent holdouts can be a very effective – gold standard? – to
trust, but verify your MMMs.]

#2. COHORT ANALYSIS.

The second-best option is to do advanced modeling to assess the impact
of changes we are making.

In English: Cohorts are groups of people who share a common
characteristic. They all signed up on date x, they all use email but
not the app, they all only connect with us on Whatsapp, they all have
purchased from us 9 times or more, they all only own one of our
products, etc.

Cohort analysis then is behavioral analysis of those people over time.

As you make changes, you can do analyze elements like:

*
Did their engagement change over time?
*
Are they more likely to churn or less?
*
How many of them switched from digital to stores?
*
Did loyalty points matter to them?
*
Did offering them tech support over WhatsApp increase their LTV?

All examples of behavioral analysis.

I am fond of this approach, as it is not always possible to use
permanent holdouts.

There is minimal invasiveness when it comes to building out these
cohorts, we just have to apply care as we use our Owned Media tactics
to control exposure.

You’ll have to become a lot more familiar with yummy analytical
methodologies like Kaplan-meier, cox proportional hazards models,
latent class analysis, my trusty random forest, gradient boosting,
decision trees, and all the emerging machine learning possibilities.

Trust me, a lot sexier than they sound. :)

Modeling is often an ideal application when you are trying to seek
answers at scale (even for mid-sized companies). You don’t have to
pay the _sequential penalty._

#3. LARGE-SCALE EXPERIMENTS.

Measure the baseline for the KPI you are interested in shifting.

Activate your new actions intended to drive an incremental impact for
treatment (one or many), vs. a control group.

Measure the new baseline.

Celebrate/cry.

Go back and measure the baseline again, after 90-days (/appropriately
longer period) to allow for “settle-in”.

Cry/celebrate.

The approach you are already using to in other contexts for measuring
incrementality, and more.

#4. PRE POST.

Every so often, you just have to get off your high horse and embrace
the suboptimal.

Pre Post is the least desirable methodology to measure incrementality
of Owned Media.

Essentially, you measure and establish a baseline before you make any
change.

Say, you are trying to impact my favorite Email Marketing tactical
metric: Click to Delivery Rate. You measure the rate for the last six
to twelve weeks (or more, depending on business realities). That
becomes your baseline rate. You can also use _same time last quarter /
year_ as a baseline, in some cases.

Then, you take the actions that are intended to deliver high
incrementality, measure again.

What’s the CtDR after?

One minus other and... LIFT!

There is a small problem with Pre Post: Causality.

Let’s say your changes were released over a month. Pre Post assumes
that absolutely everything that was happening with the business,
competitors, industry, ecosystem, seasonality, everything is exactly
the same before, during, after.

This is rarely the case.

Still, sometimes you don’t have a choice. It could be a systems
thing, it could be a process or structure thing, it could be the
complexity of the change thing, or, sometimes, you just want say f’
it, let’s just do it and see what happens.

In those situations, you have not choice but to get atleast finger in
the wind quality sense of _wait, did that have any impact? _

Pre Post will give you that.

It is better than nothing, but by a lot less than you might like.

BOTTOM LINE.

Embrace incrementality because it is the ultimate proof that you
matter (in a professional context!).

Especially for Owned and Earned Media because they tend to be
initiatives/teams that don’t get the deserved love/publicity/budget.

You prove incrementality, you get love/publicity/budget.

Carpe diem.
