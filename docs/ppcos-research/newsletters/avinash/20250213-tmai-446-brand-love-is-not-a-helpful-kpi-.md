TMAI #446: BRAND LOVE IS NOT A HELPFUL KPI.

[ Web Version [2] ]

Something that on paper is so attractive to CMOs, is so incredibly
unhelpful: "Brand Love."

A variation of this is manifested by a CMO as a vision statement
(/mission/mantra):

Our ambition is to be the most loved brand in the
world.
/
To be the best loved SAAS platform for decision
support systems.
/
We want to be the _coolest _brand for Millennials.
/
Building Brand Love to power short-term Profits.

Actually, no one who uses the phrase _brand love_ ever said that last
one. 😊

When I see “Brand Love” as the CMO’s desired outcome, if I can,
I run away.

I am a founding member of the _anti-Brand Love as a KPI _club.

In a recent TMAI, I’d recommended that you should also join the
club.

TMAI subscriber Marshall inquired:

_          Avinash, why do you unlove Brand Love so much?_

There are three layers to my conclusion that _Brand Love as a KPI_ is
an unproductive, even dangerous, objective.

In sharing them today, my minor objective is to persuade you not to
use Brand Love as a metric. I do hope you’ll agree with me.

My major objective is to share how I _think_ about these issues, and
my the 360-degree assessment of value. I hope that you’ll apply them
to other metrics your CMO might be enamored with.

The universe of Brand Marketing is uniquely blessed in having so MANY
entirely unproductive _things_ masquerading as objectives / metrics.
Let’s apply critical thinking to that universe.

SAY NO TO BRAND LOVE… #1. MEASUREMENT.

I am allergic to any “thing” that cannot be measured cleanly, and
without bias.

If your Marketing budget is tens, hundreds, of millions of dollars,
and your company makes an insane amount of Profits… Honestly,
don’t worry about measurement clarity. If you work at Google or
Coca-Cola or LVMH, your companies meet the prior two conditions,
don’t let the lack of measurement get in the way. Just spend money.
Sincerely.

For the rest of us… My first question is: _What’s the definition,
and the methodology?_

There is no commonly accepted definition for Brand Love.

That is understandable.

I love my spouse. It has become stronger and stronger and stronger
over 28-years. I could not tell you what it is. I miss her when she is
not with me. I would easily take a bullet for her. Even when she tells
me she _sometimes just likes to have a good cry_, it brings me intense
pain. I eat some green vegetables that I don’t like because of her.
_She completes me_. I can’t tell you what “love” is.

There is no single standard measurement for Brand Love.

Some use structured surveys that contribute to a compound metric
called _Brand Love Index_:

*
AFFECTION (How much do customers emotionally connect with the brand?)

*
PASSION (Do customers feel excited and engaged with the brand?)

*
COMMITMENT (Would they go out of their way to buy from this brand?)

Others define it as _the degree of emotional attachment, affection,
and positive sentiment consumers hold towards a particular brand_. It
is measured from a “combo” of:

*
STRONG POSITIVE EMOTIONS: These can range from admiration and trust
to genuine affection, passion, and even a sense of "love" in a
metaphorical sense.

*
PERSONAL IDENTIFICATION: Consumers see the brand as representing
their values, lifestyle, or aspirational self-image. They feel a sense
of "this brand is for me" on a deeper level than just product
functionality.

*
LOYALTY AND ADVOCACY: Brand Love often manifests in intense brand
loyalty, repeat purchases, and a strong willingness to advocate for
the brand to others (word-of-mouth marketing).

*
EMOTIONAL INVESTMENT: Consumers are not just transactional; they are
emotionally invested in the brand's success and are more forgiving of
occasional missteps.

*
TRANSCENDS FUNCTIONAL UTILITY: While the product or service must
still be good, Brand Love often elevates the brand beyond its core
function. Consumers are drawn to the brand's personality, story, and
overall experience, not just the features.

This second approach also ends up in a compound metrics (which we
should be extremely allergic to due to their abject non-usefulnesses
[3]).

Regardless of what you are measuring, if you don’t have a somewhat
standard, somewhat without bias way to measure it:

*
It is challenging to know if you are measuring the outcome you are
meant to be measuring.

*
If you don’t know that… It is super challenging to know what might
be influencing the metric to go up or down (the _influencing
variables_).

*
If you don’t know that… It is _colosso_ challenging to know which
of the activities you are spending cold hard cash on are having a
causal impact on improving the metric.

Brand Love ends up being an exercise is subjectivity and vagueness
(emotions are complex and challenging to quantify – see my example
re my spouse above).

There is unlimited potential for superficiality and manipulation
(especially when you have _bad boss _leadership archetype #3 [4]).

From a data collection perspective, _social desirability
bias_ actively works against a clean signal, as does an over-reliance
on vanity metrics in measuring Brand Love.

Actionability is darn near impossible – ironically, while costing a
ton of money to measure!

It all ends up being a vanity exercise.

Then the CMO is fired (remember, the shortest tenure of any CxO).

CMOs hire me. I don’t want them fired. Hence, I protect them from
exercises of vanity.

[Brand Love.]

SAY NO TO BRAND LOVE… #2. CONNECTION TO LONG-TERM FINANCIAL IMPACT.

Regardless of the KPI’s measurability, it is crucial to ask: _Does a
positive movement in this metric, also have a causal positive impact
on long-term company finances?_

Going back to my persona example: I love my spouse. We have been
married for 26 years.

Clear positive long-term impact of love.

In a business setting, this has not proven to be true in my
experience.

With support of my excellent teams at different employers/clients,
I’ve run studies and analyses to understand the connection between
some or all of the variables I’ve mentioned in reason #1 above.

I’ve spent money on running the University of Michigan methodology
with 16 variables… And after extensive custom research and analysis,
I could not find anything that would tie Brand Love to long-term
business impact (3-yr impact horizon).

Undoubtedly, given we can’t even agree what Brand Love is, and how
to influence it, it is not surprising that we can’t tie it to
business outcomes.

My attempts and experience do not mean that will also be 100% true for
you.

I’m simply stressing that be suspicious of your CMO if she is
insisting that Brand Love is The True North. Then, as I did above,
before you sink a Titanic amount of Marketing funds chasing “Brand
Love,” see if you can direct a Nemo amount of Marketing funds, as I
did above, establishing a cause and effect to financial outcomes.

SAY NO TO BRAND LOVE… #3. HUBRIS.

I am allergic to hubris.

In all my experience, I cannot bring myself to believe that a normal
human wants to love a brand.

Why the heck would a human want to love Pantene? Or Capital One? Or,
Disney? Or, LV?

Take a look at this actual survey:

[Restaurant Brand Survey. ]

The first bits, Brand Trust, all good.

Imagine the hubris it takes to ask the last three questions, Brand
Identification.

W. T. Actual. H!

Unless you are the restaurant owner, or their immediate family, why
would your personal existence be validated to a restaurant? I’m
sorry, but how disconnected from reality could we be to ask these
questions?

This is limited to my personal dataset of approx. 150 top companies on
the planet: I have seen CMOs exhibit such hubris. They have wasted
tens of millions of dollars (in one case, well, in excess of $200
million) on “Brand Love Marketing.” The efforts delivered nothing
re business results. I’ll leave you to guess what happened to the
CMO and chunks of the Marketing team.

As a normal human, I’ve purchased from a few hundred brands and know
of a few thousand more. I like many of them. I’ve purchased
repeatedly from some. I’ve spread good word of mouth for many
others. Across all of that, there is only one brand I would defend
aggressively if someone attacks them. Just. One. Brand.

Humans don’t love Disney. They love who they used to be. Disney is
just the vehicle for that memory (and a very effective one, kudos to
Disney!).

Humans don’t love Louis Vuitton. They love the sense of
accomplishment that they can afford a $6,000 purse (and kudos to LV
for delivering that!).

My last reason for not focusing on Brand Love as a KPI: I’ve
believed it is near arrogant to believe that Marketing can make humans
_love_ a Brand.

YOUR TURN.

Is there a brand that you love? In the spirit of _success of this
restaurant is my success_.

Do you measure Brand Love? Will you please share what I’ve ignored
that’s essential?

Thank you.

BOTTOM LINE.

Steps to a more data-influenced organization…

#3: Check if you are measuring hubris, or reality.

#1: Check if your metric can be measured cleanly, and without bias.

#2: Check if a positive movement in the metrics, also causally results
in a positive impact on company financials.

_Carpe diem._
