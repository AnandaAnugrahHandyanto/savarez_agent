TMAI #397: UNCOOL METRICS: “BRAND QUERIES ON GOOGLE.”

[ Web Version [3] ]

Today a lesson in the relevance of one KPI, wrapped in a lesson about
how to interrogate the value of any KPI!

Let’s start with a bang.

This KPI should no longer be on any dashboard:

# BRAND QUERIES ON GOOGLE
In fact, I recommend downgrading it to a _let's keep a partial eye on
it, and only look at it if we are specifically taking action to drive
people to Google_ metric.

That would imply looking at this metric rarely.

It was helpful at one-point, dramatic changes in how people seek
information online mean it is unrepresentative of what we think it
implies.

[NOTE: I'm using Google to represent _traditional search_. If you are
in Korea (Naver), Japan (Yahoo!), China (Baidu), Russia (Yandex),
Poland (Seznam), please replace Google with your local
dominant _traditional search _rep for the rest of this article.]

I see Nikita in the back raising her hand to ask: _What is this
metric?_

Several years ago, the go-go years of _omg we are all on Google,_ the
active recommendation was to measure, monitor, and influence the
number of queries for your brand on Google. The thought was that a
shift in _Brand Queries on Google_ was an effective proxy for the
OVERALL STRENGTH OF YOUR BRAND. More queries meant you must be doing
something right.

In fact, the shared wisdom went a step further: If you did something
to improve your brand anything by investing in brand Marketing, then a
shift in _Brand Queries on Google_ would indicate that your campaign
was highly successful!

[Pre Post measurement of Brand Queries.]

It did make sense.

Search, Google specifically, was indeed how we all found what we
wanted. As a result of that spike in spend, you saw a resulting spike,
20% above, in brand queries… Your brand Marketing campaign must have
done something right.

Measurement would quickly be followed by investing more in Paid Search
ads for brand terms to capture all this traffic.

[NOTE: Some forward-thinking VPs did ask: _Wait, if these people are
looking for me precisely, why do I need to buy these PPC ads_. We did
not have a word for it: Incrementality.]

There was one more crucial reason _BRAND QUERIES ON GOOGLE_ became
THE KPI to be tracked and reported and celebrated: The impact of brand
Marketing was challenging to measure.

Instruments of brand measurement, Surveys, Phone Calls, etc., all took
time to accumulate. Sometimes months. Add to that the fact that the
impact on Sales from brand Marketing has a lag. Even for a short-term
_salesy _objective like Purchase Intent, it would take three to six
months to see impact on Sales.

[NOTE: Premium members, see _TMAI #322: Brilliant Brand Measurement_
for impact horizon, and other key lessons. Please email me if you
can’t find it.]

And, here was _Brand Queries on Google_. Takes five minutes to get. It
feels very precise and quantified: 387,245 Queries.

It gave CMOs something to point at and say: _Hey, looksie over here,
my brand campaign is having an immediate effect!_

THE FLIP SIDE OF QUICK ACCESS TO DATA.

I hope I’ve persuaded you that the metric had merit. There is a
reason it is still on your CMO dashboard.

I’d always felt queasy recommending _Brand Queries on Google_, it is
not in either of my (best-selling!) books on Analytics. My worries:

Correlation, ok, but causality? Maybe, maybe not.

Searches on Google can be impacted by so many additional factors
beyond your brand campaign. Your brand got trashed on a bunch of blogs
[remember those?], your queries went up. For some weird reason, a
product that failed at launch suddenly becomes the hottest thing in
America, your brand queries go up [Gigantic Stanley cups! See this
[4], then this [5].]. 25x more factors (beyond your brand team).

I, like you Analysis Ninjas, do not prefer Pre – Post as a measure
of causality. I’m uncomfortable laying the entire outcome – lift
or no lift - down to one variable. In this case, your brand campaign.
Ex: Your entire lift in brand queries could have been from a
significantly richer campaign your new competitor is running.

If your brand campaign was actually successful, people will search for
you more, definitely, but they will also do other things. In hitching
a ride on _Brand Queries on Google_, we were, unintentionally,
ignoring the rest of the brand campaign generated activity.
With the arrival of auto-complete in browsers, my concern was ever
higher. If I am typing in HP, it gets auto-completed to hp.com, I skip
Google, or Bing, altogether and still end up at the right destination.

Even if you bought that the digital behavior was a_ leading indicator
_of a brand campaign’s effectiveness, it was weaker now.

Then, there was the fact that if the HP brand Marketing campaign was
remotely good, and their campaigns are, I will type in hp.com. Or,
walmart.com. Or, vodaphone.com. Or, loewe.com.

If you pull back from the KPI, understand how it was being measured,
what it was accounting for, what it was ignoring… I hope you can see
why I was always queasy about including this particular KPI in my
dashboards.

BRAND MARKETING’S BUTTERFLY EFFECT.

Some of my worries, I can probably fix with smarter measurement.

Ex: Pre – Post as a methodology is so, so, poor in this complex day
and age, you should never use it to measure the impact of anything. I
can fix that by setting up an MMT. [Premium Members see: TMAI #374:
Game-Changing Analytics: GEO Experiments]

There was a core worry of what _Brand Queries on Google_ is missing
that, I feel, was unfixable then, and is a massiv_er_ concern if you
are still using this metric.

I’m wearing a Rapha [6] jacket right now [I LOVE Rapha!]. When their
brand team executed a campaign in the year 2010, the impact would be
felt well beyond Google.

Like so...

[Brand butterfly effect, 2010]

Yes, Google is a big chunk of it. And, nearly immediately measurable.

The campaign’s butterfly effect would also extend to more direct
site visits, an increase in word of mouth – driving second and third
order effects -, some/loads of press, increase in traffic to Rapha
retail stores and to other stores that also stock Rapha merchandise.

And, that’s not even all the effects. I’ve simplified it for the
sake of my visual.

In 2010, if you did, you would never report Brand Queries on Google by
itself. You could add Site Traffic for context [or ideally % Site
Traffic from Google, or even better, % Site Traffic from Brand Queries
on Google].

Better.

But, it was still missing a vast majority of the _butterfly effect_ of
brand Marketing.

And, brands are in such different places, each have such starkly
different strengths and weakness, such different competitive dynamics,
it would be difficult to know if you were missing a lot or little when
you reported just _Brand Queries on Google_.

Do you see my reticence in reporting this metric?

Fast-forward to 2024, the butterfly effect is still there, if not more
complex, and with a big change in consumer behavior that is the locus
of my concern now.

[Brand butterfly effect, 2024.]

[NOTE: Proportions above are illustrative, not actual.]

Consumer behavior when it comes to seeking has changed dramatically.

We still search on Google, loads and loads and loads.

We also search loads and loads on Amazon [and its type of site in
different countries], shifting commercial queries from Google - Do
intent.

We are using TikTok [and its type] loads, shifting See and Think
intent clusters from Google.

A small edge chunk of us are now using Perplexity and LLMs to seek,
starting to shifting See, Think, and Do intent clusters as well.

In China or Korea or Brazil, seeking across See-Think-Do-Care has
been disintermediated entirely by new platforms, impacting Naver,
Baidu, Google, et. al.

Some seeking behavior has entirely disappeared:

I no longer use images.google.com to look for free to use stock photos
for my presentations. I simply go to bing.com/create [7] and make my
own in 10 seconds.
So.

What does the metric _Brand Queries on Google_ represent now?

What’s the implication of using it to indicate/declare
success/failure of your brand Marketing campaign?

_Brand Queries on Google_ is not nothing.

It is also no longer _something _either.

Someone in your SEO team can probably use it to make sure any emerging
set of brand phrases now used on Google are being optimized for to
ensure organic rank #1. Even then, get the SEO team to understand and
execute against all of the blue clusters above.

Beyond that… Unless there is compelling evidence that the 2024
_butterfly effect _has left your business uniquely unaffected…
Remove the metric _Brand Queries on Google_ from any report or
dashboard for any audience Directors or higher in your company.

The way humans search is evolving at a rapid clip.

Don’t be left behind in 2010 when it comes to your analytics
strategy.

EFFECTIVE BRAND MARKETING ANALYTICS.

If we lose _Brand Queries on Google _as a metric to measure the
impact of our brand campaigns, what else can we do?

Good news: Between 2010 and 2024, we’ve had an explosion in our
ability to measure the effectiveness of brand Marketing – short,
medium, and long term.

From brand trackers (macro, market measure) to BLS to CLS to nMMMs to
SEM. So. Much. Sexiness.

Jump on all those bandwagons!

I’ve covered most of these extensively in previous Premium editions.
Start with TMAI #355: The Baklava of Brand Measurement. Then see parts
2 and 3. :)

BOTTOM LINE.

There is no such thing as a perfect metric.

Each metric comes with dimensions it hides, dimensions it reveals.

We use them when the reveals bit is material, and expose to senior
leaders what it cannot explain or actively hides.

Metrics sometimes become _unuseful _over time.

Interrogate every metric on your CMO dashboard by sketching its
_butterfly effect._

Carpe diem!

an advanced reflection, exploring a crucial nuance.

Seeking/searching is not a zero-sum game. Nothing above implies that.

If this distribution was true in 2010...

And evolves to this in 2024....

It does not imply a zero-sum for seeking.

2024 could very well be that consumer behavior is _eating_ into other
forms, and it now looks like...

What is entirely unlikely to be true as you reflect on 2010 > 2024 is
this...

And... Even it were... Remember, is not :)... Using _Brand Queries on
Google_ was not and is still not wise. If you already forgot why (!),
