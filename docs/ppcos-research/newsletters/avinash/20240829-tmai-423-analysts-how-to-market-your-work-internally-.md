Turns out, just providing data is not enough.

In fact, even sending out a deck full of analysis, good analysis, is
not enough.

Everyone says they want data, they say that their goal is to make
decisions with data (vs. faith). Sadly, the reality is that outside
your immediate team, Analytics teams can be an afterthought.

This reality shows up when you ask for additional headcount for the
Analytics teams, or budget to hire more Analysis Ninjas vs. Reporting
Squirrels, or when the department is doing annual promotions and not
enough Analytics people get promoted, etc.

Early in my career, this was upsetting as I knew our team was
delivering great work. But, perhaps due to my personality, we were
just doing the good work, and not really engaging in Marketing our
work.

It took me a while, but I grew to appreciate the value of PR.

It is important to show up in the right meetings. It is important to
suck up to the right senior people. It is important to go outside the
immediate ecosystem (Email team or Ecommerce team or Paid Search team)
and meet and share your work with other adjacent team (Finance,
Customer Service, Creative, Customer Insights & Research).  It is
important to ensure you are integrating with, or forming lose
relationships, with sister Analytics teams.  It is important to
bring your Agency to do a quarterly Big Mind Blowing Learnings of the
Quarter session to a wide audience.

I am the kind of person who believes my (good) work should speak for
itself, that an impact on the business should be enough. But. It is
not. So I do things that allow me to advance the cause of data, of the
people in my team working hard on analysis.

For many Analysts, this is a new muscle to exercise. Let me share what
I do.

[IRONIC] WHAT NOT TO DO: SEND INTERNAL NEWSLETTERS!

One of the most common internal PR tactics is to whip up a
bi-weekly/monthly newsletter titled: "Glorious, Incredible, News From
The Analytics Team!".

I understand why employees and leaders of small teams feel the need to
send newsletters.

You are not being noticed at all, summarizing your accomplishments in
a newsletter might _spread the news_. Then, when at a VP-level all
employees are being calibrated for annual performance/compensation,
everyone would have heard of your work and know how business altering
it was.

Trust me: I understand.

The challenge is that after two weeks… NO ONE will read your
newsletter for one simple reason:

Who has time to engage with your exercise in public relations?
How many weeks of someone’s LOOK AT ME I’M FREAKING AMAZING AND I
DID THINGS will you be able to tolerate?

Corporate newsletters suck due to this deadly trifecta:

1. The public relations exercise is targeted to reach one (or three)
VPs, but you usually spam an entire organization.

2. For the latter group, there is nothing to learn from, nothing to
act on.

3. Let's be honest, no team is doing such fantastic work each week
that it is worth _newslettering_. Implication: Any team's internal
newsletter, quickly becomes an exercise in pomposity and lightly
polishing coal to imply it is a diamond. People see through this more
than you might imagine.

I recoil at the stunning waste of time put into internal newsletters.

Most of all, I weep that for my Analytics peers these efforts do
nothing to advance the cause of data, increasing its use, and ensuring
a wide group of teams and leaders understand your meaningful
contribution.

Do a newsletter if your VP of Analytics or Marketing insists. Do your
best to make it as useful as you can. Recognize it does nothing and
will do nothing to solve your strategic challenge.

OK, BUT HOW SHOULD I MARKET MYSELF/MY TEAM WITHIN MY COMPANY?

It is important that your senior leaders know that you exist as a
rockin' team (or you exist as a high-performing individual
contributor) and you should be highly valued as such.

Here are my recommendations to ensure you are known and appreciated
(regardless of the size of company you work at):

1. DO WORK THAT BEGETS ATTENTION.

Before we get to the PR bits, I cannot resist. I believe in creating
facts on the ground, putting points on the board, before we start
Marketing. Let me start with that.

For the last two decades (yes, I am old!), the six words that capture
my career strategy are from the  [3]inimitable Steve Martin [3]:

“BE SO GOOD, THEY CAN’T IGNORE YOU.”
I do my very best, for myself, for the teams I lead, to work on
initiatives that REALLY matter. Then, deliver the best work that your
passion and intellect allows for.

That combination is critical.
_
_
_Does the thing you are working on really matter?

__Did you deliver a non-normal impact from your work, on the thing
that really matters?_

That leads to these three filters to apply to your work, honestly:

A. Things that don't matter much + Non-normal impact = : (

B. Things that matter much + Normal impact = Good.

C. Things that matter much + Non-Normal impact = Can't Be Ignored!
The third scenario ensures you won't be ignored in your immediate
team, your adjacent teams, and, in time with consistency, all the way
up the chain of command.

Even in extreme circumstances like, say, your immediate boss dislikes
you, or some other team takes credit for your work, or your secret
enemy Tony is trying to sabotage you. Even then, staying in C will
ensure your work and impact can't be ignored.

Fame, almost always, follows accomplishments.

And, look. You don’t need to knock the ball out of the park every
day.

Hitting at .300 over 162 games is really hard in Baseball. So,
reconfigure your work, train, build partnerships to ensure you hit
.350. You will be listened to when you are doing internal Marketing.
And, if you hit .400, you'll immediately be poached, at a higher
salary, but another team!

Your paid subscription to TMAI Premium already tells me that you are
passionate about career development and new knowledge. That in turn
implies that you are not going to have trouble with part two of the
success equation (delivering non-normal impact!).

The challenge then is the first part, working on projects that
actually matter. Trust me, this is not as easy as it sounds.

I have two tips for you to work on initiatives that actually matter:

1. TMAI #140 shares a stacked bar chart. Create it for yourself,
figure out:

[4]How Important Are You? [4]

The graph will change your (professional) life, and perhaps life life.

2. Conduct an exercise in deep self-reflection.

Is our team (/am I) working:

A. where the company earns the largest money,
B. where the company spends the most money,
C. where the company has the largest cost savings.
D. where the company’s next giant blue sky opportunities are.

If either A, B, C, D are true, you are golden. (If not, redo the graph
in #1 above.)

If you do work that matters, and do it well, senior leaders, other
employees, will come seek you out because what you do matters at their
altitude.

I understand that not every job matters at a department/company level.
Perhaps you’ve just gotten started with automated reporting in a
company. Keep doing that job as well as you can, all I’m saying is
have a clear plan to take the steps needed to get a job that matters
(i.e., a job where you will blow out expectations).

Let news come find you.

2. TEACH.

The second-best PR approach is not to talk, it is to teach.

Here’s an example.

Our team does a session - an hour - every six months called
_Analytics on the Edge_. It has the coolest and most important things
that we have done in the previous six months. What the questions were,
how we went about answering them (methodology), what the impact was,
why we failed (when we failed).

Our goal: TEACH PEOPLE HOW TO ASK US (AND DATA) HARDER QUESTIONS.

The end result is that the department where your team sits will be
smarter. They learn what new things are possible. They get a chance to
interrogate you. All desirable outcomes.

A non-obvious benefit of doing something like _Analytics on the
Edge _is that you would really have to understand what you and the
team are doing, what impact you are delivering, and how to improve
your storytelling. So great (did anyone tell you advanced analytics is
complicated to explain?).

Another example.

Your company likely has an onboarding program for new employees,
Marketers. Offer to teach a class, a session, on Marketing Analytics
in the company. Get to them first, to get them off vanity metrics, :),
and give them a sense for exactly how your team can help them get to a
career jump start with data.

One more.

A couple of months before the Annual Planning/Budgeting process, offer
to the Finance team that the Analytics team will lead a special
session for Directors and VPs. The objective will be to share how to
ask for more budgets using solid forecasting, using Level 4
methodologies, and predicting outcomes (not activity!).

You can see why the VPs would be attracted to such learnings, and the
benefits to the Analytics team.

When you teach, you are giving the gift of knowledge.

When you do a really good job with the stories, and frame them in the
spirit of _t__his is how it will make your job more
intelligent/easier/automated_ you will help - and your new best
friends will ensure the stories of what you do spread.

[Tip: If you do teaching sessions, try not to have even 2% devoted to
how hard you worked. Let the results you demonstrate do that for you.]

3. ENABLE YOUR SUPERHEROINES/SUPERHEROES TO TELL YOUR STORY.

Every day, your hard work contributes to the wild success of people
who activate your insights and actions. When this happens
consistently, these individuals are rightly seen as
_superheroes_ inside the company - because consistent winning is
rare!

Let them tell your stories.

No matter how small your company or department, there are always
meetings that your leadership pulls together to share updates, rile up
positive spirit, and more.

Find ways to have your Superheroines showcased in these sessions. By
the VP. Or, by one of the teams that you helped transform via smart
analytics.

If you get a chance to share your stories, obviously take it. But, in
these instances, I find that it is optimal for others to do the
talking. People who benefited from your work. Let them tell others and
carve a path for your team.

Often these meetings have a lot of people who are interested in
presenting, so the bar is higher for your work to be included. Hence,
my guidance that #1 above is the most important thing you can do to
help you, your team, your collective work - work on initiatives that
matter.

[Tip: One thing that’ll get your CMO to talk about you is if your
team wins an external award or some kind of industry recognition. A
lot of analytics work is private. Hence, it is harder to get awards.
But, if there is a chance, and your legal team approves, don’t be
shy about submitting for awards/recognitions/patents.]

4. CONSISTENTLY DELIVER.

Most people are shooting stars.

You want to be like the slow-moving comets that orbit our solar
system. :)

Most people (myself included) have a hard time delivering impact
through their work consistently.

Challenges you have to solve for:

Company cultures value non-valuable shiny objects.

It is difficult to stay focused on important things that are hard.

Finding insights is relatively easy when compared to how insanely
difficult it is to convert insights into action.

There’s always good old politics.
Reflect for a moment: Can you think of two teams in your entire
company that consistently delivered excellence for 24 months?

Hard, right?

To deliver consistently, I recommend building your annual plan with
Analytics action items clustered in each of these three clusters:

[5]Incremental innovation.  [5]

Incremental innovation with a side effect. [5]

Transformational innovation [5].
This will ensure you are obsessively focused on initiatives that will
result in consistently delivering _stories worth spreading via
delivered impact_.

An additional benefit: It is very exciting to work on these efforts.
To push our personal learning goals, and grow.

If you consistently deliver non-normal value, it is hard not to
ignore. Because. It is rare.

BOTTOM LINE.

Don’t write. Do.

Don't talk. Teach.

Don’t be a shooting star. Be consistent.

Don’t self-praise. Be worthy of others doing it for you.

I hope you see this as good advice for how to both market analytics
(and yourself),  and as a way to have a professional career that
soothes your soul.
