TMAI #391: CARE, DO, IMPACT: A PCA STORYTELLING FRAMEWORK -P2

[ Web Version [2] ]

You’ve either completed a Post-Campaign Analysis (PCA) in the recent
past, or are working on one right now.

PCAs are a key part of the fabric of our existence as Analysts.

Chances are close to 99% that the last PCA you created had 98 slides.
If one was created for you by a consulting company or an Agency, it
was probably 134 slides.

[As Agency people, and I am one, there is an understanding that
clients more often than not react positively to quantity.]

In TMAI #389, part one, I’d made the case for an end to these data
pukes that take up so much precious oxygen, and fall so far, far,
short of driving even minimal business change.

How do you change such deeply entrenched behavior?

Simple, with a storytelling framework that constrains the _ink _and
liberates the _think_.*

My framework for transformative PCAs is called: CARE-DO-IMPACT.

Please go back and read TMAI #389 [3], one more time. It will be
helpful for you to be familiar with the crucial importance of the Big
Open, especially _what did I get for my money_ slide.

I’d also shared slide examples you can emulate, along with the
game-changing influence of the Big Close slide with _Do Now, Impact
Now, Do Later_, and _Impact Later_.

CARE DO IMPACT FRAMEWORK | THREE STORIES, ONLY.

My framework recommends only sharing three short stories in the PCA.

This feels shocking._ WHAT! I SPENT $18M ON THIS CAMPAIGN!!_

Yes. Just three.

I genuinely understand that if you spent $18m, or $98m, the number of
reports you could produce would fill the inside of the Las Vegas
sphere.

I genuinely understand that for $18m, you probably executed 50
marketing tactics (though, 50 might have been a bit much).

I genuinely understand that if you are an internal analytics team, or
an external agency, you want to deliver a large quantity of data on
slides to show that you worked very hard.

Here’s the problem: Most PCAs, if you are lucky, will be an hour. In
that time, you have to deliver insights that will lead to action that
is big enough that it will be remembered – and you are given credit
for it.

That cannot happen if you present 120 slides with everything big,
medium, small, and minute.

There is also a tendency at Agencies to have the most junior employees
trawl through all kinds of data and reports, dump them into a 350
slide deck. Step two: Some senior employees will critique for the ink
and ask fonts and colors on sides and pie charts be changed. Step
three: A couple of people will come in after the _first preview_ to
the client to cut 350 slides into 120.

This _get me everything, the trees, weeds, dirt, roots, soil below the
roots_ and somehow we’ll see the forest view [4] is flawed.

This is exponentially more effective:

_Let’s find the five biggest lessons from this campaign, and present
the top three._
It works better because you will immediately discard tiny things, you
will ignore shiny objects, you will focus on the largest pools of
spend, start with the biggest experiments, and…. Start the analysis
by looking at where the biggest outcomes came from!

Focus. You get focus.

This approach, _start big_, won’t save you any time compared to the
350 slides _trawl for everything_ approach (though _start big_ will be
a lot less frustrating). Same amount of time, but spent going deeper
into avenues that matter. _Narrow deep, vs. shallow wide_.

Three also because in my work across dozens upon dozens of clients,
I’ve found that by the time you cover three big things, you’ve
covered 80 to 90% of what’s important. Rest is the _long fragmented
tail_.

Oh, and looking at all 50 marketing tactics, the _long fragmented
tail_, is in the $18m campaign at a detail level is of tactical value.
Do that. Work with the client’s smaller teams responsible for them.
No need to waste time making “pretty” slides, default GA reports,
Sheets, bullet points in an email, use the cheapest (in effort)
presentation technique to deliver your insights.

In the meeting with limited time with your senior most
leaders/clients, focus. Just three big stories. In detail. With
nuances exposed, your powerful actions can be discussed in context of
the predicted (big!) impact on the business.

The best PCAs spend 30% of the time on the data, 70% of the time
discussing, arguing about, the actions that need to be taken in the
next 60 days (_DO NOW_) and 120 days (_DO LATER_).

I cannot tell you how hard this is to do. Max hard.

But. When you get good at it, you immediately add a zero to your
hourly rate!

CARE DO IMPACT FRAMEWORK | CARE-DO-IMPACT.

Each big story in the PCA has three slides. One for Care. One for Do
Now (on left of the slide), and Impact Now (on the right). The last
one for Do Later, and Impact Later.

_1. WHY SHOULD YOU CARE?_

From the 17 reports you dug into to identify what's important,  your
challenge is to separate the wheat from the chaff. Then, to sketch the
perfect visual to represent the key insight.

It is essential that this slide does not have text next to the table
(/graph) that simply describes the information in the table/graph.
C_onversion rate for Martians during June was 1.7% vs. 2.7% for
Earthlings_. I can see that in the table, no need to also write it out
in text!

Explain WHY the line is up or down, the numbers in the table are
sad/happy.

The _why _is the magic.

Your greatest challenge is to lay out on your _why should you care_
slide, the connective tissue with what you are going to say in the
next slide that contains the _what should you do_.

[PRO TIP: Don’t make this slide first. Have three or four
candidates, written down. Leave them in the Sheet or Data Studio
report. Come back after you’ve done the _what should you do_ and
_what's the business impact_.]

[Example slide: Why Care.]

_2. WHAT SHOULD YOU DO?_

Recommend two or three big actions. Specific: _H__ence do this, do
that exact thing, _etc_._

[PRO TIP: Doing more analysis or more experiments or gathering more
data, are NOT business actions. Occasionally, they are necessary. But.
Your job is to recommend actions based on the data you do have. Your
actions can't just be more data work streams.]

Identifying business actions can be hard for Analysts, especially if
they sit at an Agency. Their challenge is that they are immersed in
data, but there are several degrees of separation between them and the
business.

To identify business action, you will have to become closer to the
business. Attempt to be BFFs with business leaders, the strategy team
doing competitive analysis, and front-line Marketers. Attend staff
meetings of your partner teams, if you can. Certainly, listen to their
All-Hands meetings and get a more profound understanding of their
priorities. Request access to the financial targets at any level you
can find them (targets = focus on this, this is important).

Make recommendations in two clusters. DO NOW – actions to take over
the next 60 days. DO LATER – actions to take over the next 120 days
(because they are more complicated, need a new vendor, etc.).

Worth repeating: Every DO is a specific action, tied to a specific
_Why Care_. Ex: Eliminate bids on brand keywords (Do) because CLS
indicated low incrementality (Care).

[PRO TIP: Do not finalize this slide quit yet, in fact don't even
create is quite yet. Note the actions, but come back to it after the
next step. You'll thank me.]

_3. WHAT’S THE PREDICTED BUSINESS IMPACT?_

This part is super challenging. And, exciting!

Look. It is difficult to find insights (CARE) in an ocean of data.
Then, challenging to find actions to recommend (DO). To have to
predict the outcomes from those actions (IMPACT), you need to be the
best.

Consider this simple example…

If your Do is:

_Invest more in Email Marketing as the Conversion Rate is 3x higher
than Facebook Ads._
Following my storytelling framework requires you to  predict the
Impact of the above Do. Which means answering:

_How much more revenue/profit will that result in over 60/120 days?_
To get to that answer…. You’ll have to answer all these questions:

_Where will all these new subscribers come from? _
_
How much efficiency can we eek out of the current creative being used
in the newsletters? _
_
What happens to Email frequency (and what’s the cost in both $$$ and
customer annoyance)? _
_
More promotions, fewer? _
_
How to balance between ecommerce and support content?_
_
And, a thousand more._
See what I mean?

Being able to predict the impact on the business from actions
recommended by data is what separates good Analysts from great
Analysts.   While incredibly challenging, it is such fun – besides
being immensely satisfying (/rewarding).

Oh, and if you are unable to compute business impact…

You most likely don’t have a clear enough Do to recommend.

At this point, often, it’ll hit you that you’ve not identified the
right Care either.
: (

Other times you get to the Impact stage, and it is so small…
You’ll realize you chased a mirage of a Care. Back to the drawing
board.

But. See the benefit of Impact?

You will NEVER go in front of a senior leader, and waste their
attention.

Also… See why I kept saying above, _don’t make your slides yet?_

Once you have a meaningful Impact, go make the slides for the Do-s
that tie to that Impact, and finally go make the slides for the Care-s
that tie to that Do. #winning!

With practice will come confidence, and you’ll codify the work.

At our agency, we have a spreadsheet with like 25 tabs. Each tab
computes the Impact of an action you can take on your site. Reduce
Bounce Rate or Cart Abandonment. Increase Page Depth or Recency.
Change Click-Through Rate or Amplification Rate. And, so on and so
forth.

Every client has their odd nuances, but, we have an excellent starting
point when we practice Care-Do-Impact.

[PRO TIP: Rank order your stories by business impact. Story One will
have the _gigantist _impact, you’ll present it when you have the
maximum attention from the senior leader.]

[PRO TIP: You’ll recognize that you are a Super Star Analyst when
you notice that along with computing Business Impact, you are also
computing Cost of Taking Action (CoTA). The latter is extremely hard,
especially when you are to compute _fully loaded costs_. But when you
do, you’ll notice clouds now spell your name!]

Spoiler Alert #1: Telling Stories with Data is Hard.

Look, you are not going to get this right the first time, or in a
large company/agency even for the first six months.

Your client Sr. Leaders will try to reject this change with the same
vociferousness they might a new kidney. In part because they don’t
know just how good you will be at actually bringing meaningful
insights and hugely impactful recommendations! In part because they
don’t trust that you will be able to work on the tactical bits with
their numerous, fragmented teams.

It will also take you a while to get good at this.

Most people in your Analytics team are report writers, _data puking_
is the norm (it is also so much easier!). And, even your Analysts are
going to have to stretch to get context, to understand the
business/strategy, to get better at predictive analytics.

It is not easy going for $40/hr work to $400/hr to $4000/hr work.

It takes a while.

It will take you a while.

It is ok.

Mandate the framework.

Give yourself a bit of time to live up to all the sky-high
expectations encoded in it.

Know that you are going to fall well short of the sky-high
expectations of the framework the first time you deliver a 12-slide
deck instead of an 80-slide data puke. But, you have my word, even the
first time you do it, your Sr. Leaders will fell the power of focusing
on three material stories and rank-ordered recommendations (vs. just
data).

Over time, your confidence will grow, so will your influence. You will
discover you are being called in during the genesis of the campaign,
so your voice can influence the future. You'll be invited into Board
meetings to explain how Marketing delivers incremental value!

Joy.

SPOILER ALERT: NO APPENDIX.

One of the things I discovered, with the normal numerous slides PCA,
was that the 120 slides presented were not the end. There were another
60 to 100 slides in the appendix!

A sure sign of PCAs built by going on, random, fishing expeditions.

My heart was broken from the recognition of all the precious _data
people _time wasted. It felt worse that all that slide making took
away from doing robust analysis of the data, which might yield
meaningful Care-Do-Impact stories.

Internal Analytics teams, Agencies, tend to do this because VPs,
Marketing Managers, CMOs often ask random, detailed, useless
questions. Then, quantity rules the day.

The only way out of this trap is reprogramming.

First, no appendix. Let’s please not waste time making slides.

Lots of reports. Sure. Lots of using tools to do analysis. Absolutely.
Lots of Sheets, if it helps. Fine. But. No slides (yet). No wasting
time in the wrong place.

Second. If the VPs, Marketers insist on data pukes, let’s deliver
them in the most efficient manner: Automatically produced
Excel/Sheets.

The last slide in our PCAs at Google, slide 13, said two things: A.
Thank you. B. A hyperlinked text: “Link to Data Puke.”

[Literally, that is what is said to emphasize what it was.]

It incentivized lots of good behavior. And, it got all the tactical
data with all the little bits that the individual teams would find
helpful - without time wasted making 100 more slides!

BOTTOM LINE.

The most important thing to take way from this series: Telling stories
with your data will transform data’s influence, and yours.

Leveraging the Care-Do-Impact framework will give your stories a
focus, a method through the madness that is analysis.

Got stories?
